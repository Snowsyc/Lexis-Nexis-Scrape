OUTCOME: Appellant's conviction and sentence in the lower court for complicity to smuggle drugs into prison was in violation of the Double Jeopardy Clauses of the Ohio and United States Constitutions.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDING: Criminal appeal from Common Pleas Court Case No. CR-257815.



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of a judgment from the Cuyahoga County Common Pleas Court (Ohio), which classified him as a sexual predator in accordance with Ohio Rev. Code § 2950.09(B), challenging the constitutionality of the sexual predator law, the adequacy of the trial court's hearing held prior to making the sexual predator determination, and the court's refusal to appoint an expert.


OVERVIEW: Nearly seven years after appellant pleaded guilty to two sexual battery counts with specifications, appellant was subjected to a hearing under Ohio Rev. Code § 2950(B)(1), which resulted in a determination that he was a sexual predator. Holding that the determination was not an unconstitutional ex post facto punishment, and that there was sufficient evidence to classify appellant under the law, the court affirmed the judgment. The court held that § 2950(B)(1) violated neither U.S. Const. art. I, § 10, nor Ohio Const. art. II, § 28. The court held that clear and convincing evidence supported the finding, including the evidence of the victims' young ages, the disparity in ages between the victims and appellant, and the variety of sexually deviant acts committed by appellant. The court held that the Ohio Rules of Evidence were not strictly applicable to a § 2950(B)(1) hearing, and that the court did not abuse its discretion in denying appellant's request for a court-appointed psychological expert, because appellant had cited no grounds for the appointment.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment classifying appellant as a sexual predator. Although the sexual predator law was passed after the commission of appellant's crimes, it was not a criminal proceeding, nor designed to punish appellant, and was, therefore, not an unconstitutional ex post facto determination. Clear and convincing evidence existed to classify appellant as a sexual predator.
