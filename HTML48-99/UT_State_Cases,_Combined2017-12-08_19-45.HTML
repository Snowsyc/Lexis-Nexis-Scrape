CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a jury verdict and various orders from the Third District Court, Salt Lake Department (Utah), in favor of plaintiff on plaintiff's claim for a partnership accounting and for one-third of the assets belonging to that partnership.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff accepted defendant's offer to become partners in a communications business. Years later, plaintiff left the business and filed an action requesting an accounting. Defendant appealed from a jury award, arguing that the trial court erred by: (1) instructing the jury on the applicable statute of limitations, (2) awarding prejudgment interest, and (3) failing to disqualify plaintiff counsel. Finally, plaintiff counsel's prior representation of defendant was not so substantially factually related to the present litigation that denial of motion to disqualify counsel prejudiced defendant.


OUTCOME: Judgment was affirmed in part and reversed in part. There was sufficient evidence for the jury to conclude that plaintiff's complaint was filed within the statute of limitations. Plaintiff was not, however, entitled to prejudgment interest because plaintiff failed to establish the factual predicate supporting his claim.

DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded for a new trial on the section 76-3-203.1 charge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a judgment of the Third District Court, Salt Lake County (Utah), convicting him of murder, and appealed enhanced sentences imposed by the district court pursuant to Utah Code Ann. § 76-3-203(1) and -203.1.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, prisoner sought appeal from a decision of the Third District Court, Salt Lake County (Utah), which imposed a minimum mandatory prison term of ten years for four counts of aggravated sexual assault under Utah Code Ann. § 76-5-405(1) (Supp. 1983).


OVERVIEW: The trial court imposed a minimum mandatory prison term of 10 years for the four counts of aggravated sexual assault, ruling that the volume and violent nature of the crimes, and the indications of prior criminal conduct constituted aggravating circumstances, which were mitigated by expert testimony, defendant's sincere sorrow, and the psychological trauma that defendant suffered as a result of his military service in Vietnam. On appeal, defendant challenged the constitutionality of the minimum mandatory terms for the convictions, arguing that the sentences imposed were unconstitutionally disproportionate and that the minimum mandatory sentence unconstitutionally denied him medical treatment. The court rejected defendant's claim that the trial judge failed to specifically state the grounds for sentencing, since the trial judge mentioned the mitigating factors which was all he was required to do when his sentencing choice was the middle term sentence.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's conviction and sentence of defendant for a minimum mandatory prison term of 10 years.

In re Crim. Investigation


No. 20268


Supreme Court of Utah


754 P.2d 633; 79 Utah Adv. Rep. 3; 1988 Utah LEXIS 31



March 31, 1988, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant attorney general challenged the decision of the district court (Utah), which held unconstitutional an act entitled Subpoena Powers for Aid of Criminal Investigation and Grants of Immunity, Utah Code Ann. §§ 77-22-1 to -3 (1982).


OVERVIEW: The state legislature passed an act that created a second method for formally investigating criminal activities in addition to the already authorized state grand jury. It provided the attorney general and county attorneys with the power to subpoena witnesses, grant transactional immunity, and conduct investigations in secret, all with limited judicial supervision. The district court held that the Subpoena Powers for Aid of Criminal Investigation and Grants of Immunity Act, Utah Code Ann. §§ 77-22-1 to -3 (1982) was unconstitutional. The court held that the differences between the state grand jury system and the investigative system permitted under the Act were not enough, standing alone, to rise to the level of a violation of the uniform operation of laws requirement of the state constitution. The mere existence of a discretionary power in the state's attorney to select which mechanism to use did not offend the uniform operation of the law requirement. The court reversed the district court's ruling that the Subpoena Powers Act was facially unconstitutional, holding that the Act survived constitutional scrutiny.


OUTCOME: The judgment was reversed that held that the Subpoena Powers Act (Act) was unconstitutional. The differences between the state grand jury system and the investigative system permitted under the Act were not enough, standing alone, to rise to the level of a violation of the uniform operation of laws requirement of the state constitution.

Supreme Court of Utah


754 P.2d 41; 77 Utah Adv. Rep. 11; 1988 Utah LEXIS 134



March 10, 1988, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant Public Service Commission of Utah (PSC) sought review of a declaratory judgment from the Third District Court (Utah), which awarded damages to plaintiff telecommunications companies upon the determination that one-way paging services were within PSC's jurisdiction. The telecommunications companies challenged a rule formulated by PSC as well as PSC's denial of an application for a certificate of public convenience and necessity.


OVERVIEW: As an initial matter, the court noted that common to each of the actions under review was the propriety of PSC's conclusion that it had no jurisdiction to regulate one-way paging communications services. The court then found that subject matter jurisdiction was nonexistent with respect to the request to review the dismissal of the application for a certificate, as the telecommunications companies failed to comply with Utah Code Ann. § 54-7-15 (1986) by not applying for a rehearing with the PSC prior to seeking a writ to the court. The court similarly held that the failure to request rehearing left the court without subject matter jurisdiction over the petition to review the PSC's rule-making procedure. Nevertheless, the court then held that it did have subject matter jurisdiction over the civil action for damages and subsequently determined that the declaratory judgment in favor of the telecommunications companies had been improper. The court concluded that the PSC's decision construing its authority to exclude one-way paging services was rational and reasonably based and represented an appropriate, strict construction of the statutes authorizing jurisdiction.


OUTCOME: Although the court determined that it lacked subject matter jurisdiction to contemplate the denial of the application for a certificate as well as the petition for review of the PSC's rule, the court reversed the declaratory judgment in favor of the telecommunications company and remanded that matter for further proceedings.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the order from the trial court (Utah), which convicted him of aggravated robbery, aggravated burglary, and aggravated assault. He contended that the prosecution used an inappropriate racial, racist method to select the jury by using a peremptory challenge to remove a panel member with a Hispanic surname.


OVERVIEW: Defendant, who was Hispanic, was tried in connection with the aggravated robbery, aggravated burglary, and aggravated assault of a 68-year-old woman. Defendant sought to quash the jury panel on the ground that the selection procedure used did not ensure a fair cross-section, and the trial judge supplemented the panel with two venirepersons with Hispanic surnames. After one was removed by the prosecution on a peremptory challenge, defendant unsuccessfully objected, claiming that the challenge was predicated upon race. The court held that the mere fact that the subject of the peremptory strike was a minority member did not alone raise the inference of discriminatory intent, and that it was not unconstitutional, without more, to strike one or more Hispanics from the jury.


OUTCOME: The court remanded the matter to the trial court for a determination of whether the peremptory challenge violated defendant's right to equal protection.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged his convictions on two counts of first degree murder in the trial court (Utah) on the ground that the evidence was insufficient to prove the first degree murder of his son or to establish aggravating circumstances in the death of his son's mother; photographs were improperly admitted; evidence was improperly admitted; the jury was "death qualified;" and the jury was racially biased.


OVERVIEW: Defendant allegedly threw his infant son into a river, where he drowned, and shot his son's mother to death, hiding her under a tree. The court rejected defendant's argument that the "death-qualified" jury did not represent a cross-section of society. Defendant had requested no hearing on the racial make-up of the jury below. The issue was waived.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for two counts of first-degree murder.

Salt Lake City v. Womack


No. 20712


Supreme Court of Utah


747 P.2d 1039; 71 Utah Adv. Rep. 37; 1987 Utah LEXIS 820



December 4, 1987, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a decision from the Fifth Circuit Court (Utah), which convicted her of driving under the influence of alcohol.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of driving under the influence of alcohol after she was stopped by the police during a routine traffic stop. The officer smelled alcohol and requested that defendant take a series of field sobriety tests. Based on her performance on the tests, she was arrested and later given a breathalyzer test that showed a blood alcohol content of .16 percent. Defendant challenged the admission of the breath test result on the ground that it did not have an adequate evidentiary foundation. Defendant asserted that her due process rights were violated by the short delay in the administration of the breathalyzer test. The lower court sustained the conviction. The court held that defendant's privilege against self-incrimination was not violated when she submitted to the breathalyzer test. The court noted that the 45 minutes prior to the test was clearly adequate to render the breathalyzer result reliable and that it could be inferred that the result accurately reflected the defendant's blood alcohol level at the time of the initial traffic stop.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the conviction of defendant for driving under the influence of alcohol, because her privilege against self-incrimination was not violated when she submitted to a breathalyzer test.
