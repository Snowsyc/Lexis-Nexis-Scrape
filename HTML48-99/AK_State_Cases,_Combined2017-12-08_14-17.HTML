Rutherford v. State


No. 1375


Supreme Court of Alaska


486 P.2d 946; 1971 Alas. LEXIS 304



July 1, 1971 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed a motion to dismiss the indictment returned against her on charges of second degree murder, alleging that her constitutional right to a speedy trial was violated. The superior court (Alaska) denied the motion and defendant was convicted of involuntary manslaughter. Defendant sought review.


OVERVIEW: Defendant's trial did not begin until 14 months after the original complaint, arrest, and arraignment were filed. For 10 of those months, defendant's case remained completely dormant. The court found that defendant's failure to demand a speedy trial did not constitute a waiver and that the burden was on the State to give a speedy trial. The court determined that it was irrelevant whether the State or the congested trial calendar caused the delay because defendant had been substantially prejudiced either way. Reversing, the court held that the delay in bringing defendant to trial was excessive and violated her constitutional rights. The court gave retroactive effect to a prior decision that held a less than 14 month delay excessive because the purpose of the holding was primarily related to the integrity of the fact-finding process and application of the holding was not outweighed by a negative effect on the administration of justice.


OUTCOME: The court reversed defendant's judgment of conviction and remanded the case with instructions to dismiss the indictment with prejudice.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment from a trial court (Alaska), which convicted him of felony escape in violation of Alaska Stat. § 11.30.090 (Michie).

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner state challenged the decision of the Superior Court, Fourth Judicial District, Fairbanks (Alaska), which ruled that the disparate treatment of respondent minor who was allocated the burden of proving his amenability to treatment pursuant to Alaska Stat. § 47.12.030(a) after being charged with a serious felony and convicted of a lesser offense denied respondent the equal protection of the law guaranteed by Alaska Const. art. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In consolidated appeals, defendants were arrested for driving while intoxicated and either refused to take a breath test or tested at higher than the legal limit. After having their licenses revoked, the defendants filed motions asking the District Court for the Third Judicial District (Alaska) to dismiss the pending criminal prosecutions on the grounds of double jeopardy. All but one motion for dismissal was granted.


OVERVIEW: In the various cases before the trial court, the defendants' faced prosecution by either the State of Alaska or the Municipality of Anchorage. All the defendants' argued that because they had already lost their driver's licenses for the same conduct that formed the basis of the criminal prosecutions they had already been punished once and could not be punished again. The court found that the criminal prosecutions did not violate double jeopardy because the license revocations served a remedial not a punitive goal. The court rejected the defendants' argument that the license revocation was a punitive measure for the purposes of double jeopardy merely because it did not compensate the government for a loss. As such, revocation was remedial in nature. The court also found that the mere fact that revocation served to deter misconduct, a goal of punishment, did not defeat the primarily remedial character of revocation.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the dismissals of the criminal prosecutions of the defendants granted by the trial court and affirmed the one decision to deny dismissal.
