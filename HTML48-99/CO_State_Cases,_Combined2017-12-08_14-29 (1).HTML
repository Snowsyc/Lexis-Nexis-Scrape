PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Interlocutory Appeal from the District Court of the City and County of Denver. Honorable Raymond Dean Jones, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Ruling Reversed and Cause Remanded with Directions.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The People appealed an order from the District Court, City and County of Denver (Colorado) that suppressed a gold watch seized during the execution of a search warrant that did not include the watch as one of the articles to be seized.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was charged with criminal attempt to commit felony-theft, stemming from a claim made by defendant to an insurance company for a loss due to an alleged theft of the gold watch from his residence. The watch was seized from his home during a search pursuant to warrant for stolen property believed to be located there. The district court suppressed the watch on the ground that the People failed to establish an adequate nexus between the watch and the specific criminal activity being investigated under the warrant. Reversing, the court concluded that the district court applied an inappropriate standard in suppressing the evidence. The standard was unduly restrictive. Under the plain view doctrine, there was a prior valid intrusion into defendant's residence. The discovery of articles, including the watch, was inadvertent. The parties conceded that the articles were discovered during the course of a permissible search for numerous articles of jewelry particularly described in the warrant. They also conceded that a police officer had no reason to anticipate the discovery of the gold watch and other suppressed items during the execution of the warrant.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the order and remanded with directions to permit the parties to offer evidence directed to the proper standard.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant taxing authorities appealed the judgment of the District Court, City and County of Denver (Colorado), which granted a pre-seizure hearing to plaintiff secured creditors before the execution of tax liens against the taxpayer.


OVERVIEW: The taxing authorities, the city and state departments of revenue, seized the taxpayer's property and sold some of it to satisfy delinquent taxes. The taxpayer's property and fixtures secured the creditors' liens. The creditors brought the instant suit to prevent the sale of the remaining chattels. The district court stayed the sale pending outcome of the action and the creditors deposited the amount of the delinquent taxes into the registry of the court. The taxing authorities were ordered to look to the monies on deposit, and not to the taxpayer's remaining chattels, for satisfaction of their liens. The taxing authorities contended that the creditors were not entitled to a pre-seizure hearing. A secured creditor who did not have possession of a security was not entitled to a hearing before seizure of a security under a prior tax lien. OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the district court.

OVERVIEW: The trial court found § 18-7-206 unconstitutional because its language could be construed to apply to nearly anyone knowingly selling goods or services to a prostitute, including doctors, lawyers, grocers, and tradespersons generally. OVERVIEW: Defendant allegedly threatened to hurt a victim until she submitted to intercourse with him. He was convicted of first-degree sexual assault under Colo. Rev. Stat. § 18-3-402(1)(b) (1973) and the court affirmed his conviction. Under § 18-3-402(1)(b) (1973) an actor was guilty of sexual assault if he threatened to inflict "extreme pain" on a victim in order to compel submission assuming that the victim believed his threats. Although defendant asserted that § 18-3-402(1)(b) (1973) was unconstitutionally vague, the court found that he had not met his burden of demonstrating the statute's unconstitutionality beyond a reasonable doubt. An information was sufficient if it advised the defendant of the charges he faced so that he could adequately defend himself and be protected from further prosecution for the same offense. Finally, the court held that it was for the jury to decide the nature of the threats defendant allegedly made.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction.
