CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The City of Portland challenged an order from the District Court of Multnomah County (Oregon), which held that the "unlawful camping" ordinance under which defendant was charged was unconstitutionally vague and overbroad under the Oregon Constitution.


OVERVIEW: The trial court granted defendant's demurrer to a complaint charging him with "unlawful camping." OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's ordering sustaining defendant's demurrer to the complaint charging him with "unlawful camping." The court remanded the cause for trial.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, four persons whose cases were consolidated for trial, sought review of their convictions for disorderly conduct, in the Washington County District Court (Oregon). Defendants were charged with unlawfully and recklessly creating a risk of public inconvenience, annoyance, and alarm by obstructing vehicular traffic on a public way by passing out leaflets and attempting to sell newspapers, in violation of Or. OVERVIEW: Defendants primarily argued that the facts failed to constitute an offense within the purview of §166.025(1)(e), which was unconstitutionally overbroad when applied to protected free speech, and was unconstitutionally vague. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff individual and defendant city appealed from a judgment of the Circuit Court, Jackson County (Oregon), which granted summary judgment to defendant property owners and dismissed the complaint as to the property owners and dismissed the city's cross-claim. The individual sought to recover damages against the city and the property owners for injuries incurred when she fell on a sidewalk in front of the property owner's property.


OVERVIEW: Prior to the enactment of the Oregon Tort Claims Act, Or. Rev. Stat. § 30.260 et seq., a city was liable for damages to persons injured as a result of a dangerous sidewalk; the abutting landowner had no liability. The city could exempt itself from liability by making the abutting landowner liable to persons injured as a result of an owner's failure to maintain the sidewalk, but the alternative remedy was required to be adequate. Here, the city's charter imposed liability on the abutting landowner to persons injured as a result of the owners' failure to maintain the sidewalk, but it expressly limited the owner's liability to an amount no greater than one-half the value of the owner's land. The court declined to sever the invalid portion of the charter because the limitation on the owners' liability was such an important part of the entire provision that the court could not say the provision would have been adopted without it. Thus, the charter failed to exempt the city from liability.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
