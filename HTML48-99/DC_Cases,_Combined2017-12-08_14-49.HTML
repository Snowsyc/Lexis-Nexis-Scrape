Roberts v. United States

743 A.2d 212; 1999 D.C. App. LEXIS 301


September 30, 1999, Argued  
December 30, 1999, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the Superior Court of the District of Columbia. (Hon. Henry F. Greene, Motions Judge). (Hon. Stephanie Duncan-Peters, First Trial Judge). (Hon. Colleen Kollar-Kotelly, Second Trial Judge).



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed his conviction by a jury in the Superior Court of the District of Columbia, of one count of carnal knowledge of a child under sixteen years of age and one count of rape, in violation of D.C. Code § 22-2801 (repealed 1995).


OVERVIEW: Defendant repeatedly molested and raped his cousin over a two-year period, beginning when she was fifteen. He was convicted of carnal knowledge of a child under sixteen and rape, both in violation of D.C. Code § 22-2801 (repealed 1995). Defendant appealed, contending the indictment was unconstitutionally vague and that the prosecution's evidence constructively amended the allegedly defective indictment. In affirming, the court noted that the particularity of time was not essential to the indictment as the young victim could not be expected to recall exact dates and times, especially where she was afraid to report the abuse contemporaneously. As the indictment provided a time frame with a distinct beginning and a clear end within which the crimes were committed, it satisfied constitutional requirements. The court rejected defendant's argument of constructive amendment as it construed the indictment of alleging a series of incidents and not as one such incident.


OUTCOME: Defendant's conviction was affirmed as the indictment against him was not unconstitutionally vague where it provided a precise time frame during which defendant was alleged to have committed the crimes even though it did not contain exact dates.

DISPOSITION: Remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant mother was convicted of violating the Compulsory School Attendance Act, D.C. Code Ann. § 31-402 (1995), in the Superior Court of the District of Columbia. Appellant challenged the statute as unconstitutional for vagueness and asserted that the lower court abused its discretion by refusing to authorize defense counsel to hire an expert child psychologist at government expense.


OVERVIEW: The court rejected appellant mother's constitutional argument, but remanded for entry of an order authorizing the services of a child psychologist. It held that the lower court judge correctly construed the Compulsory School Attendance Act, D.C. The court remanded the case with instructions to authorize appellant mother's counsel to hire a child psychologist.


OUTCOME: The court determined that the Compulsory School Attendance Act was not constitutionally vague because it established a reasonable efforts standard, gave notice of proscribed conduct, and provided warning as to criminal liability. However, where a reasonable attorney would have defended on a school phobia theory, the court found error in refusing to authorize the hiring of a child psychologist and remanded for an order to do so.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant owner appealed a decree from the Superior Court of the District of Columbia, which declared a forfeiture and condemnation of his truck under the Safe Streets Forfeiture Act of 1992 (Act), D.C. Code Ann. § 22-2723 (1996), claiming a violation of the Eighth Amendment Excessive Fines Clause.


OVERVIEW: The owner was convicted of soliciting a supposed prostitute, who was actually an undercover police officer. After the trial court declared a forfeiture and condemnation of his truck under the Act, the owner appealed. The court reversed. The forfeiture was deemed a constitutionally excessive fine. The forfeiture was, at least in part, punishment for his solicitation of the undercover officer and was therefore a fine subject to Eighth Amendment scrutiny. Solicitation for prostitution, particularly for a first conviction, was treated as a minor crime in the District of Columbia. The owner was a first time offender. The Act sought to form part of the arsenal of steps aimed at the problem of prostitution. The Act had at least some punitive aspect and, as such, came within the protection of the Excessive Fines Clause.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's decree of forfeiture and condemnation of the owner's truck.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, District of Columbia (district) and officials, sought review of a declaratory judgment entered by the Superior Court of the District of Columbia, Tax Division that the Gross Receipts Tax Amendment Act of 1987 and the Gross Receipts Tax Amendment Emergency Act of 1987 were unconstitutional. On cross-appeal, appellee carriers requested reversal of the decision insofar as it rejected their claims of unconstitutionality on other grounds.
