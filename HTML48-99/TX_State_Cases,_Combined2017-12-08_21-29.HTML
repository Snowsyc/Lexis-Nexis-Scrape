Adams v. State


No. 36679


Court of Criminal Appeals of Texas


376 S.W.2d 832; 1964 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 908



April 1, 1964 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the judgment from a trial court (Texas), which entered a conviction of driving while license suspended. Defendant contended the complaint did not sufficiently apprize him under which statute and subsection he was charged.


OVERVIEW: Defendant appealed his conviction of driving while license suspended. Defendant contended the complaint did not sufficiently apprize him under which statute and subsection he was charged. The complaint and the information merely charged that defendant drove a motor vehicle upon the public highway when his operator's license was suspended On appeal the court held that under the Motor Vehicle Law, Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6687b, and the Texas Motor Vehicle Safety Responsibility Act, Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6701h, a complaint and information charging the offense of driving while license suspended was subject to exception or motion to quash on the ground that it failed to recite under which article the license was suspended. The court overruled defendant's contention that both articles were unconstitutional because they both charged the same offense but provided different punishments. The court held that each was a separate offense, the difference being the grounds upon which the license was canceled or suspended. The court reversed the judgment and dismissed the prosecution under the complaint.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment and dismissed the prosecution under the complaint. A complaint and information charging the offense of driving while license suspended was subject to exception or motion to quash on the ground that it failed to recite under which article the license was suspended.

Mason v. State


No. 36438


Court of Criminal Appeals of Texas


375 S.W.2d 916; 1964 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 880



March 4, 1964 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of his conviction for aggravated assault of a peace officer, arguing that the trial court (Texas) erred in denying his motion for a change of venue and challenging the sentence imposed as excessive.


OVERVIEW: Based on evidence that appellant struck a police officer while in jail, the jury convicted appellant of aggravated assault of a police officer and imposed the maximum punishment provided by law. Appellant challenged his conviction, arguing that the trial court erred in overruling his motion for a change of venue and that his punishment was excessive. In a substituted opinion, the court affirmed the conviction and sentence. Because there was no evidence that appellant was prejudiced by the change of venue, a reversal was not required for a refusal to change venue. Moreover, appellant was not entitled to a change of venue in the misdemeanor case. With regard to punishment, the court explained that it was without authority to reduce appellant's sentence or to set aside the conviction upon the theory that the punishment was excessive despite its belief that a reduction of punishment was appropriate. Authority to grant pardons, reprieves, and commutations of punishment was vested in the governor on the recommendation of the Board of Pardons and Paroles (Texas).


OUTCOME: The court affirmed appellant's conviction for aggravated assault of a peace officer and his corresponding sentence. Appellant was not entitled to a change of venue in the misdemeanor case and the court did not have authority to reduce appellant's sentence despite its belief that such a reduction was appropriate.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment from the County Court at Law No. 2, Lubbock County (Texas), convicting him and fining him for speeding. Defendant appealed on grounds that officers impermissibly remained in hiding in order to trap him while speeding, and that failure to grant his motion for instructed verdict for insufficiency of the evidence was error.


OVERVIEW: Officers parked in a cotton field with their lights off observed defendant going 75 miles per hour in a 55 mile per hour speed zone. Officers used a portable radar device to measure defendant's car speed. Defendant appealed his conviction for speeding on grounds that officers impermissibly set up a speed trap in violation of Tex. Defendant claimed that officers were not qualified to testify about how the radar device worked and that experts were needed for this kind of testimony. The officers had testified that they had received instructions and had been trained in the device's operation and how to properly test for its accuracy. They conducted these tests both before and after defendant's speed was measured. The results were confirmed by a speedometer test. The court found the officers' testimony was sufficient, refusing to hold that expert testimony was needed to prove the device's accuracy or the speedometer's accuracy. The judgment was affirmed.


OUTCOME: Defendant's conviction and fine for speeding were affirmed. The statute prohibiting officers from lying in wait in order to observe speeding motorists was found unconstitutional. Expert testimony was not needed to prove the accuracy of the radar device that recorded defendant's car speed.
