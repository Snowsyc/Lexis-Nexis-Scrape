Supreme Court of New York, Warren County


87 Misc. 2d 592; 386 N.Y.S.2d 313; 1976 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2261



July 20, 1976 



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Accordingly, respondent's motion is denied in all respects.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner citizens filed a proceeding, pursuant to N.Y. Pub. Off. Law § 36, to remove respondent elected official from the office of town supervisor. The matter was before the court on the elected official's motion to dismiss on the grounds that § 36 was unconstitutional or, in the alternative, to adjourn the proceeding pending termination of all criminal charges against him.


OVERVIEW: The citizens sought to remove the elected official from his position as town supervisor due to his alleged misconduct in office. The elected official filed a motion to dismiss on the ground that N.Y. Pub. Off. Law § 36 was unconstitutional. In the alternative, the elected official sought to adjourn the proceeding pending termination of all criminal charges pending against him. The court denied the elected official's motion to dismiss and his motion to adjourn the proceeding. According to the court, § 36 was constitutional and was designed to protect the citizens from the misconduct or malfeasance of elected officials. The court found that the elected official could be removed from office for violations of § 36 incurred in his prior term of office and that his re-election did not constitute a condonation of the alleged acts of misconduct. The court further found that the citizens were not collaterally estopped from asserting grounds for the elected official's removal because there had been no litigation to that date, and the criminal proceeding was of a totally different nature. The court stated that the citizens were required to prove their case by a preponderance of the evidence.


OUTCOME: The court denied the elected official's motion to dismiss the citizens' action to have him removed from the office of town supervisor or, in the alternative, to adjourn the proceeding pending termination of all criminal charges against him.


HEADNOTES

Public officers -- removal for misconduct -- section 36 of Public Officers Law, providing for removal from office of town or village official for misconduct, maladministration, malfeasance or malversation, is not unconstitutionally vague or overbroad, since case law distinguishes actual breach from mere oversight, reducing requirements to determinable scope of conduct -- respondent may be removed for crimes committed in previous term -- since electorate was not apprised of all charges against respondent, principle of condonation of disclosed conduct cannot be considered -- removal proceedings should not be stayed pending resolution of criminal charges, since removal is appropriate and exclusive statutory remedy -- there is no collateral estoppel, since there has been no prior litigation, and removal differs totally from criminal proceedings -- respondent's Fifth Amendment rights can be fully protected regardless of order in which criminal and removal proceedings are held -- petitioners must establish guilt by preponderance of evidence.

1. Section 36 of the Public Officers Law, which provides for removal from office [***2] of a town or village official for misconduct, maladministration, malfeasance or malversation, is not unconstitutionally vague or overbroad, since case law distinguishes between actual breach and mere oversight, reducing the statute's requirements to a determinable scope of conduct.

2. While section 36 of the Public Officers Law refers to wrongdoing "in office", nothing therein restricts its operation to a current term in office and respondent may be expelled for crimes committed in a previous term.

3. Since the electorate was not apprised of all charges against respondent, there can be no consideration of the principle of condonation of disclosed conduct.

4. Removal proceedings should not be stayed pending resolution of criminal charges, since removal is the appropriate and exclusive statutory remedy, and, in the interests of fairness to both respondent and the citizenry, should proceed expeditiously.

5. The Grand Jury recommendation that the District Attorney proceed against respondent does not collaterally estop the assertion of grounds for removal. There has been no litigation, and removal proceedings differ totally from criminal proceedings.

6. Respondent's Fifth Amendment [***3] rights can be fully protected regardless of the order in which removal and criminal proceedings are held.

7. Petitioners must establish guilt by a preponderance of the evidence, and no analogy exists between a removal proceeding and the reasoning applying the adult criminal standard to juvenile delinquency proceedings.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner mother sought counsel fees in a proceeding that was brought against respondent father to collect child support. The father argued that the award was unjustifiable because of the mother's and/or the father's income and that N.Y. Fam. Ct. OVERVIEW: The mother moved for an order directing the father's payment of the mother's counsel fees in the mother's proceeding for child support. In response, the father argued that the award was unjustifiable because of the mother's and/or the father's income and that § 438, which authorized the fee, unconstitutionally differentiated between males and females. In awarding the mother counsel fees, the court held that it was inappropriate to consider the mother's resources in relation to her application for counsel fees because precedent viewed child-support proceedings as of legal interest only to children, who were obviously without means to pay counsel. However, in reducing the fee award, the court held that it was required to take into account the father's reduced means and substantial debts. Finally, in upholding § 438's constitutionality, the court held that the statute could be construed in accordance with its fundamental purpose to permit an award of legal fees to a male as well as a female, even if the legislature had not specifically contemplated the economic or legal developments relevant to fee awards on an equal basis for both sexes.


OUTCOME: The court awarded but reduced the mother's counsel fees.

 [***1] Parent and child -- child support -- counsel fees -- while petitioner mother's source of income from gifts is stable income which would be includible in determining her means and while mother benefits from father's child-support payments, her resources should not be considered in fixing counsel fees in child-support proceeding, since it would work too great departure from precedent holding that payments are for children's benefit and legal services provided are necessaries for which father is liable -- requested counsel fee of $ 10,000 is too great in view of respondent's reduced means, and fee of $ 950 should be awarded, with $ 3,000 payable if respondent's taxable income increases to $ 50,000 -- no escalating support order should be made based upon respondent's income, since petitioner, also liable for child support, may have increased income -- section 438 of Family Court Act is not nullified by unconstitutionality in allowing counsel fees to females and not males, since infirmity is curable by allowing fee awards on equal basis to both sexes in accord with legislative policy to authorize appropriate fees for spouses and children.

1. While the petitioner mother's source [***2] of income from gifts is as stable and predictable as income from earnings or investments and would be includible in determining her means, the resources of the mother should not be utilized in fixing counsel fees in a child-support proceeding. Although the mother benefits from any payments awarded and is also obligated to provide support, to consider the mother's resources would work too great a departure from precedent holding that support payments are for the benefit of the children and the legal services provided are necessaries for which the father is liable.

2. The requested counsel fee of $ 10,000 is too great in view of respondent's reduced means; and a fee of $ 950 should be awarded, with an additional $ 3,000 payable if respondent's taxable income increases to $ 50,000.

3. There should not be an escalating support order dependent upon respondent's income, since petitioner's income may also increase and she also has an obligation for child support.

4. Section 438 of the Family Court Act is not nullified by unconstitutionality in allowing counsel fees to females and not to males, since any infirmity is curable by allowing fee awards on an equal basis to both sexes in accord [***3] with the legislative policy to authorize appropriate fees for spouses and children.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff mortgagors brought action pursuant to N.Y. C.P.L.R. 902, 903, and 904 for an order permitting action against defendant mortgagee under Truth-in-Lending Act, 15 U.S.C.S. § 1601 et seq., to be maintained as four class actions.

Plaintiffs then filed a motion to permit action to be maintained as four class actions. Defendant contended that the Act did not authorize class actions. The court held, however, that the Act referred to the bringing of a class action and set forth a maximum total recovery, thus class actions were specifically authorized by the Act. The court applied the predominance test and held that plaintiffs had established that there were questions of law and fact common to the proposed classes that predominated over any questions affecting individual class members. The fact that defendant's proposed defense was circumstantial or complex was insufficient to divest the class action of superiority. The court also noted that plaintiffs' claims were typical of those of proposed class.


OUTCOME: The court permitted plaintiffs to certify one class. 1. An action to [***2] impose a penalty against defendant bank for alleged violations of the Federal Truth-in-Lending Act (US Code, tit 15, § 1601 et seq.) and Regulation Z (12 CFR Part 226) in connection with the financing of real property transactions, may properly be maintained as a class action under CPLR article 9 with a class consisting of all persons who have received mortgage loans from defendant between July 15, 1974 and July 17, 1975 and who were not provided with the required disclosures. Plaintiffs obtained a mortgage loan from defendant in order to purchase a three-family house in Rochester, New York. Plaintiffs allege that between July 15, 1974 and July 17, 1975 the disclosure statements delivered by defendant to mortgagors disclosed inaccurate annual percentage rates, that defendant unlawfully imposed insurance premiums for the benefit of the mortgagee which were not included in calculating the annual percentage rates, that defendant disclosed inaccurate amounts as title examination fees, and that defendant disclosed inaccurate amounts as "prepaid finance charges". The Truth-in-Lending Act, by setting the maximum total recovery in class action cases at "the lesser of $ 100,000 or 1 [***3] per centum of the net worth of the creditor" (US Code, tit 15, § 1640, subd [a], par [2], cl [B]) permits recovery of the civil penalties provided for therein through the vehicle of a class action even though a statement expressly authorizing the maintenance of such class action to recover a penalty is not set forth therein.

2. The action may properly be maintained as a class action under CPLR article 9 since the common factual questions relating to the procedures which defendant maintained to avoid errors in the required disclosures and the common questions of law of whether defendant's disclosures violated the statute predominate over any questions affecting individual class members. Plaintiffs will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class notwithstanding the fact that they seek their full $ 1,000 maximum statutory penalties as individuals (US Code, tit 15, § 1640, subd [a], par [2], cl [A]) while also attempting to recover potentially reduced awards for the represented class. Plaintiffs' proposed notice to potential members of the class fully disclosing in plain and easily comprehensible language, the ranges of recovery as a class member and as an [***4] individual, as well as the relative rights, risks and responsibilities of each class member in a class action as opposed to an individual action, satisfies the interests of justice by giving each proposed class member a choice of procedural remedies and allowing participation by aggrieved persons who might otherwise be deprived of a remedy because of the practical hardships characteristic of such litigations. The fact that plaintiffs may receive a larger monetary award than the class members is not sufficient to render their representation inadequate. The interest of plaintiffs is coextensive with that of the proposed class members to the extent that plaintiffs will be obliged to prove, even if they sue individually, that defendant's procedures were inadequate to the entire class.

DISPOSITION:  [***1] For the foregoing reasons, the motion is, in all respects, denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In moving dismiss an indictment, defendant attacked the constitutionality, on due process and equal protection grounds, of N.Y. Jud. Law § 664, by which government employees were barred from grand jury service.


OVERVIEW: Defendant attacked the constitutionality of § 664, which barred government employees from grand jury service, claiming that the statute violated due process and equal protection. In upholding § 664 on equal protection grounds, the court held there was no showing of how the statutory exclusion adversely affected defendant. The court held that it was not alleged, nor could it be alleged, that defendant was singled out for special or unequal treatment, different from that afforded every other indicted defendant in New York. The court also held that because defendant, who was not a public employee, was not a member of the excluded class, it was all the more tenuous to claim that the class's expulsion had a constitutionally unequal impact upon defendant. In upholding § 664 on due process grounds, the court held that § 664 was constitutionally sound and that it did not excise, without justification, a segment of the population in a way which unsuitably deprived defendant of grand jury cross-sectionality. In so holding, the court noted that § 664 prevented public employees from investigating official corruption and allowed public employees to serve other compelling state interests.


OUTCOME: The court denied the motion.


HEADNOTES

Grand Jury -- constitutionality of exclusion of government employees -- upon motion by defendant seeking dismissal of indictment, whereby he attacks constitutionality, on due process and equal protection grounds, of section 664 of Judiciary Law by which government employees are barred from Grand Jury service, as result of which, defendant claims, Grand Jury which indicted him was illegally constituted, motion is denied; on neither ground did defendant carry his burden of establishing any constitutional ailment.

Upon a motion by defendant, seeking dismissal of an indictment, whereby he attacks the constitutionality, on due process and equal protection grounds, of section 664 of the Judiciary Law by which government employees are barred from Grand Jury service, as a result of which, defendant claims, the Grand Jury which indicted him was illegally constituted, the motion is denied. The mere employment of the term "unequal protection" in defendant's moving papers does not even begin to make it so, and there is no showing of how he is affected at all by it, much less adversely affected. For equal protection [***2] purposes, the motion is rejected that defendant, not alleged to be a public employee, is somehow unequally treated by the elimination of public employees from all Grand Juries in New York State. Nor, for due process purposes, has defendant carried his burden of establishing any constitutional ailment. The disqualification is based on the belief that Grand Juries, because they investigate official corruption, should not be composed of public employees, and it has long been recognized that the Sovereign may constitutionally exclude from jury service certain professions or groups because the exclusion would free its members to serve other compelling State interests.

State ex rel. McNeil v. New York State Board of Parole


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Supreme Court of New York, Dutchess County


87 Misc. 2d 497; 385 N.Y.S.2d 731; 1976 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2239



June 30, 1976 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a decision by the New York State Board of Parole, which revoked defendant's parole and ordered him to be held to the maximum expiration date of his sentence without consideration of any good behavior allowances.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of robbery in the third degree, after which he was paroled. Subsequently, he was arrested for alleged parole violations and given a parole revocation hearing. At the conclusion of the hearing the board revoked defendant's parole and ordered him to be held to the maximum extent of his sentence, without consideration of any good behavior allowance. Defendant moved by way of writ of habeas corpus for his release. On appeal the court ordered the board to recompute defendant's sentence by allowing him any good behavior credits to which he may have become entitled. The court held that N.Y. Correct. Law § 803(1), denying good behavior allowances to a parolee with less than one year of his maximum term remaining, effected unequal treatment of inmates who were paroled and then returned to custody. The court stated that there was no logical basis for differentiating between violators who owed more than one year on their maximum sentences and those who owed less in determining if they were eligible for good behavior allowances. The court found that such a procedure clearly discriminated against a certain segment of the class and resulted in unequal treatment.


OUTCOME: The court ordered the board to recompute defendant's sentence by allowing him any good behavior credits to which he may have become entitled since his re-incarceration.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Parole -- revocation -- good behavior time allowance -- subdivision 5 of section 803 of Correction Law, which permits reincarcerated parole violator to receive good behavior time allowance against unexpired maximum of his sentence only if remaining portion of maximum term "is more than one year", unconstitutionally results in unequal treatment for inmates in same class -- plastic bottles containing methadone residue and unanswered parking tickets discovered in petitioner's car by parole officer and 40 rounds of ammunition discovered in petitioner's room were not obtained as result of unlawful search and seizure; parolee expressly consents to search of his person or residence as condition of his parole ( 7 NYCRR 1915.10) -- parole board must furnish petitioner with statement of reasons for determination revoking parole and of evidence relied upon ( 7 NYCRR 1925.35 [1]).

1. Subdivision 5 of section 803 of the Correction Law, which provides that a parole violator is entitled to good behavior time allowances against the unexpired maximum of his sentence following his reincarceration only if the remaining portion of his maximum term "is more than one year", unconstitutionally results [***2] in unequal treatment of inmates. Parole violators who have been returned to custody are all members of the same class. There is no logical or reasonable basis for differentiating between violators who owe more than one year on their maximum sentences and those who owe less in determining whether they shall be eligible to receive good behavior allowances against the remaining portion of their sentences.

2. Petitioner's parole was properly revoked, resulting in his reincarceration, following a parole revocation hearing for illegally possessing methadone, amassing a large number of unpaid parking violations and possessing 40 rounds of ammunition for a .35 caliber rifle. After observing a puncture mark on petitioner's arm, petitioner's parole officer searched an attache case that petitioner was carrying, discovering a plastic bottle containing methadone residue. A subsequent search of petitioner's car disclosed five more plastic bottles containing methadone residue and a number of unanswered parking summonses. The search of petitioner's room at his father's house disclosed the 40 rounds of ammunition. The plastic bottles, the unanswered parking tickets and the ammunition were not [***3] obtained as the result of an unlawful search and seizure. Any parolee expressly consents to a search of his person or residence as a condition of his parole ( Rules of the Board of Parole, 7 NYCRR 1915.10). Since the residence of petitioner's father was petitioner's "approved residence", the search was proper. There was no necessity for scientific tests to determine the nature of the residue in the plastic bottles since petitioner admitted that it was methadone.

3. The parole board must furnish petitioner with a statement of the reasons for the determination revoking parole and of the evidence relied upon ( 7 NYCRR 1925.35 [1]).

DISPOSITION:  [***1] By reason of the existence of an appropriate constitutional statutory plan for the supplying of special education for handicapped children as provided by the Legislature of the State of New York together with a procedure for the payment of the costs thereof, the motion made by the County of Rockland is denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The movant county brought a motion to dismiss two petitions filed under § 232 of the Family Court Act to determine educational services for alleged handicapped children. The county sought to consolidate the proceedings with 24 other similar petitions and to join as parties the eight school districts in the county as well as the education department. The motion was opposed by the school districts, the commissioner, and by some parents.


OVERVIEW: The parents of the alleged handicapped children filed petitions with the court asking for an order directing the county to pay the balance of the tuition cost, if any, and to pay whatever portion of the maintenance cost for the child in his or her special school the parents were unable to afford. The portion paid by the county, pursuant to N.Y. Educ. Law § 4403, was subject to 50 percent reimbursement by the state. The county, by its motion, objected to any order being made against the county that would compel it to bear the tuition or maintenance expenses on the grounds that the costs were properly and legally the burden of the respective school districts and the education department. The motion was opposed by the school districts, the commissioner, and by some of the parents of the handicapped children. The court held that the county failed in its burden to establish the unconstitutionality of N.Y. Educ. Law § 4403(2) and § 232(a)(1) of the Family Court Act based upon the classification of being designated a handicapped child. There existed an appropriate constitutional statutory plan for the supplying of special education for handicapped children.


OUTCOME: The court denied the county's motion to dismiss two petitions that were filed to determine educational services for alleged handicapped children.


HEADNOTES

Schools -- special education costs for handicapped children -- county liability -- eight school districts and Commissioner of Education may be joined in proceedings by petitioners, parents of handicapped children, to have cost of tuition and maintenance of such children made county charge, since neither school districts nor commissioner are strangers to proceedings, having prepared and received petitions -- delay of county in answering petitions is immaterial, since parties were sufficiently apprised of county's intentions -- classification of child as handicapped is not constitutionally invalid -- Legislature has prescribed appropriate statutory plan for supplying special education to handicapped children, whereby petitions filed in Family Court and consequent school district contracts result in county payment with 50% State reimbursement up to $ 2,500; direction to  [***2] school districts to supply special education does not alter order of payment, and such delegation is not improper -- accordingly, motion by county to dismiss petitions for payment of special education costs is denied.

1. The motion of the county to join as parties eight school districts and the Commissioner of Education in two consolidated proceedings by petitioners, parents of handicapped children, to have the cost of tuition and maintenance of such children made a county charge should be granted. Neither the school districts nor the commissioner are strangers to the proceedings, since school district personnel prepared the petitions which were then reviewed by the commissioner.

2. The delay of the county in answering or moving with respect to the petitions is immaterial, since the parties were sufficiently informed of the intentions of the county.

3. Classification of a child as handicapped is not constitutionally invalid, since the Legislature has acted in compliance with the mandate to provide free education and it has not been shown that beyond a reasonable doubt there has been any violation of fundamental law.

4. The Legislature has prescribed an appropriate statutory [***3] plan for supplying special education to handicapped children, whereby petitions filed in the Family Court (Family Ct Act, § 232) and consequent school district contracts result in payment of the costs of special education by the county with 50% reimbursement by the State, up to $ 2,500 (Education Law, §§ 4403, 4407). While the Education Law directs the school districts to supply special education, such provision does not alter the order of payment, and it is not improper for the State to delegate the duty of payment to the county.

5. Accordingly, the motion by the county to dismiss the petitions against it for payment of special education costs is denied.

DISPOSITION:  [***1] The motion to dismiss the information is granted and the defendant is discharged.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Before the court was defendant's motion to dismiss an information on the ground that the application of a city ordinance to the sale or distribution or newspapers was illegal, unconstitutional, and in violation of the First and Fourteenth Amendments.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was accused of offering newspapers on the city streets without first obtaining a license from the city clerk. The city sought to include newspapers within the definition of goods, wares, and commodities so that a license would have been required for defendant to sell newspapers on the city streets. OUTCOME: The court granted the motion to dismiss the information and discharged defendant.


HEADNOTES

Newspapers -- licensing of distribution -- application of city "Hawking and Peddling Ordinance" to require license for defendant's sale of newspapers on street infringes on freedom of press, since it constitutes censorship and prior restraint by its inherently pervasive threat, regardless of whether license is for distribution and sale, rather than publication and free distribution.

The application of a city "Hawking and Peddling Ordinance" to require a license for defendant's sale of newspapers on the street violates the First Amendment protection of freedom of the press, since it constitutes a form of censorship and a prior restraint by its inherently pervasive threat, regardless of whether the license is for distribution, rather than publication, or sale, rather than free distribution. A newspaper may not be deemed a mere commodity offered for sale, and any dangers and annoyances occasioned by its sale may be controlled by other means than the requirement of a license.

DISPOSITION:  [***1] Plaintiffs' motion is denied and defendants' cross motion dismissing the complaint is correspondingly granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Before the court were cross motions for summary judgment filed by plaintiffs, the collective bargaining unit and the employees, and by defendants, the city, the employer, which was an agency of the State, and the State, in an action challenging the "wage freeze" provision of § 10 of the New York State Financial Emergency Act for the City of New York (FEA), 1975 N.Y. OVERVIEW: There was no dispute that employer refused to pay the employees the salary increments due under the parties' collective bargaining agreement because of the enactment of FEA. The court held that in response to the city's then present fiscal crisis, a suspension of all wage increases was a reasonable exercise of the State's police power. While no certain date was provided for the payment of the suspended wages, provision was made that the payment would have been no later than the end of the "emergency period." The court held that the suspension of the benefits due to the employees did not impair their collective bargaining agreement. The employees' right to an increment under the subject bargaining agreement had been temporarily suspended; it had not been permanently abolished. Consequently, there was no merit to the argument that the employees had been deprived of property without just compensation. The court also held that the employees were not denied equal protection of the laws. They did not demonstrate that they were bearing a disproportionate share of the burden in the fiscal crisis as compared to other interest groups in the public and private sector.

1. The New York State Financial Emergency Act for the City of New York (L 1975, ch 868, § 2), which suspended all wage increases for municipal employees because of the city's [***2] present fiscal crisis, is a reasonable exercise of the State's police power which does not unconstitutionally impair plaintiffs' collective bargaining agreement (US Const, art I, § 10, subd 1). Plaintiffs form an unincorporated association that bargains collectively for the physicians employed by defendant New York City Health and Hospitals Corporation. As a result of the enactment of the "wage freeze", defendants refused to pay plaintiffs the salary increments due. The police power of the State may be reasonably exercised in directly preventing the immediate enforcement of contractual obligations, by a temporary and conditional restraint, where public interests would otherwise suffer. Payment of the suspended wages is to be made no later than the end of the "emergency period" which is to be determined by the Emergency Financial Control Board in accordance with the accounting method prescribed for the expense budget of the city by the State Comptroller.

2. The "wage freeze" legislation does not unconstitutionally deprive plaintiffs of property without just compensation. Plaintiffs' right to a salary increment has been temporarily suspended, not permanently abolished.

3. The [***3] "wage freeze" does not contravene the Bankruptcy and Supremacy Clauses of the Federal Constitution (US Const, art I, § 8, subd 4; art VI, § 2). The suspension of plaintiffs' increased wages constitutes an extension plan rather than a composition plan (US Code, tit 11, § 403, subd [a]).

4. Plaintiffs have not been denied equal protection of the law. Plaintiffs were given the equal opportunity to enter into a voluntary deferral agreement but instead chose to challenge the constitutionality of the freeze legislation. Therefore, they may not validly complain that their rights to increased wages should be deferred instead of suspended.

OUTCOME: The court convicted defendant of second degree murder.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Crimes -- murder -- "extreme emotional disturbance".

Defendant, a narcotics counselor at a public school who killed a 14-year-old girl who threatened to report him to his superior for smoking marijuana, was not acting under the "influence of extreme emotional disturbance" (Penal Law, § 125.25, subd 1, par [a]) at the time of the homicide and is, therefore, guilty of murder in the second degree. Defendant's conduct and statements do not demonstrate the influence of extreme emotional disturbance which would have reduced the crime to manslaughter in the first degree. Extreme emotional disturbance is the emotional state of an individual who has no mental disease or defect (Penal Law, § 30.05) and who has an extreme emotional reaction to an extremely unusual and overwhelming stress, as a result of which there is a loss of self-control and reason is overborne by intense feelings such as passion, anger, distress, grief or excessive agitation. Although defendant was exposed to an unusual stress, the threat of a 14-year-old student was neither extremely unusual nor overwhelming. Defendant's actions were too contrived and calculating to conclude that he lost his self-control. Defendant drove the victim around for hours trying to dissuade her before killing her. Defendant made a calm decision to kill the victim and then made sure that she was not still alive to "get him". Defendant's taped confession was inconsistent with a claim of extreme emotional disturbance. Instead of exhibiting unusual excitement or speaking in the slow monotone of depression, defendant spoke directly, coherently, relevantly and productively. He was candid, expressed no guile and was very matter of fact.

Defendant was convicted of criminal sale of a controlled substance in the fifth degree, a class C felony under the amended law. Defendant, a second felony offender, claimed that due to his predicate felony status, sentencing, pursuant to the amended penal laws, could result in the imposition of a longer minimum sentence. Defendant's motion to be sentenced under the old penal law was denied. The new statutes were designed to ameliorate the severe penalties imposed under the old drug laws and, therefore, were not an unconstitutional retroactive application of the law. Defendant was convicted of criminal sale of methadone, a class C felony (Penal Law, § 220.34). As a second felony offender convicted of a C felony, defendant must be [***2] given a maximum sentence of at least six years and no more than 15 years, with a minimum sentence fixed at one half of the maximum (Penal Law, § 70.06). The maximum term for a class A felony is life imprisonment and the minimum may be set at not less than one year nor more than eight years and four months. An indeterminate sentence is regarded as a sentence for the maximum term. Defendant's maximum exposure of 6 to 15 years, as opposed to a mandatory maximum sentence of life imprisonment, would be reduced under the new statutory scheme. Moreover, legislative amendments increasing the minimum sentence under an indeterminate sentence structure where maximum sentence remains constant, do not increase punishment so as to violate the ex post facto clause.

HEADNOTES

1. Notice and a best interests hearing should be accorded only if there is substantial justification for burdening the adoption process. Accordingly, the subject child is free for [***3] adoption without his putative father's consent, without efforts by petitioner foster-care and adoption agency to establish a relationship between him and the child, and without the institution of an abandonment proceeding.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant State Tax Commission made tax assessments against plaintiff exporter of cigarettes, liquor, and other items. The exporter filed an action against the commission seeking a declaration that the assessments were unconstitutional and void under the commerce, import-export, and supremacy clauses of the United States Constitution and pertinent statutes and regulations. OVERVIEW: The exporters had a warehouse in New York and sold liquor and cigarettes duty free to people traveling into Canada. The goods were sold for export only. The commission assessed taxes on those sales because the sale and delivery occurred in the state. There was no claim that the exporters violated any of the prescribed state or federal laws or regulations in conducting their business. The state's purpose in assessing the taxes was not to regulate or control the passage of alcoholic beverages within or through the state to prevent unlawful diversion, but rather to raise revenue. Such state action was not constitutionally permissible. The exporters followed the precise procedure prescribed by the federal government for selling merchandise for export. On that basis, federal governmental entities had exempted merchandise sold by the exporters from federal taxes or duty. The exporters' claimed that that determination was controlling on the state, thereby precluding the imposition of state taxes on the liquor. HEADNOTES

1. Plaintiffs sell cigarettes, liquor and other items for export at locations within the United States to persons crossing the border into Canada. All of the merchandise is produced or originates outside New York State and is packaged and marked solely for the Canadian market. Where bonded liquor is sold and delivered under Federal regulation and supervision and segregated from the common mass of goods in this country with the intention of uniting such liquor to the mass of goods belonging to some foreign country, the liquor is not subject to State taxes. No incident occurs in New York which is subject to the excise tax sought to be imposed. Nor is it controlling that the locus of the transactions here involved is not under the joint control of the United States and a State.

3. Since plaintiffs attacked the constitutionality of State taxing statutes as applied to them, a CPLR article 78 proceeding should have been brought rather than a declaratory judgment action. However, if jurisdiction has been obtained over the parties, the court will convert it into the appropriate proceeding and decide the matter.
