CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In an action that involved appellee, the executor of the estate of a decedent, appellant, the guardian of the estate of the decedent's widow, sought review of a decision of the Knox County Probate Court (Ohio), which granted reconsideration of the election it made for the widow to take under the statute of descent and distribution, rather than in accordance with the decedent's will.


OVERVIEW: The decedent died and was survived by the widow. A commissioner proposed that the probate court enter an election on the widow's behalf to take against the decedent's will. The probate court entered an election on the widow's behalf to take against the will of the decedent. The executor filed a motion for reconsideration, new trial, or relief from judgment as to the election. The widow died. The probate court vacated the election that had been made and elected on behalf of the widow to take under the will. In reversing and remanding the decision, the court found that the probate court erred in reconsidering its prior judgment. However, the defect was waived for want of timely objection under Ohio R. Civ. P. 19(A). The probate court had considered the motion that sought reconsideration, a new trial, or relief from judgment on the wrong track. The guardian did not show that Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2107.45 was unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the probate court and remanded the cause for further proceedings according to law and consistent with the court's opinion.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant father challenged the judgment of the Court of Common Pleas for Franklin County (Ohio) that upheld the decision of appellees, a school principal, superintendent, and school board, to impose a three-day suspension on the father's son for fighting with another student.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellee blood recipient filed a motion to compel appellant blood bank to provide the identification of an HIV infected blood donor whose blood was transfused into the blood recipient. The County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) granted the order. The blood bank appealed, alleging that disclosure was barred by the constitutional right to privacy, statutory privilege, and Ohio's Privacy Act (Privacy Act), Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 1347.01 et seq.


OVERVIEW: The trial court's order releasing the blood donor's identity was capable of review as a final appealable order because the donor's substantial rights of privacy and confidentiality were affected and subsequent review of the order provided an inadequate remedy. The court held that the physician-patient privilege in Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2317.02 did not apply because (1) the blood was not drawn by a physician; (2) the donor's information was not a communication relating to his treatment; and (3) the donor was not a patient. Moreover, maintaining the blood supply was a vital public interest furthered by denying discovery of the donor's identity. The limitations imposed by the Privacy Act were inapplicable, as the information requested was made within the confines of the discovery process.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's findings that the identity of the donor was not protected by the statutory physician-patient privilege and Ohio's Privacy Act. The court overruled the trial court's order compelling discovery of the blood donor's name and address.
