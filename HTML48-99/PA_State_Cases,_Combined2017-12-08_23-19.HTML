CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the sentence imposed by a trial court (Pennsylvania) under the Mandatory Sentencing Act, 42 Pa. Cons. Stat. Ann. § 9712(a) (1997), after he was convicted of robbery and possession of an instrument of crime. Defendant claimed that the "weapon" he used was a wooden plate holder and a hubcap nut remover fashioned to look like a gun and was not a replica of a firearm under the provisions of the Act.


OVERVIEW: Defendant claimed that the item used for the threat did not constitute a replica of a firearm under the Act and that the Act was unconstitutional. The court held that the Act was properly applied. Whether defendant was or was not skilled in fashioning his simulated gun, he wanted his victim to think it was a real gun. The witnesses testified that the way defendant brandished the simulated weapon they believed that it was a firearm. Replica of a firearm was defined as an item that could reasonably have been perceived to be a firearm. The testimony established that defendant possessed a replica of a firearm and that the victim thought it was a gun. Therefore, the Act was properly applied. The constitutionality of the Act was never contested at trial or at sentencing. Because that issue was not raised during trial or sentencing it should be considered waived. Even in the event that the issue was properly preserved, the Act was constitutional. Although the use of the word "item" as used in the definition of replica of a firearm covered a broad spectrum of articles it provided sufficient limitation to those situations where there was a reasonable belief a real firearm was brandished.


OUTCOME: The court held that the Mandatory Sentencing Act was properly imposed.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant telephone company filed preliminary objections to the complaint of plaintiff service provider, contending that the Public Utility Commission (PUC) had exclusive jurisdiction over the complaint, which alleged that the telephone company improperly compensated and improperly billed the service provider and that it improperly retroactively applied a federal excise tax.


OVERVIEW: The service provider filed a complaint against the telephone company, alleging that the telephone company compensated the service provider on the basis of estimated call counts, that the telephone company billed the service provider an excessive amount, and that the telephone company improperly retroactively applied a federal excise tax. The telephone company filed preliminary objections, asserting that the complaint raised questions within the exclusive jurisdiction of the PUC. The court sustained the preliminary objections and dismissed the complaint. On the service provider's appeal, the court sustained the preliminary objections and dismissed the complaint with prejudice. The court held that the dispute centered on rates. The allegations sounded in assumpsit, but the essence of the claims actually disputed the telephone company's rates. The PUC specifically had exclusive jurisdiction and the service provider was required to first exhaust its administrative remedies under the Public Utilities Law, 66 P.S. § 1101 et seq., before seeking a judicial remedy, even though it requested both legal and equitable relief against the telephone company.


OUTCOME: The court sustained the telephone company's preliminary objections and dismissed with prejudice the complaint of the service provider, which alleged that the telephone company improperly compensated and improperly billed the service provider and improperly retroactively applied a federal excise tax.

Commonwealth v. Hefner


no. 89-2874


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF BUCKS COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1990 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 39; 10 Pa. D. & C.4th 22



February 13, 1990, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant minor appealed from a one-year suspension of his driving privileges, pursuant to 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 6310.4(b)(2), following his conviction for a second violation of 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 6308. The minor, who had been issued a citation for underage drinking, contended that the penalties imposed by 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 6310.4 were unconstitutional, both on their face and as applied to the minor individually.


OVERVIEW: The minor contended that § 6310.4 was unconstitutional because it violated the Equal Protection Clause by discriminating against him because of his age. He claimed there was no rational relationship between the penalties imposed by § 6310.4 and the harm sought to be avoided. The court stressed that the United States Supreme Court had not held that a classification based upon age was a suspect classification warranting review under the strict scrutiny test. Furthermore, the minor failed to establish that driving was a fundamental right requiring review under the strict scrutiny test. The court found that the classification, i.e., status as a minor, served the Commonwealth's legitimate government interest in controlling alcohol use and abuse among minors. Because operating a vehicle on state roads was a privilege and preventing operation of vehicles by drivers under the influence promoted public safety, a rational basis could be found between underage drinking and traffic safety. In denying the appeal, the court held that the minor should have addressed his conviction for underage drinking within the criminal appeals process rather than collaterally attacking his license suspension.


OUTCOME: The court entered an order by which the minor's license suspension appeal was denied and dismissed.

Costello v. Celotex Corp.


No. 3117


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF PHILADELPHIA COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


20 Phila. 419; 1990 Phila. Cty. Rptr. LEXIS 28



February 6, 1990 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: After there was a verdict for plaintiff executor in his action for damages for asbestos-related injuries to his decedent, allegedly due to exposure to products manufactured or sold by various asbestos companies including defendant corporation, the court considered the corporation's post-trial motion requesting judgment notwithstanding the verdict, or in the alternative, a new trial or remittitur. The executor also moved for delay damages.


OVERVIEW: The executor filed an action for damages from the decedent's asbestos-related injuries. The jury returned a verdict awarding compensatory damages, and the parties stipulated that that the corporation's liability was 17 percent of the verdict. The corporation filed a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict, or in the alternative, a new trial or remittitur. The court denied the motion. There was competent evidence to uphold the jury's verdict that the executor timely filed his action in that the decedent knew or should have known that he had an asbestos-related injury or disease in 1980. Moreover, a new trial was not appropriate because the verdict was not contrary to the weight of the evidence, and the trial court's failure to charge on apportionment was proper because any apportionment would have been a result of speculation. Also, remittitur was denied because there was no unfairness, mistake, partiality or prejudice, and the verdict was not excessive. The court granted the executor's motion for delay damages.


OUTCOME: The court denied the corporation's motion and granted the executor's motion for delay damages.

Commonwealth v. Soto


Nos. 4473, 4477


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF PHILADELPHIA COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


22 Phila. 7; 1990 Phila. Cty. Rptr. LEXIS 99



January 28, 1990 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged the sentence imposed by the court following his guilty plea to charges of murder in the third degree and aggravated assault. Defendant asserted that the court erred by enhancing his sentence for third degree murder by imposing an additional 12 to 24 month deadly weapon enhancement under § 303.4(a) of the Pennsylvania Sentencing Guidelines, 204 Pa. Code § 303.4(a), 42 Pa. Cons. Stat. Ann. § 9721.


OVERVIEW: After defendant beat two men with a baseball bat, one of the men died. When defendant pleaded guilty to charges of murder in the third degree and aggravated assault, the court imposed sentences that included a deadly weapon enhancement. Following the denial of defendant's motion to reduce the sentences, the court considered the issue of the legality of the enhancement under § 303.4(a) of the Pennsylvania Sentencing Guidelines. The court rejected defendant's challenge, holding that no abuse of discretion occurred in applying the enhancement because the court was not vested with such discretion. The words "possessed" and "deadly weapon" in § 303.4 were not unconstitutionally vague, and the application of the enhancement did not deprive defendant of due process or equal protection rights. Application of the enhancement to a murder in the third degree charge was proper because the Pennsylvania Sentencing Commission was vested with power to promulgate the guidelines. No double punishment occurred because a third degree murder could be accomplished without use of a deadly weapon, and defendant's acquittal of the possession of an instrument of crime charge did not prevent the enhancement.


OUTCOME: The court rejected defendant's challenge to his sentence.
