DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded for trial.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The state appealed an order of the Oakland Circuit Court (Michigan), which denied the state's untimely application for leave to appeal a district court order which granted defendant's motion to suppress evidence obtained pursuant to a search warrant.


OVERVIEW: The height of defendant's grass violated a city ordinance, and when he failed to respond to a violation notice, a cut order was issued and assigned to a lawn service for execution. While executing the cut order, a lawn service employee discovered several marijuana plants in buckets. The police were called, and the lawn service's owner provided the detective with two buckets taken from defendant's property along with a statement that he had observed additional plants through a basement window of defendant's home. The detective obtained a search warrant, which he executed at defendant's home. Marijuana and equipment was discovered. Defendant's motion to suppress was granted, and the state appealed. The court reviewed the evidence and held that (1) the search warrant was legally obtained; (2) the objective of the city ordinance, to require property owners to remove or destroy proscribed weeds, was a proper exercise of municipal police power; (3) due process did not require a preabatement hearing and defendant's constitutional guarantees were not infringed; and (4) the lawn service employees did not enter defendant's property with the intent to conduct a search of the property.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the district court, which granted defendant's motion to suppress the evidence obtained from his residence pursuant to a search warrant. The court remanded the case to the circuit court to conduct a trial.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Detroit Recorder's Court (Michigan) denied defendant's motion to withdraw his guilty pleas of second-degree murder and conspiracy to commit second-degree murder. Defendant appealed the denial of his motions and his convictions.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that his conviction of conspiracy to commit second-degree murder was infirm and must be vacated because there was no such criminal offense. The court vacated defendant's conviction for conspiracy to commit second-degree murder but affirmed his conviction for second-degree murder. The court held that, as a matter of law, there was no crime of conspiracy to commit second-degree murder. The court found that the elements of a conspiracy were inconsistent and incompatible with second-degree murder. The gist of a criminal conspiracy was the specific, mutual agreement to perform the crime in question. Second-degree murder was distinguishable from first-degree murder in that it did not require premeditation and in fact did not necessarily require a specific intent to kill. The court stated that a person could not plan to commit an unplanned substantive crime. It was not absence of the elements but the inconsistency of the elements, which lead the court to conclude that one conspires to commit first-degree murder but not second-degree murder.


OUTCOME: Defendant's conviction of second-degree murder was affirmed. The lower court's denial of defendant's motion to withdraw his plea of guilty of conspiracy to commit second-degree murder was reversed. The case was remanded to the lower court for further proceedings consistent with the opinion.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The state challenged the decision of the Kent Circuit Court (Michigan), which dismissed the case against defendant on the ground that Michigan's negligent homicide statute, Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.324 (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 28.556), was unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: Defendant drove his pickup truck through a red light, striking an automobile. The passenger in that car subsequently died from injuries resulting from the collision. Defendant was charged with negligent homicide. The trial court dismissed the case, holding that Michigan's negligent homicide statute, Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.324 (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 28.556), was unconstitutional. The state appealed, and the court reversed. The court examined the penalties under § 750.324, which were not more than two years in prison and a fine of no more than $ 2,000, and it found that they satisfied the requirements for the elimination of criminal intent.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the decision of the trial court and held that Michigan's negligent homicide statute was constitutional. The court remanded for a trial.

Ct. R. 2.605(A). The committee sought declaratory relief in its effort to have a series of statutes declared unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: A committee brought a complaint challenging the constitutionality of several statutes. The trial court found for the Secretary of State (Michigan) and held that it lacked subject-matter jurisdiction over the complaint under Mich. Ct. R. 2.605(A). The court affirmed this decision, finding that where no actual case or controversy exists, a court lacks the subject-matter jurisdiction to enter a declaratory judgment. An actual controversy exists only where a declaratory judgment is necessary to guide a litigant's future conduct in order to preserve his legal rights. In its complaint, the committee challenged the constitutionality of statutes that the secretary had not yet invoked against it.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's decision finding that it lacked jurisdiction over this matter.

Defendant's motion to suppress was denied and he was convicted of receiving and concealing stolen property with a value of more than $ 100. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants attorney general and Department of Natural Resources (state) challenged an order of the Wayne Circuit Court (Michigan) denying its motion for summary judgment an granting partial summary judgment in favor of appellee city in an action brought by the state challenging the constitutionality of a city ordinance. The ordinance restricted access to a park.


OVERVIEW: A citizen challenged the ordinance, and reached a settlement with the city. The state joined the action and filed a motion for summary judgment. The court denied summary judgment in favor of the state and granted partial summary judgment in favor of the city on the issue of the constitutionality of the ordinance. The state sought review, and the court affirmed. The court held that the fact that the park was exempt from the property tax under Mich. Comp. Laws § 211.7x (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 7.7(4j)) required that the park be open to the public generally under Mich. Comp. Laws § 211.7x (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 7.7(4u)). Although the city failed to comply with that requirement, the proper remedy would have been to revoke the tax-exempt status of the park. The decision to either open the park to the public or forfeit its tax-exempt status was up to the city.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order denying the state's motion for summary judgment and granting partial summary judgment in favor of the city in the state's action challenging the constitutionality of the ordinance.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A district court (Michigan) convicted defendant of permitting three of its trains to obstruct a street for a period longer than five minutes. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: During defendant's trial, it claimed that in order to comply with Mich. Comp. Law § 466.23, it would be forced to reduce the number of cars in each train, which would increase the number of trains. A defense witness claimed the charges would constitute a tremendous financial burden. The district court found that the prosecution had shown convincingly that the regulations were necessary and effective in reducing the response time of emergency vehicles. The district court concluded that the necessity for public safety outweighed the interference with interstate commerce. On appeal, the court found that defendant advanced no evidence or argument that the statute burdened interstate commerce generally. The court determined that there was, at most, a de facto limitation on the length of defendant's trains at but one location, a restriction which resulted from the curvature of defendant's own track. Therefore, the court concluded that defendant had not overcome the presumption of validity attached to the statute or the State's interest in facilitating timely responses to emergencies.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the district court's judgment.
