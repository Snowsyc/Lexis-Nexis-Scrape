Court of Appeals, Tenth Appellate District of Ohio


1986 Ohio App. LEXIS 5573



February 4, 1986 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL from the Franklin County Municipal Court.



DISPOSITION: Judgment reversed and case remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Franklin County Municipal Court (Ohio), which convicted him of a fourth degree misdemeanor for violation of Westerville, Ohio, Ordinance § 523.05(c) regarding transportation of firearms.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was stopped by a police officer because the rear wheel of his vehicle was wobbling. Upon approaching defendant's car, the arresting officer saw a rifle slung over the back of the driver's seat, secured in a homemade rack or holder made for that purpose. It was clear that the rifle was readily visible to someone standing along side the vehicle. Defendant was convicted for violation of Westerville, Ohio, Ordinance § 523.05(c) which provided that no person shall knowingly transport or have a firearm in a motor vehicle, unless it was unloaded, and was carried in one of the following ways: in plain sight and secured in a rack or holder made for the purpose. Defendant appealed his conviction and the court on appeal reversed. The trial court construed plain sight to mean that the weapon had to be visible from a distance to other drivers on the highway. Criminal statutes were to be strictly construed in favor of defendant. Hence, plain sight meant clearly visible to someone looking into the interior of the vehicle. It was improper to extend the meaning of plain sight to require that the firearm had to be visible some distance away to a person riding in another motor vehicle.


OUTCOME: Defendant's conviction for violation of the city ordinance in regard to transportation of firearms was reversed and the case was remanded to the trial court with instructions to enter final judgment for defendant.

DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff property owners sought review of a judgment from the Common Pleas Court of Cuyahoga (Ohio), which upheld a decision of defendant city's board of zoning appeals denying their initial application for permission for a zoning permit or a variance to construct a 60-foot radio tower on their residential property.


OVERVIEW: The property owners, who already had a 120-foot radio tower on their residential property, wanted permission to erect an additional, lower tower in the back of their one-acre lot so that they could better enjoy being amateur radio operators. The city contended that under the applicable ordinance, the property owners could build such a tower provided that it was attached to their home. The landowners maintained that the ordinance resulted in unconstitutional disparate treatment because there was no rational basis between a tower attached to a house and one that was not given that freestanding towers affected the community in the same manner as did towers attached to residences. The court agreed with the property owners and reversed on this ground because the city was unable to premise the ordinance on any traditional police power customarily exercised by a municipality. OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment was reversed on grounds the ordinance was unconstitutional.

1985 Ohio App. LEXIS 9361

DISPOSITION: This decision is affirmed and remanded for assessment of costs. Costs to appellant.

A certified copy of this entry shall constitute the mandate pursuant to Rule 27 of the Rules of Appellate Procedure. See also Supp. R. 4, amended 1/1/80.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, administrator of decedent's estate, appealed from a judgment of the Court of Common Pleas of Lucas County (Ohio), following a jury trial, awarding the administrator damages on a wrongful death claim against appellee driver. The administrator argued that the trial court's failure to award him punitive damages violated the Equal Protection Clause of the United States Constitution.


OVERVIEW: The decedent was struck from behind by a car that was operated by the driver as she walked alongside a road on her way home one fall evening. The decedent suffered severe head injuries and died in the hospital approximately two days later without regaining consciousness. The administrator of decedent's estate was awarded damages on a wrongful death claim against the driver. No punitive damage award was made. The dispositive issue was whether the administrator was just as eligible for the proceeds from a punitive damage award as the decedent herself. The court held that the administrator was not entitled to punitive damages. The administrator sought to take advantage of an opportunity in which he had no personal stake whatsoever. Moreover, the imposition of punitive damages was for the public good, and not for the compensation or gratification of wronged family members. The court concluded that the disallowance of punitive damage claims in wrongful death actions did not violate the Equal Protection Clause of the United States Constitution, as the administrator had contended.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court that awarded the administrator of decedent's estate damages on a wrongful death claim against the driver of the automobile that struck and killed the administrator's decedent. The case was remanded for the assessment of costs to the administrator.

OVERVIEW: The parties obtained a dissolution of their marriage. She filed suit against appellants for malicious prosecution, false arrest, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and civil rights violations under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983. The trial court directed a verdict in favor of appellees. Upon review the court affirmed, with the exception of the claim for malicious prosecution against the police officer and the city, and for the civil rights violation claim against the police officer. The court held that the police officer, in the scope of his employment, initiated the judicial proceedings against the neighbor, and whether he did so maliciously and/or without probable cause were issues for the trier of fact. The court determined that whether the actions of the police officer violated the neighbor's civil rights was also an issue for the trier of the facts.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment as to the mothers on all claims, and as to the city and the police officer on the claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, and as to the city on the civil rights cause of action. The court reversed the judgment as to the police officer and the city for malicious prosecution, and as to the police officer for the civil rights violation.

APPEAL NO. C-840182, TRIAL NO. A-8302625


COURT OF APPEALS, FIRST APPELLATE DISTRICT OF OHIO, HAMILTON COUNTY


1985 Ohio App. LEXIS 5579



January 31, 1985 


PRIOR HISTORY: Civil Appeal from: Court of Common Pleas



DISPOSITION: Judgment appealed from is: Affirmed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff teacher appealed a judgment from the Court of Common Pleas of Hamilton County, Ohio, which affirmed defendant board of education's order terminating his contract on the basis that the teacher engaged in immoral conduct in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 3319.16.


OVERVIEW: The teacher was arrested on two charges of public indecency. The board of education adopted a resolution declaring its intent to terminate his teaching contract, pursuant to § 3319.16. A hearing was held before a referee, who found that the public indecency did in fact occur and that such conduct constituted immorality as that word was used in § 3319.16. The board of education reviewed the referee's recommendation and terminated the teacher's contract. The teacher appealed. The appellate court affirmed. The court held that there was no constitutional defect in § 3319.16 because a person of ordinary intelligence would have known that one act of public indecency could put his teaching contract in jeopardy. The court found no error in the trial court's conclusion that the board of education's decision to terminate the teacher's contract was supported by the manifest weight of the evidence. The board of education was not required to prove that the immoral conduct was job-related; the effect of the immorality on the teacher's job was a determination for the referee and the board of education.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of robbery and receiving stolen property in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2911.02 and Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2913.51. Defendant sought review and contended that the trial court erred in failing to grant his motion to suppress evidence because his arrest and search were unconstitutional. Defendant, a retail employee, was detained by his employer's private security personnel after they received reports that he had stolen merchandise. Under Ohio Rev. The court determined that the search of defendant by the employer and its security personnel was not a governmental action.
