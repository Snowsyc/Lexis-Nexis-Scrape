CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed three orders of the Superior Court of Penobscot County (Maine), which convicted defendant of two counts of operating a motor vehicle while a revocation prohibiting its operation for being an habitual offender was in effect and for giving a false name to the police.


OVERVIEW: Defendant appealed his three convictions, and contended that: (1) his earlier acquittal of the charge of operating while his driver's permit was under revocation for being an habitual offender precluded the Secretary of State from reclassifying him an habitual offender, (2) that it was unconstitutional to imprison him for operating while an habitual offender, because his reclassification as such was based in part on a prior uncounseled conviction, and (3) his conviction for giving a false name was reversible error because of police misconduct in illegally seizing evidence of his true identity. The court denied the appeal and held that defendant was not permitted to use the instant proceeding to collaterally attack his earlier reclassification because defendant did not attack the administrative classification on direct appeal of the Secretary of State's decision. The court held that the use of an uncounseled conviction of OUI as the basis in part for imposing a civil motor-vehicle-operation disability, enforceable by a criminal sanction of imprisonment for his subsequent misconduct, did not violate defendant's Sixth Amendment rights.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction and sentence for operating a motor vehicle while a revocation prohibiting its operation for being an habitual offender was in effect and for giving a false name to the police.
