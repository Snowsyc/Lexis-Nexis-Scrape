CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant insurer challenged an adverse decree of the Circuit Court of Cole County (Missouri) in its action to enjoin respondents, the Director of Revenue (Missouri) and the Superintendent of the Division of Insurance of the Department of Business and Administration of the State of Missouri, from collecting the two percent premium tax imposed upon it, pursuant to Mo. Rev. Stat. § 148.370 (1949), for the year 1951.


OVERVIEW: The insurer argued that respondents should not be permitted to collect the two percent premium tax assessed for the year 1951 because certain portions of Mo. Rev. Stat. § 148.370 (1949), the statute that authorized the tax, had been declared unconstitutional. The insurer took the position that the portions of § 148.370 relating to an exemption for intangible personal property, which had been deleted due to unconstitutionality, were an integral part of the statute and thus the entire statute was invalid. Respondents argued that the stricken portions were severable and the remainder of § 148.370 were still in force. On appeal, the court noted that it had jurisdiction pursuant to Mo. Const. art. V, § 3 (1945). The court also held that, even assuming the entire § 148.370 was invalid, respondents would still have the authority to collect the premium tax by virtue of a prior statute, 1945 Mo. Laws, p. 993, which had also authorized such a tax. The court noted that Mo. Rev. Stat. § 148.370 (1949) was enacted to repeal that prior statute. The court further noted that the rule was that when a repealing statute was invalidated, the prior general law was left unrepealed.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the circuit court's decree adverse to the insurer in its action to enjoin respondents from collecting the premium tax.

DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded (with directions).


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant car dealers sought to enjoin respondents Kansas City, Mayor of Kansas City, and the Kansas City Chief of Police from enforcing Kansas City, Mo., Ordinance 15794 § 37-95 (August 22, 1952), which prohibited a dealer from keeping his business open on Sunday and six named national holidays. The car dealers sought review of the judgment from the Jackson Circuit Court (Missouri), which sustained the motion to dismiss brought by respondents.


OVERVIEW: While the car dealers acknowledged the City's power to provide for the general welfare of the inhabitants by proper ordinances relating to Sunday closings of businesses, they argued that the ordinance was void because it was a special law and violated Mo. Const. art. III, § 40. Under § 40, the general assembly was prevented from passing any local or special law where a general law could be made applicable. The court noted that a test of a special law was the appropriateness of its provisions to the objects it excluded, not what the law included. The disputed ordinance excluded all persons engaged in the business of selling all commodities and all merchandise except automobiles. The court stated that there was no reasonable basis for singling out those people who were engaged in the business of selling automobiles and excluding those people who sold all other commodities and merchandise who were permitted to keep open their places of business on Sundays and the six named holidays. The trial court's grant of the motion to dismiss was in error.


OUTCOME: The trial court judgment was reversed and the cause was remanded to the trial court with directions for it to enter a new judgment in conformity with the opinion.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Relator state filed an action against respondent judge seeking a writ in prohibition forbidding the enforcement of the judge's order requiring disclosure of the names and addresses of persons that might be entitled to a refund of taxes and ordering the appointment of a receiver.


OVERVIEW: A group of taxpayers filed an action seeking a refund of use taxes collected pursuant to Mo. Rev. Stat. § 144.748 (1992) before it was declared unconstitutional. OUTCOME: The court granted the state's request for a writ in prohibition forbidding the enforcement of the judge's order in a tax refund case.

OVERVIEW: The claimants and the decedent were involved in an automobile accident, which killed the decedent. The decedent' wife presented his will for probate. DISPOSITION: REVERSED AND REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant City of Bridgeton challenged a decision from the Circuit Court of St. Louis County (Missouri), which entered summary judgment in favor of plaintiff billboard company in the company's action for declaratory judgment.


OVERVIEW: The billboard company brought suit for declaratory judgment against the City and sought a declaration that the City's Ordinance, Bridgeton, Mo., Municipal Code § 610.090, was unconstitutional. The ordinance in question established a yearly tax on every billboard located within the City's limits. Both parties filed motions for summary judgment. The trial court granted the billboard company's motion and declared that the ordinance was unconstitutional. The City appealed. On appeal, the court reversed and held that the ordinance did not prohibit that which Missouri Billboards Act, Mo. Rev. Stat. § 226.500-.600 (Cum. Supp. 1993) permitted, nor did it permit that which the Act prohibited. The court found that the ordinance did not conflict with the purposes of the Act in validating and affirming the use of outdoor advertising signs on interstate and primary highway systems. The court further held that the tax imposed by the ordinance did not implicate the First Amendment and was not unreasonable as applied to the billboard company because the billboard company's evidence showed that it was earning annual net revenues far in excess of the tax.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded the trial court's decision, which had granted summary judgment in favor of the billboard company.

OVERVIEW: Defendant, allegedly a recovering chemical dependent, appealed from his conviction of illegal delivery of a controlled substance, cocaine base.
