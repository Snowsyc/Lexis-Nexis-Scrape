Whipple v. Department of Revenue


No. 2677


OREGON TAX COURT


11 OTR 117; 1988 Ore. Tax LEXIS 26



December 9, 1988, Decided  
December 9, 1988, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff taxpayers sought review of a decision of defendant Department of Revenue, State of Oregon, which disallowed a deduction claimed by the taxpayers.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayers paid taxes in Canada and claimed a foreign tax credit on their federal income tax returns for 1981 through 1985. The taxpayers claimed the Canadian taxes as deductible business expenses on their Oregon income tax returns. The Department disallowed the claimed deduction. The court held that: (1) for foreign taxes, ORS 316.690 took precedence over ORS 316.716(3); (2) it was assumed that whenever the legislature enacted a provision, it had in mind previous statutes relating to the same subject matter, and in the absence of any express repeal or amendment, the new provision was presumed in accord with the legislative policy embodied in those prior statutes; (3) the deduction allowed the taxpayers was limited by ORS 316.690, which did not distinguish between foreign taxes paid as a business expense and taxes paid as a personal expense; and (4) in effect, the legislature had determined to treat taxes paid to foreign countries, whether they were a business expense or personal expense, in the same manner as federal income taxes and under the same general limitation.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the Department's opinion and order.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff taxpayer appealed an order of the Oregon Department of Revenue adjusting her personal income tax returns by including half of her husband's earnings from a community property state pursuant to ORS 150-316.048 (Oregon). The question presented was whether the taxpayer wife was taxable in Oregon on half of the husband's earnings in Washington.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayer wife lived in Oregon. Her husband had moved to Washington for a job, but there was no evidence that the parties intended to sever their marital relationship. The court rejected the argument that the husband's earnings were not community property because the wife was not domiciled in Washington. The fact that spouses lived in different states did not terminate the community. Washington law provided that half of the husband's earnings vested in the wife. Therefore, the court held that the Oregon taxpayer wife was taxable on half of her husband's earnings from Washington.


OUTCOME: The court denied relief to the taxpayer and upheld the Department's adjustments to the taxpayer's income tax returns.
