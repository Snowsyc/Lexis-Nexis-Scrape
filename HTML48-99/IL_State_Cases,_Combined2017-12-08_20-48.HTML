PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Circuit Court of Sangamon County; the Hon. J. Waldo Ackerman, Judge, presiding.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part and reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant township challenged a decision from the Circuit Court of Sangamon County (Illinois), which ruled that the township expansions under Ill. Rev. Stat. ch. 139, paras. 127-133a (1973), originally enacted as 1877 Ill. Laws 212, occurring since the effective date of Ill. Const. art. VII, § 5 were invalid. The trial court also held that municipal annexations were unaffected by Ill. Const. art. VII, § 5.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff land owners cross-appealed from that portion of the decision that held valid the annexations by defendant city under § 7-1-13 of the Municipal Code without holding public referenda. The trial court rolled back the boundaries of the township to those existing on July 1, 1971, the effective date of the 1970 Illinois Constitution and Ill. Const. art. VII, § 5. Only the township appealed from the decision regarding township boundaries. The issue for the court's consideration was whether the annexation of territory by the township resulted in one or more townships being "divided" within the meaning of Ill. Const. art. VII, § 5. On appeal, the court held that: (1) "divided' logically referred only to those situations in which one or more townships were separated in such a fashion as to create additional townships that did not previously exist; (2) simply boundary changes between townships did not need to be preceded by public referenda in the affected townships; (3) the annexation of territory by the city and the township involved nothing more than boundary changes; and (4) the township and its surrounding townships all continued in existence.


OUTCOME: The court reversed that portion of the trial court's judgment ruling that the township expansions were invalid and rolling back the boundaries of the township to those existing on July 1, 1971. The court affirmed that portion of the trial court's judgment approving annexation by the city without a public referendum.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from the Circuit Court of Cook County; the Hon. John A. Ouska, Judge, presiding.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff State of Illinois (state) appealed a judgment from the Circuit Court of Cook County (Illinois) that granted defendant employer's motion to dismiss the complaint in which the State charged the employer with violating Ill. Rev. Stat. ch. 48, para. 2c (1973) by advertising for new employees during a strike but not including within such advertisement the fact that a strike was in progress.


OVERVIEW: The trial court concluded that Ill. Rev. Stat. ch. 48, para. 2c (1973), which made it a petty misdemeanor to advertise for new employees without also stating in the advertisement that a strike was in progress, was preempted by federal labor law. The court agreed and affirmed the trial court's judgment. The starting point for the court's analysis was the rule that whether an activity was protected by either § 7 of the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), 29 U.S.C.S. §157, or § 8 of the NLRA, 29 U.S.C.S. § 158, the state's law must yield. From there the court reasoned that even if the activity described in Ill. Rev. Stat. ch. 48, para. 2c (1973) was neither protected nor prohibited by the NLRA, the state's statute was preempted. That conclusion was premised upon the United State Supreme Court's observation that the touchstone for preemption ultimately depended upon whether a state law operated to frustrate the purposes of the NLRA. The state's statute clearly frustrated Congress' intent by impinging upon an area of labor, an employer's right to hire new employees to replace strikers, that Congress had already decided in favor of an employer.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment that held that a state law that made it a petty misdemeanor for the employer to advertise for new employees during a strike without also stating in the advertisement that a strike was in progress was preempted by the National Labor Relations Act.
