CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant real estate broker sought review of a judgment of the District Court of Tarrant County (Texas) that upheld an action of appellee, the Texas Real Estate Commission, in suspending appellant's real estate broker license for a period of 90 days.

The court therefore held that the trial court was correct in passing on the question of whether the suspension of appellant's license was supported by substantial evidence. The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court because it found that the trial court was correct in passing on the question of whether the suspension of appellant's real estate broker license was supported by substantial evidence.

The court reversed and held that caselaw principles applicable to the oil and gas production tax were applicable with equal force to the franchise tax because the definition of "income tax" contained in 4 U.S.C.S. § 110(c) of the Buck Act, encompassed business-privilege taxes in both instances because each tax related to and was measured by income or receipts. The court noted that the Buck Act defined "income tax" in terms of a tax measured by either net income, gross income, or gross receipts and found that the legislative intent behind the Buck Act was to include any state tax within the definition of income tax within § 110(c).


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment in favor of appellee corporation. The court held that caselaw principles applicable to the oil and gas production tax were applicable with equal force to the franchise tax because the definition of "income tax" contained in the Buck Act encompassed business-privilege taxes in both instances because each tax related to and was measured by income or receipts.

Estate of Hutchings v. Parkland Mem. Hosp.

COURT OF CIVIL APPEALS OF TEXAS, SIXTH DISTRICT, TEXARKANA

PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Appealed from the 134th Judicial District Court of Dallas County, Texas


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, estate and survivors, challenged the order of the 134th Judicial District Court of Dallas County (Texas), which granted appellee hospital's motion for judgment non obstante veredicto and disregarded the jury's finding that appellee was liable for a wrongful death under the Texas Tort Claims Act, Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6252-19.


OVERVIEW: The deceased was erroneously diagnosed with inoperable cancer of the mediastinum. He died of respiratory failure as a result of the radiation therapy given at appellee hospital before the doctor's error was discovered. Appellants, estate and survivors, brought a wrongful death action against appellee under the Texas Tort Claims Act, Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6252-19. The jury awarded damages and found that appellee, by the negligence of its employees, proximately caused the death. The trial court granted appellee's motion for judgment non obstante veredicto and disregarded the jury's finding for lack of supporting evidence. Appellant sought review. The court affirmed because the negligence of the doctor and resident physicians, who were not appellee's officers or employees, could not form the basis of an action against appellee. The court found that appellee did not have the legal right to control under Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6252-19, § 2(3) because the doctors were employees of the university medical school, an independent contractor, which had the obligation to direct and control the details of their performance of medical tasks.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment non obstante veredicto because appellee hospital was not liable to appellants, estate and survivors, under the Texas Tort Claims Act for the negligence of a doctor and resident physicians who were not its officers or employees.
