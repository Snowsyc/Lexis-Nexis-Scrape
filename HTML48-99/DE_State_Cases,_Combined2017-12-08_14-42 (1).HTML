Lacy v. Green


Civil Action No. 79C-JN-53


Superior Court of Delaware, New Castle


428 A.2d 1171; 1981 Del. Super. LEXIS 553


December 18, 1980, Submitted  
March 9, 1981, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [**1] Plaintiffs' Motion to Strike Demand for Malpractice Review Panel Denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, husband and wife, brought a motion to strike defendant doctor's demand for the convening of a Malpractice Review Panel, pursuant to Del. Code Ann. tit. 18, § 6802 on the grounds that this procedure was unconstitutional in that it violated the Seventh and Fourteenth Amendments of the Constitution of the United States and the State of Delaware.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs had brought a medical malpractice claim against the doctor based upon the doctor's alleged reckless and negligent disregard for the wife's health and safety regarding a malignant, inverted mass in her right breast. The doctor sought the Malpractice Review Panel (panel), a right that was created by the Delaware Health Care Malpractice Act (Act), Del. Code Ann. tit. 18, § 68 (1980 Cum. Supp.). On review, the court denied plaintiffs' motion. The court found that the Act did not impair the right to trial by jury as guaranteed by Del. Const. Art. I, § 4 and Art. IV, § 19, because the jury still remained the ultimate arbiter of the factual questions raised at the trial. The court determined that although the panel opinion adverse to the claimant casts an increased burden upon the claimant, plaintiffs were still free to continue their complaint and proceed to present their case to a jury. The parties remained as free to call, examine and cross-examine witnesses as if the pretrial panel opinion had not been made; Del. Code Ann. tit. 18, § 6812 cut off no defenses, interposed no obstacle to full consideration of all the issues, and took no question of fact from the jury.


OUTCOME: The court denied plaintiffs' motion to strike the demand for the convening of a Medical Malpractice Review Panel in plaintiffs' case against the doctor based upon the doctor's alleged reckless and negligent disregard for the wife's health and safety regarding a malignant, inverted mass in her right breast.

Harmon v. Eudaily


No. 78C-SE-62 CIVIL ACTION


Superior Court of Delaware, New Castle


407 A.2d 232; 1979 Del. Super. LEXIS 96


March 30, 1979, Submitted  
September 5, 1979, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Upon defendant Eudaily's motion to dismiss.



DISPOSITION: Denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff injured parties filed an action for negligence against defendants, doctor and hospital. Service was made on the doctor, a non-resident, pursuant to the Delaware Long Arm Statute, Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 3104. The doctor filed a motion to dismiss on the grounds that § 3104 could not be applied to him.


OVERVIEW: The doctor had been a resident of Delaware when the alleged negligent acts occurred but had since moved to Montana. Service was made on the doctor, pursuant to the Delaware Long Arm Statute, Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 3104, which became effective after the doctor left the state. The doctor filed a motion to dismiss and alleged that § 3104 could not be applied retroactively to a cause of action that arose prior to its passage and that § 3104 was not meant to apply to individuals who were residents of Delaware at the time the cause of action arose. The court denied the doctor's motion to dismiss and found that § 3104 could be applied retroactively in this case because retroactive application did not undermine any expectations the doctor had in relying on the law as it existed at the time of the conduct. The doctor was subject to personal service in Delaware at the time the cause of action arose. Retroactive application of § 3104 did not deprive him of any substantive rights he had before passage of § 3104. The court also found that the term, "non-resident," as used in § 3104, referred to the status of the person at the time service was sought, not the time the cause of action arose.


OUTCOME: The court denied the doctor's motion to dismiss in an action by the injured parties for negligence against the doctor and the hospital.

Respondent was now 19 years old and the parties sought a determination of whether respondent was amenable to the rehabilitative processes of the court or whether he should be tried as an adult. The court weighed the factors set forth in § 1010 and determined that respondent was amenable to the rehabilitative process of the court. The court recognized that a serious offense was involved and that physical injury was suffered, but the court noted that respondent did not use a weapon, but only displayed it, and he had no prior criminal record. OUTCOME: The court declared respondent amenable and ordered that a trial be scheduled immediately.

OVERVIEW: The court found that no act of abuse had been committed and denied defendant spouse's request for a protection from abuse order. Defendant claimed that the allegations in the criminal complaint mirrored those in the civil one and violated double jeopardy. He argued that actions under the state statute were criminal or at least quasi-criminal in nature. As to the collateral estoppel issue, the court found that it only barred relitigation of questions put in issue and determined adversely to the party against which the estoppel was asserted. The state was not a party to the civil action and could not be collaterally estopped from litigating the issue of harassment in the criminal action.


OUTCOME: The court denied defendant's motion to dismiss the criminal charges of harassment.

State v. Harbor House Seafood


Cr. A. No. 96-08-0397


COURT OF COMMON PLEAS OF DELAWARE, SUSSEX


1997 Del. C.P. LEXIS 7


March 21, 1997, Submitted  
April 2, 1997, Decided


DISPOSITION:  [*1] Motion to Dismiss denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Town of Dewey Beach charged defendant restaurant with conducting business within the corporate limits without a business license. The restaurant challenged the validity of the ordinance on the ground that it imposed an impermissible tax on non-resident businesses. The issues were whether the ordinance was a proper exercise of the Town's authority and was applicable to non-resident businesses.


OVERVIEW: The Town's Business License Chapter subjected licensees to more than the payment of a fee and allowed the Town to refuse an applicant who did not meet certain criteria. Therefore, on its face, the ordinance was not a revenue-raising measure, but exercised the Town's police power. Consequently, the correct standard was one of reasonableness of the regulatory fee. The restaurant, as the moving party and the challenger of the ordinance, carried the burden of proof. As it failed to address the reasonableness of the fee in relation to the regulatory costs of the Town, the court had to presume that the fee was reasonable. The question was not whether the restaurant bore an unequal share of the tax burden in light of the benefits it received or did not receive, but whether the fee exacted was reasonable in light of the Town's cost in regulating the business activity. Therefore, the restaurant did not overcome the threshold presumption of validity and constitutionality of the ordinance. Dewey Beach, Del., Charter, § 23(a)(15) granted the Commissioners the authority to regulate any activity within the corporate limits. That included deliveries made by non-resident businesses.


OUTCOME: The court denied the restaurant's motion to dismiss.
