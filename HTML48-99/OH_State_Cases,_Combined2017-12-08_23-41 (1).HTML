STATE v. CUNNINGHAM


NO. 48558


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, EIGHTH APPELLATE DISTRICT, COUNTY OF CUYAHOGA


1985 Ohio App. LEXIS 5612



January 31, 1985 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDING: Criminal appeal from Common Pleas Court, Case No. CR-187,860



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT: Modified and affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment of the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio), which convicted him of two counts of kidnapping, two counts of aggravated robbery, and a penalty enhancing firearm specification.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of two counts of kidnapping, two counts of aggravated robbery, and a penalty enhancing firearm specification. Defendant sought review and contended that he was denied his right to a speedy trial. The court held that, for purposes of the speedy trial rule, the computation did not include the day on which defendant was arrested under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 1.14 and Ohio R. Crim. P. 45(A). The court found that because defendant's trial started on his 90th day in jail, the trial court had not erred in denying his motion to dismiss. Under Ohio R. Evid. 614(B), the trial court was permitted to interrogate any witness in an impartial manner, whether called by itself of by a party. The court held that simply because two statutes proscribed the same conduct had not meant that the Double Jeopardy Clause precluded the imposition of cumulative punishments in the same trial. Because the legislature authorized cumulative punishment, the State was allowed to seek it. The court found that the State presented insufficient evidence to support the firearm specification and modified the sentence accordingly. The court affirmed as modified the judgment of the trial court.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed as modified the sentence because the State presented insufficient evidence to support the firearm specification.

SHUBA v. AUSTINTOWN BD. OF EDUC.


CASE NO. 83 C.A. 81


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, SEVENTH APPELLATE DISTRICT, MAHONING COUNTY


1985 Ohio App. LEXIS 5594



January 25, 1985 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, an auxiliary service teacher, brought suit against defendants, a local board of education, its superintendent, and its director of instruction, alleging breach of contract, age discrimination, and a deprivation of constitutional rights in violation of 42 U.S.C.S. §§ 1983, 1985. The Common Pleas Court (Ohio) granted summary judgment for defendants, but denied their motion for attorney fees under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1988. Both sides appealed.


OVERVIEW: Under a series of non-year limited contracts, the teacher worked for the board in four out of five school years. The board eventually informed the teacher that her contract would not be renewed. In addition to breach of contract and age discrimination, the teacher filed several constitutional claims against defendants. The trial court entered summary judgment in favor of defendants. The teacher appealed the dismissal of her case and defendants appealed the denial of their motion for attorney fees. The court affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded in part, holding that summary judgment was proper as to all of the teacher's claims except for the breach of contract claim. Although the teacher was not entitled to a continuing contract on her reemployment, summary judgment was precluded because factual questions existed as to whether the board told her of the reason for its nonrenewal (poor classroom performance) and whether she was allowed to have witnesses testify at a board meeting. However, the board was entitled to attorney fees under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1988 with respect to the teacher's meritless constitutional claims that were filed pursuant to 42 U.S.C.S. §§ 1983, 1985(3).


OUTCOME: The court reversed in part summary judgment entered for defendants, holding that certain factual questions remained on the teacher's breach of contract claim even though she had no right to a continuing contract on re-employment. Otherwise, the summary judgment was affirmed. As for defendants' appeal, the court reversed the overruling of the board's motion for attorney fees as to three of the teacher's claims and remanded as to one of her claims.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from his conviction, in the Franklin County Municipal Court (Ohio), for operating a motor vehicle at a time when he had a concentration of .10 gram or more by weight of alcohol per 210 liters of breath, in violation Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4511.19(A)(3), and raised as a single assignment of error that the trial court erred in overruling his motion for dismissal based on the unconstitutionality of § 4511.19(A)(3) as applied.


OVERVIEW: The court noted that it had upheld the official constitutionality of Ohio Rev Code Ann. § 4511.19(A)(3), but that defendant raised a slightly different issue, contending that § 4511.19(A)(3) was unconstitutional as applied to him. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment.

DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff inmate appealed from a judgment of the Franklin County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) sustaining a motion of defendant Ohio Department of Rehabilitation and Correction to dismiss his complaint for a declaratory judgment upon the ground that there had been no violation of the inmate's legal rights. In support of his appeal, the inmate raised three assignments of error.


OVERVIEW: Although first or second degree murder were specifically mentioned in former Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 5143.03, these were the only types of murder contained in the Criminal Code when that statute was adopted. In 1980, the types of murder defined by the Criminal Code were aggravated murder and murder, which replaced the former crimes of first and second-degree murder. Accordingly, in interpreting § 5143.03, the sensible approach consistent with valid statutory-construction principles was to delete the words "first or second degree" and to give continued meaning to the last paragraph of § 5143.03 by interpreting it to mean that those convicted of murder, regardless of degree, are not eligible for commitment to the reformatory. The declaration that no legal right of the inmate had been violated served the same purpose as a more specific declaration that the Department had the authority to transfer him to the penitentiary because he was ineligible to be sentenced to the reformatory. Under Ohio law, the place of incarceration was not primarily a judicial function but, rather, was ultimately vested in administrative agencies by virtue of statutes, including Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2967.20.


OUTCOME: The court overruled all three assignments of error and affirmed the judgment.

1983 Ohio App. LEXIS 13031



December 28, 1983 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDING: Criminal Appeal from Common Pleas Court.



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged a decision from the Common Pleas Court of Logan County (Ohio), which convicted him of aggravated murder, aggravated robbery, and grand theft. Defendant contended that the trial court erred in failing to grant his motion for severance of the counts for which he had been indicted, that the prosecutor committed prejudicial misconduct, and that he was convicted of aggravated murder against the manifest weight of the evidence.


OVERVIEW: Defendant and the decedent were seen together throughout the afternoon and evening of the decedent's death. They were also observed in a roadway with decedent on his hands and knees, bleeding from at least three wounds. Defendant was later found near a partially burned car, which contained the decedent's partially burned body. Defendant was convicted of aggravated murder, aggravated robbery, and grand theft. In affirming on appeal, the court held that: (1) the defendant failed to make an affirmative showing of prejudice arising from the joinder of the charges against him or to show that the court's refusal to sever the charges was unreasonable, arbitrary, or unconscionable; (2) where defendant failed to seek sanctions from the trial court when the prosecution furnished requested witness statements but the witnesses refused to discuss the case with defense counsel, the issue was not properly before the court on appeal; (3) there was substantial evidence, if believed, to support defendant's conviction; and (4) defendant's conviction for aggravated robbery was not against the manifest weight of the evidence because his knife was not conclusively proven to have been open.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for aggravated murder, aggravated robbery, and grant theft.

13 Ohio App. 3d 340; 469 N.E.2d 881; 1983 Ohio App. LEXIS 11415; 13 Ohio B. Rep. 419



December 21, 1983, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of his conviction for arson, an offense under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2909.03, in the Court of Common Pleas of Hamilton County (Ohio).


OVERVIEW: A witness testified that defendant told him that he was experiencing financial problems and asked if he knew anyone who would burn down a certain building. Defendant was tried and found guilty of arson. On review, the court affirmed the judgment of the trial court. The court rejected defendant's argument that the trial court erred in overruling his motion to dismiss, which was made based on defendant's challenge to the constitutionality of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2909.03(A)(4). In so holding, the court found that § 2909.03(A)(4) was not void for vagueness, nor was it overbroad; it gave fair notice to a person of average intelligence of the specific conduct that was forbidden. Furthermore, the court rejected defendant's contention that the judgment was against the manifest weight of the evidence. The court found that the record contained substantial evidence of probative value upon which the trier of fact could reasonably conclude that the State established all the elements of arson beyond a reasonable doubt.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

Lovelace v. Kieninger


NO. 83 CA-10, and 83 CA-11


COURT OF APPEALS, SECOND APPELLATE DISTRICT, GREENE COUNTY, OHIO


1983 Ohio App. LEXIS 14044



December 20, 1983 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, a third-party debtor of a judgment debtor and his attorney, sought review of a decision of the Greene County Common Pleas Court (Ohio), which ruled that a previous order in aid of execution issued pursuant to Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2333.13 was valid and ordered that certain funds deposited with the trial court be turned over to appellee judgment creditors.


OVERVIEW: The creditors were awarded a judgment against a judgment debtor, who in turn was awarded a judgment against the third-party debtor. The creditors' attorney filed an affidavit in aid of execution stating that appellants and their bank had certain money owing to the judgment debtor. The trial court issued an order in aid of execution ordering the three parties to hold all monies owing the judgment debtor. Appellants negotiated and paid a reduced judgment amount. The trial court found that the original attachment was valid. The court affirmed, finding that it was unnecessary for the affidavit in aid of execution to state the statutory grounds for attachment, because the creditors had commenced a garnishment process under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2333.13, and not a proceeding in aid of execution under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2333.01. The court held that notice of the garnishment process effectively created a lien on the judgment debtor's property in favor of the creditor, thereby rendering unnecessary the utilization Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2333.14. The court also held that appellants were not deprived of their constitutional rights because they were accorded a hearing by the trial court.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's ruling finding that the attachment was valid and ordering the payment of court-deposited funds to the creditors.

State v. Jones


No. 83AP-377


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Tenth Appellate District, Franklin County


13 Ohio App. 3d 65; 468 N.E.2d 158; 1983 Ohio App. LEXIS 11378; 13 Ohio B. Rep. 70



December 15, 1983, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a trial court (Ohio), defendant entered of a plea of no contest to the offenses of carrying a concealed weapon under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2923.12 and having a weapon while under disability under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2923.13. The second count included specifications as provided in Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2941.141. Defendant appealed and challenged the three years of actual incarceration mandated by Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2929.71(A).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was sentenced under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2929.71(A) for possessing a firearm during the commission of a felony and possessing a weapon while under disability. Defendant claimed that there were no discernible standards and the statute gave the prosecution too much discretion in determining a sentence. The court decided that the statute gave a person of ordinary intelligence reasonable notice. The court pointed out that there was no showing that defendant was charged with a specification based upon an unjustifiable standard because the general assembly could reasonably conclude that the possession of firearms by persons under disability merited the mandatory prison term. The court found that the record did not indicate any evidence of unconstitutional discriminatory enforcement. The court pointed out that prosecutors had discretion over the charges brought and noted that the grand jury returned an indictment including the specification. The court determined that the statute was definite and contained adequate guidelines for enforcement. The court concluded that defendant demonstrated no due process violations and showed no infringement of equal protection rights.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment.

December 14, 1983, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment reversed and case remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, employees and Department of Industrial Relations, sought review of a dismissal by the trial court (Ohio) in favor of appellee employer in the employees' and Department's suit for enforcement of wages as required under Ohio Rev. Code §§ 4115.03-4115.15.


OVERVIEW: The employer paid the employees less than the prevailing wage as required under Ohio Rev. Code § 4115.04. The employees and the Department brought suit to enforce the statutory wage. The trial court granted the employer's motion to dismiss. The employees and Department appealed. The court found that the statute was not unconstitutional as was contended by the employers. The court found that although the statute required that the employees earn wages comparable to those paid in the private sector, there had been no delegation of duty or discretion by the legislature to the unions or other employers. The court reversed the prior dismissal of the employees' and Department's action.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the prior dismissal of the employees' and Department's suit for enforcement of the prevailing wage statute and remanded for further proceedings.

State v. Jackson


No. 83-CA-3


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Second Appellate District, Greene County


13 Ohio App. 3d 416; 469 N.E.2d 872; 1983 Ohio App. LEXIS 11430; 13 Ohio B. Rep. 503



December 12, 1983, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reporter's Note: A motion for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Ohio was overruled on April 18, 1984 (case No. 84-322).



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment from a trial court (Ohio), which found the defendant guilty of violating Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 3113.06 in that he neglected or refused to pay the Welfare Department the reasonable cost of maintaining his legitimate child when he was able to do so by reason of his property, labor, or earnings.


OVERVIEW: Defendant challenged the trial court judgment as it found him guilty of violating § 3113.06 for neglecting or refusing to pay the Welfare Department the reasonable cost of maintaining his legitimate child. Defendant argued that the jury's verdict was against the manifest weight of the evidence and that § 3113.06 violated the Equal Protection Clause of the U.S. Const. amend. XIV because: (1) it failed to provide the same classification of the criminal offense as Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2919.21, thereby discriminating against those persons whose children received ADC, (2) it failed to provide the affirmative defense contained in Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2919.21(B), and (3) it failed to treat the sexes equally. The court determined that Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 3113.06 was constitutional because there was no classification that treated persons charged under § 3113.06 differently than persons charged under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2919.21. There was sufficient evidence for the jury to find that defendant failed to make support payments and that he "neglected or refused" to pay the reasonable cost of support to the Welfare Department as provided by Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 3113.06.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court judgment.

State, ex rel. Bramley vs Willoughby


CASE NO. 9-228


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, ELEVENTH DISTRICT, LAKE COUNTY


1983 Ohio App. LEXIS 11203



December 9, 1983 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDINGS: Civil appeal from Lake County Common Pleas Court, Case No. 81 CIV 1153



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT Affirmed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff former employee sought review of an order of the Lake County Common Pleas Court (Ohio), which denied his petition for a writ of mandamus against defendants, a city and its civil service commission. His petition sought to force the commission and city to reinstate his employment. The city's termination of his employment at the end of his probationary period was approved by the commission after a full hearing.


OVERVIEW: The employee challenged his termination on grounds that his probationary period had already expired; therefore the reasons given for his termination, complaints and an unfavorable review by a supervisor, were not sufficient to support the decision. The trial court dismissed his petition upon defendants' motion pursuant to Ohio R. Civ. P. 12(B)(6). The employee contended that dismissal was in error because his termination was contrary to the rules of the commission and that he had no plain and adequate remedy at law. He argued that he had no adequate remedy at law, in part, because a common pleas court did not have the power to review the commission's factual and legal conclusions. On appeal, the court found that argument without merit because a common pleas court had the authority to review the commission's decision with respect to both questions of law and fact pursuant to Ohio Rev. Code §§ 2506.01, 2506.04. Although the trial court, under its applicable standard of review, did not hear questions of fact in a de novo sense, it did evaluate the evidence to determine whether the commission's findings were supported by reliable, probative, and substantial evidence.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's dismissal of the employee's petition for a writ of mandamus.
