CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff husband filed a marital dissolution action against defendant wife that sought custody and financial relief. The husband asserted jurisdiction under the Connecticut long-arm statute, Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-46(b). The wife, a resident of New York, filed a motion to dismiss and alleged that the court did not have jurisdiction under the Due Process Clause of U.S. Const. amend. XIV.


OVERVIEW: The husband became a resident of Connecticut more that one year prior to the commencement of the dissolution action. Prior to moving to Connecticut, the husband lived with the wife and the parties' two minor children in New York. Neither the wife nor the children ever resided in Connecticut. OUTCOME: The court granted the wife's motion to dismiss the action. The court indicated that the husband was free to file an amendment to the complaint to seek a dissolution of the marriage as the only claim for relief.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff organization brought an action seeking a temporary injunction to restrain defendant, the Commissioner of the Department of Environmental Protection (department), from causing, permitting, participating in, or sanctioning actions, which were likely to result in the killing of fawn deer. The department filed a motion to dismiss the action for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.


OVERVIEW: The department claimed that the organization had no standing to bring the action because no hunts were taking place and none were scheduled or planned to take place at any specific time in the future. The court determined that the hunts that the organization sought to enjoin were not only capable of repetition, but the department's regulations established future hunts as a policy. The court explained that the organization had standing under Conn. Gen. Stat. § 22a-16, which provided that any person could have brought an action against the state for declarative and injunctive relief to protect the public trust in the natural resources of the state. The court noted that the organization could have sought declaratory relief, which was an exception to the general rule of sovereign immunity, because such relief did not seek to control the activities of the state. The court concluded that even under the most strict construction of § 22-16a, the organization had standing to bring the action because the conduct of the department was in combination with others and was reasonably likely to unreasonably destroy the public trust in the natural resource of deer.


OUTCOME: The court denied the department's motion to dismiss the organization's action seeking a temporary injunction to restrain the department from causing, permitting, participating in, or sanctioning actions, which were likely to result in the killing of fawn deer.

The court also ruled that delays in approving the application for permit modification did not deny due process, because approval was discretionary and no "clear entitlement" to approval was shown, and did not deny equal protection or the right to pursue a lawful business or amount to a governmental "taking." However, the court held that estoppel was available as a special defense because inaction in approving the company's improvements to its treatment process could make civil penalties inequitable or oppressive, or mitigate them.


OUTCOME: The court granted the Commissioner's motion to strike as to three of the company's special defenses but denied the motion as to the special defense of estoppel.
