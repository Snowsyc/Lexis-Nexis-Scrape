OVERVIEW: The inmate had been convicted and sentenced for felonies on three separate occasions. Relying upon Ark. Stat. PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] On writ of certiorari to the Court of Appeals to review its affirmance of the Pulaski Chancery Court, Second Division, John T. Jernigan, Chancellor.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant city filed a writ of certiorari to challenge the judgment of the Court of Appeals (Arkansas), which affirmed the chancery court's decree in favor of appellee landowners that found that the city acted unreasonably in denying the landowners' application for rezoning.


OVERVIEW: The landowners' tract was located in a one-family zoning district. The landowners filed an application with the city's planning commission to rezone the area to commercial. The commission denied the application. Upon judicial review, the chancery court found that the property involved was located in an established and expanding business district and that the city acted in an arbitrary and capricious or unreasonable manner in rejecting the landowners' application. On appeal, the court reasoned that zoning decisions of the city were legislative in nature and that the city had the power to plan various areas of the city into zones. The question before the chancellor was solely whether the city acted arbitrarily. Here, the decision of the chancellor that the property was adjacent to an existing and expanding business district was against the preponderance of the evidence. After reviewing all of the evidence, the court could not say that the city's actions were without a reasonable basis. Rezoning was not justified solely on the grounds that it was necessary to put a tract of land in its most remunerative use.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the decree of the chancellor, which was affirmed by the court of appeals. The city's action in refusing to rezone the landowners' property was not arbitrary and capricious or unreasonable.
