1955 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 317; 3 Pa. D. & C.2d 259



February 23, 1955, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Appeal from Secretary of Revenue.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Exceptant Commonwealth filed exceptions to a decision of the court, which overruled a suspension ordered by appellee, the Pennsylvania Secretary of Revenue, of appellant inspector's privileges to inspect motor vehicles as an official inspection station.


OVERVIEW: The Commonwealth filed exceptions on the basis that the inspector had no right to appeal her suspension under § 823(f) of the Pennsylvania Vehicle Code of May 1, 1929, P. L. 905, Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 75, § 431. Upon consideration, the court overruled the Commonwealth's exceptions and affirmed the reinstatement of the inspector's privileges. The court determined that a specific right of appeal of a suspension was given in § 813(b) of the Pennsylvania Vehicle Code. After reviewing both sections, the court held that they should be considered together and that the discretionary suspension authorized by § 823(f) of the Pennsylvania Vehicle Code was entitled to be appealed. The court held that such a construction avoided a determination that § 823(f) was unconstitutional by depriving a suspended person of due process or equal protection. The court found that the Pennsylvania Administrative Agency Law did not apply to the Department of Revenue, Bureau of Motor Vehicles.


OUTCOME: The court overruled the Commonwealth's exceptions and affirmed the reinstatement of the inspector's privileges.

After unsuccessfully applying for a variance for a retail dry cleaning store, the businessmen secured a variance for a restaurant. November 18, 1954, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Petition to strike mechanic's lien.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff contractor filed a mechanic's lien claim against defendant owner pursuant to § 2 of the Pennsylvania Mechanic's Lien Act of June 4, 1901, P. L. 431, 49 PS § 21, for work performed and materials supplied pursuant to a written contract and subsequent oral modifications thereto, including the leveling and demolition of buildings and the grading and filling in of the resulting plot. The owner filed a petition to strike the lien claim.


OVERVIEW: The owner argued that a mechanic's lien could not be asserted for demolition and grading work which was not preparatory to new construction. Based on that claim, the court issued a rule to the contractor to show cause why the lien claim should not have been stricken. After a hearing, the rule was made absolute. The contractor relied on § 2 as authority for the filing of the lien. However, after construing § 2, the court concluded that services rendered in the demolition of a building and the grading or clearing of a plot of ground did not form a basis for the filing of a mechanic's lien claim unless such work was incidental to or preparatory to the alteration, erection, and construction of a building or other structure. Moreover, mechanic's lien legislation was special legislation that was valid only insofar as it did not create or extend such liens beyond their scope as enacted prior to the Pennsylvania Constitution of 1874, when such liens could not have been filed for demolition and removal work unrelated to an erection or construction process. Because no new construction was done or even contemplated in the case at bar, the contractor's lien claim was filed without authority.


OUTCOME: The rule granted upon the contractor to show cause why the mechanic's lien claim should not be stricken off was made absolute.

OUTCOME: The court decreed the ordinances illegal and unenforceable.

DISPOSITION:  [*1] Appeal from Pennsylvania Liquor Control Board.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant corporations sought review of a decision of appellee Pennsylvania Liquor Control Board, which refused to approve the corporations' applications for liquor licenses under the provisions of the Pennsylvania Liquor Control Act.


OVERVIEW: The corporations applied for liquor licenses. The Board refused to approve their applications under the provisions of the Pennsylvania Liquor Control Act. On appeal, the corporations contended that the Board erred in refusing to approve their applications. The court held that: (1) when a Pennsylvania Act of Assembly, such as the Pennsylvania Quota Act of June 24, 1939, P.L. 806 (Pa. Stat. OUTCOME: The court dismissed the corporations' appeal.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant veterans' association sought review of the decision of appellee Pennsylvania Liquor Control Board, which denied the application of the association for a liquor license.

On the associations' appeal, the court overruled the action of the Board. The court determined that P. OUTCOME: The court sustained the appeal of the association.

Richardson v. Jereb


March term, 1947, no. 10


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF CAMBRIA COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1948 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 20; 68 Pa. D. & C. 301



March 16, 1948, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Complainant husband sought an injunction to prevent respondent tax collector from attempting, under §§ 542 and 557 of Pennsylvania's School Code, 1911 P.L. 309, to collect from him school taxes allegedly owed by the husband's wife.


OVERVIEW: The husband argued that § 542, which imposed upon married men liability for school tax owed by their wives, violated Pa. Const. art. IX, § 1, which required all taxes to be uniform as to the same class of subjects. In dismissing the bill, the court disagreed. Applying the presumption that the legislature did not pass an unconstitutional statute, the court concluded that § 542 of Pennsylvania's School Code, 1911 P.L. 309, did not unlawfully discriminate between married and unmarried men and that the legislature had sovereign power to place upon the former a duty to pay his wife's tax because such duty flowed from the marriage relationship. The court reasoned that the family relationship between a husband and wife justified the creation of the separate classes of married men and single men. The court further reasoned that, under the doctrine of necessaries, a husband had an obligation to pay for liabilities of his wife and that such obligation extended to the payment of taxes as well as to payments arising on a contractual basis. Finding that the husband failed to carry his burden to show that a conflict existed, the court held that § 542 did not offend Pa. Const. art. IX, § 1.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the husband's bill for an injunction.

Burns v. Paulak


no. 1475

1948 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 369; 63 Pa. D. & C. 388



March 8, 1948, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Petition for stay of proceedings.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant tenant filed a petition to stay proceedings after plaintiff landlord was issued an execution to evict the tenant contrary to an order of the City Housing Rent Commission of the City of Philadelphia. The landlord claimed that Philadelphia, Pa., Rent Control Ordinance (1947), upon which the tenant's relief from eviction was based was invalid.


OVERVIEW: A judgment of possession was entered for the landlord under a confession of judgment in the lease signed by the parties. At the time the judgment of possession was entered, the tenant applied to the Commission for relief under the ordinance, which had been recently approved. In accordance with the ordinance, the Commission held a hearing and entered an order, directing the landlord not to institute any eviction proceedings for six months. Despite the order, the landlord immediately issued an execution on the judgment that had been entered under the lease. The landlord argued that the ordinance was unconstitutional. The court upheld the ordinance, finding that the ordinance was valid and that it properly established the Commission under Philadelphia, Pa., Rent Control Ordinance §§ 4, 6, and 9 (1947). Additionally, the court held that the Pennsylvania legislature properly delegated the exercise of the police power as to rent control to the city of Philadelphia under Pa. Const. of 1874 art. XV, § 1 and under the Philadelphia City Charter Act, Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 53, § 3251 (1919). The court also noted that the ordinance was necessary to alleviate the housing shortage emergency.


OUTCOME: The court stayed the proceedings and set aside the execution to evict the tenant.

Hartman v. Columbia Malleable Castings Corp.


September term, 1947, no. 38


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF LANCASTER COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1948 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 218; 62 Pa. D. & C. 507



January 30, 1948, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In plaintiff employee's action to recover money deducted by defendant employer from the employee's gross wages, the employee filed a demurrer to the employer's answer that it was deducted pursuant to a written notice and demand of a tax collector of the School District of the Borough of Columbia, Pennsylvania, under the Act of May 18, 1945, P. L. 687 (Pennsylvania), and the Local Tax Collection Law of May 25, 1945, P. L. 1050 (Pennsylvania).


OVERVIEW: The employer received a written notice and demand from the tax collector stating that the employee and his wife owed the per capita tax for one year, with a penalty. The sum was deducted from the employee's wages and paid to the tax collector under the provisions of the Act of May 18, 1945, P. L. 687 (Pennsylvania), and the Local Tax Collection Law of May 25, 1945, P. L. 1050 (Pennsylvania). The employee filed an action to recover the money, and the employer answered. The employee filed a demurrer, which the court overruled. The court held that the two statutes did not violate Pa. Const. art. IX, § 1 because diversity in the methods of tax assessment and collection was proper. Absolute equality in taxes was unattainable, so as long as collection methods were provided by general laws and resulted in practically uniform collection they were not to be condemned for minor inequalities. The court held that the assessment of the per capita tax for school purposes did not fall within the power of eminent domain, for the latter acted only by a direct taking of property for a public use. Pa. Const. art. I, § 10 was a limitation not on the taxing power but on the right of eminent domain.


OUTCOME: The court overruled the employee's demurrer.

Tremont Township v. Schuylkill Mining Co.


May term, 1947, no. 389


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF SCHUYLKILL COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1948 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 232; 62 Pa. D. & C. 179



January 12, 1948, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant company filed preliminary objections to plaintiff township's complaint in its action of assumpsit to recover township road taxes assessed against the company as the owner of certain seated lands during the years at issue.


OVERVIEW: The township brought an action of assumpsit, pursuant to the Pennsylvania Local Tax Collection Law of May 25, 1945, P.L. 1050, § 21(b), Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 72, § 5511.21(b), against the company to recover township road taxes assessed against the company as the owner of certain seated lands during the tax years at issue. The company filed preliminary objections to the complaint, which challenged the provisions of § 5511.21(b) as being violative of Pa. Const. art. II, § 3. The court overruled the objections and allowed the company 20 days to file an answer. The title of Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 72, § 5511.21(b) was sufficient, as the subject matter of the Act was expressly stated in the title to have been the "collection of taxes." The court held that all the Pennsylvania Constitution required was that the title to an act should put persons of a reasonably inquiring state of mind on notice of the general subject matter of the act. The incidental provisions of the statute need not have been enumerated or indexed in the title if they were germane to the legislation as a whole. Nothing could be more germane to a tax collection act than a provision establishing the method of such collection.


OUTCOME: The court overruled the preliminary objections to the complaint and allowed the company 20 days to file an answer.

Whitpain v. Douglass


no. 15


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF MONTGOMERY COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1948 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 397; 63 Pa. D. & C. 566



January 9, 1948, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Exceptions to decree in equity.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants filed exceptions to an action filed against them by plaintiff that sought to restrain defendants from violating an ordinance prohibiting the storage of abandoned or junked automobiles on public or private property. The exceptions contained defendants' contentions that the ordinance was not applicable to its business and that it was unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: A city ordinance prohibited the storage of abandoned or junked automobiles on public or private property. Defendants were conducting a used car and parts business on their property, where they obtained used cars, dismantled them, and stored the removed parts for future sales. Pieces of the bodies or chassis that could not be used remained in various stages of disassembly, and at periodic intervals were hauled away. Plaintiff filed an action against defendants to restrain them from violating the ordinance. defendants field objections to the suit, arguing that the law did not apply to a used car parts business and that it was unconstitutional as an arbitrary confiscation of private property. The court dismissed the exceptions, holding that the ordinance was not directed at any particular type of business, but at the accumulation of garbage and rubbish, storage of junked cars, and carrying on an offensive business. Three types of offenses were prohibited, and one of them is involved in the case at bar. The inquiry was simply whether there had been a storage of abandoned or junked automobiles, and under the evidence, there was such storage and violation.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the exceptions.
