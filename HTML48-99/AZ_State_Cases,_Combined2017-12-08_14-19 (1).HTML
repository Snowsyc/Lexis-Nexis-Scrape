PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant state sought review of a decision of the Court of Appeals (Arizona), which reversed the trial court's judgment convicting defendant of having committed a felony while free on his own recognizance in violation Ariz. OVERVIEW: Defendant raised two questions: (1) whether the second count was a violation of the double jeopardy clause of the state and federal constitutions, and (2) whether the second count was a violation of Ariz. Accordingly, the court held that the second count was not a violation of § 13-1641.


OUTCOME: The court vacated the opinion of the court of appeals and affirmed the judgment and sentence of the trial court.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant property owners filed an action against appellees, city, city officials, and newspapers, seeking to enjoin the annexation of certain territory to the city. The trial court (Arizona) rendered judgment for appellees. The property owners sought review.


OVERVIEW: The property owners sought to enjoin appellees from annexing certain territory. On appeal, the court affirmed the trial court's judgment in favor of appellees. The property owners contended that the petition seeking annexation was not signed by the owners of 50 percent of the property valuation subject to taxation in the event the territory was annexed. The court found that two properties were improperly included, but that the remaining aggregate value exceeded the needed valuation. Although that portion of Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 59-601 which empowered the board of supervisors to assess damages and award compensation was invalid, the remainder of §59-601 was valid and enforceable. Thus, the board's action in setting the boundary of the territory was proper and the annexation ordinance did not encompass more territory than that set forth in the petition. The fact that the city's annexation petition was not the first filed did not deny the city of jurisdiction. The city acquired jurisdiction first because it was the first to present its petition to its council for action. The court also found that the map of the territory annexed met the requirements of Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 9-471.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment that found in favor of appellees in the property owners' action that sought to enjoin the city from annexing certain territory.

OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

Giss v. Jordan


No. 6408


Supreme Court of Arizona


82 Ariz. 152; 309 P.2d 779; 1957 Ariz. LEXIS 206



April 6, 1957 



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Writ quashed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners, state senator and representative, sought mandamus to compel respondent state auditor to approve reimbursement of expenses incurred as legislators and draw warrants therefor on the state treasurer. The issues were whether Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 41-1103(D), (E) infringed upon an executive department, preventing the auditor from determining whether the claims were proper under Ariz. Const. art. IV, part 2, §§ 1, 17 and whether mandamus lay.


OVERVIEW: Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 41-141 implemented Ariz. Const. art. III, art. V, § 1, and art. V, § 9 and Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 35-181 supplemented § 41-141. The court held that the creation of the constitutional office of auditor contemplated that someone should audit claims against the state, and, under § 41-141, the state auditor had that duty unless it was expressly given to another. Auditing was an executive function, and the framers of the Arizona Constitution intended that the auditing of public officials' accounts would be performed by an officer independent of those officials. Section 41-1103(D), (E) violated the concept of an independent auditor. The power to audit the expense claims of the members of the legislative branch was delegated by the Constitution to the Executive Branch and impliedly placed in the hands of the state auditor. Therefore, § 41-1103(D), (E) was unconstitutional and void. Mandamus would not lie, because the auditor had discretion in auditing petitioners' claims to determine whether they were excessive in amount and, therefore, inconsistent with Ariz. Const. art. IV, pt. 2, § 17 prohibiting extra compensation. The court quashed the alternative writ of mandamus.


OUTCOME: The court quashed the alternative writ of mandamus, which the court issued when it assumed original jurisdiction. The statute was unconstitutional.

DISPOSITION:  [***1] Reversed and remanded for new trial.

In seeking review, the heirs claimed a jury instruction had violated Ariz. Const. art. XVIII, § 5 by usurping the jury's function of finding contributory negligence.


OVERVIEW: The heirs brought a negligence action following the decedent's death in a railroad crossing accident, and in instructing the jury, the trial court mandated a finding of contributory negligence if a failure to exercise proper vigilance or a failure to yield the right-of-way was found. Moreover, the court held an engineer's statement regarding the hazards of the railroad crossing had been admissible as defendants' admission, notwithstanding the opinion rule.


OUTCOME: In reversing a judgment which found for defendants in the heirs' negligence action that was brought after the decedent was killed in a railroad crossing accident, the court held a jury instruction had unconstitutionally usurped the jury's function of finding contributory negligence.

79 Ariz. 403; 291 P.2d 764; 1955 Ariz. LEXIS 186

DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff corporation filed suit against defendant state land commissioner to determine the corporation's rights to the use of subterranean waters underlying its lands. OVERVIEW: The corporation urged that the Groundwater Code, 1948 Ariz. Sess. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment from the trial court in favor of the commissioner.

Supreme Court of Arizona


79 Ariz. 240; 286 P.2d 752; 1955 Ariz. LEXIS 154



July 15, 1955 



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Defendant ousted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, Attorney General of the State of Arizona, brought an original proceeding in quo warranto to exclude defendant purported judge from the office of Judge of the Yuma County Superior Court (Arizona) upon the ground that the purported judge had usurped, intruded into, and unlawfully purported to hold that office.


OVERVIEW: Acting upon the authority of Ariz. Code Ann. § 19-301 (1955), the Governor of Arizona appointed the purported judge to the office of superior court judge. The Attorney General sought ouster. Upon consideration, the court ruled in favor of the Attorney General and ordered that the purported judge by ousted and excluded from office. The clause of § 19-301 which read "for the purpose of this act, the counties of Maricopa, Pima, Pinal, and Yuma are hereby declared to have, and shall be held to have, inhabitants respectively, in excess of 460,000, 205,000, 55,000, and 45,000" was unconstitutional and void. Because the purported judge was appointed by virtue of that clause, he was guilty of intruding into and usurping the office of judge. These words "a census enumeration" as found in Ariz. Const. art. VI, § 5 were words of limitation, and thus the legislature was precluded from substituting for "a census enumeration" a declaration that the counties of Maricopa, Pima, Pinal, and Yuma had a population in excess of certain named amounts. "A census enumeration" was an official counting of heads, compiled by the federal government, the State, or the county.


OUTCOME: The purported judge was ordered ousted and excluded from office.
