PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant department of transportation challenged the order of the Court of Common Pleas of Lancaster County (Pennsylvania), which reversed the suspension of appellee licensee's vehicle operator's license, which was mandatorily revoked under 75 P.S. § 1413, in violation of U.S. Const. amend. XIV, for failing to determine whether a certified judgment resulted from appellee's negligent driving.


OVERVIEW: Appellee licensee executed a judgment note that contained a confession of judgment clause for an insurance company based on his involvement in a motor vehicle accident. 1967 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 198; 43 Pa. D. & C.2d 177



June 30, 1967, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Request to stay execution.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner garnishee requested pursuant to Pa. R. Civ. 3121(a)(4) that execution by plaintiff judgment creditor be stayed as to the proceeds of a life insurance policy the garnishee had issued to defendant husband and had made payable to defendant wife after the creditor had obtained a judgment against the husband and wife.


OVERVIEW: The creditor obtained a judgment against the husband and wife and acquired a writ of garnishment against a life insurance policy issued to the husband. The garnishee filed a petition under Pa. R. Civ. 3121(a)(4) to stay execution by the creditor as to the proceeds of the policy on the basis that Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 40, § 534 made the policy exempt from garnishment. The creditor replied to the petition, contending that the proceeds of the policy were not exempt from execution and that § 534 was otherwise unconstitutional. The court stayed execution, finding that under § 534 the proceeds paid to the wife were exempt from garnishment to pay the debts of the husband and the wife. The court also ruled that § 534 did not violate the creditor's right to equal protection because the classification imposed by the statute was founded upon a reasonable basis and that the creditor otherwise did not overcome its burden to show that the statute was unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The court stayed execution as requested by the garnishee.

Hickory v. Chadderton


no. 265


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF MERCER COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1967 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 220; 43 Pa. D. & C.2d 319



May 31, 1967, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Appeal from justice of the peace.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged a decision from a justice court (Pennsylvania), which convicted him of violating plaintiff town's zoning ordinance. It was shown that the owner violated Hickory Township, Pa., Ordinance of 1965, § 47.22(c), which provided that the owner or lessee of an airport could have a maximum of one full-time employee live with his immediate family on the airport property to act as a guard or watchman.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was notified by letter that he violated the ordinance because he had a total of three families living on his airport property, one living in a house thereon and the other two in trailers, which had been moved onto the premises some time prior thereto. The airport manager and his family lived in one of the trailers; a portion of the house was used for general administration and control purposes and at the time of the notification of violation, two families, in addition to the airport manager and his family lived on the premises. Defendant contested the conviction on the ground that the ordinance was unconstitutional. The court found that the house located on the premises and the two trailers in question were residential dwelling units as contemplated by the statute. Because of a mandate by an appellate court that the ordinance be liberally construed, it was not unconstitutional, but rather it was a reasonable regulation by which the government body was properly planning for the future, in the light of the conditions then existing. The municipality properly exercised its responsibility of ensuring that the land set aside for airport use was utilized to its fullest extent.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed defendant's appeal.

Huber v. Pennsylvania Human Relations Commission


no. 318


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF DAUPHIN COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1967 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 236; 43 Pa. D. & C.2d 437



May 29, 1967, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Appeal from order of Pennsylvania Human Relations Commission.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant landlords sought review of a decision and final order of appellee Pennsylvania Human Relations Commission, which determined that the landlords committed unlawful discriminatory practices under § 5(h) of the Pennsylvania Human Relations Act (Act), Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 43, § 951 et seq., in that they refused to rent commercial housing to certain tenants because of their race.


OVERVIEW: The Commission found that the landlords committed unlawful discriminatory practices in refusing to rent to certain tenants because of the tenants' race. The final order of the Commission directed the landlords to cease and desist from this unlawful discriminatory practice. The landlords appealed the order, arguing that notice was insufficient and that the Act was unconstitutional. The court disagreed, dismissed the appeal, and affirmed the Commission's adjudication. The court concluded that the landlords had adequate notice of the hearing before the Commission and filed prompt responses thereto. The court further concluded that the landlords were not denied the equal protection of the law and the due process of law because of the malapportionment of legislatures which passed the Act and the pertinent amendments thereto. Finally, the court held that the Act was a proper exercise of the police power to prohibit discrimination in leasing, selling, or financing of commercial housing against any person because of race, color, religious creed, ancestry, or national origin, and did not violate the Fourteenth Amendment.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the appeal and affirmed the adjudication.

Frick v. Stevens


no. 1


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF CUMBERLAND COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1967 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 182; 43 Pa. D. & C.2d 6



May 25, 1967, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Adjudication in equity.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff daughter filed a complaint in equity against defendant author for a permanent injunction to enjoin and prohibit the sale and distribution of a book that included information about the daughter's father.


OVERVIEW: The author was the chairman of a historical society and had written a historical book. The book made reference to the daughter' s father, who was an industrialist. After reading a copy of the book, the daughter filed suit in equity to enjoin its sale and distribution. The daughter alleged that the statements contained in the book concerning her father were malicious and defamatory. The author claimed that the statement were true and not malicious and absent such maliciousness were privileged under the First Amendment. The author also argued that relief could not be granted because he neither published nor distributed the book but merely wrote it. The court dismissed the complaint, holding that the daughter was not entitled to injunctive relief because no substantial right of the daughter would be impaired and the remedy at law was not inadequate. There has been no wrong done by defendant and plaintiff has suffered no injury so there is nothing to redress in this case. Injunctive relief could not be applied in the case because the court lacked jurisdiction to order the publisher to cease publication and distribution because the publisher was not party to the action.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the daughter's complaint.

Weaver v. Brandywine Township


no. 63


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF CHESTER COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1967 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 217; 43 Pa. D. & C.2d 309



March 16, 1967, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Complaint challenging validity of ordinance.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff aggrieved parties filed a complaint under the provisions of The Second Class Township Code of May 1, 1933, as amended by the Act of June 6, 1963, Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 53, § 65741, against defendant Brandywine Township (Pennsylvania). Plaintiffs sought a declaration that a mobile home park ordinance was invalid.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs claimed that they were aggrieved by an ordinance regulating mobile home parks. The court held that the ordinance was invalid because it was not properly adopted, as the ordinance was not properly recorded as required by The Second Class Township Code of May 1, 1933, as amended, the Act of June 6, 1963, Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 53, § 65741. The court's conclusion was supported by Pa. Stat. Ann. tit. 53, § 67004. No seal was required. It was only when recording was accomplished through stapling or gluing that a seal was needed and then only for the purpose of preventing a substitution of pages. No signatures were required to be affixed to an ordinance upon its adoption because the minutes of the Township's supervisors were adequate evidence that the ordinance was voted upon and approved.


OUTCOME: The ordinance was declared to be without legal effect. The complaint as to the ordinance's legality was sustained.

Commonwealth v. Manbeck, Inc.


no. 97


COMMON PLEAS COURT OF DAUPHIN COUNTY, PENNSYLVANIA


1967 Pa. Dist. & Cnty. Dec. LEXIS 69; 45 Pa. D. & C.2d 549



March 9, 1967, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Appeal from order of Board of Finance and Revenue.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant taxpayer filed an appeal from an order of the Pennsylvania Board of Finance and Revenue affirming appellee Commonwealth's denial of the taxpayer's petition for a refund of sales and use taxes as untimely under § 553(b) of the Pennsylvania Tax Act of May 29, 1963 for Education, P.L. 49.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayer paid the taxes due under the Commonwealth's assessment of sales and use taxes on equipment and supplies used directly for its business. Approximately 14 months after the assessment, the taxpayer filed its petition for a refund for the taxes paid. The Commonwealth's Department of Revenue denied the refund on the grounds that the petition was untimely because it was not filed within six months of the assessment. On appeal, the court dismissed the appeal. The court held that the taxpayer's claim was untimely under § 553(b) of the Pennsylvania Tax Act of May 29, 1963 for Education, P.L. 49, because it was not filed within six months of the assessment. The five-year statute of limitations in § 553(d) of the Act did not apply because the taxpayer did not allege that in his case it was held that a prior interpretation affecting the taxpayer's liability was erroneous. The court concluded that the Act of August 23, 1961, P.L. 1092, did not authorize the taxpayer's petition for a refund without regard to the time limitations contained in § 553 of the Tax Act of May 29, 1963 for Education.


OUTCOME: The court entered an order dismissing the taxpayer's appeal.
