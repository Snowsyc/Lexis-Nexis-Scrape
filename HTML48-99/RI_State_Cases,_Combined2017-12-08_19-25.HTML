OVERVIEW: The Rhode Island Ethics Commission adopted a code of ethics, prohibiting members of the general assembly from serving as members of governmental public boards or commissions and from participating in the appointment process to those entities. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: City that sold property at a tax sale appealed a decision of the Superior Court of Providence County (Rhode Island) that the sale was invalid because R.I. Gen. Laws § 44-9-11, which provided for notice of the sale, was unconstitutional as violative of due process. City claimed that it provided adequate notice of the sale and that § 44-9-11 was constitutional.


OVERVIEW: After owner died, city sold her property for nonpayment of taxes to petitioner, a trust. The remaindermen, who had an interest in the property but were not notified of the sale, sold the property to respondents. After petitioner tried to enforce the sale respondents challenged its validity, claiming it was void because remaindermen were not notified of it. The trial court held that the notice statute, R.I. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff municipalities appealed a judgment from the Superior Court, Providence County (Rhode Island), in which the trial court declared R.I. Gen. Laws §§ 44-43-1 and 44-34-2, which permitted the variance of a motor vehicle tax according to plaintiffs' personal property tax rates, constitutional.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff municipalities brought an action, challenging the constitutionality of R.I. Gen. Laws §§ 44-34-1 and 44-34-2. Specifically, plaintiffs argued that the statutes impermissibly provided for a motor vehicle tax that varied according to plaintiffs' personal property tax rate. The trial court entered a final judgment declaring the tax constitutional. Plaintiffs appealed, asserting that the statutes violated the due process and equal protection clauses of the United States Constitution and the Rhode Island Constitution because excise tax rates were required to be uniform throughout the state. The court affirmed the decision, concluding that the tax was a property tax and not an excise tax. The tax, which was based on a vehicle's value, was incidental to ownership. Further, although the nonpayment of the tax impeded registration of the vehicle, the tax was based on a vehicle's uniform value as determined by a statewide commission. The actual assessment of the tax was levied according to plaintiffs' particular tax rates and the collection of the property tax merely was aided by the fact that a delinquent taxpayer could not renew the vehicle's registration if the tax was not paid.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment, holding that the statutes, which permitted the variance of a motor vehicle tax according to plaintiff municipalities' personal property tax rate, were constitutional where the tax computed upon the valuation of a motor vehicle was a property tax, rather than an excise tax.

The court held that exclusion of the daughter's testimony was beyond the bounds of the lower court's jurisdiction. The court found the disorderly conduct statute was unconstitutional as applied.

Supreme Court of Rhode Island


444 A.2d 855; 1982 R.I. LEXIS 850



April 27, 1982 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged the judgment from the superior court (Rhode Island), which convicted him of cruelly killing an animal under R.I. Gen. Laws § 4-1-2.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that R.I. Gen. Laws § 4-1-2 was unconstitutionally vague. The court affirmed and found that the criminal complaint provided defendant with adequate notice of the offense with which he was charged. The court found that defendant was interviewed more than once by police officers who were investigating the incident. Defendant's involvement was such that he could have had no doubt concerning the offense with which he was charged. The term "cruelly kill" appeared in § 4-1-2 within an extensive list of criminally culpable acts and omissions. Read in the context of the entire statute, it was anything but vague. The trial justice, sitting without a jury, found that defendant placed a healthy cat in a microwave oven and turned the oven on causing injuries resulting in the cat's death. He further found that defendant was indifferent to the pain and suffering he caused. The court found that reasonable inferences of severe suffering were easily drawn from the evidence. The evidence before the court was more than sufficient to satisfy the burden of proving the charge beyond a reasonable doubt.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction and denied the appeal.
