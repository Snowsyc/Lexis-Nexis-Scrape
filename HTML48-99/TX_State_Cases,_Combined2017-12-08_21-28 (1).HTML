May 12, 1965 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from Travis County


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant convict sought review of the judgment from the County Court at Law Number 2 in Travis County (Texas), which convicted and sentenced appellant by a jury trial for the offence of loitering after appealing his conviction in a municipal court.


OVERVIEW: Appellant convict was arrested when he was found going into and about a private premises and building at nighttime and was charged with loitering in violation of Austin, Tex., City Code of 1354 § 21.8, which prohibited any person to be around public or private buildings at nighttime without permission, under suspicious circumstances, and without giving a satisfactory account for their being there. After having appealed his conviction from a lower court, appellant was found guilty and sentenced in a jury trial. Appellant argued on appeal that the ordinance was unconstitutional as an unreasonable restraint upon personal liberty. The court affirmed the conviction. The court held that the ordinance was constitutional as a reasonable exercise of the city's police powers because it was definite in its application at nighttime on private premises and a private building where appellant had no right to be on or upon without consent.


OUTCOME: Judgment finding appellant convict guilty of the offense of loitering in violation of a local ordinance was affirmed because the statute was the proper exercise of the city's police powers in restraining conduct at nighttime around private premises and buildings where appellant had no right to be without consent.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Certiorari Denied, November 22, 1965. Reported at 382 U.S. 928.


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from Brazos County


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the decision of the 85th District Court, Brazos County (Texas), which convicted defendant of bribery.


OVERVIEW: Defendant, along with four codefendants, was charged with the offense of bribery of a Harris County probate judge. The cause was severed and transferred from Harris County to Brazos County. Defendant's case was the first to be tried and defendant was convicted of bribery. The court affirmed and held that the evidence was sufficient to support the judgment of conviction. The court ruled that the trial court properly denied defendant's motion to quash the indictment, finding that the indictment followed the statute, and the allegations contained therein were sufficient to charge the offense of bribery under Tex. Penal Code Ann. art. 158. The court noted that a motion to quash an indictment could not have been based upon a claim that it was returned upon evidence illegally obtained in a court of inquiry. The court ruled that the trial court did not err in ordering a change of venue and in determining that defendant be tried first. The found that the trial court properly admitted into evidence the testimony of the deputy county clerk and evidence of a list prepared by the clerk showing all the appraisers appointed by the Harris County probate judge.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction, holding that the judgment of conviction was supported by the weight of the evidence.

Garrett v. State


No. 38,274


The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals


391 S.W.2d 65; 1965 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 1037



Jun. 2, 1965 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Woodley, Judge


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged his conviction under Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6701h, §§ 31, 32(d), for failure immediately to return his license and registration to the department after they were suspended.


OVERVIEW: Appellant's conviction for failure immediately to return his suspended license and registration in violation of Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. art. 6701h, §§ 31, 32, was reversed and the prosecution was ordered dismissed. Under § 31, a suspended license and registration had to be returned to the department immediately. Under § 32, anyone who willfully failed to return a suspended license or registration as required by § 31 was subject to a fine, imprisonment, or both. The court held that § 31 was vague and uncertain as to the time in which the suspended license and registration had to be returned. The court further held that Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 6701h, § 32, construed with § 31, was invalid as a penal statute because it was void on its face for indefiniteness and uncertainty of those particular acts or omissions which constituted the offense described therein.


OUTCOME: The court reversed appellant's conviction for failing immediately to return to the department his suspended license and registration. The requirement that a suspended license or registration must be returned "immediately" was vague and uncertain as to time and was invalid as a penal statute because it was void on its face for indefiniteness and uncertainty of those particular acts or omissions which constituted the offense.

Nichols v. State

386 S.W.2d 795; 1964 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 1195



November 25, 1964 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Rehearing Denied January 20, 1965; Second Motion for Rehearing Denied March 3, 1965


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged the judgment of the Texas district court that convicted him of willfully killing one head of cattle of over the value of $ 50 by shooting the said one head of cattle with a gun with the intent to injure the owner in violation of Tex. Penal Code Ann. art. 1373.


OVERVIEW: A cattle owner reported to the police that he found one of his calves dead in the pasture with several bullet holes in its head. A car that had driven by the pasture several times was traced back to defendant. Defendant executed a written statement detailing the events that happened. Several witnesses corroborated defendant's statement. The lower court convicted defendant of willfully killing one head of cattle of over the value of $ 50 by shooting the said one head of cattle with a gun with the intent to injure the owner in violation of Tex. Penal Code Ann. art. 1373. Defendant challenged its decision asserting that the statute was unconstitutional. The court affirmed defendant's conviction because art. 1373 provided sufficient notice that "intent to injure the owner" would be included and, therefore, did not violate Tex. Const. art. 3, § 35. The court held that the jury was properly charged on the term "willful." The court also held that there was sufficient evidence to support defendant's conviction.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for willfully killing one head of cattle of over the value of $ 50 by shooting the said one head of cattle with a gun with the intent to injure the owner because the statute that listed the offense was constitutional, and the jury was properly charged on the term "willful." The court also found that there was sufficient evidence to support the conviction.

Ex parte Reyes

Court of Criminal Appeals of Texas


383 S.W.2d 804; 1964 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 1126



November 11, 1964 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner inmate sought application for writ of habeas corpus for review of an order of the trial court (Texas) that convicted him of passing as true a forged instrument, with three prior convictions for felonies alleged for enhancement, and sentenced him to life imprisonment.


OVERVIEW: Petitioner inmate filed an application for writ of habeas corpus review of an order of the trial court that sentenced him to life imprisonment, with three prior convictions for felonies alleged for enhancement, for passing as true a forged instrument. Petitioner contended that Tex. Code. Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 63 was unconstitutional because the indictment, including the allegations as to the prior convictions, was read to the jury before evidence was introduced. It was his position that he was prejudiced thereby and denied a fair and impartial trial as guaranteed by the 14th Amendment to the United States Constitution. U.S. Const. amend. IV. The court noted that the constitutionality of article 63 had been attacked many times. The court declared that it lacked the rulemaking power to declare the statute unconstitutional. It found petitioner's contention to be without merit and denied his writ of habeas corpus.


OUTCOME: The court denied petitioner inmate's application for writ of habeas corpus review of his life sentence for passing as true a forged instrument, with three prior convictions for felonies alleged for enhancement, where the court was not authorized to declare that the statute petitioner complained of was unconstitutional.

Ex parte Smith


No. 37109


Court of Criminal Appeals of Texas


383 S.W.2d 401; 1964 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 1089



October 28, 1964 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Relator accused filed an original petition for a writ of habeas corpus seeking release from custody and discharge from a fine imposed after he was held in contempt by a justice of the peace, Precinct No. 2 of Harris County (Texas), for his refusal to testify at a public court of inquiry. Relator having been indicted, the district attorney joined in the prayer that relator be discharged and took the position that the matter had become moot.


OVERVIEW: Relator accused was summoned before a public court of inquiry investigating a scheme in which attorneys paid kickbacks to city officials. Relator was denied the right to counsel, denied the right to cross-examine witnesses, denied the right to bring witnesses in his own behalf, and was not informed of the nature of the offense being inquired into. Relator refused to testify on the ground that his rights under U.S. Const. amends. V, VI, and XIV, and Texas Const. art. 1, §§ 10 and 19 were being violated. Relator was held in contempt, fined, taken into custody, and remanded to jail until he paid his fine and testified. Relator petitioned for a writ of habeas corpus. After relator was indicted, the district attorney joined in the prayer that relator be discharged from custody, and took the position that the matter was moot. The court held that the matter was not moot because relator had not paid the fine. The court granted the writ. When called to testify, relator was in the position of an accused and it was incumbent upon the justice who convened the court of inquiry to provide the procedural safeguards guaranteed an accused under the United States and Texas constitutions.


OUTCOME: The petition was granted and a writ was issued ordering relator accused discharged. Although relator was no longer in a position to purge the contempt because he had been indicted, the matter was not moot because the fine also assessed as a punishment for contempt was still unsatisfied. The court of inquiry placed relator in the position of an accused, and compelling him to testify deprived him of his constitutional rights.

Crain v. State


No. 36,919


Court of Criminal Appeals of Texas


394 S.W.2d 165; 1964 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 802



October 7, 1964 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] On Motion for Rehearing, January 13, 1965, Reported at: 394 S.W.2d at 169.


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from Harris County.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of the judgment entered by the Harris County trial court (Texas), convicting him of murder, contending that the evidence was insufficient to support the jury's verdict which resolved the issue of insanity against appellant. Appellant was sentenced to death. Appellant also filed a motion for rehearing upon decision of this case.


OVERVIEW: Appellant, who relied on a defense of temporary insanity, was convicted of murder for choking and strangling an 11-month old boy and was sentenced to death. Appellant had confessed to the crime, but testified that he blacked out and did not realize he was committing the act until it was over. On appeal, the court affirmed the conviction. The court held that the evidence was sufficient to support the verdict. Next, the court held that the trial court did not err in refusing appellant's motion that he be examined at the expense of the state by a competent psychiatrist who was not connected with the prosecution because such services were not authorized. Appellant failed to preserve for review an issue relating to the admissibility of his military record because he never moved for admission of the record. The trial court did not err in refusing to instruct the jury to not consider questions to appellant about his military convictions. The use of a phrase regarding insanity did not mislead the jury and his rights were fairly and adequately protected in the charge. Finally, the jury's agreement not to discuss their deliberations was not cause for a reversal.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's conviction of appellant for murder because the evidence was sufficient to support the verdict. Appellant failed to preserve other issues for review and those issues which were preserved did not constitute error. The court further overruled appellant's motion for rehearing.
