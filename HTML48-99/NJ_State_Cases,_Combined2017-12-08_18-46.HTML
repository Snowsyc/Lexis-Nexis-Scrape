CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: After defendants were found guilty of murder in the first degree and sentenced to death, and the convictions were affirmed by the court on appeal, defendants appealed from an order of the Superior Court (New Jersey), which denied defendants' fifth application for post-conviction relief.


OVERVIEW: The jury found defendants guilty of murder in the first degree and the trial court sentenced them to death. On consideration of the trial court's denial of defendants' fifth application for post-conviction relief, the court affirmed. The court rejected defendants' allegations that prior to and at the time they confessed, they were subjected to physical and mental coercion and held incommunicado, because the court previously found their stories unbelievable. As to defendants allegations for the first time that they asked for and were denied an opportunity to consult with an attorney and were not advised of their right to remain silent, the court found that it had already ruled that the confessions were voluntary, and held that the decision in Escobedo v. Illinois, 378 U.S. 478, 84 S. Ct. 1758, 12 L. Ed. 2d 977 (1964) did not have retroactive effect. Defendants' convictions were obtained as a result of a procedure not considered fundamentally unfair at that time, and subsequent judicial decisions cast no substantial doubts upon the reliability of the determination already made, and so no compelling reason existed for disturbing a decision no longer subject to direct appeal.


OUTCOME: The order denying defendants' fifth application for post-conviction relief was affirmed, because the court had previously found unbelievable defendants' stories of physical and mental coercion prior to their confessions, and because a recent U.S. Supreme Court decision did not have retroactive effect.

November 6, 1963, Argued  
November 25, 1964, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reargued October 5, 1964.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant voters sought review of an order, entered by the Superior Court (New Jersey), in favor of appellee public officials, holding that certain state constitutional provisions fixing the composition of the state legislature were not invalid under the Equal Protection Clause contained in U.S. Const. amend. XIV.


OVERVIEW: Appellant voters filed suit against appellees, a county and state officials, claiming that state constitutional provisions fixing the makeup of the state legislature were invalid under U.S. Const. amend. XIV. After the trial court ruled for appellees, appellants sought review, and the court, which had certified the case prior to appellate division hearing, reversed. It found that N.J. Const. art. IV, §§ 1, 2, and 3, did not satisfy the federal constitutional "one person, one vote" requirement, and were invalid under Reynolds v. Sims, 377 U.S. 533, 12 L. Ed. 2d 506 (1964); that U.S. Const. amend. XIV required population-based apportionment of all state legislative seats; and that in the absence of such apportionment, individual rights to vote for state legislators had been unconstitutionally impaired. The court rejected appellees' request for abstention; found no merit in appellees' claim of "federal analogy;" rejected a claim that the state, having not ratified it, was not bound by U.S. Const. amend. XIV; ordered the legislature to undertake reapportionment within a certain time frame; and recommended use of a constitutional convention to design a new legislative structure.


OUTCOME: The order rejecting appellant voters' claim that N.J. Const. art. IV, §§ 1, 2, and 3, were unconstitutional as violative of the "one person, one vote" principle in U.S. Const. amend. XIV was reversed. The court, declining to abstain, directed appellee county and state officials to promptly remedy the unconstitutional impairment of the value of citizens' votes by reapportioning the state legislature within a specified time frame.

43 N.J. 390; 204 A.2d 853; 1964 N.J. LEXIS 166


September 21, 1964, Argued  
November 16, 1964, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant liquor licensees sought review of a judgment of the Superior Court New Jersey), which upheld the constitutionality of N.J. Stat. Ann. §§ 33:1-12.31 et seq., a statute designed to limit retail liquor licenses to two per person, in appellants' action challenging that law.


OVERVIEW: Appellant liquor license holders challenged the decision that upheld that constitutionality of N.J. Stat. Ann. §§ 33:1-12.31 et seq., which was designed to limit retail liquor licenses to two per person. Appellants argued that the statute was enacted without justification to any observable public purpose, only for the economic enhancement of private competitive interests, and that it was ambiguous. The court affirmed the judgment and held that §§ 1-12.31 et seq. was constitutional. The public purpose of the statute was to curb relationships and practices calculated to stimulate sales and impair the state's policy favoring trade stability and the promotion of temperance. Appellants had no constitutional rights to acquire additional license privileges contrary to the policy in §§ 1-12.31 et seq. The legislature could, without at all infringing upon the due process clause, either terminate or severely regulate all liquor sales within the state. The statute applied equally and indiscriminately, by its terms, to all retail licensees similarly situated.


OUTCOME: The judgment upholding the constitutionality of a statute that was designed to limit retail liquor licenses to two per person was affirmed, because said statute was enacted on a rational basis, and it was applied equally and indiscriminately to all retail licensees similarly situated.

State v. Sullivan


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Supreme Court of New Jersey


43 N.J. 209; 203 A.2d 177; 1964 N.J. LEXIS 147


June 1, 1964, Argued  
July 22, 1964, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants appealed from a judgment of the Superior Court (New Jersey), which convicted defendants for murder in the first degree based, in part, on testimony of co-indictees who later recanted their confessions, and which denied their motions for new trials.


OVERVIEW: Defendants were convicted of first-degree murder, due in part to testimony of co-indictees, who had pleaded non vult, and later recanted. Defendants were denied a new trial, and appealed. The court held that limits on defendants' examination of the co-indictees were not prejudicial; rejected untimely claims as to involuntariness of a confession; upheld the denial of acquittal motions; and upheld the convictions. The court agreed that the co-indictees' recantations were fabricated; rejected error as to jury questions about non vult pleas; found no error in the jury's non-sequestration; and held that the state's failure to disclose a grand jury transcript did not merit reversal. The court rejected a constitutional claim relating to alleged discrepancies between penalties imposed after convictions on non vult or not guilty pleas, because defendant had not tried to enter such a plea, and thus could not raise the issue. While the lower court's refusal to recuse itself was proper, it was error to refuse to let counsel examine notes to which a witness referred during the new trial motion hearing, and the matter was remanded for production of the notes and assessment of their effect.


OUTCOME: The judgment convicting defendants for first degree murder and denying their motions for a new trial were affirmed despite a post-trial recanting of inculpatory testimony by two co-indictees, because the lower court correctly deemed the new testimony a fabrication. However, the lower court erred in refusing to order the production to defendants of notes used by a witness in the post-trial proceedings, and that issue was therefore remanded.

Supreme Court of New Jersey


42 N.J. 191; 199 A.2d 834; 1964 N.J. LEXIS 195


January 20, 21, 1964, Argued  
April 20, 1964, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant state treasurer sought review of a ruling, by the Superior Court of New Jersey, denying him a declaratory judgment that the New Jersey State Area Redevelopment Assistance Act, N.J. Stat. Ann. §§ 13:1B-15.13 et seq., as administered by appellee commissioners, constituted a prohibited lending of the credit of the state or its subdivisions in violation of N.J. Const. art. VIII, §§ 2(1), 3(2), and 3(3).


OVERVIEW: Appellant New Jersey State Treasurer sought a judgment that the New Jersey State Area Redevelopment Assistance Act, N.J. Stat. Ann. §§ 13:1B-15.13 et seq. (ARAA), as overseen by appellee commissioners, violated N.J. Const. art. VIII, §§ 2(1), 3(2), and 3(3), by allowing public credit to be lent to private entities. After a trial court ruling of constitutionality, appellant sought review. The court certified the case prior to appellate division argument, and affirmed, holding that appellant had standing; that the ARAA's goal of alleviating unemployment was a "public purpose" consistent with the state's historical role in helping the poor; that the use of public money to pay private entities for ARAA projects was constitutional, because third parties could be hired and paid to do lawful acts; that the private entities would not only be required to repay the loans, but would devote efforts to the public purposes, essentially becoming special public agents; that their receipt of an incidental private benefit did not violate the constitution, nor result in an improper delegation of power; and that the ARAA was not a "special" law in violation of N.J. Const. art. IV, § 7(9).


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's determination of constitutionality of a law authorizing appellees, as commissioners of an independent state agency, to lend money to private entities who were operating redevelopment programs targeted at locales with high unemployment, rejecting appellant state treasurer's claim that those loans were a prohibited lending of public monies to private entities which violated the state constitution.

Supreme Court of New Jersey


42 N.J. 56; 198 A.2d 765; 1964 N.J. LEXIS 183

March 17, 1964, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: McAllister v. Board of Education, 79 N.J. Super. 249, 191 A.2d 212, 1963 N.J. Super. LEXIS 400 (App.Div., 1963)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant employer sought review of the judgment of the Superior Court, Appellate Division (New Jersey), which affirmed the judgment upholding the decision of the Workmen's Compensation Division awarding respondent widow dependency benefits upon the death of appellant's employee.


OVERVIEW: Decedent, who fell from a ladder at work and suffered injury, filed for workmen's compensation and was found totally and permanently disabled. After his death from severe emotional upset, respondent widow filed her dependency claim. At a hearing before the workmen's compensation division, appellant employer and respondent submitted conflicting medical testimony on the causal relationship between decedent's accident and death. The division found for respondent and determined that the applicable compensation rates were those in force under N.J. Stat. Ann. § 34:15-13 at the time of the decedent's death, and appellant sought review. The county court affirmed, and, on appeal, so did the appellate court. Appellant's petition for certification was granted. The court affirmed, as nothing warranted disturbance of the appellate court's judgment. The court concluded that it was not required to make new findings of fact where the appellate court's decision was unanimous. The court found that respondent's right to dependency benefits accrued on decedent's death, and the rates in effect on that date applied.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the appellate court upholding the judgment of the county court that affirmed the decision of the workmen's compensation division awarding respondent widow dependency benefits upon the death of appellant employer's employee was affirmed, as no reason existed to overturn the appellate court's decision, and the rates effective on employee's date of death applied to respondent's disability benefits award.

Supreme Court of New Jersey


42 N.J. 32; 198 A.2d 753; 1964 N.J. LEXIS 181


January 20, 1964, Argued  
March 16, 1964, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant corporation challenged a judgment from the Superior Court (New Jersey), which denied defendant's motion for relief after judgment was entered in favor of plaintiff state government in an action for custodial escheat of unclaimed dividends, interest, wages and other cash obligations in the possession of defendant.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff state government sought custodial escheat of unclaimed dividends, interest, wages and other cash obligations in the possession of defendant corporation. After defendant consented to entry of partial judgment, the trial court directed defendant to pay plaintiff and retained jurisdiction over the action. When the United States Supreme Court reversed a state's judgment of escheat, defendant filed a motion seeking to be relieved of the escheat judgment. Defendant's motion was denied. On appeal, the court held that the trial court had personal jurisdiction, because there was due service of process on the defendant. Jurisdiction of the subject matter was present, because the trial court had power to determine cases of the class to which plaintiff's action belonged. The court determined that when defendant chose not to appeal from the judgment, it deliberately subjected itself to the binding effects of res judicata. Plaintiff's claims were superior to defendant's claims, and the trial court was within its discretionary authority when it denied defendant's motion. Therefore, the judgment was affirmed.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment denying defendant corporation's motion for relief after judgment was entered in favor of plaintiff state government in an action seeking custodial escheat of unclaimed dividends, interest, wages and other cash obligations in the possession of defendant was affirmed, because defendant's motion was barred by res judicata, and the trial court acted within its authority.
