PRIOR HISTORY: From Trousdale County, The Honorable R. H. Bradshaw Judge. Post-conviction petitions.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner inmates appealed a judgment of a trial court of Trousdale County (Tennessee), which denied their petition for relief in a post-conviction action challenging the constitutionality of their commuted life sentences.


OVERVIEW: The inmates were jointly indicted, tried, and convicted of first degree murder. They were sentenced to death by electrocution, which was the mandatory penalty for first-degree murder under then-existing state law. While their convictions were on appeal, the governor commuted their sentences to life imprisonment. The judgment was affirmed by the Tennessee Supreme Court, including the commutation. Nonetheless, the inmates contended that when the mandatory death penalty statute was held to be unconstitutional, the effect was to revive the prior penalty statute, 1919 Tenn. Acts ch. 5, which provided a range of punishment for first-degree murder of death or imprisonment for life or some period of 20 years. The court agreed; however, the court noted that the death penalty provisions of the 1919 statute could not have been severed because there was no severability clause. Therefore, the 1919 statute had been held to be unconstitutional in its entirety, which gave rise to the revival of an even earlier statute, 1915 Tenn. Acts ch. 181, providing for a life sentence for first degree murder. Hence, the inmates were serving the only available penalty for the offense.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.
