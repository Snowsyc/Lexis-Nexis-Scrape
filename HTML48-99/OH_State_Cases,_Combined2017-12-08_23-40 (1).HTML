State v. Helbig


No. 52949


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Eighth Appellate District, Cuyahoga County


1988 Ohio App. LEXIS 27



January 14, 1988, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Criminal appeal from Common Pleas Court, Case No. CR-209272.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was found guilty by a trial court (Ohio) as charged of failure to obtain an inspection for a vehicle assembled from component parts not by the manufacturer prior to issuance of title in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. §. 4505.111. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant contended that § 4505.111 was void for vagueness based on the following: (1) The statute failed to state against whom it was to be applied; (2) it did not define "assembled from component parts;" and (3) it did not define "a person other than a manufacturer." Clearly, the plain unambiguous and definite language of the statute indicated it was to be applied to anyone who was or would be the titleholder to the motor vehicle. It did not apply to one who was repairing or assembling the vehicle or who was in possession of the vehicle unless such person was or was to become the titleholder. The language of the statute "assembled from component parts" was sufficiently definite and clear to appraise ordinary persons of what conduct was proscribed. The statute was clear as to the demarcation between assemblage and repair. A reasonable trier of fact could have easily found beyond a reasonable doubt defendant violated all the essential elements of § 4505.111 and could infer based upon the evidence presented, the vehicle was repaired while defendant was the titleholder. Thus, defendant's failure to obtain a vehicle inspection constituted a violation of the law.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed.

OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the lower court.

1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 9389



October 27, 1987, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Petition for Writ of Habeas Corpus



DISPOSITION: PETITION DENIED. 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner, a prisoner in a county jail, sought a writ of habeas corpus against respondent sheriff pursuant to Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2725.02. He claimed that he was entitled to be released because he should have been given a good time credit under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2967.19. The court granted the writ the day after it held a hearing on the matter, at which time it took the matter under advisement.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of a misdemeanor charge. A trial court refused to order his release because the mandatory good time provisions of § 2967.19 did not apply to misdemeanor offenders who were serving a sentence in a county jail. Defendant argued that this violated equal protection because a state prison inmate who received good time credits could thereby serve a shorter sentence for a more serious offense than that committed by defendant. The court stated that the legislature was free to discriminate on a rational basis between different classes of criminal offenders as long as the different treatment was not based on a constitutionally impermissible classification. Good time was an inducement to rehabilitation, and rehabilitation was not a primary function of detention in a county jail. Thus, it was not unreasonable to deny a county jail inmate the right to have a reduction in his sentence for good behavior. Moreover, in Ohio, misdemeanor offenders had sentencing alternatives such as work release programs, which provided specific benefits that furloughs from state prisons did not provide. Thus, there were rational bases for treating different classes of offenders differently.


OUTCOME: The court quashed the writ of habeas corpus it had previously granted. It denied the relief requested by petitioner.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL FROM JUDGMENT ENTERED IN THE COMMON PLEAS COURT COUNTY OF SUMMIT, OHIO, CASE NO. CV 85 9 2883.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent state officials challenged the judgment of the Common Pleas Court of Summit County (Ohio), which ruled that relator was eligible for parole after serving 10 full years of imprisonment. Relator had been convicted of first-degree murder in violation of former Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2901.01 and sentenced to life imprisonment.


OVERVIEW: The officials argued that the trial court erred in finding that relator was convicted of a "noncapital" offense. On appeal, the court agreed. Former Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2967.13(C) (1974) did provide for parole eligibility after serving 10 full years of imprisonment for a noncapital offense. However, after the death penalty was declared unconstitutional in Furman v. Georgia, 408 U.S. 238 (1972), the Supreme Court of Ohio ruled that a first-degree murder in violation of former Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2901.01 was still a capital offense. The legislature's 1974 use of the term "noncapital offense" in the face of the prior judicial declarations of the unconstitutionality of the death penalty, as it was then provided for by for statute, was a legislative recognition that capital offenses continued independent of the death penalty.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court.

Court of Appeals of Ohio, Eighth Appellate District, Cuyahoga County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 7170



February 12, 1987, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Appeal from the Court of Common Pleas Division of Domestic Relations, Cuyahoga County, No. D-36907



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant wife sought review of a judgment of the Court of Common Pleas Division of Domestic Relations, Cuyahoga County (Ohio), which modified her divorce decree to read that no children were born of her marriage to appellee husband. The husband had filed a motion under Ohio R. Civ. P. 60(B)(5), claiming that he was not the father.


OVERVIEW: The parties were granted an uncontested divorce, stating that two children were born of the marriage. The husband was ordered to pay support for the children. He did not pay support, but for six years no action was taken to enforce his obligation. He was eventually charged with contempt of court for failing to pay. He filed the complaint to determine the parent-child relationship, and the decree was modified. The wife argued that the trial court erred in granting relief more than nine years after judgment. The court affirmed the trial court. The husband might have pursued the matter more diligently. However, the trial court did not abuse its discretion in considering the motion to be timely. The trial court had to consider whether it was in the best interest of the children to grow up believing that he was their father when, in fact, he was not. A more important factor was the fact that a fraud may have been perpetrated on the trial court. The judgment had been obviously wrong as to a substantial issue and was properly modified.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment granted to the husband.

In re Dupont


No. 52713


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Eighth Appellate District, Cuyahoga County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 7202



February 12, 1987, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Criminal appeal from Juvenile Court, No. 8506507.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, a juvenile, challenged a judgment of the Juvenile Court (Ohio) in favor of the State, which found her delinquent on a charge of complicity to rape, under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2907.02 (Act), and placed her on probation under her parents' supervision.


OVERVIEW: The State sought to have defendant found delinquent, based on a charge of complicity to rape, under the Act. The charge stemmed from an incident involving two children under the age of 13. Defendant alleged that they were simply "playing doctor." The juvenile court overruled defendant's motion to dismiss for want of a speedy trial. Defendant was found delinquent. She was sentenced to probation under her parents' supervision. On appeal, the court noted that defendant failed to provide a transcript of the proceeding in the juvenile court. The court stated that it would therefore presume those proceedings were regular. The court held that defendant waived the issue of the constitutionality of the Act as applied to juveniles by failing to raise that issue below. The court noted that the right she sought to vindicate was in existence before or at the time of trial. Further, the court held that her motion to dismiss was properly overruled. The court noted that the time for a speedy trial did not commence until after a juvenile court relinquished its jurisdiction.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the juvenile court's judgment, which found defendant delinquent on a charge of complicity to rape. The court ordered that the State should recover from defendant its costs taxed herein. The case was remanded to the juvenile court for execution of sentence.

STATE v. HOLLEY


C.A. No. 12701


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Ninth Appellate District, Summit County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 5884



February 11, 1987, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL FROM JUDGMENT ENTERED IN THE COMMON PLEAS COURT COUNTY OF SUMMIT, OHIO CASE NO. CR 86 03 0308


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of his conviction by the Common Pleas Court of Summit County, Ohio, for sexual battery in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2907.03(A)(1).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of sexual battery in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2907.03(A)(1). Upon appeal, the court affirmed. The court held that the victim testified that when defendant lifted her nightshirt and removed her pajama bottoms she was too frightened to scream. She further testified that she experienced a great deal of pain during and after the attack. The court found that this evidence failed to persuade it that the trier of fact lost its way and created a manifest miscarriage of justice in convicting defendant of knowingly coercing the victim to submit to sexual intercourse by a means that would prevent resistance by a person or ordinary resolution in violation of § 2907.03(A)(1). The court found that defendant's enhanced sentence was proper because the State legislature has manifested a clear intent to provide a harsher penalty for any offender who caused his victim physical harm during the commission of a third or fourth degree felony. Therefore, the application of the enhanced penalty statute, to an offender convicted of sexual battery did not constitute a violation of the Double Jeopardy Clause.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for sexual battery.

No. 87AP-115


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Tenth Appellate District, Franklin County


33 Ohio App. 3d 168; 514 N.E.2d 946; 1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 9778; 126 L.R.R.M. 3108



February 11, 1987, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Cause dismissed. 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Relator public employer filed an action seeking a writ of prohibition to enjoin respondent State Employment Relations Board from exercising jurisdiction pursuant to Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4117.16 in a proceeding filed by the employer to enjoin a strike. The Board and respondent union filed motions to dismiss the action.


OVERVIEW: The employer commenced a proceeding under § 4117.16 to enjoin a strike. Under § 4117.16, the employer was required to seek authorization from the Board to enjoin a strike beyond the effective period of a temporary restraining order issued by the court of common pleas. The employer sought a writ of prohibition to enjoin the Board from exercising jurisdiction under § 4117.16, claiming that the Board would purport to exercise quasi-judicial power which it could not constitutionally possess. The court held that the employer was not entitled to a writ of prohibition because it was debatable whether the power being exercised by the Board was judicial or quasi-judicial, courts were to construe statutes to avoid unconstitutionality if possible, and the employer had adequate remedies at law. The court also held that prohibition would be denied in doubtful cases and was to be granted only where a relator had a clear right to relief.


OUTCOME: The court sustained the motions to dismiss.

State v. Watkins


C.A. No. L-85-303


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Sixth Appellate District, Lucas County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 5780



February 6, 1987, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL FROM LUCAS COUNTY COMMON PLEAS COURT NO. CR 85-6102



DISPOSITION: On consideration whereof, the court finds that the defendant was not prejudiced or prevented from having a fair trial, and judgment of the Lucas County Court of Common Pleas is affirmed. This cause is remanded to said court for execution of sentence and assessment of costs. Costs assessed against appellant.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged a judgment of the Lucas County Common Pleas Court (Ohio), which convicted him of aggravated murder in the commission of aggravated burglary.


OVERVIEW: The jury at defendant's trial was composed of nine white and three black persons. Two black jurors were removed by peremptory challenges, and a third was removed for cause. The court held that the jury in the case was properly constituted. Defendant did not make a prima facie case of discrimination in the jury selection process. The record reflected proper questioning of each of the prospective jurors during the voir dire proceedings, and the proportion of black jurors on the panel exceeded the percentage of black citizens in the county. The trial court did not abuse its discretion in granting the State's motion for an order that limited defendant's questioning of witnesses regarding a death of a third-party that was allegedly caused by the witnesses. The trial court was correct in its refusal to allow defendant to participate as co-counsel in his own defense because defendant was adequately represented at trial by counsel. There was no support for defendant's contention that involuntary manslaughter in the commission of a misdemeanor was in any sense a proper charge for the trial court to give the jury.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for aggravated murder in the commission of aggravated burglary and remanded the cause to the trial court for execution of sentence and assessment of costs.

No. 1246


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Eleventh Appellate District, Ashtabula County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 5804



February 6, 1987 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Civil Appeal from the Ashtabula County Court of Common Pleas, Case No. 80801.



DISPOSITION: The Judgment of the trial court is affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant liquor commission sought review of a decision of the Ashtabula County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio), which found that appellee owner did not violate Ohio Liq. Control Comm'n R. 4301: 1-1-53.


OVERVIEW: The commission ordered a suspension of the owners liquor permit for violation of Regulation 4301:1-1-53. The owner was charged with having, keeping, or possessing in and upon the permit premises a slot machine that was used for gaming or wagering. The commission found the owner in violation of Regulation 4301:1-1-53 and ordered a suspension of her liquor license. The trial court reversed the decision. On appeal, the court affirmed the decision. The court rejected the commission's argument that the trial court erred in ruling the order of the commission was not supported by reliable, probative, and substantial evidence, and was not in accordance with law. The court held that, making every reasonable presumption in favor of the judgment of the trial court, it was clear that the trial court correctly found that there was no proof of usage of gambling devices. The court stated that there was no evidence that the possession of the gambling devices was with intent to violate the Ohio Rev. Code Ann. The court also rejected the commission's argument that slot machines were gambling devices per se, and that intent and purpose to violate the law was therefore implied.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

State v. Gardner


No. 51678


Court of Appeals of Ohio, Eighth Appellate District, Cuyahoga County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 7182



February 5, 1987, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Criminal appeal from Court of Common Pleas, Case No. CR-200,754.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed his conviction by the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) for felonious assault, a violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2903.11, with a gun specification, a violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2929.71. He argued that the trial court erred in charging the jury that he had the burden of proving self-defense, it erred in denying an acquittal motion, and that the conviction was not supported by sufficient evidence.


OVERVIEW: Several youth gathered in the parking lot adjacent to defendant's business. Defendant ordered them to leave and fired shots in the air when they did not leave. The victim almost hit defendant with his car when he left and the two exchanged words. Defendant thought the victim reached for a gun and shot into the car. On appeal, the court found that the trial court did not err in charging that defendant had the burden of proving the affirmative defense of self-defense. The requirement was not unconstitutional because the defense did not negate any of the elements of felonious assault, which the State was required to prove beyond a reasonable doubt. The defense of self-defense did not negate criminal intent. The evidence presented was sufficient to sustain the conviction when defendant admitted to firing into the car with the intent of hitting the victim and he used a deadly weapon. The jury's determination that defendant did not establish that he acted in self-defense was not against the manifest weight of the evidence. Defendant did not present any evidence that he was reasonably in fear of imminent bodily injury when he went out into the parking lot.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the conviction.

STATE v. BABB

Court of Appeals of Ohio, First Appellate District, Hamilton County


1987 Ohio App. LEXIS 5747



January 30, 1987, Decided and Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] Hamilton County Municipal Court, TRIAL NOS. 85 TRC 22747A, 85 TRC 22747B, 85 TRC 22747D.



DISPOSITION: The judgment of the trial court is affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the decision of the Hamilton County Municipal Court (Ohio), which found him guilty of DUI in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4511.19 after he was found guilty of violating a village ordinance prohibiting DUI.


OVERVIEW: A village police officer pursued defendant for suspected DUI. After the police officer lost contact, the city police in another jurisdiction apprehended defendant after he crashed his car while driving on the same road as he had been driving in the village. Defendant was charged with DUI by the city under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4511.19(A)(3), and by the village under a local ordinance, which did not require minimum incarceration. Defendant pled guilty to violation of the ordinance and was fined and sentenced to three days incarceration. The municipal court of the city then tried defendant for DUI under § 4511.19(A)(3). Defendant pled no contest and the trial court found him guilty. On appeal, defendant argued that his second conviction was barred under the doctrine of double jeopardy. The State cross-appealed, arguing that the ordinance was in conflict with State law and unconstitutional because it did not require incarceration. The court affirmed, first holding that by driving on the same road in the village, and then continuing into the city, defendant's conduct constituted two separate and distinct offenses for which he could be convicted and sentenced by each jurisdiction.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the decision of the trial court, which found defendant guilty of DUI.
