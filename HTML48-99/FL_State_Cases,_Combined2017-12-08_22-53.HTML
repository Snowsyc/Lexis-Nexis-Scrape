Appellant state contended the trial court erred in ruling that it was required to show that appellee either intended to communicate threats to kill three law enforcement officers directly to them, or intended that his probation officer communicate the threats to the officers.


OVERVIEW: The state charged appellee with three counts of retaliating against a witness in violation of Fla. Stat. ch. 914.23 (1991). The state asserted that appellee threatened to kill three law enforcement officers who gave deposition testimony against appellee in a pending civil lawsuit. Appellee filed a motion to dismiss. The trial court granted the motion because the state did not show that appellee either intended to communicate the threats to the officers, or intended that his probation officer communicate the threats to the officers. On appeal, the state argued that the trial court applied an improper standard. The appeals court reversed and remanded for reinstatement of the charges. The court ruled that the only intent requirement contained in the statute was that appellee make the threats with the intent to retaliate against the witnesses. Moreover, the intent issue was a question for the jury, which could not be decided on a motion to dismiss. Thus, the court ruled that evidence that appellant told his probation officer and a psychologist that he wanted to hunt the officers down and kill them because of their testimony, was sufficient to establish a prima facie case.


OUTCOME: The appeals court reversed and remanded for reinstatement of the witness retaliation charges. The court ruled that the state established a prima facie case under the statute because the state demonstrated that appellee verbally made detailed threats to kill three officers in retaliation for their deposition testimony against him.
