CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant bar owner challenged a decision by the Circuit Court in the Sixth Circuit (Connecticut), which entered judgment for appellee patron in an action brought under Connecticut's Dram Shop Act, Conn. State v. Harrington

Appellate Division of the Circuit Court of Connecticut


3 Conn. Cir. Ct. 674; 223 A.2d 316; 1966 Conn. Cir. LEXIS 240


November 29, 1965, Argued  
July 1, 1966, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Information charging the defendant with the crime of illegally conducting a promotional drawing, brought to the Circuit Court in the tenth circuit and tried to the court, Hamill, J.; judgment of guilty and appeal by the defendant.



DISPOSITION: Error; judgment directed. 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, who was the sales manager of a supermarket chain, challenged an order from the Circuit Court in the Tenth Circuit (Connecticut), which convicted him of the illegal operation of a promotional drawing, in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-290.


OVERVIEW: The supermarket chain for which defendant worked operated stores that sold dry goods, meats, household goods, produce, and other items. Defendant, acting for the chain, operated a promotional drawing for prizes for advertising purposes in which members of the general public were entitled to participate without making a purchase. Section 53-290 prohibited retail grocers from offering such drawings. In convicting defendant of violating the statute, the trial court ruled that the supermarket chain was a retail grocery chain. In setting aside the judgment, the court ruled that had the legislative purpose been to include a supermarket in the definition of a retail grocer, it could have so stated. Because the statute was penal, it was to be strictly construed. The court could not speculate as to what the legislative intent may have been. Neither could it enlarge upon the definition or, by a process of interpretation, amend the statute. That was a legislative function. The dividing line between what was lawful and unlawful could not be left to conjecture. Persons were to know with certainty whether they were committing a crime.


OUTCOME: The court set aside the judgment and remanded with direction to render a judgment declaring that defendant was not guilty.

DISPOSITION: Error; judgment directed. 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed conviction entered by the Circuit Court in the Sixteenth Circuit (Connecticut) on information charging him with selling drug sundries below the manufacturer's wholesale price, in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 19-242.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of selling drug sundries below the manufacturer's wholesale price, in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 19-242. The items at issue were cream rinses and deodorants. Defendant appealed, contending § 19-242 was an unconstitutional price-fixing statute. The court agreed and the conviction was overturned. The court found nothing to show that the items at issue here had any reasonable or necessary relation to the control of the drug trade over which the state could exercise its police powers. In addition, the public concern over "cutthroat" competition between retailers was served by Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-114. That statute required proof of intent to injure competition, which was essential to the constitutionality of unfair sales practices legislation, and which was lacking from § 19-242. Thus, on the facts of this case § 19-242 was unconstitutional price-fixing legislation unaffected with a public interest.


OUTCOME: Conviction overturned, as under facts of the case statute was unconstitutional price-fixing legislation. The sundries sold by defendant had no reasonable or necessary relation to the control of the drug trade over which state could exercise police powers, and required proof of intent to injure competition was lacking.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Information charging the defendant with the crime of operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of intoxicating liquor, brought to the Circuit Court in the first circuit and tried to the court, Williams, J.; judgment of guilty and appeal by the defendant.

Certification for appeal denied by the Supreme Court of Errors July 27, 1962.



DISPOSITION: No error.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of an order of the Circuit Court, First Circuit (Connecticut), which entered a judgment finding defendant guilty of the crime of operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of intoxicating liquor in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 14-227.


OVERVIEW: The error assigned, that the trial court's conclusion that defendant was guilty of operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of liquor, Conn. Gen. Stat. § 14-227, could not be substantiated, was too indefinite and general, and did not conform to the court's practice. Although not required to do so, the court examined the evidence and found that there was ample evidence to justify the conclusion that defendant was guilty beyond a reasonable doubt of the crime of operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of liquor. Defendant claimed that the trial court had no jurisdiction and that the creation of the circuit under the provision of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 51-251 was an unconstitutional delegation of authority by the general assembly. The court held that the circuit court rules indicated an approval, inferentially at least, of the delineation of circuit boundaries. The court concluded that the legislature had spoken with reasonable clarity in establishing the statewide jurisdiction of the circuit court, Conn. Gen. Stat. § 51-1, and had defined within proper limits the power of the chief judge to designate circuits and sessions. Conn. Gen. Stat. § 51-251.


OUTCOME: The court found no error in the circuit court's judgment convicting defendant of operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of liquor.

State v. Miller


File No. CR 7-0930


Superior Court of Connecticut


1 Conn. Cir. Ct. 147; 177 A.2d 478; 1961 Conn. Cir. LEXIS 99; 23 Conn. Supp. 121; 44 Lab. Cas. (CCH) P50,453



August 22, 1961, Memorandum Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Memorandum on demurrer to information.



DISPOSITION: Demurrer sustained.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant demurred to an information that charged him with the unlawful exhibition or employment of children in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-25.


OVERVIEW: Defendant conducted a public gymnastic exhibition by a group of 9 children who were between the ages of 4 and 13. No child performed for more than five minutes and defendant had the consent of the children's parents. Defendant, the children, and the parents neither received nor expected to receive any remuneration for the performance aside from a $ 50 contribution towards defendant's expenses by a local business. Defendant acknowledged that the performance violated Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-25, but contended that the statute violated his rights under Conn. Const. art. I, § 9 and U.S. Const. amend. XIV. The court held that: (1) § 53-25 fell within the State's police power to enact laws to protect the safety, health, and morals of children under 16 years of age; and (2) the statute's all-inclusive prohibition for all purposes was too broad and did not have a reasonable relationship to the public health, safety, morals, and welfare of children under 16 years of age.


OUTCOME: The court sustained the demurrer.

State v. Trumbull


File No. CR 7-0662


Superior Court of Connecticut


1 Conn. Cir. Ct. 130; 176 A.2d 887; 1961 Conn. Cir. LEXIS 96; 23 Conn. Supp. 41



July 19, 1961, Memorandum Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Memorandum on defendant's motions to suppress illegally obtained evidence and return it, and for disclosure by production for inspection and copying.



DISPOSITION: Motion to suppress granted; motion for return of articles seized denied; motion for disclosure denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was charged with forgery or counterfeiting stamps or labels in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-347, making untrue and misleading statements in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-365, and conspiracy in violation of Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-197. Defendant sought suppression of evidence seized from his factory without a warrant, turnover of seized property, and disclosure of his statements in the state's possession.


OVERVIEW: Police and private investigators went to defendant's factory, where they convinced another person to let them in. When defendant came to the factory, the officers and investigators convinced defendant to let the police take certain items away with them. The investigators and police also searched defendant's papers and files. The investigators and police had no search warrant, but later got an arrest warrant for defendant, charging him with forgery or counterfeiting stamps or labels, making untrue and misleading statements, and with conspiracy. Defendant sought to suppress the fruits of the search, obtain a return of the seized articles, and obtain disclosure from the state of his statements. Defendant secured partial relief. The search was illegal because the police had no warrant and defendant's "consent" was illusory. Defendant did not obtain the return of the seized items. There was no provision for such return under statute. Defendant also failed in his attempt to secure a copy of his statements. Defendant could not compel the prosecution to so produce.


OUTCOME: The court granted defendant's motion to suppress evidence, but denied defendant's motions for return of his property and to compel the state to turn over his own statements.
