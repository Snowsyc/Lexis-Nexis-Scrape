Samuels v. Oberly


Civil Action No. 83M-JN-16


Superior Court of Delaware


1984 Del. Super. LEXIS 652


November 14, 1983, Submitted  
February 7, 1984, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff licensee sought a writ to restrain defendant state from enforcing Del. Code Ann. tit. 4, § 714, which prohibited certain licensees from selling liquor on Good Friday. In the alternative, the licensee prayed for a declaration that the statute was unconstitutional in that it violated the Establishment Clause of the United States Constitution.


OVERVIEW: The licensee brought an action against the state in which the licensee sought to prevent enforcement of Del. Code Ann. tit. 4, § 714, which prohibited certain licensees from selling liquor on Good Friday. In the alternative, the licensee sought a declaration that the statute was unconstitutional because it violated the Establishment Clause of the United States Constitution. The court held that insofar as it prohibited taverns and taprooms from selling liquor on Good Friday, the statute violated the First Amendment bar against the establishment of religion. The court held that: (1) the writ of prohibition was not an applicable remedy; (2) the licensee had successfully rebutted the presumption of the constitutionality of § 714; and (3) the state had failed to persuade the court that the statute had a clearly secular purpose, had a primary effect of neither advancing nor inhibiting religion, and avoided excessive government entanglement.


OUTCOME: The court held that a state statute that prohibited certain licensees from selling liquor on Good Friday was unconstitutional.

DISPOSITION: Granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed a motion to dismiss his reindictment on a charge of criminally negligent homicide that was based upon his involvement in an automobile accident that resulted in the death of the other driver.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was previously involved in an automobile accident in which the driver of the other car was killed. After the accident, defendant was arrested on a charge of driving under the influence of alcohol. Following the administration of an intoxilyzer test, defendant was arrested for criminally negligent homicide in violation of Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, § 631. The court, after considering the prejudice claimed by defendant, granted defendant's motion to dismiss the reindictment. The court noted that the state's appeals were an attempt to unfairly manipulate the criminal process, and that such appeals resulted in at least a two and one-half year unnecessary delay in bringing defendant to trial.


OUTCOME: The court granted defendant's motion to dismiss the reindictment filed against him that charged him with criminally negligent homicide for his involvement in an automobile accident that resulted in the death of the other driver.

State v. Cooley

Superior Court of Delaware


1983 Del. Super. LEXIS 822


May 25, 1983, Submitted  
July 22, 1983, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was reindicted for criminally negligent homicide. The court considered defendant's motion to dismiss.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was twice before indicted for criminally negligent homicide. Both times, the trial court had granted defendant's motions to suppress evidence, the state had certified that the evidence was essential to its case, and the trial court had dismissed the action pursuant to Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 9902(b) so that the state could appeal the grant of defendant's motion. The state supreme court affirmed the trial court's second suppression order and dismissal. When the state reindicted him, defendant filed a motion to dismiss, arguing, inter alia, that there was insufficient evidence to support the charge and that reprosecution was barred by Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 207. The court granted defendant's motion to dismiss. The state's certifications that the suppressed evidence was essential were binding judicial admissions; therefore, the state could not reprosecute defendant for the same charge based on the same evidence it had when it made the certifications. The court concluded that Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 207(1) barred reprosecution because dismissal pursuant to Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 9902(b) was the equivalent of a determination that there was insufficient evidence.


OUTCOME: The court granted defendant's motion to dismiss his indictment for criminally negligent homicide.

Schluderberg-Kurdle Co. v. Director of Revenue


No. 82A-AP-1


Superior Court of Delaware


1983 Del. Super. LEXIS 733


April 11, 1983, Submitted  
May 27, 1983, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a foreign corporation qualified to do business in Delaware, sought review of the decision of appellee Tax Appeal Board, which assessed appellant a business license and gross receipts tax. The tax was asserted on sales of certain meat products distributed by appellant, which were ultimately sold at retail in Delaware.


OVERVIEW: Appellant sought review of a decision of the tax appeals board, which assessed appellant a business license and gross receipts tax on the sales of certain meat products distributed by appellant, which were ultimately sold at retail in Delaware. Appellant asserted that there was no basis for requiring it to pay gross receipts tax as a food processor. The court affirmed the decision of the tax appeals board, holding that the board's analysis was correct. The court noted that the inquiry of whether a foreign corporation was doing business involved three different considerations: was the foreign corporation doing sufficient business in the state to: (1) extend the state's regulatory power over it; (2) provide sufficient nexus for jurisdiction for service of process; and (3) grant the state power to tax its activities? The court found that appellant was in Delaware of the pursuit of profit through the sales of its meat products. The court concluded that a sufficient connection with the state to warrant the imposition of the business license and gross receipts tax was evidenced by the record.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed appellee's decision, which upheld the assessment of the business license and gross receipts tax against appellant's activities.

Cheswold Volunteer Fire Co. v. Lambertson Constr. Co.


No. 82C-JA-9


Superior Court of Delaware, Kent


462 A.2d 416; 1983 Del. Super. LEXIS 639


November 30, 1982, Submitted  
May 17, 1983, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Defendant Lambertson's motion to dismiss the complaint.



DISPOSITION:  Granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, a volunteer fire company that contracted for a new firehouse, filed suit against defendants, the contractor and roofer, because of serious defects that necessitated the replacement of the roof before the end of its 20-year warranty. Defendants filed a motion to dismiss based on the statute of limitations found in Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 8127. The roofing materials supplier was voluntarily dismissed because of a defect in service.


OVERVIEW: The fire company's four-count complaint alleged breach of contract, breach of the roof bond and warranty contract, breach of the implied warranty of habitability, and breach of the implied warranty for fitness for a particular purpose. The contractor defendant argued that the cause of action existed only during the first six years after the date of completion. The court held that (1) § 8127 began to run at the earliest of several designated dates, irrespective of the date of injury; (2) the discovery rule could not be used to create a cause of action for an injury discovered after the six-year period; (3) § 8127 did not violate Del. Const. art. I, § 9 or the Due Process or Equal Protection Clauses; (4) the favored statutory class was reasonably broad and encompassed persons similarly situated whose inclusion advanced a legitimate legislative goal, and there was a rational relationship between the provisions of Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 8127 and legitimate legislative objectives; and (5) the fact that the bond secured an express warranty that extended over a 20-year period did not remove the underlying contract from the statutory time limitation.


OUTCOME: The court granted the contractor's motion to dismiss in a suit involving the application of a certain statute of limitations regarding deficiencies in the construction of improvements to real property.

Superior Court of Delaware


1982 Del. Super. LEXIS 823

January 19, 1982, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff corporation brought suit challenging the constitutionality of Newark, Del., Code art. XVI, § 32-26.4 and the validity of defendant city council's (council) decision to deny a special use permit pursuant to that ordinance. Both parties filed a motion with the court for summary judgment.


OVERVIEW: The council denied the corporation's request for a special use permit, and, as a result, the Delaware Alcoholic Beverage Control Commission denied an application for a liquor license. Both parties filed motions with the court for summary judgment. The court held that the ordinance represented a valid exercise of the council's zoning power, but that the case had to be remanded to the council so that it could make the findings of fact necessary to allow for judicial review of the ordinance's application to the corporation. XVI, § 32-56.4 merely regulated the sale of alcoholic beverages, and represented a valid exercise of the council's authority to enact zoning legislation in furtherance of the public interest.


OUTCOME: The court found a local ordinance regulating the sale of alcoholic beverages a valid exercise of the council's authority but remanded the case to the council so that it could make necessary findings of fact regarding the corporation's case.
