PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the District Court of Cascade County. Eighth Judicial District. Hon. John McCarvel, Judge Presiding. 



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part; vacated in part; and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant state sought review of a judgment from the District Court of the Eighth Judicial District, Cascade County (Montana), in favor of plaintiff crime victim in a personal injury action.


OVERVIEW: The crime victim alleged that she sustained severe emotional injuries when she was attacked by a mental patient who escaped due to the state's negligence. The trial court granted summary judgment for the crime victim and held that Mont. Code Ann. § 2-9-104, which limited governmental liability for damages in tort, and Mont. On appeal, the court found that although the state had a valid interest in protecting its treasury, payment of tort judgments was simply a cost of doing business. Under the strict scrutiny test, § 2-9-104 was unconstitutional because the Montana Constitution guaranteed that all persons would have a speedy remedy for every injury. However, § 2-9-105 was constitutional. Under the rational basis test, an award of punitive damages against the state punished only the taxpayers and created a windfall to the injured party.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed in part, vacated in part, and remanded the trial court's judgment in favor of the crime victim in her personal injury action against the state.

Defendant Department of Administration denied all statutory and constitutional violations and moved for a summary judgment.


OVERVIEW: Mont. Code Ann. § 5-17-101 provided that the Capitol Building and Planning Committee (committee) was to serve as the Legislature's representative in planning the remodeling of the Capitol. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment.

Clontz v. Clontz


No. 12658


Supreme Court of Montana


166 Mont. 206; 531 P.2d 1003; 1975 Mont. LEXIS 620


November 15, 1974, Submitted  
February 14, 1975, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from District Court of Hill County. Twelfth Judicial District. Honorable R. D. McPhillips, Judge presiding. 



DISPOSITION: Trial court judgment of February 28, 1974 reversed and trial court judgment of October 16, 1973 reinstated in its entirety.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant husband and respondent wife sought review of a judgment from the District Court of Hill County, Twelfth Judicial District (Montana), which modified the alimony provisions of a divorce decree. Upon review, the court was asked to determine the constitutionality of the alimony statute, the appropriateness of alimony when a divorce had not been granted to a wife, and the sufficiency of the trial court's increase in alimony.


OVERVIEW: The trial court held that the husband and the wife had been guilty of mental cruelty and that each was entitled to a divorce, but because the husband had been the only party to petition for a divorce, the husband was granted the divorce. Thereafter, the trial court divided the marital property and awarded the wife alimony. Later, the wife petitioned for a modification of the decree, and when alimony was increased, both parties took exception to the new order. Thereafter, the trial court held that the increase had been unconstitutional and void. However, the trial court also held that because the husband had failed to appeal from the alimony award and had paid the wife, it would be against public policy to allow the husband to set aside the award. Upon review, the court reversed, holding it was unable to decide the constitutionality of the alimony statute because the husband's constitutional attack was procedurally improper under Mont. R. App. P. 38. The court also held that even though it had been inappropriate to award alimony to the wife, as the wife had not been granted a divorce, the husband's payment of alimony had estopped the husband from seeking relief from its burdens.


OUTCOME: In reversing a judgment which modified the alimony provisions of a divorce decree, the court refused to determine the constitutionality of the alimony statute and upheld the sufficiency of the trial court's increase in alimony. The court also held that even though the wife's alimony award had been inappropriate, as the wife had not been granted a divorce, the husband had been estopped from seeking relief from the award's burdens.
