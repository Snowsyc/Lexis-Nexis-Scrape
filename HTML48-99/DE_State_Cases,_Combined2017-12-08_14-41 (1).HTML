Phillips v. State


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Supreme Court of Delaware


330 A.2d 136; 1974 Del. LEXIS 247


February 13, 1974, Argued  
November 27, 1974, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant deed holders sought review of a decision for appellee state by the Sussex County Court of Chancery (Delaware) in an action by the state to establish its ownership of the holders' land.


OVERVIEW: The holders claimed that they owned the land because their title came through the heirs of William Penn, the original owner of land that is now Delaware. The holders claimed that Penn held the land as a private owner and not merely as a representative of the British government. The court affirmed the decision for the state because title to the land passed by sovereign succession from Penn's heirs to the state based upon the basic nature of Penn's title. The court reasoned that Penn and his heirs held a dual interest, both private and governmental, on behalf of the British government, in the territory that became the state of Delaware. Therefore, Penn's heirs stood in the shoes of the British government upon the colony's separation from England. When British governmental powers ended, by sovereign succession so did those of the Penn heirs and so did their title to all of their unconveyed land in the state. Thus, the state was the owner of the land. Also, the land was always considered to be state property after Delaware became a state.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the decision for the state and ruled against the holders in the state's action to establish its ownership of the holders' land.

Supreme Court of Delaware


56 Del. 571; 195 A.2d 379; 1963 Del. LEXIS 164



November 6, 1963, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant petitioned the Superior Court (Delaware) to certify the following question: whether the state judiciary could constitutionally entertain a trespass prosecution against a person, who was denied service and was requested to leave a restaurant, but refused to do so, when the denial of service was because the person was colored, without ascertaining his offensiveness to other customers, under Del. Code Ann. tit. 24, § 1501.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was denied service when he entered a hotel and requested food at the hotel's restaurant. The proprietor, relying upon § 1501, requested defendant to leave after denying defendant service because of defendant's race. Upon certification, the court answered the certified question negatively. The court first held that, with exceptions, the private actions of the owners or proprietors of a place of public accommodation in refusing service to a patron, which private actions were predicated upon racially discriminatory grounds, did not contravene the requirements of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution. If the state acted in such a manner, the judicial power would have favored racial discrimination, and such action was forbidden by the Fourteenth Amendment.


OUTCOME: The court answered the certified question in the negative, holding that the private actions of owners or proprietors of a place of public accommodation in refusing service to a patron, based upon race, did not contravene the Fourteenth Amendment; and holding that such owner or proprietor could not ask the state to assist in the enforcement of such private policy of racial discrimination, which enforcement was forbidden.

1978 Del. Ch. LEXIS 697


November 4, 1977, Date Submitted  
January 27, 1978 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In plaintiff employees' action against defendants, county and officials, requesting the back pay they lost through a period of suspensions and requesting injunctive relief, the county and officials filed a motion for summary judgment based on lack of subject matter jurisdiction in that the employees had an adequate remedy at law which they failed to exercise.


OVERVIEW: The county and officials contended that there were no material factual issues with respect to the sufficiency of the notice informing the employees of the charges against them, and with respect to the competency of the evidence presented at a personnel board hearing of those charges. The court held that even if a collective bargaining agreement grievance procedure controlled in this case, the employees had raised questions concerning the adequacy of that grievance procedure to protect their procedural due process rights. Therefore, the motion for summary judgment had to be denied. The court further held that if either party could avoid submission of their dispute to binding arbitration, the procedure did not provide an adequate remedy at law, and the court would have jurisdiction to determine the controversy. The court concluded that no right of appeal from the county personnel board to the court had been provided for, and that the employees had no adequate remedy at law. Therefore, the court had jurisdiction to determine if the employees were afforded the right to a fair hearing.


OUTCOME: The court denied the county and officials' motion for summary judgment.

Mekler v. Delmarva Power & Light Co.


C.A. 3693.


Court of Chancery of the State of Delaware


1975 Del. Ch. LEXIS 190



December 11, 1975.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, class of similarly situated residents and consumers (consumers), filed a motion for the entry of judgment on the pleadings in the action for tax refunds against defendants, state public utilities, regulatory agency, and state department of finance (state). The consumers argued that a bill, which imposed a sales tax and a gross receipts tax on the public utilities, was unconstitutional and improper legislation.


OVERVIEW: The Delaware legislature passed a bill, which amended the Delaware Code to provide for a sales tax on intrastate telephone commodities and services and a gross receipts tax on pipeline gas, electricity, and telegraphic and cable television communication commodities and services. The consumers filed an action against the state, arguing that the bill was unconstitutional and seeking tax refunds. The consumers filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings that the court treated as a motion for summary judgment. The court denied the motion and dismissed the complaint. The court held that the bill did not violate Del. Const. art. III, § 16 because it did not embrace more than one subject, and the subject of the bill was properly expressed in its title. The tax imposed by the bill did not violate due process merely because it was indirect. The bill was not special legislation requiring notice and public hearings. The bill did not violate equal protection by excluding gross receipts of sales of electricity in existence when the bill was passed. The bill did not impose an unconstitutional burden on interstate commerce. The tax measured by gross receipts was constitutional.


OUTCOME: In the action brought by the consumers against the state alleging that a sales and gross receipts tax on certain public utilities was unconstitutional, the court denied the consumers' motion for judgment on the pleadings, which it considered a motion for summary judgment, and it dismissed the consumers' complaint for tax refunds.

HEITNER v. GREYHOUND CORP.


No. 4514.


Court of Chancery of the State of Delaware, Sussex


1975 Del. Ch. LEXIS 244



May 12, 1975.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, corporation and corporate officials, filed a motion to dismiss and the corporation filed a motion to quash the sequestration order under Del. Code tit. 10, § 366 in plaintiff stockholder's derivative lawsuit.


OVERVIEW: The stockholder filed a derivative action against the corporation and the officials for activities that occurred outside of Delaware. While none of the parties resided in Delaware, the corporation was incorporated in Delaware. To obtain jurisdiction, the court issued a sequestration order (order) under § 366 in which the court held all corporate stock until the corporation and officials filed an appearance. The corporation and officials claimed that the order was invalid because it unconstitutionally provided personal jurisdiction where there was none. The court denied the corporation's and official's motions. The court held that the order did not violate due process where a hearing for release of the stock was unnecessary because the stock was to be released once the parties entered an appearance. The court had jurisdiction, though the stock was held in other states, where Del. Code tit. 8, § 169 provided that the situs of the stock ownership was Delaware if the corporation was incorporated there. The order merely provided in rem jurisdiction and, thus, did not compel personal jurisdiction. The order was not quashed because the stock was still freely negotiable.


OUTCOME: The court denied the corporations' and officials' motions to dismiss and denied the corporation's motion to quash the sequestration order in the stockholder's derivative action.

[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Court of Chancery of Delaware, New Castle


318 A.2d 135; 1974 Del. Ch. LEXIS 89



March 28, 1974 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff university and defendant priests each filed motions for summary judgment in the university's action to permanently enjoin the holding of worship services on university property as now sanctioned and/or participated in by the priests and others of the Roman Catholic faith. The university relied on Del. Code Ann. tit. 14, § 5103, and the priests based their argument on the First Amendment.


OVERVIEW: The university did not allow religious worship services of any denomination on any university property. The priests began holding Sunday morning Roman Catholic services in a university residential building. The university sought to stop the services. A temporary injunction was issued against the university, and subsequently, each party filed motions for summary judgment. The court granted summary judgment in favor of the university and revoked the temporary injunction. The court found that the university stood in the same shoes as the state. The Establishment Clause of the First Amendment required the state to exercise neutrality toward religion in any form. While the university could have established a constitutional policy that allowed all religious services on campus, the university was not required to do so. Furthermore, under §5103 the university could not favor any particular denomination. The university policies neither prevented the establishment of religion nor the free exercise of religious beliefs.


OUTCOME: The court granted the university's motion for summary judgment and denied the defendant's motion thereby revoking a temporary injunction that had been issued against the university and prohibiting the worship services that were being conducted on university property by the priests.

Kelley v. Dover


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Court of Chancery of Delaware, Kent


314 A.2d 208; 1973 Del. Ch. LEXIS 128



December 4, 1973 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, officers and members of a property owners' association, filed an application for a permanent injunction to prevent annexation of land in the county by defendant city, which would have extended the city's boundaries to include plaintiffs' property as well as property owned by defendant corporation and others.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs sought to enjoin the special annexation election concerning the expansion of the city's boundaries by annexing plaintiffs' and others' property. The election was not enjoined, but it was agreed that all procedures necessary to complete annexation would not be taken pending a decision on plaintiffs' challenges to the election. In the election, 27 people voted for annexation and 89 voted against. However, due to the weighting of the votes by assessed land value, the official result was 15,373 votes in favor of annexation and 9,608 against. The court granted plaintiffs' application for a permanent injunction and held that the annexation election was void and of no effect. The court held that the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment rendered the weighted voting unconstitutional in the context of annexation elections. Each qualified voter had the right to decide whether he wanted his home within the boundaries of the city, and to have his vote weigh equally with that of every other voter. The city failed to demonstrate that the weighted voting scheme rested upon a compelling state interest and was necessary to further that interest.


OUTCOME: The court granted plaintiffs' application for a permanent injunction against defendants' annexation of property that would expand the city's boundaries.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff citizens brought an action against defendants, who were corporation, a town, and its mayor, for a preliminary injunction to prevent the corporation's construction of a drive-in theatre in the town. The primary issue in dispute was whether the zoning dispute was governed by Kent County, Del., Zoning Ordinance for the Unincorporated Area of Kent County, Del.


OVERVIEW: The citizens brought an action to enjoin the corporation from building a drive-in theatre in the town, based on their contention that the Ordinance applied to the town under the agricultural-conservation zoning classification in § 4 of the Ordinance. OUTCOME: The court ruled against the citizens in their action against defendants for a preliminary injunction to prevent the corporation from building a drive-in theatre in the town.

Kelley v. Dover


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Court of Chancery of Delaware, Kent


300 A.2d 31; 1972 Del. Ch. LEXIS 109



December 13, 1972 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff landowners filed a motion seeking summary judgment in its suit seeking to have a special election for annexation held by the defendant city declared null and void, arguing that procedural irregularities required invalidation and that certain votes were improperly recognized and counted.


OVERVIEW: Land owners within an area sought to be annexed petitioned for an injunction and a declaration voiding the special annexation election. A preliminary injunction was issued, and the land owners thereafter petitioned for summary judgment. While the court rejected the land owners' argument that procedural irregularities required invalidation, it nevertheless granted summary judgment in favor of the land owners after concluding that the 13,912 votes cast by an individual property owner should not have been recognized and counted by the city. The votes cast by the individual clearly affected the election's outcome; had they not been counted, the ballots against annexation would have outnumbered those in favor by more than 2,000 votes. The city charter permitted only natural persons to vote, and because the property at issue was owned by a corporation, the corporation transferred title to an individual solely to qualify that individual to vote in favor of the annexation. In applying general principles of equity, the court concluded that the city should not have recognized the property transfer for election purposes, and invalidation of the election was required.


OUTCOME: The land owners' motion for summary judgment was granted, and the special election for annexation was declared invalid.
