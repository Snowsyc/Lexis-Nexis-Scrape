Const. art. 7, § 6 violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment by dividing voters into two classes, one of which wished to vote for a candidate 21 years old or older, and one comprised of voters who wished to vote for a younger candidate. The court held that as the establishment of a minimum age was necessary to insure the fitness of those who hold state office, it followed that the attending discrimination between candidates over and those under the minimum age was reasonably necessary to the accomplishment of legitimate state objectives. Plaintiff candidate also contended that art. 7, § 6 divided aspirants to office into a favored class comprising those candidates over 21 years of age and a disfavored class comprising those who were younger. The court held that the state was entitled to set a minimum age for holding office greater than the voting age without violating the equal protection clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. It also held that the Twenty-Sixth Amendment did not require that no person 18 years of age or older be precluded from holding office on account of age and did not supersede Minn. Const. art. 7, § 6.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part; reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants sought review of a judgment of the Hennepin County Municipal Court (Minnesota), which convicted them of violating Bloomington, Minn., City Code §§ 10.01 and 11.13, respectively, for their failure to pave certain off-street parking areas.


OVERVIEW: The first defendant was convicted of violating Bloomington, Minn., City Code § 10.01 for failing to pave its parking lot. The second defendant was convicted of violating § 11.13 for failing to comply with a conditional use permit requiring it to pave its parking lot. Both defendants sought review. On appeal, the court reversed the second defendant's conviction. The court found that § 11.13 contained no clear indication that the failure to comply with the conditions attached to the second defendant's use permit constituted a crime. The court affirmed the first defendant's conviction. The court found that the ordinance was a reasonable exercise of legislative discretion and rejected the first defendant's contention that § 10.01 was void for vagueness because it did not contain a specific definition of "parking lot," noting that the term had a commonly accepted meaning and had been adequately defined in prior judicial decisions. The court rejected the contention that the city was discriminatorily enforcing the ordinance, noting that it had enforced the ordinance against several other businesses in the area and that its enforcement practices were reasonable under the circumstances.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the first defendant's conviction for violating Bloomington, Minn., City Code § 10.01. The court reversed the second defendant's conviction for violating Bloomington, Minn., City Code § 11.13.
