Jackson v. State


No. 6 Div. 689


COURT OF APPEALS OF ALABAMA


37 Ala. App. 335; 68 So. 2d 850; 1953 Ala. App. LEXIS 416



October 6, 1953 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Rehearing Denied October 27, 1953.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the judgment entered by the Circuit court of Jefferson County (Alabama) of conviction of the offense of possessing a pistol having previously been convicted of committing a crime of larceny, under Ala. Code tit. 14, § 172 (1940).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of violating Ala. Code tit. 14, § 172 (1940), upon evidence that showed that defendant took a pistol from somebody with whom he had been arguing. Defendant previously was convicted of larceny. On appeal, the court affirmed. The court held that the evidence sustained the judgment of conviction. That larceny was classified as a crime of violence under §174 was not wholly devoid of any semblance of reason to support it. Thus, the classification did not amount to mere caprice.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction.

Connor v. Birmingham


No. 6 Div. 555


Court of Appeals of Alabama


36 Ala. App. 494; 60 So. 2d 474; 1952 Ala. App. LEXIS 316



June 10, 1952 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Rehearing Denied June 30, 1952.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and rendered.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the judgment of the Recorders Court of the City of Birmingham (Alabama), which convicted him of occupying jointly and privately a hotel room with a person of the opposite sex, other than his wife or parent or minor child, contrary to and in violation of Birmingham, Ala., General City Code § 686 (1944).


OVERVIEW: The trial court overruled defendant's demurrer to the complaint. The issue was the validity of ordinance. The court determined that under the terms of the ordinance, it was unlawful for any parent to visit a hotel room of his or her adult child of the opposite sex, for a brother to visit his sister, and a salesman or saleswoman could not have lawfully shown wares to a prospective buyer of the opposite sex, unless the door was left open or there were chaperones. The court explained that because the ordinance applied to any room in a hotel, even the manager or other official of a hotel would have been within the operation of the ordinance if he went into his private office with a secretary of the opposite sex for the purpose of carrying on the business of the hotel. The court noted that the broad sweep of the language of the ordinance covered many lawful, innocent, and rightful acts, which could not have been capriciously denounced. The court concluded that defendant was directly within the purview of the ordinance because he was charged, tried, convicted, and sentenced under it. The court discharged defendant from prosecution because the ordinance was unconstitutional and void.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court, which convicted defendant of occupying jointly and privately a hotel room with a person of the opposite sex, other than his wife or parent or minor child, contrary to and in violation the ordinance. The court held that the ordinance was unconstitutional and void and defendant was ordered discharged from the prosecution.

Fiorella v. Birmingham


No. 6 Div. 825


COURT OF APPEALS OF ALABAMA


35 Ala. App. 384; 48 So. 2d 761; 1950 Ala. App. LEXIS 438



May 9, 1950 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [*1] Rehearing Denied June 20, 1950.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Circuit Court (Alabama), which convicted him of violating Birmingham, Ala, Code § 600 (1944) in a de novo trial after defendant was convicted in the recorder's court. Defendant claimed that the complaint did not sufficiently allege which part or parts of § 600 were violated, that the ordinance was unconstitutional, and that the trial court should not have told the jury of his prior conviction.


OVERVIEW: The ordinance prohibited the possession of articles that were customarily used in the operation of a lottery or other game of chance. Detectives testified to seeing defendant exit a car with a package and return without it, and to finding a package where defendant had been with numbered plastic balls in it. They also found two balls from the set in a car. Defendant argued that innocent possessors could be convicted and that a defense attorney would be guilty of violating the ordinance even though judicial and enforcement officers could lawfully have possession in connection with a prosecution or investigation. Defendant claimed that the ordinance improperly placed the burden to show innocence on the accused. His motion to discharge the jury based on the prior conviction information was denied. On review, the court found that the complaint was sufficient, that the statutory exception applied to defense attorneys, and that the burden of proof provision merely provided for prima facie evidence of a violation. The court found no prejudicial error in the trial court's explanation of the basis of its jurisdiction to the jury.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment of conviction.

Birmingham v. Reed


No. 6 Div. 841


COURT OF APPEALS OF ALABAMA


35 Ala. App. 31; 44 So. 2d 607; 1949 Ala. App. LEXIS 505



October 5, 1949 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [*1] Reported at 35 Ala. App. 31 at 36. Certiorari Denied by Supreme Court in City of Birmingham v. Reed, 253 Ala. 414, 44 So.2d 614.


PRIOR HISTORY: Original Opinion of July 19, 1949, Reported at 35 Ala. App. 31.



DISPOSITION: Application overruled.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the decision of the circuit court (Alabama), which dismissed defendant's appeal of her conviction in the recorder's court of a violation of Birmingham, Ala., Ordinance 258-F.


OVERVIEW: Defendant asserted that she was deprived due process of law in that her appeal to the circuit court was not set for trial in accordance with the notice served on her. Defendant also asserted that the city ordinance under which she was convicted was invalid and unconstitutional for want of a standard. On appeal, the court overruled defendant's application for rehearing and found that, based on the record, defendant's case was properly in the circuit court. The court reasoned that defendant had a duty to follow her case in all its aspects until finally disposed of, and no duty rested on the circuit court or the prosecution to advise defendant of the setting of the case for trial. The court also found that the city ordinance did not lack a standard and that unless common sense was ignored in interpreting the ordinance, the ordinance was not vague. The court found that the ordinance clearly prohibited papers customarily used in the operation of a lottery or some other gambling operation. Therefore, possession of a tablet of paper was not prohibited as argued by defendant. The court found the requirements and standards of the ordinance clear.


OUTCOME: The court overruled defendant's application for rehearing.

Walter v. State


No. 6 Div. 718


Court of Appeals of Alabama


34 Ala. App. 268; 38 So. 2d 609; 1949 Ala. App. LEXIS 368; 24 L.R.R.M. 2607; 17 Lab. Cas. (CCH) P65,366



January 11, 1949 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Rehearing Granted February 1, 1949.


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from Circuit Court, Jefferson County; Alta L. King, Judge.

Clem S. Walter, Jr., was convicted of violating the code sections declaring every person free to join or refrain from joining any labor organization, without interference by force, coercion or intimidation, and prohibiting use of force or violence or threat thereof to prevent any person from engaging in lawful vocation, and he appeals.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded on rehearing.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, an employer, appealed the judgment from the Circuit Court, Jefferson County (Alabama), which convicted defendant of violations of §§ 8, 9 of the Bradford Act, Ala. Code tit. 26, §§ 383, 384 (1940), after defendant discharged an employee who refused to pay his labor union dues.


OVERVIEW: The war labor board requested or directed that defendant suspend an employee for four weeks and then discharge him if he did not pay his labor union dues. Defendant followed the board's instructions, and the employee was discharged. Defendant was then convicted of violating Ala. Code tit. 26, §§ 383, 384. On appeal, the court affirmed. The court noted that a closed shop was not outlawed in Alabama. The court held that §§ 383, 384 did not violate the due process or equal protection clauses of the United States Constitution or Ala. Const. § 1. Sections 383, 384 merely prevented force, threats, or other unlawful means from being used to enforce a person's decision to join a union. Union membership could be a condition of employment so long as unlawful means were not used to force an employee to join the union. The court further noted that the board had been able to act only in an advisory or recommendatory capacity and had been without power to defeat the provisions of §§ 383, 384.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction.
