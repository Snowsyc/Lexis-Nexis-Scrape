CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff car dealer brought an action for damages against defendant Commissioner of Motor Vehicles alleging that N.J. Stat. Ann. § 39:5-47 was unconstitutional. OVERVIEW: Plaintiff car dealer was the bona fide purchaser and subsequent seller of two cars that it did not know and could not have reasonably discovered were stolen. OUTCOME: The court dismissed plaintiff car dealer's complaint because the challenged statute was constitutional. Even though plaintiff was a bona fide purchaser, defendant Commissioner of Motor Vehicles was allowed to seize stolen motor vehicles from plaintiff's possession without compensation because forfeiture was reasonably necessary to accomplish the state's goal of stopping the transfer of stolen vehicles.

The court agreed with the township and dismissed the taxpayer's petition. The court found that the residency requirement for receipt of state benefits did not violate equal protection. The court also found that the object of § 54: 4-3.30 was to benefit veterans, which was a legitimate classification. The court further found that there was a rational basis for the residence requirement because the state was entitled to benefit its resident veterans as long as it benefited all resident veterans equally. The court also held that the taxpayer was not denied the right of migration provided by the Privileges and Immunities Clause. The court held that the denial of the tax exemption was not based on the taxpayer's recent move and that she was not treated differently from surviving spouses of non-resident veterans who had lived in the state longer than she had.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the taxpayer's petition.
