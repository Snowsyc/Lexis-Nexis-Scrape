PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1]  

Superior Court of Fresno County, No. 260366-0, Nickolas J. Dibiaso, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, executors and academy, challenged the judgment of the Superior Court of Fresno County (California), which denied their motion for attorney's fees pursuant to either to Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 5152, or Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 1021.5. Appellants had obtained a summary judgment in their action for a refund of real property taxes paid to respondent county.


OVERVIEW: During the probate administration of a will, respondent county assessed taxes on the real property received by appellant academy from the will. Appellant executors brought an action on behalf of appellant academy for a refund of these taxes because appellant academy was exempt from ad valorem property taxation. The trial court rendered summary judgment to appellants. Appellants then brought a motion for costs and attorney's fees pursuant to Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 5152, and Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 1021.5, which the trial court denied. On appeal, the court held that because the assessments were made prior to the enactment of Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code § 538, the assessor was under no mandate to bring a declaratory relief action in lieu of making the assessment. Thus, because the § 538 procedures were not in effect, appellants were not entitled to attorney's fees under § 5152. The court held that appellants were not entitled to attorney's fees under § 1021.5, because the action did not benefit a large class of people, and appellants failed to show that the financial burden in pursuing the litigation was disproportionate to the value of private benefits gained.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment, which denied attorney's fees to appellants, executors and academy, in their action against respondent county for refund of property taxes. Appellants could not recover attorney's fees for respondent's failure to seek declaratory relief prior to making assessment because respondent was under no duty to do so at time assessment was made.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant county challenged the judgment of the Superior Court of Fresno County (California), which denied its claim against respondents, state and Commission on State Mandates, and found that Cal. Gov't Code § 17556(d), did not violate Cal. Const. art. XIII B, § 6. Appellant had brought a claim to respondent commission seeking reimbursement for costs incurred in implementing the Hazardous Materials Release Response Plans and Inventory Act.


OVERVIEW: Appellant county brought a claim with respondent Commission on State Mandates, seeking reimbursement from respondent state for costs incurred in implementing the Hazardous Materials Release Response Plans and Inventory Act, Cal. Health & Safety Code § 25500 et seq., under Cal. Const. art. XIII B, § 6. Respondent commission denied the claim, finding reimbursement prohibited by Cal. Gov't Code § 17556(d), and finding that the program was not state-mandated. Appellant sought a writ of mandate and declaratory relief, which the trial court denied. On appeal, the court held that § 25500 et seq., constituted a state-mandated program under art. XIII B, § 6, because it imposed unique requirements on local governments. The court held that § 17556(d), which provided that costs were not mandated by respondent state if the local agency could levy service charges, fees, or assessments to pay for the mandated program, was not unconstitutional under art. XIII B, § 6. The legislature's interpretation of "costs" for which respondent state was required to reimburse a local agency as set forth in § 17556(d), was reasonable, and the court had a duty to accept this meaning.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment, which denied appellant county's claim for relief from respondent Commission on State Mandate's decision that appellant was not entitled to reimbursement from respondent state for costs incurred in implementing state hazardous materials release response program. Legislature's interpretation that "costs" only included costs that could not otherwise be recouped by agency was reasonable.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant state sought review of an order of the Superior Court of Butte County (California), which granted a preliminary injunction in favor of respondent county and held that respondent would probably prevail in its dispute with appellant regarding the state's power to require that local funds be directed to a state-mandated program, pursuant to Cal. OVERVIEW: Respondent county challenged appellant state's power to require that local funds be directed to state-mandated programs under Cal. Const. art. XVI, § 11and Cal. Const. art. XIII A. The trial court issued a preliminary injunction ordering appellant to fund the entire program. On appeal, the court reversed and held that the injunction was not supported by the constitution, home rule, or impossibility. The court held that respondent could not challenge appellant's statutory scheme for fundings on due process grounds because political subdivisions of a state could not challenge the validity of a state statute under the U.S. Const. amend. OUTCOME: The court reversed the injunction, remanded to the trial court, and held that the trial court had abused its discretion when it issued the injunction because there was not sufficient evidence that respondent county would prevail in its claim against appellant state on constitutional grounds or on the theories of home rule or impossibility of performance. The court ordered respondent to pay appellant's costs of appeal.

Defendant contended that Cal. Penal Code § 1538.5(i) was unconstitutional because it permitted the prosecution to recall witnesses at the special hearing in the superior court while denying defendants the same right.


OVERVIEW: Following denial of his motions to suppress evidence in both the municipal and superior court, defendant pleaded guilty to the transportation and possession for sale of narcotics. On appeal, he contended that Cal. Penal Code § 1538.5(i) was unconstitutional because it permitted the prosecution to recall witnesses at the special suppression hearing while forbidding defendants the same right. The court affirmed. Defendant was not improperly denied the right to present new witnesses because if they had been unavailable for the preliminary hearing, defendant could have requested a continuance to further investigate his case.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for transportation and possession for sale of narcotics, following denial of his motions to suppress evidence in both municipal and superior court. The court found that the statute permitting the prosecution to recall witnesses at the special suppression hearing was constitutional because the prosecution's right came into play only when defendant was allowed to present evidence at the special hearing.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The people appealed the decision of the Superior Court of Sacramento County (California), which ordered that evidence in a narcotics case be suppressed since the warrant was ruled to be facially deficient. The people contended that the good faith exception to the exclusionary rule applied to the search warrant, which was issued in violation of the rule dealing with out-of-county search warrants.


OVERVIEW: The people appealed the decision ordering that evidence in a narcotics case be suppressed after the warrant was ruled to be facially deficient. The affidavit in support of the warrant failed to state that the search, which would take place in another county, related to a crime in the issuing county. Despite that omission, the court disagreed with the trial court and held that the good faith exception applied. Since it was undisputed that the drug transactions had occurred in the issuing county, there could be no conceivable bad faith motive for omitting that information from the affidavit. That would amount to unwarranted speculation. The question was whether reliance on the validity of the warrant was objectively reasonable. The search warrant was regular on its face and the affidavit in support of the search warrant was not so constitutionally deficient as to render belief in probable cause entirely unreasonable. Exclusion of the evidence would not further the purposes of the exclusionary rule, so that rule was inapplicable. The trial court erred in granting the motion to suppress the evidence. The order of dismissal was reversed and the matter was remanded.


OUTCOME: The order of dismissal was reversed and the matter was remanded to the trial court with directions to set aside its order suppressing evidence and to enter a new and different order denying the motion to suppress. There was no bad faith motive for omitting information from the affidavit and reliance on the validity of the warrant was objectively reasonable. Exclusion of the evidence would not further the purposes of the exclusionary rule.
