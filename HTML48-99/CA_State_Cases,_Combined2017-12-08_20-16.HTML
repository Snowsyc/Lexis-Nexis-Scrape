PRIOR HISTORY: APPEAL from a judgment of the Superior Court of the City and County of San Francisco. Edward Molkenbuhr, Judge.

Action for damages for personal injuries arising out of a collision of automobiles.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed. Judgment for defendants notwithstanding a verdict for plaintiff, affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff and defendants' automobiles were involved in a collision. Both parties brought personal injury actions in the Superior Court, San Francisco (California). A jury returned a verdict in favor of plaintiff, but the court granted defendants' motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict on the ground that the claim was barred by Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 439. Plaintiff appealed.


OVERVIEW: The first of two actions was settled by the parties and dismissed with prejudice. On appeal, the issue was whether § 439 was applicable to bar the present case. The court held that § 439 was applicable, and that the prior judgment of dismissal was res judicata not only as to the claim asserted by defendant against plaintiff in the that action, but as a result of the compensation paid by plaintiff to defendant in return for the prior dismissal, it was also a bar to the claim plaintiff asserted against defendant and his employer in the present case. The court noted that it was the act of plaintiff himself which caused the defense of res judicata provided for § 439, to mature. Under such circumstances, he could hardly be heard to complain that he was not afforded reasonable notice and an opportunity to be heard. Finally, the court rejected plaintiff's equal protection argument, finding that there was no showing action that plaintiff had received any different treatment at the hands of the court than any other person would have received under like circumstances.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the superior court that entered a judgment for defendants notwithstanding the verdict for plaintiff.

OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of conviction of the superior court.
