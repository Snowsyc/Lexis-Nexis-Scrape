CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The House of Representatives of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts set forth in an order questions regarding the constitutionality of a bill entitled "An act requiring a period for voluntary prayer or meditation in public schools," that would have amended Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 71, § 1A.


OVERVIEW: The order presented the questions of whether the bill would have been constitutional under Mass. Const. art. II and U.S. Const. amend. XIV. Therefore, the court answered the question regarding the constitutionality of the bill under the federal constitution and determined that the bill would have been unconstitutional and it was unnecessary for the determination of whether the bill was constitutional under the state constitution.


OUTCOME: The court decided that the bill, if enacted, would have been unconstitutional under the federal constitution's establishment clause.

Attorney Gen. v. School Committee of Essex


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts


387 Mass. 326; 439 N.E.2d 770; 1982 Mass. LEXIS 1678


May 3, 1982, Argued  
August 31, 1982, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Essex.

Civil action commenced in the Superior Court Department on September 30, 1980.

The case was heard by Adams, J., and a question of law was reported by him to the Appeals Court. The Supreme Judicial Court ordered direct review on its own initiative.



DISPOSITION: So ordered.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant school committee appealed from a judgment entered by the Superior Court Department, Essex (Massachusetts), which held that Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 76, § 1, required it to provide transportation to private school students under the age of 16 years to the extent that it was provided to public school students. Plaintiff Attorney General filed a cross-appeal. The court ordered direct review on its own initiative.


OVERVIEW: The Attorney General sought to enforce § 1. The trial court held that the school committee lacked standing to challenge the constitutionality of that provision and required it to provide transportation to private school students. On appeal, the court found § 1 to be constitutional and affirmed the trial court's judgment. Section 1 required school committees to provide students attending private schools in fulfillment of compulsory attendance requirements the same transportation privileges as those afforded to public school students. The "aid" to private schools proscribed by the Massachusetts Constitution had to be more than minimal. Rather, only "substantial assistance" was proscribed. Because busing was a community safety measure that was not entirely dissimilar from fire or police protection and played no role in the education process, any benefit provided to the schools was remote and did not constitute substantial aid. However, a measure of reasonableness on the transportation of private school students was implicit in § 1. Therefore, the school committee was only required to provide transportation to schools that were the same distance as or closer than the public school.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment, which required the school committee to provide transportation to private school students. The matter was remanded for further proceedings.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the decision of the Superior Court (Massachusetts), which convicted him of possessing obscene magazines with intent to disseminate them in violation of Mass. Defendant contended that the statutory scheme was unconstitutional, that the trial court erred in the admission of evidence, and that the denial of his motion to dismiss for failure to comply with Mass. OVERVIEW: The court determined that the obscenity statutes, Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 272, §§ 28C-32, were not unconstitutionally vague in their proscription of the dissemination of obscene matter. The court also found that they did not violate the equal protection provisions of the state and federal constitutions in their different procedural treatment of books and other matter. The failure to unambiguously define "books" in the obscenity statutes did not render the statutory scheme unconstitutionally vague because defendant had notice of his potential liability for dissemination of such obscene materials. The reference to the obscene material as "magazines" at trial did not constitute prejudicial error because the appropriate classification of materials was a question to be decided by the trial court, not by the jury. The jury's duty was only to decide if the publication was obscene. The court remanded for a hearing on the proper classification of the materials and defendant's motion to dismiss for the State's failure to proceed against the books in rem under Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 272, §§ 28C-32.


OUTCOME: The court remanded to the trial court for a hearing on defendant's motions to dismiss.
