CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a concursus proceeding, defendant heirs of the owner of a two-thirds interest in land sought review of a decision from the trial court (Louisiana), declaring that defendant claimants held a one-third ownership interest in mineral royalties in the same land.


OVERVIEW: In 1886, a landowner sold a 30-acre parcel of land to two men, who held the property as co-owners. The man with the two-thirds undivided interest farmed the land for 55 years. The man with the one-third interest was an absentee owner and failed to pay taxes on his interest with the result that his interest was sold at tax sale in 1911. The tax sale buyer conveyed his one-third interest to the two-thirds interest owner in 1913. After the two-thirds interest owner died in 1941, the two men's heirs disputed their respective mineral rights in the property. On appeal, the court held that the two-thirds interest owner did not acquire the land by adverse possession because he failed to put his co-owner on notice. Although the two-thirds interest owner had filed a petition for partition in 1910, the suit was never prosecuted to judgment. Judicial cancellation of the tax sale was proper because the tax sale was based on an invalid assessment that treated the one-third interest as severable when, in fact, the two men were co-owners of undivided interests. The heirs of the one-third interest owner were entitled to a pro-rata portion of mineral royalties.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed.

April 9, 1951 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants city, water and sewage board, and board clerk sought review of a decision from the trial court (Louisiana), holding that the clerk was improperly certified as a classified civil servant under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 33:2416(A) (1940).


OVERVIEW: After the water and sewage board's clerk successfully passed the city's civil service commission's examination for a "clerk I" position, his name was placed last on a list of 71 eligible candidates. Nevertheless, the water and sewage board appointed the clerk to an open "clerk I" position. On appeal, the court held that the clerk was included among those employees required to be certified as having passed the city's civil service examination by La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 33:4076. The statute was not unconstitutional because it did not, in effect, add a new system or code of laws "by general reference thereto" as barred by La Const. art. III, § 18 (1921). Because the clerk's name was the last name appearing on the employment register, rather than being among the top three names as required by § 33:2416(A), his appointment to the city's classified civil service was illegal.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed.

Stauss v. Kober


No. 19591


Court of Appeal of Louisiana, Orleans


51 So. 2d 121; 1951 La. App. LEXIS 605



March 12, 1951 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff realtor complained of a judgment of the trial court (Louisiana), which dismissed his suit for a commission against defendant purchasers who signed an agreement to buy certain property but who, because of undisclosed title restrictions, refused to make the required deposit and refused to accept title to the property.


OVERVIEW: The realtor conceded that title restrictions were not disclosed and that such undisclosed restrictions would ordinarily justify rejection of the title, but argued that the city zoning restrictions would have restricted the purchasers' use or disposition of the property to the same extent as the title restrictions, so that they might not claim that the title restrictions adversely affected the title, and were bound by the agreement to purchase. The court held that there was a fatality about title restrictions that did not exist in zoning regulations in that the latter might be appealed or amended while the former, being in the title itself, could not. Even if Louisiana applied the general rule, that a private restriction not exceeding the limitations of public regulation would not constitute an encumbrance where the possibility of abrogation of the public regulation was remote, there would still be the condition that the possibility of abrogation must be remote and that, where there was a special board set up regularly to consider applications for abrogation, the possibility of change was not so remote as to justify the application of the general rule.


OUTCOME: Concluding that the defendants were justified in rejecting title to the property, the court affirmed the judgment appealed from.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff citizens sought review of a judgment from the trial court (Louisiana), which dismissed the citizens' suit seeking an injunction to permanently enjoin defendant city from carrying out the provisions of an ordinance that authorized the city to exchange a portion of a public playground for property owned by intervenor parish school board.

OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment that dismissed the citizens' suit for a permanent injunction to enjoin the city from exchanging property.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff insurance corporation, as subrogee of its insured, sought review of a judgment of a trial court (Louisiana), which dismissed its suit against defendant, the insurer of the owner of a motor truck, which was involved in an accident with the automobile of plaintiff's subrogor. Defendant filed exceptions of no cause or right of action based on Act No. 55 of 1930 (Louisiana), which were sustained.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that the words of the Act No. 55 of 1930 (Louisiana) limited the right of a direct action against an insurer to the injured person or his or her heirs and that, as a consequence, it followed that the statute did not extend the privilege to the assigns of an injured party. The court agreed with defendant's argument. Unquestionably, the Act was a special act in derogation of general law, and it followed that it was stricti juris. Thus, the plain provisions of the Act with respect to the granting of the right of a direct action against an insurer could not be extended by implication to include any class or group or individual that was not comprehended under the definition of an injured party or his heirs. Plaintiff argued that the happening of the accident created a contractual relationship between the injured party and the wrongdoer's liability insurer, which vested in the injured party a cause of action that should be considered as a property right, the sale of which was valid and enforceable. Affirming, the court remarked that it did not follow that the right of assignment could be exercised in violation of the plainly restricted provision of the statute at issue.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment at plaintiff's cost.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant Town of Kinder (Louisiana) sought review of a judgment of a district court (Louisiana) in favor of plaintiff, a retail dealer in alcoholic beverages, in a suit to recover money paid by her for licenses under ordinances of the Town that she alleged it had no right to levy. The Town's exception of no cause of action was overruled.


OVERVIEW: The dealer paid a certain sum of money to the Town for licenses under ordinances dealing with the selling of alcoholic beverages. She filed a suit that sought to recover the money on the ground that the Town had no right, power, or authority to levy the ordinances. An exception of no cause of action was filed to the dealer's petition. The exception was overruled, and judgment by default was entered in favor of the dealer. The judgment was confirmed and made final. On appeal, the court reversed the judgment, sustained the exception, and dismissed the suit. The court held that compliance by a taxpayer with the requirements of 1938 La. Her suit was filed far more than 30 days after she had made the payment in each case. It followed, therefore, that her suit could not be maintained. The exception of no right of action filed on behalf of the Town should have been sustained.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment and sustained the exception of no right of action.

Court of Appeal of Louisiana, First Circuit


33 So. 2d 754; 1948 La. App. LEXIS 379

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant State of Louisiana, citing 1946 La. Acts 252, 1936 La. Acts 135, and 1916 La. Acts 206, appealed a judgment of a district court (Louisiana) in favor of plaintiff ambulance owners in the ambulance owners' negligence action seeking to recover damages to an ambulance inflicted when a gate on a highway bridge was lowered immediately in front of and upon the ambulance without any warning as the ambulance approached the bridge.


OVERVIEW: The State contended on appeal that the bridge tender was not negligent and that the trial court erred in allowing interest on the judgment and casting the State with the costs of court. The court amended the judgment and affirmed the judgment as amended. The court observed that regardless of any theories as to how the accident might otherwise have occurred, it had in the record the well reasoned opinion of the trial judge who accepted the testimony of the two witnesses for the ambulance owners against that of the bridge tender, and the court found itself unable to hold that in doing so he committed manifest error. OUTCOME: The court amended the judgment of the trial court in favor of the ambulance owners in their negligence action against the State by eliminating that part of the decree which taxed the entire costs of court against the State and decreeing that the costs to be paid be restricted to those of the fees of the stenographer, and it affirmed the judgment as thus amended.
