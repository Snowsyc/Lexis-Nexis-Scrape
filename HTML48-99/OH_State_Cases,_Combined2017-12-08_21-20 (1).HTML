CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff opposing member of a school board sought review of the order from defendant Ohio Civil Rights Commission, which found that the opposing member had discriminated against a candidate for school superintendent and ordered the opposing member to cease from further participation in employment decisions.


OVERVIEW: When a school board needed to hire a new superintendent, one member proposed a candidate (who was Catholic) who had unsuccessfully applied before. Another member (opposing member) expressed opposition to hiring a Catholic. Two other board members filed a complaint with the Commission against the opposing member. The Commission gave the board specific directions how to proceed in hiring the superintendent as part of its conciliation efforts. The candidate's application was rejected. The Commission then ordered the opposing member to stop taking part in the Board's employment decisions. The member challenged the Commission's actions. The court reversed the actions of the Commission. The court held that hiring the candidate would have been a prohibited action under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 3319.01 because he did not have a superintendent's certificate. The Commission's "conciliation proposals" were not conciliatory at all as required by Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4112.05(B) but were mandates. The Commission's findings of discrimination by the opposing member were not supported by the evidence. The Commission violated the opposing member's civil liberties of freedom of speech and religion.


OUTCOME: The court set aside the Commission's finding that the complaining member committed an unlawful discriminatory practice and set aside the Commission's order prohibiting the complaining member from participation in employment decisions.

Thereafter, the company, whose land was in an agricultural district, brought an action against defendants, praying for injunctive relief. OUTCOME: The court declared the township's zoning ordinance invalid after finding the zoning ordinance had arbitrarily prohibited the company's strip-mining operation.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff baker filed an action to enjoin defendant State Director of Agriculture from interfering with the sale of the baker's rolls under Ohio Gen. Code § 1090-37, which regulated the sale of bread.


OVERVIEW: The statute prohibited the sale of loaves of bread weighing less than 16 ounces. The director stated that the rolls fell within the prohibition. Claiming that its rolls came within an exemption, the baker filed an action to enjoin the director from interfering with the sale of the rolls. The court denied the injunction. The director adopted a rule that three ounces was the maximum weight of a roll. Revising the definition of a roll was within the director's discretion. The court had to preserve the law by refusing to permit a capricious and unreasonable use of an exception to destroy the public protection provided. A broad restraint upon the powers of the executive department was not justified in light of the separation of powers. There was no showing of an abuse of discretion by the director in failing to prescribe rules and regulations. The term "rolls" was sufficiently identified in the baking industry. The state alleged that the company was a common carrier within the state and as such, was subject to the regulations of the Public Utilities Commission of Ohio (PUCO). The company argued that PUCO had no power or authority to regulate the discontinuance, reduction, impairment or abandonment of services furnished by the company and that insofar as PUCO required an application and approval prior to such abandonment and service change, it was in conflict with the Commerce Clause of the U.S. Constitution. The court determined that the changes to be made did not constitute an abandonment or termination of service but merely a change in the way the service was rendered. As such, there was no duty on the company to make application with PUCO for such a change or to seek its approval. The decision overruling the demurrer was affirmed.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the decision overruling the demurrer filed by the state of Ohio and found that the telephone company's answer constituted a proper defense to the allegations.
