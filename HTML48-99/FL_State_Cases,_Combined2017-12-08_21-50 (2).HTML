PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] An Appeal from the Circuit Court for Dade County, Herbert M. Klein, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Rehearing En Banc Granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In an action on a deed, appellant, the personal representative of the estate of the original grantor, requested a rehearing after affirmance of a decision from the Circuit Court for Dade County (Florida), that granted a money judgment in favor of appellant against appellee grantee; however, it refused to cancel appellee third party purchaser's deed.


OVERVIEW: In an action on a deed, where the sole consideration was love and affection, the trial court rendered a money judgment in favor of appellant, the personal representative of the estate of the original grantor, against appellee grantee; however, it refused to cancel appellee third party purchaser's deed. The appellate panel affirmed and the appellant sought a rehearing en banc. The en banc court held that the general principles of constructive and implied actual notice of defects in title by which one was put on inquiry were so well established, and clearly applicable in the case, that the judgment below could be affirmed only if a deed to a non-relative for love and affection alone was not invalid. It found that the primary function of the en banc rule, Fla. R. App. P. 9.331(a), was to standardize the decisions of each district and that the en banc rule was available to resolve contradictions in appellate decisions.


OUTCOME: The court granted appellant grantor representative's motion for a rehearing and cancelled the deed, finding that a deed to a non-relative for love and affection alone was invalid.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the Circuit Court for Polk County; John H. Dewell, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The state challenged an order from the Circuit Court for Polk County (Florida), which entered a judgment holding that the "bookmaking" statute, Fla. Stat. ch. 849.25 (1981), was unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: The state filed an information charging appellee with violating the "bookmaking" statute, Fla. Stat. ch. 849.25 (1981). The trial court found that the statute was unconstitutional, holding it either violated the equal protection clauses of the federal and state constitutions, or was so vague that it failed to adequately inform those affected by the statute of the conduct it prohibited. The trial court subsequently dismissed the information. The court held that the language and intent of ch. 849.25 were sufficiently clear to meet the strict test required to be applied when examining the language of a penal statute. The court also held that the bookmaking statute did not apply to a single act of gambling. Neither was it so vague that people of common intelligence must necessarily guess at the statute's meaning and differ as to its application. The order below was reversed and the cause remanded for further proceedings.


OUTCOME: Trial court's judgment holding that the state's "bookmaking" statute was unconstitutional was reversed and the cause remanded, because the court found that the language and intent of the statute were sufficiently clear to meet the strict test applied in examining the language of a penal statute. The court held that it did not violate the equal protection clauses of either the federal or state constitutions, and was not void for vagueness.
