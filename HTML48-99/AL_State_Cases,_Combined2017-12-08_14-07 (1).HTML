DISPOSITION: REMANDED WITH DIRECTIONS.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged the order of the Circuit Court of Mobile County (Alabama), which entered judgments of conviction for the intentional murder of two victims and thereafter, in accordance with the recommendation of the jury, imposed the death penalty.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of the intentional murder of two individuals and received the death penalty. Defendant appealed. The court remanded for a Batson hearing. The court held that: (1) the allowance of the imposition of the death penalty did not violate Ala. Const. art. I, § 11; (2) the bifurcated trial process was advisory; (3) the sentencing judge's discretion was sufficiently guided and channeled by the capital murder statute to eliminate the danger of arbitrariness or capriciousness in the imposition of the sentence; (4) the trial court did not err in refusing to suppress the shotgun found as a result of defendant's statement and directions; and (5) the trial court properly considered the factors in aggravation and mitigation in its imposition of the death penalty. The court determined that defendant clearly and sufficiently brought to the attention of the trial court his objection to the prosecution's striking of black veniremen from the jury. The court held that a Batson hearing was necessary to determine whether a new trial was warranted.


OUTCOME: The court remanded the case for a Batson hearing. The court ordered that should defendant not prevail on his Batson challenge, defendant was to be executed on a date certain.

542 So. 2d 292; 1987 Ala. Crim. App. LEXIS 4652



April 14, 1987 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from Mobile Circuit Court.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the judgment from Mobile Circuit Court (Alabama), which convicted him of the capital offense of murder of a law enforcement officer after three prior mistrials. He contended that the fourth trial violated his due process rights and double jeopardy.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was indicted for the capital offense of murder of a law enforcement officer. On three separate trials, the jury was unable to reach a verdict and mistrials were declared. Prior to a fourth trial, he filed a petition for a writ of mandamus, claiming that a fourth trial would violate his due process rights and double jeopardy. The court denied the petition. He was found guilty after the fourth trial, and the jury was unable to reach a verdict on the sentence and the trial court sentenced him to life without parole. On appeal, the court affirmed the conviction. The court found no ineradicable prejudice caused to defendant and he was not deprived of a fair and impartial trial. The court held that a jury's inability to agree either on a verdict or punishment was a proper reason for the declaration of a mistrial. After such a mistrial, the retrial of defendant was not barred by double jeopardy. It had also been held in Alabama that a mistrial was no trial and, therefore, that retrial did not place a defendant in double jeopardy.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court that convicted defendant of the capital offense of murder of a law enforcement officer.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from the judgment of the Jefferson Circuit Court (Alabama), which convicted him of obtaining personal property by false pretenses in violation of Ala. Code § 13-3-90 (1975).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted after he obtained roofing materials by false pretenses. Defendant argued that the one-for-one jury strike procedure used in the county when coupled with the Alabama Habitual Offender Statute, Ala. Code § 13A-5-9 (1975) operated to deny him equal protection of the law. Defendant also argued that the trial court erred in overruling defense counsel's objections to statements made by the district attorney in closing argument that the burden of proof rested on defendant. The court concluded that: (1) even if defendant had standing, he produced no evidence that the convictions were more easily obtained in the county because of the one-for-one jury strike system than in other counties of the state following the two-for-one strike procedure; (2) the trial judge properly and correctly instructed the jury on the burden of proof, the presumption of innocence, and the principles of reasonable doubt; and (3) while the prosecutor's remarks and the failure of the trial judge to sustain the objections of defense counsel ventured very near the area of reversible error, in view of the judge's comments and his instructions to the jury, there was no reversible error.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment convicting defendant for obtaining personal property by false pretenses.
