DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State sought review of the judgment of the Court of Common Pleas, Lucas County (Ohio), which granted defendant's pretrial motion in limine on the State's charges against him of three counts of aggravated vehicular homicide, in violation of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2903.06.


OVERVIEW: Three individuals were in a vehicle that came to a 12-track railroad crossing. The car slowed to about 10 miles per hour and started slowly across the tracks. They safely crossed the first 11 sets of tracks and were approaching the final set, when suddenly, a 61-car freight train appeared from behind a parked train. The train was going about 25 miles per hour. There was a faint whistle, and the engine of the train struck the vehicle. All three occupants of the car were killed. Defendant was the train's engineer, and he was indicted for three counts of aggravated vehicular homicide. Subsequently, defendant filed a motion in limine to preclude the State from making any reference to the Toledo, Ohio, Mun. Code § 32-2-1 during the impending trial. The trial court granted the motion and found that the municipal code constituted an unlawful and unconstitutional restraint upon interstate commerce. The court affirmed the judgment and held that the total effect of the municipal code as a safety measure in reducing accidents was so slight as not to outweigh the national interest in keeping interstate commerce free from interferences that seriously impeded it.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment, which granted defendant's pretrial motion in limine on the State's charges against him for three counts of aggravated vehicular homicide.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL FROM COMMON PLEAS COURT, No. CR-51251



DISPOSITION: Judgment is Affirmed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the judgment of the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio), which convicted defendant of kidnapping and aggravated murder during the commission of a felony. Defendant allegedly kidnapped an 11-year old boy for his sexual desires. Defendant was adjudged mentally ill and ordered by the trial court to be committed to a state hospital.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, defendant contended that once he went forward with evidence of his insanity, after having placed the presumption of his sanity at issue simply by pleading insanity as a defense, the State was required to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that he was sane when he committed the act. The court held that insanity was properly classified as an affirmative defense under Ohio's statutory scheme and that defendant was not denied his constitutional right to due process of the law by bearing the burden of proving his insanity by a preponderance of the evidence. The court found that defendant's convictions were not against the manifest weight of the evidence and that the trial court properly separately indicted defendant on the two counts because they were allied offenses of similar import committed with a separate animus. The court affirmed the trial court's judgment because sufficient evidence was presented upon all material elements of the two crimes and reasonable minds could have found defendant guilty beyond a reasonable doubt of both offenses.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

Peachtree Dev. Co. v. Paul


Nos. C-790820, C-790886


Court of Appeals of Ohio, First Appellate District, Hamilton County


1980 Ohio App. LEXIS 9998



November 19, 1980 


NOTICE:

PURSUANT TO RULE 2(G) OF THE OHIO SUPREME COURT RULES FOR THE REPORTING OF OPINIONS, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS MAY BE CITED SUBJECT TO CERTAIN RESTRAINTS, LIMITATIONS AND EXCEPTIONS.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff development company and defendant Board of County Commissioners of Hamilton County (Ohio) sought review of a judgment from the trial court, Hamilton County (Ohio), which declared that the Zoning Resolution for the Unincorporated Territory of Hamilton County (Ohio) and the Board's Resolution No. 575, which applied to the company's proposed development of a tract of land as a community unit plan (CUP), were both invalid.


OVERVIEW: At issue were whether: (1) the approval of a CUP under the Resolution was a legislative act subject to referendum; (2) the Resolution was invalid because it improperly delegated legislative authority; and (3) the Board's Resolution No. 575, which approved the company's CUP, was illegal because it improperly delegated legislative authority and was based on a preliminary, rather than a final, plan. The court answered all questions in the negative and reversed. The Board's approval of the CUP by adoption of the Resolution was an administrative act not subject to referendum because it was a step in the application of a zoning regulation already in existence. It did not expressly change the underlying zoning, and the court inferred no such change was intended because the Board found that the average area per dwelling of the CUP was not less than the lot area per family required by the resolution. Further, the Resolution fell within the authority of the enabling statute and properly delegated authority to subordinate boards in a constitutional manner; the zoning regulations contained standards and guidelines that were sufficiently definite to meet the constitutional test.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment declaring the zoning resolutions to be invalid, and remanded the matter for rendering of a final judgment declaring the zoning resolutions to be valid and legal in all respects.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: After defendant's arrest for the offense of domestic violence under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2912.25, his wife obtained a temporary protection order against him pursuant to Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2919.26. The Clermont County Court (Ohio) denied defendant's motion for discharge on the ground that the statutory framework underlying the charge against him was unconstitutional. Defendant was convicted and sought review.


OVERVIEW: Defendant challenged the constitutionality of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2919.25 and its companion provisions, which stated the grounds for the arrest and pre-trial release of a person charged with the offense of domestic violence. He argued that the provision for the issuance of a temporary protection order against the offending spouse as a condition of pre-trial release was a denial of the right to bail. He also argued that the failure to impose a statutory burden of proof in hearings on motions for temporary protective orders was a deprivation of due process. Finally, defendant argued that Ohio Rev. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
