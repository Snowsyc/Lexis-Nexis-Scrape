Ochs v. Hot Sulphur Springs


No. 21878


Supreme Court of Colorado


158 Colo. 456; 407 P.2d 677; 1965 Colo. LEXIS 606



November 15, 1965, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Error to the District Court of Grand County, Hon. Addison M. Gooding, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and Remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff property taxpayers appealed the judgment of the District Court of Grand County (Colorado), holding that certain frontage taxes claimed by defendant town were valid, in the taxpayers' declaratory judgment action that sought a ruling that the frontage taxes were illegal and void and beyond the power of the town to exact.


OVERVIEW: As owners of land situated within the town's corporate limits, the taxpayers objected when they were assessed property taxes on an ad valorem basis for a general levy, a school district levy, a tunnel levy, a town levy, and a frontage tax and water frontage tax, which resulted in taxes that were in excess of 23 percent of the total assessed valuation of their lands. On appeal, the taxpayers argued that if the frontage taxes were special assessments on their properties, the revenues therefrom were required to be used to improve the properties, resulting in an equivalent benefit thereto. Rejecting the trial court's conclusion that the frontage taxes were authorized by Colo. Rev. Stat. § 139-32-1(5) (193), the court held that the evidence adduced at trial clearly demonstrated that the assessments were not used in any manner that specially enhanced the value of the taxpayers' lands. Instead, the frontage taxes were nothing more than a devise or a scheme, unsupported by any permissive revenue-producing authority, to provide funds for the general community benefit. Therefore, the frontage taxes were violative of the due process of law guarantees of the federal and state constitutions.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the cause with directions to the trial court to enter judgment as the taxpayers had prayed for in their complaint.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant State sought review of the decision of the District Court, 20th Judicial District (Colorado), which dismissed the information charging defendants with possession of cannabis after finding Colo. Rev. Stat. § 48-5 (1963) unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: Defendants were accused of violating Colo. Rev. Stat. § 48-5 (1963) in an information filed in the trial court. By motion to dismiss, they challenged the constitutionality of § 48-5 in so far as it purported to prohibit the possession of cannabis. The trial court granted their motion to dismiss. No person was entitled to assail the constitutionality of a statute except as he himself was adversely affected. The legislative classification of cannabis as a narcotic bore a reasonable relation to the purpose of the narcotics law.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded the trial court's decision.

Rev. Stat. § 139-11-6 (1953), which entered a judgment on their behalf in their action challenging the legality of the city's annexation of county land, and which enjoined defendant-in-error county government to continue to collect taxes on the subject property.


OVERVIEW: When the city sought to annex certain industrial areas of the county, several county citizens successfully stopped the annexation under Colo. Rev. Stat. § 139-11-6 (1953). On review, the court held that the citizens did not have standing as "aggrieved parties" to bring the action as they did not suffer any detriment peculiar to themselves, as distinguished from the public at large. They neither owned the land in question nor occupied it. The court held that in taking the property, the city would deprive the county of tax revenue, but that alone did not give individual county residents standing under the statute. OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded with directions to vacate the mandatory injunction entered therein; to dismiss the county residents' claims two through eight; to grant the city's motion to dismiss as party plaintiffs all individual county residents as well as the county assessor and treasurer; and to proceed to trial on the merits of the county resident's remaining claim for relief.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant company petitioned for a writ of error to review the judgment of the District Court of Arapahoe County (Colorado), which awarded damages in the amount of $ 25,000 in favor of plaintiff parents in their action for the wrongful and negligent death of their minor daughter who was killed when a truck driven by the company's employee struck her as she walked across a street.


OVERVIEW: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment on liability. There was competent and credible evidence to support the finding that the company's employee was astride the centerline of the street and that this negligent act was a proximate cause of the fatality. OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment in favor of the parents and remanded the cause for a new trial on the issue of damages only.

OVERVIEW: Claimant sustained a compensable injury and received medical treatment at state insurance fund's expense.
