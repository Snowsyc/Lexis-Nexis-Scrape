Warren Kay Vantine Studio v. Portsmouth


No. 3736


SUPREME COURT OF NEW HAMPSHIRE


95 N.H. 171; 59 A.2d 475; 1948 N.H. LEXIS 207



June 1, 1948, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment for plaintiff.

All concurred.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff photographer filed an action against defendant City of Portsmouth, New Hampshire, which sought to declare an ordinance that charged license fees to nonresident photographers unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: The ordinance required nonresident photographers to pay a license fee, but did not require resident photographers to pay the same license fee or any substantially equivalent charges. The court found that even if the challenged ordinance was not designed for the purpose of discriminating against the non-resident photographer, it clearly had that effect. The cumulative burden of such an ordinance in various municipalities throughout the state, as applied to the photographer, who photographed high school graduating classes year after year, constituted a barrier to interstate commerce that was impermissible. The court stated that while interstate commerce could be required to pay its way, it had to be placed on a plane of equality with local trade and commerce. The present ordinance did not accomplish that objective and was invalid.


OUTCOME: The court entered judgment for the photographer and found the ordinance invalid.

McIntire v. Borofsky


No. 3737


SUPREME COURT OF NEW HAMPSHIRE


95 N.H. 174; 59 A.2d 471; 1948 N.H. LEXIS 208



June 1, 1948, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Case discharged.

All concurred.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Misdemeanor criminal charges were brought against defendant store for violating the Unfair Sales Act, N.H. Laws 204. The store challenged the constitutionality of the Act.


OVERVIEW: The store challenged N.H. Laws 204, § 2, which prohibited the sale of merchandise below cost with the effect of injuring competitors or destroying competition. The store argued that § 2 was unconstitutional because it was unduly oppressive and contrary to accepted economic and social standards. The court found § 2 to be valid because the Legislature required either intent or the effect of injuring or destroying competition. The court also upheld the portion of § 2 which provided that advertisements or sales below cost were prima facie evidence of a violation because no conclusive presumption of guilt was created and "prima facie evidence" had limited effect in New Hampshire. However, the court found that the latter portion of N.H. Laws 204, § 1 (IV), applicable to computing cost, was invalid due to its indefiniteness and uncertainty, but also found that the invalid provision was severable from the rest of the Act. The court further held that, because the enactment of the Act was within the power of the Legislature granted to it by the Constitution, its wisdom, effectiveness, and economic desirability was not a judicial question.


OUTCOME: The court upheld the Act, severing the portion as to the definition of "cost."
