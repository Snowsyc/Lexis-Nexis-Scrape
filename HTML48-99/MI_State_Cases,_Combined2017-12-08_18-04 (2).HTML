DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff fireworks seller appealed from the order of the Ingham Circuit Court (Michigan), which denied him declaratory relief that the items he sought to sell were not banned by Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.243a (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 28.440(1)). The trial court also held that the statute was not pre-empted by federal laws or regulations and was unconstitutionally vague.


OVERVIEW: The fireworks seller presented expert testimony that the fireworks statute was ambiguous and contradictory and that it neither specifically restricted nor specifically allowed certain items seized by the police. On appeal, the court agreed that the statute was poorly written but held that Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.2 (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 28.192) modified the rules of statutory construction for statutes found in the Michigan Penal Code, so that the rule of strict construction did not apply. The court examined the legislative history and determined that the legislature intended to forbid all fireworks that were not specifically listed in an exception. The court, therefore, concluded that the seller's items, which were not listed, were banned. The court then found that federal regulations not only dealt with the packaging and classification of fireworks, as opposed to the sale or use, but also mentioned the applicability of state laws, so that it was clear that state laws were not preempted. The court then examined the statute for vagueness and concluded that it provided fair notice of prohibited conduct and did not provide for arbitrary and discriminatory enforcement.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order of the trial court that denied the seller's requested declaratory relief that the state fireworks statute either did not apply to his items or that the statute was either preempted or unconstitutionally vague.
