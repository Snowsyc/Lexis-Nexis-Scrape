The court noted as an initial matter that it declined constitutional review of a statute absent necessity or extraordinary circumstance. Here the matter was deemed subject to reversal on the complaint itself, alleviating the necessity of constitutional review. The complaint must have been sufficient to inform either defendant or the court as to what acts involving defendant's conduct or language occurred and amounted to the ultimate fact, here disorderly conduct. The complaint must have been worded with sufficient specificity to enable the court to make a determination as to whether the speech was patently offensive or was subject to some constitutional protection. Here the complaint wholly failed to provide any information that would have met the specificity requirements. Accordingly, the appeal was sustained, and the complaint was dismissed.


OUTCOME: The appeal was sustained, and the complaint was dismissed.

The court held that, in the light of the meaning given by Maine courts to the word "threat" and the construction given by the courts to § 3701, the argument that the statute was vague or overbroad failed. Defendant, while temporarily in a detention cell at a police station under arrest for intoxication, had stated that when he got out of jail, he was going to go home, get his gun, and kill some police officers. The court held that the jury could have properly inferred that this threat was directed at the police officers in attendance in the adjoining room and particularly at the arresting officer, that the statement was not made in jest but in deadly earnest, and that it contained a promise of evil to be taken seriously by the ordinary hearer. The court affirmed the conviction because it found that the jury could and did find a true threat, not constituting constitutionally protected speech but properly prohibited by statute.


OUTCOME: The court denied defendant's appeal and affirmed the conviction.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the judgment of the trial court (Maine), which convicted him of being an accessory before the fact to an armed robbery.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted by a jury of being an accessory before the fact to an armed robbery. On appeal, the court affirmed the judgment. It was not error to appoint as counsel for the prosecution's witness the same attorney who was representing defendant in another court on a separate and distinct criminal charge. The sentence imposed by the trial court did not constitute cruel, unusual, or unreasonable punishment, because it was within the statutory limits. The trial court erred in requiring the jury to respond to interrogatories in the event of a "not guilty" verdict, but the error was not of constitutional dimension, because the court was satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the error was harmless. Evidence of defendant's active participation in the commission of a felony did not preclude his conviction as an accessory before the fact thereto.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of conviction.
