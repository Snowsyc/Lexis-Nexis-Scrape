June 10, 1999, Oral Argument Date  
December 16, 1999, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] As Corrected January 4, 2000. As Corrected February 24, 2000.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant petitioned for direct review of a Clark County Superior Court (Washington) decision that permanently enjoined it, and persons acting in concert with it, from entering respondent's retail premises for purposes of soliciting initiative signatures, in respondent's action for declaratory and injunctive relief.


OVERVIEW: Appellant, an initiative signature gathering company, challenged an injunction against it, prohibiting the gathering of signatures on the property of respondent, a discount retailer. The court affirmed, balancing the competing interests of private property against the right to initiative petitioning under Wash. Const. art. II, § 1(a) (amend. 72), considering factors such as the nature and use of the property, the scope of the invitation extended to the public by the property owner, and the impact on the initiative process. Although the court recognized the narrow exception to a property owner's sovereignty in favor of initiative petition activities in shopping centers bearing the earmarks of a town square or public forum, the exception did not apply because respondent's stores bore none of the characteristics of a town center and respondent had never expressly invited the public into its store for any noncommercial purpose.


OUTCOME: Decision was affirmed. The narrow exception to a property owner's sovereignty in favor of initiative petition activities in shopping centers bearing the earmarks of a public forum did not apply because respondent's stores were not characteristic of a town center and respondent discouraged public activities in its store.

SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


139 Wn.2d 604; 989 P.2d 542; 1999 Wash. LEXIS 874


May 18, 1999, Oral Argument Date  
December 16, 1999, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants challenged an order of the Superior Court of Snohomish County (Washington) granting summary judgment to appellee in a property tax matter.


OVERVIEW: Appellants, rental property businesses, filed a class action against the City of Mukilteo, Washington (respondent) challenging the constitutionality of Mukilteo, Wash., Mun. Code § 5.04.070(A)(3). This ordinance imposed a residential dwelling unit (RDU) fee per dwelling unit in addition to the basic business licensing fee, regardless of whether the unit was currently being rented. The court held that the RDU fee was subject to the taxing provisions of the Washington Constitution. The incident of this tax was on rental property as such and a tax on rental property was no less a tax on property. The RDU tax violated Wash. Const. art. VII, § 1, amend. 81's prohibition of non-uniform taxation of real property; violated the ad valorem requirement of Wash. Const. art. VII, § 2, amend. 79; and, because it was not authorized by statute, it was in excess of respondent's taxing authority. Wash. Const. art. VII, § 5.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the grant of summary judgment to respondent because the challenged residential dwelling unit tax violated the state constitutional prohibition of non-uniform taxation of real property, the ad valorem requirement, and was in excess of respondent's constitutional taxing authority because the tax was not authorized by statute.

OVERVIEW: Respondent convict was ordered to pay restitution for multiple convictions for arson. Before the period of restitution expired, the statute authorizing the restitution was amended to permit extension of the initial period of restitution orders, and appellant state applied to extend respondent's restitution period. Holding that the amendments did not violate U.S. Const. art. I, sec. 10, or Wash. Const. art. I, sec. 23, due process, or double jeopardy, the court reversed the judgment for respondent and remanded. OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment dismissing appellant state's application, and remanded for further proceedings, because the extension of respondent convict's restitution did not inflict any additional punishment on respondent for ex post facto or bill of attainder purposes, did not affect a vested right without due process, and was not a direct consequence that would render his guilty plea not knowing and voluntary.
