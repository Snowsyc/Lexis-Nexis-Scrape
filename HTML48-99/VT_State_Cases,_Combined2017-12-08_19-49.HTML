Judgment is entered that the plaintiffs, and others similarly situated, are not entitled to hearing before the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles before becoming subject to the provisions of the financial responsibility law contained in 23 V.S.A. § 801(a)(4) as amended.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant Commissioner, Vermont Department of Motor Vehicles appealed from the finding of the Orleans Superior Court (Vermont), that under Vt. Stat. Ann. tit. 23, § 801(a)(4), a conviction based upon a nolo plea did not provide an indication of fault, and that, if an individual so requested, the convicted individual had to have been afforded a hearing on the question of fault prior to suspension.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff drivers had pled nolo contendere in cases involving the motor vehicles laws. They sought to secure a judicial interpretation of § 801(a)(4) that because their convictions were based on nolo contendere pleas they should have been given a hearing before the Commissioner on the issue of fault before having been subjected to the provisions of the financial responsibility law. The trial court agreed with the drivers. The court struck the judgment and held that the drivers were not entitled to a hearing. What was involved was the fact of conviction, not the plea upon which the conviction was secured. The financial responsibility requirements were not predicated upon an admission of guilt by a plea of nolo or of guilty, but upon conviction on any plea of an accident-related offense, required to have met constitutional mandates by involving an element causative of the accident. It was neither the plea taken as an admission, nor the conviction sought to have been used as proof of underlying facts, which triggered the operation of the financial responsibility law. It was, rather, the fact of the fault-related conviction itself, which sent the statutory requirements into effect.


OUTCOME: The court struck the trial court's judgment and ruled that the drivers and other similarly situated individuals were not entitled to hearing before the Commissioner before becoming subject to the provisions of the financial responsibility law.

The owner asserted there was insufficient evidence of the person's apparent intoxication at the time he was served.


OVERVIEW: The Liquor Control Board passed a regulation which provided that no alcoholic liquor could be sold or furnished to a person "apparently under the influence of liquor." Section 6214 gave a person injured by an intoxicated person a right of action against the entity who sold or furnished the person the intoxicating liquor. A third party had been drinking heavily when he went to the owner's restaurant, where he drank one or two more beers. While there, he was staggering. When he left, he went to a filling station with his sister, who was holding him up. He was staggering and his speech was incomprehensible. He got into his car, drove on the wrong side of the street, and collided with plaintiff. The court held that there was ample evidence to support the jury's finding that the third party had been "apparently under the influence" of intoxicating liquor when the owner served him beer. The evidence of his intoxication at the filling station after leaving the bar was circumstantial evidence from which it was assumed that his condition was continuous. The court upheld the regulation, holding the Liquor Control Board was constitutionally delegated the duty to regulate sales of alcohol.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment entered in favor of plaintiff.

DISPOSITION: Order granting defendant's motion to dismiss and judgment for defendant reversed and cause remanded. 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff homeowner challenged an order from the Rutland County Court (Vermont) that granted a motion filed by defendant builder, which sought to dismiss the homeowner's tort action for damage done to his house.


OVERVIEW: The homeowner, a resident of Vermont, owned and occupied a house therein. The builder, a foreign corporation, re-roofed the house and put metal edgings on the sides thereof. While so doing it negligently placed holes in the roof and sides of the buildings that caused it to leak water. The builder's motion to dismiss the homeowner's action was granted on the ground that the homeowner's complaint failed to recite the requisite jurisdictional facts. The builder sought to support the ruling on the ground that the complaint did not allege the existence of a duty on the part of the builder to the homeowner. The court disagreed and held that although the builder gratuitously made repairs, it was still liable to the homeowner. The court held that even a volunteer was liable for an injury negligently inflicted on the property of another. The law imposed an obligation upon everyone who attempted to do anything for another, even gratuitously, to exercise some degree of care and skill in the performance of what he had undertaken, for nonperformance of which duty an action lied.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the order of trial court and remanded the action.
