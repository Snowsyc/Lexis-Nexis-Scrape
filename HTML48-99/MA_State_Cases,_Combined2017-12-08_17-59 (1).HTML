327 Mass. 731; 101 N.E.2d 132; 1951 Mass. LEXIS 677; 26 A.L.R.2d 918


June 26, 1951.  
September 26, 1951.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] PETITION, filed in the Probate Court for the county of Suffolk on April 17, 1950.

The case was heard by Dillon, J.

In this court the case was submitted on briefs.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner specific legatees sought to revoke a decision in favor of respondents, executor and residuary legatee, by the Probate Court for the County of Suffolk (Massachusetts) in an action to require the residuary legatees to reimburse part of the federal estate taxes paid by the specific legatees.


OVERVIEW: At the time that the testator died, Mass Gen. Laws ch. 65A, § 5 required that the estate tax was to be apportioned and paid among interested parties. The trial court ordered that only the specific legatees pay the tax. The trustee paid the taxes before he distributed the funds to the specific legatees but after he distributed the funds to the residuary legatees. 1948 Mass. Acts ch. 605, § 1 changed the law so that the taxes were not to be apportioned. As a result, the specific legatees claimed that the residuary legatees owed them money for the taxes paid from their distributions. The court held that the specific legatees were not entitled to contributions form the residuary legatees. The court reasoned that the application of § 1 after the law changed would have violated Mass. Const. Decl. Rights art. 30 and the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution. Mass Gen. Laws ch. 65A, § 5 was properly applied because it required the apportionment and payment of the federal estate tax by the specific legatees. Because the specific legatees did not appeal the original apportionment, they were not entitled to a new trial because the law changed.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment and dismissed the petition.

Sears v. Treasurer & Receiver General


Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts, Suffolk


327 Mass. 310; 98 N.E.2d 621; 1951 Mass. LEXIS 602


April 2, 3, 1951.  
May 3, 1951.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] PETITION for a writ of mandamus, filed in the Supreme Judicial Court for the county of Suffolk on January 3, 1951. Also a

PETITION in equity, filed in the same court on February 1, 1951.

The cases were reserved and reported, without decision, by Counihan, J., and Ronan, J., respectively.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner citizens filed for a writ of mandamus to command respondents, the state treasurer and commissioner of public welfare, to refrain from paying out money under a newly passed initiative law. Petitioner taxpayers, in a companion case, brought an action in equity, praying that the treasurer and commissioner of public welfare and other respondent state officials be enjoined from expending money in connection with the new law.


OVERVIEW: The citizens and taxpayers claimed that the new law, which dealt with assistance for the elderly, was not adopted within the requirements for initiative laws laid down in arts. 48 and 74 of the amendments to the Massachusetts constitution. The court noted that initiative legislation, which could be enacted by direct popular vote, had to strictly comply with the provisions and conditions of the constitution and its amendments. The court held that the legislation at issue did not comply with Mass. Const. art. 74, § 1, which required that a fair and concise summary of the proposed measure appear on the initiative petition. The court noted that the proposed measure was eight pages long and called for expenditures of large sums of money. The summary failed to state how the money was to be obtained or which agency, state, or municipality was to administer the funds. There was also no mention as to the standards used to determine need, or the type of need covered by the assistance. Because the summary did not comply with the constitution, the court held that the proposed law could not take effect.


OUTCOME: The court issued the writ of mandamus ordering that the assistance to the elderly continue under the prior legislation, and enjoined the expense of funds of the new legislation.

Commonwealth v. Publicover


Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts, Middlesex


327 Mass. 303; 98 N.E.2d 633; 1951 Mass. LEXIS 599


April 2, 1951.  
May 1, 1951.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] INDICTMENT, found and returned on November 8, 1949.

The case was tried in the Superior Court before Dowd, J.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a decision of the Superior Court (Massachusetts), which convicted defendant of eavesdropping and sentenced him to payment of a fine.


OVERVIEW: Defendant excepted to the superior court's denial of his request for instructions and to the denial of his motion for a directed verdict, raising the question of the constitutionality of Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 272, § 99, which prohibited unauthorized eavesdropping. Defendant contended that ch. 272, § 99 violated the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments. The court disregarded the Fifth Amendment argument, holding that the Fifth Amendment protected only against conduct of the federal government. As to defendant's argument that ch. 272, § 99 denied him due process and equal protection in contravention of the Fourteenth Amendment, the court disagreed, finding that the Legislature had merely exempted persons having written permission to eavesdrop from the Attorney General or a district attorney from the scope of the act. It did not unconstitutionally delegate any power to those officers to determine who was to receive such written permission. Thus, because apart from the reference to the Attorney General and to the district attorneys, defendant did not argue that there was any constitutional defect, the court overruled defendant's exceptions.


OUTCOME: The court overruled defendant's exceptions to his conviction for eavesdropping.

Treasurer of Worcester v. Department of Labor & Industries


Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts, Worcester


327 Mass. 237; 98 N.E.2d 270; 1951 Mass. LEXIS 583; 19 Lab. Cas. (CCH) P66,293


January 30, 1951.  
March 29, 1951.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] BILL IN EQUITY, filed in the Superior Court on June 30, 1949.

The case was heard by O'Brien, J.

In this court the case was submitted on briefs.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff Treasurer of the City of Worcester challenged a decision of the Superior Court (Massachusetts), which determined that defendant Commissioner of the Department of Labor and Industries had authority to issue an order regarding employee payroll checks, that the order was valid, and that the treasurer and city were bound to comply with the order. The trial court further dismissed the treasurer's complaint for lack of jurisdiction.


OVERVIEW: The order required a hospital to maintain enough cash on the premises to allow employees to cash payroll checks when received. The trial court dismissed the complaint based on a finding that the treasurer's exclusive remedy was under Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149, § 9. On appeal, the court reversed the order dismissing the complaint, but dismissed the appeal. First, chapter 149, § 9 contained a sentence expressly reserving other lawful remedies. As such, it was not exclusive and did not have to first be exhausted, and the treasurer was free to bring his declaratory judgment action under Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149, § 148. Accordingly, the trial court erred in dismissing the complaint for lack of jurisdiction. However, the determination that the treasurer was bound to comply with the order was correct. Chapter 149, § 148 did not authorize the Commissioner to retroactively determine that payment facilities were unreasonable; rather, it authorized the Commissioner to act prospectively by enacting the order applicable to the hospital. Thus, the order was a reasonable exercise of that authority, and it was not arbitrary because it prescribed a reasonable standard of providing services.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's order for a decree dismissing the complaint for lack of jurisdiction. In its place, the court ordered a decree declaring that the order was not invalid. The court further dismissed the treasurer's appeal.
