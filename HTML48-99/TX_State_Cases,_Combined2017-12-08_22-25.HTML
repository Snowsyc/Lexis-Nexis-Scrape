CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged his conviction by a jury for theft of property of the value of $ 200.00 or more but less than $ 10,000.00 from a trial court (Texas). Appellant raised three points of error and in one asserted that the first statement he made, which was to a police intern, was illegally obtained and improperly admitted into evidence.


OVERVIEW: Appellant was convicted of theft of property of the value of $ 200 or more but less than $ 10,000 by a jury. OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment and remanded the cause to the trial court and held that the trial court erred in allowing appellant's statements before the jury and indicated that the state could not seriously contend that appellant's statement made to the police intern, while he was led to believe a gun was being held to his head, was freely and voluntarily made.

PRIOR HISTORY: FROM THE DISTRICT COURT OF TRAVIS COUNTY, 126TH JUDICIAL DISTRICT


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged the judgment from the 126th Judicial District Court of Travis County (Texas) convicting him of murdering his mother. Appellant alleged, on statutory and constitutional grounds, that the trial court erred in several of its rulings related to his defense of insanity pursuant to Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. arts. 46.02, 46.03, particularly the admission of expert psychological testimony from four witnesses of appellee state.


OVERVIEW: After being convicted of murdering his mother, appellant sought review on grounds related to his defense of insanity under Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. arts. 46.02, 46.03. The court affirmed the conviction and held that the admission of testimony of psychological expert witnesses called by the state to testify in rebuttal of similar testimony was not error. The court concluded that the testimony did not violate appellant's U.S. Const. amend. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment convicting appellant of murder and held that appellant, by pleading and attempting to prove his insanity through expert testimony of psychological witnesses, waived his Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination. Moreover, the Sixth Amendment did not require the presence of counsel at any of the psychological examinations.
