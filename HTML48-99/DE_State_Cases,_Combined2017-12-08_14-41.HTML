DISPOSITION: The answer to the first certified question is "yes." The answer to the second certified question is "no." This matter is remanded for further proceedings in accordance with this opinion. The mandate shall issue immediately.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff judge and defendant state pension board of trustees stipulated, in Superior Court of the State of Delaware, in and for Sussex County, to certification of questions to state's highest court in plaintiff's action challenging the constitutionality of a 1998 amendment to Del. Code Ann. tit. 29, § 5605, regarding judicial pension plans.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff judge contended that an amendment to the State Judicial Pension Act was invalid because it violated Del. Const. art. XV, § 4 by diminishing an "emolument" of his judicial office. By amending Del. Code Ann. tit. 29, § 5605(b), in 1998 to include a date certain when members of a closed judicial pension plan were required to exercise their option to participate in a revised judicial pension plan, the legislature diminished an emolument of judicial office unequivocally granted by a 1983 amendment: the right to participate in the revised plan at any time prior to the conclusion of active judicial tenure. The amendment violated Del. Const. art. XV, § 4 by diminishing plaintiff's vested right to make that election. Plaintiff's execution of a pension election form pursuant to the unconstitutional amendment did not constitute waiver of plaintiff's vested right concerning the election.


OUTCOME: Court held that amendment diminished plaintiff's vested right to elect, at any time, to participate in one of two judicial pension plans, violating constitutional provision prohibiting diminishment of emolument of judicial office, and that plaintiff's execution of election form pursuant to unconstitutional amendment did not constitute waiver of vested right.

Appellant asserted that Delaware's tender years statute, Del. OVERVIEW: The court affirmed appellant's convictions for unlawful sexual contact and attempted unlawful sexual intercourse with his niece. The trial court, pursuant to Delaware's tender years statute, Del Code Ann. tit. 11, § 3513, admitted out-of-court statements made by the four-year old niece to her mother, a pediatrician, and the police. The court found that the tender years statute was not unconstitutional when it required a judicial determination of particularized guarantees of trustworthiness. The court found that the statute was constitutionally applied in appellant's case and that the state's notice of the intent to use the statute, given the day of trial, did not prejudice appellant.

720 A.2d 1139; 1998 Del. LEXIS 472


September 14, 1998, Submitted  
December 1, 1998, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Released for Publication December 17, 1998.


PRIOR HISTORY: Court Below: Superior Court of the State of Delaware in and for New Castle County.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: State appealed the decision of the Superior Court of the State of Delaware in and for New Castle County, which affirmed the judgment of the trial court that Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4177 was void as it was unconstitutionally overbroad and vague. The cases involved driving while intoxicated charges against two criminal defendants, who had been in traffic accidents and alleged that after, rather than before, the accidents they consumed alcohol.


OVERVIEW: The state appealed a ruling by the appellate court, which held Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4177 void for overbreadth and vagueness. The ruling involved two separate cases in which defendants were involved in accidents, alleged that they consumed alcohol after the accidents, and were arrested after they tested above .10 for blood alcohol concentration (BAC). The court held that as § 4177 did not require the state to prove, as an element of the offense of driving while intoxicated, that the defendants had a BAC of .10 or greater when they drove, as compared to after they drove, it was unconstitutionally overbroad and void for vagueness. The court held the legislature intended § 4177 to prohibit intoxication only while driving and, therefore, the state was required to prove intoxication while driving. The court held § 4177's provision of an affirmative defense, which allowed a defendant to prove that his BAC was .10 or over because he consumed alcohol after he drove, impermissibly shifted to a criminal defendant the burden of disproving an element of proof and, therefore, violated the due process clause.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the appellate court that the driving while intoxicated statute was void for vagueness and overbreadth as the legislature intended to proscribe intoxication while, not after, driving. The court held an affirmative defense violated a defendant's due process rights as it shifted from the state the burden to prove that the defendant was intoxicated when he drove.
