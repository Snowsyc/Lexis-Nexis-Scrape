OVERVIEW: The taxpayer argued that the property was erroneously removed from the agricultural to the industrial category for tax assessment and should have been returned to valuation as farmland despite its industrial rezoning. He also claimed that Md. Ann. Code art. 81, § 19(b)(2)(A)(1), was unconstitutional for violating equal protection under the Fourteenth Amendment and, being retroactive, was an ex post facto law. The supervisor and the county took issue with these contentions as to the manner, motive, and for who's benefit the subject property was rezoned. They also claimed that the issue as to preferential agricultural use assessment was res judicata based on the rezoning documents and prior judicial action. The hearing examiner found that a principal-agent relationship existed between the taxpayer and the realtor who handled the rezoning and that there was no question that the issue was res judicata. He found the taxpayer's constitutional argument frivolous and pointed out that the ex post facto limitation in the U.S. Constitution applied only to criminal matters. He recommended affirming the assessment.


OUTCOME: The hearing examiner recommended to the court that the assessment appealed be affirmed.

HUTTON v. REGISTER OF WILLS


Miscellaneous No. 170


MARYLAND TAX COURT


1979 Md. Tax LEXIS 3



December 13, 1979 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner representative appealed a decision of respondent Register of Wills for Prince George's County, Maryland, who taxed estate property for inheritance purposes on the basis of its fair market value instead of using the farm use appraisal method under which the property had previously been taxed.


OVERVIEW: The decedent's estate included several parcels of land in Maryland which, prior to her death, had been evaluated for property tax purposes on the farm use appraisal method under Md. Ann. Code art. 81, § 19(b)(1) and had been taxed accordingly. However, when it was time to determine inheritance taxes, the Register taxed the estate property on the basis of its fair market value. The deceased's foreign representative appealed, arguing that the farm use method should have been used. The Register argued that § 19(b)(1) was applicable only to ordinary taxation and that Md. Ann. Code art. 81, § 6 defined the inheritance tax as a special tax. The court sustained the Register's decision, holding that § 6 made a distinction between special and ordinary taxes, and § 6(4) provided that inheritance taxes were special taxes. Md. Ann. Code art. 81, § 19(b)(1) applied to ordinary or direct taxes, such as those directly imposed with respect to real and personal property, and it did not apply to inheritance taxes.


OUTCOME: The court sustained the decision of the Register.

TRUSTEES OF THE FIRST BAPTIST CHURCH OF SILVER SPRING v. SUPERVISOR OF ASSESSMENTS OF MONTGOMERY COUNTY


No. 1094 A & B


MARYLAND TAX COURT


1979 Md. Tax LEXIS 19



May 29, 1979 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner church trustees sought review of a decision from the Property Tax Assessment Appeal Board of Montgomery County (Maryland) assessing property taxes on two single family dwellings located on lots contiguous to property used by the church.


OVERVIEW: The trustees contended that each of the two homes in question were used as parsonages and should have been exempt from property taxation pursuant to Md. Ann. Code art. 81, § 9(c). Respondent Supervisor of Assessments of Montgomery County (Maryland) argued that the statute was intended to allow only one exemption for a parsonage per church. The facts indicated that the church employed three "ministers." The court found that the legislature only intended that an exemption for a parsonage be allowed to the head of the congregation. Since that minister owned his own property, no parsonage exemption could have been allowed to the church. However, the home used by the minister in charge of the educational functions of the church was entitled to an exemption because it was property owned by the church and was actually used exclusively for educational purposes. The evidence indicated that that minister was in charge of all educational functions and the court believed that providing him with a home was appropriate and convenient to carry out the purpose of the church. The home of the minister of music was not subject to an exemption.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the decision from the Board as to the home occupied by the music minister and reversed the decision of the Board which denied an exemption from property tax as to the home occupied by the education minister.
