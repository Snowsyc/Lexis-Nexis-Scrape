National Linen Service Corp., Trading, etc. v. Norfolk


Record No. 4254


Supreme Court of Virginia


196 Va. 277; 83 S.E.2d 401; 1954 Va. LEXIS 221



September 8, 1954 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from a decree of the Circuit Court of the city of Norfolk. Hon. Clyde H. Jacob, judge presiding.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded. 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant cloth towel supplier sought review of an order by the Circuit Court of the City of Norfolk (Virginia) dismissing the supplier's action against appellee, a city and one of its officials, for a declaration that Norfolk, Va., Code art. 1, § 19-28 (1950) was invalid and for an order enjoining the official from prohibiting the use of sanitary cloth towels in restaurants in the city.


OVERVIEW: The supplier challenged the validity of § 19-28, which prohibited the use of cloth towels in restaurants within the city, and sought to enjoin the city official from enforcing it. The court reversed the circuit court's dismissal of the supplier's action, remanded the matter for a new trial, and found that § 19-28 was invalid insofar as it prohibited restaurants from using sanitary cloth towels solely because they were not made of paper exclusively. As applied, § 19-28 had no real or substantial relation to the public health, was an unreasonable and arbitrary enactment, provided no reasonable basis for distinction between the two types of towel material, and arbitrarily discriminated in favor of those selling paper towels by giving them an unfair monopoly against those furnishing sanitary cloth towels. A new trial was required because the circuit court failed to determine whether the types of cloth towels that the supplier provided were "common" towels, which were prohibited and which the court defined as any towel, including a "roller towel," intended or reasonably available for common use by more than one person without being laundered after such use.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded for a new trial the circuit court's dismissal of the supplier's action to invalidate the city's ordinance prohibiting the use of cloth towels in restaurants and to enjoin the city official from enforcing that ordinance.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged a judgment of the Circuit Court of Arlington County (Virginia), which overruled his demurrer on appeal from a county court warrant charging him with violating a county ordinance by keeping a vicious dog. The circuit court held that the ordinance was within the county's police power and that its first two sentences were not inconsistent with state law, but that the last sentence was inconsistent and invalid.


OVERVIEW: Defendant admitted the warrant's allegations and was convicted of violating the ordinance. The court affirmed the judgment overruling his demurrer to the charge. The court found the ordinance to be a valid exercise of the county's police powers. The state's dog laws, Va. The first sentence prohibited the act of keeping a vicious dog, the second sentence fixed the punishment, and the additional penalty fixed by the third sentence did not affect the operation and effect of the remainder.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the circuit court judgment overruling defendant's demurrer to the warrant charging him with violating a county ordinance by keeping a vicious dog.

CIRCUIT COURT OF THE CITY OF WINCHESTER, VIRGINIA


40 Va. Cir. 244; 1996 Va. Cir. LEXIS 357



August 21, 1996 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff former employee brought a wrongful discharge action against defendant employer in which he alleged claims of retaliation. The employee filed a motion for judgment. The employer demurred to the motion for judgment on the ground that the employee's exclusive remedy was provided by statute, Va. Code Ann. § 40.1-51.2:2, and also on statute of limitations grounds.


OVERVIEW: The employee worked as a safety manager at one of the employer's facilities. The employee brought an action claiming that he was discharged on June 7, 1994, in retaliation for his complaining to his superiors that the employer was violating the Virginia Department of Labor and Industry safety regulations and that therefore, his discharge violated the public policy of Virginia. The employee filed a motion for judgment, to which the employer filed a demurrer. The court dismissed the motion for judgment and sustained the employer's demurrer. The court determined that Va. Code Ann. § 40.1-51.2:2 was applicable and that it provided a remedy for the employee because he believed that he had been discriminated against because he filed a safety or health complaint. The court found that because § 40.1-51.2:2 created a right and provided a remedy for the vindication of that right, then that remedy was exclusive. The court held that the one-year statute of limitations governed the employee's action and that because the time had run, the action was time barred.


OUTCOME: The employer's demurrer to the employee's wrongful discharge complaint was sustained because the statute of limitations expired. The employee's motion for judgment was dismissed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff accident victim filed a motion to set aside the jury verdict and for a new trial, arguing that the jury failed to follow the jury instructions in awarding damages in the accident victim's action against defendant driver for injuries sustained in an automobile accident.

The court explained that it had to presume the constitutionality of the acts of the general assembly in the absence of clear indication to the contrary. The court concluded that the accident victim failed to demonstrate that the additur statute violated the constitution.


OUTCOME: The court denied the accident victim's supplemental motion for reconsideration of the jury verdict, which returned a verdict in her favor in her action against the driver for injuries sustained in an automobile accident.
