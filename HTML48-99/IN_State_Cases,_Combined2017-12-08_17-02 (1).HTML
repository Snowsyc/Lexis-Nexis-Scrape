PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Full Industrial Board No. 82-45-D-75.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, a minor child and her legal custodians, sought review of the denial by defendant State of Indiana, through the Full Industrial Board, of an application for financial assistance under the Indiana Compensation for Victims of Violent Crimes Act (Violent Crimes Compensation Act), Ind. Code § 16-7-3.6-1 et seq.


OVERVIEW: The minor's mother was strangled and stabbed to death by her estranged husband when their divorce was pending. The minor, who was a little over two years old at the time, was present at the scene of the murder but was not physically harmed. She claimed she was entitled to compensation. The trial court denied compensation to the minor on the basis that the Violent Crimes Compensation Act did not apply to family members. The minor claimed that Ind. Code § 16-7-3.6-5(b) violated her right to equal protection under U.S. Const. amend. XIV. On review, the court disagreed. The court concluded that the minor was not entitled to compensation under Ind. Code § 16-7-3.6-1(e), which defined a "victim" as an individual who suffered bodily injury or death as a result of a violent crime. The child could not recover for direct harm, nor under a derivative claim based on harm to her mother, because of the statutory exclusion of family members. Thus, the trial court correctly interpreted Ind. Code § 16-7-3.6-5(b) as disallowing assistance based on a familial relationship with the offender.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the Industrial Board.

PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from a denial of defendant's petition for post-conviction relief relating to his conviction and sentencing under a former statute.

From the Fountain Circuit Court, Vincent F. Grogg, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment of the Fountain Circuit Court (Indiana), which denied his petition for post-conviction relief relating to his conviction and sentence for possession of burglary tools by a convicted felon under former Ind. Code § 35-13-8-1.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, defendant argued that the trial court (1) erred in ordering that the sentence for the possession of burglary tools offense and for another offense committed while he was released on appeal bond were to run consecutively pursuant to former Ind. Code § 35-8-7.5-1; and (2) erred in not vacating his conviction and sentence for possession of burglary tools in light of the repeal of Ind. Code § 35-13-8-1. Upon review, the court found defendant's argument without merit. Defendant was not deprived of any right which he had at the time he committed the crime of possession of burglary tools on June 24, 1975. Regardless of the applicability of former Ind. Code § 35-8-7.5-1, defendant had to serve the sentence he received for that crime before he could have been released. Defendant was not deprived of any right he had at the time he committed the crime of entering to commit a felony by having to serve his sentence for that crime consecutively to his sentence for possession of burglary tools. He had more than an adequate opportunity to modify his behavior so that he would not commit an offense for which he would have to serve a sentence consecutively to another sentence.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

From the Jefferson Circuit Court, Paul R. Schnaitter, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Remanded with instructions.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant decedent's estate sought review of a judgment of the Jefferson Circuit Court (Indiana), which ordered it to pay appellee State of Indiana for the maintenance of the decedent's son, a resident patient at a State mental hospital.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, the estate argued that (1) the judgment was contrary to law; (2) the judgment was contrary to the evidence; and (3) the award of recovery to the State was excessive. Upon review, the court found that the award to the State was excessive and that it needed to be reduced. The trial court's judgment was correct insofar as it determined that the estate was liable to the State for those maintenance charges of which the decedent had timely notice of the State's determination to consider him liable for such costs. However, the State failed to prove by sufficient evidence that the decedent received the notice required to establish the liability of his estate for the son's maintenance costs prior to October 21, 1966. The failure of proof of the decedent's liability for the son's maintenance costs prior to October 21, 1966, mandated the court's agreement with the estate's contention that the trial court's award of recovery to the State was excessive.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the matter for a reduction in the award of recovery and for the entry of a final judgment pursuant to the opinion.

From the Bartholomew Circuit Court, William M. Lienberger, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment of the Bartholomew Circuit Court (Indiana), which convicted defendant of distributing obscene matter in violation of Ind. Code § 35-30-10.1-2.


OVERVIEW: Defendant challenged his conviction, alleging that Ind. Code § 35-30-10.1-1(c), (d) was unconstitutionally vague in their definition of obscene. On appeal, the court held that § 35-30-10.1-1(c), (d) was not unconstitutionally vague because it gave sufficient notice of the proscribed conduct. Defendant also argued that the trial court erred by refusing to give a requested instruction that absolved defendant of liability if the jury found that defendant did not personally distribute the materials. The court held that the requested instruction did not correctly state the law, finding that the legislature did not mean to exclude all those with a financial, managerial, or proprietary interest in the distribution process from liability because they did not directly sell the material. Defendant also alleged that there was insufficient evidence to show that he knew the specific content of the purchased material. The court held that defendant's knowledge could be inferred from evidence showing that the adult bookstore posted the typical warning notices, that defendant ordered inventory and looked through some magazines, and that the covers of the matter depicted sexual activity.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for distributing obscene matter.

From the Howard Superior Court II, Carl E. Van Dorn, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant landfill challenged an order of the Howard Superior Court (Indiana) that held valid a local ordinance that regulated the collection and disposal of garbage. The landfill claimed that Indiana had preempted the field of sanitary landfill regulation; that the ordinance encroached upon the statutory powers of the Howard County Board of Zoning Appeals; and that the ordinance violated the due process and equal protection clauses.


OVERVIEW: A local ordinance regulating the disposal and collection of trash was passed and the landfill sought a declaratory judgment declaring the ordinance void. The trial court found the ordinance valid and the landfill sought review. On review, the court invalidated "only" the part of the local ordinance. The court found that the invalid portions of the ordinance were "sufficiently severable" from the valid parts. The court found that the portion of the ordinance that addressed landfill site approval was invalid and violated the due process clause of the U.S. and the Indiana Constitutions. The court indicated that this was an indication that the EMA was never intended to preempt the field of solid waste disposal from local regulation.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court to the extent that it upheld the validity of the part of the ordinance that addressed land fill site approval. The court affirmed the validity of the ordinance in all other respects.

From the Marion County Superior Court, Room No. 6, Mercer M. Mance, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, bargaining unit and Indiana Education Employment Relations Board (Board), sought review of an order of the Marion County Superior Court (Indiana), which granted appellee bargaining agent's request for relief and enjoined the board from holding an election to determine school teacher representation.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, the bargaining unit and the board claimed that the lower court lacked jurisdiction to issue an injunction preventing the holding of an election and that the board's decision that a certain election contract was no longer in effect was improperly reversed. The bargaining agent countered that the board's order impaired its contract rights and that res judicata controlled on that issue. The court affirmed the lower court's judgment in favor of the bargaining agent. The court held that the lower court had subject matter jurisdiction over the action. Thus, judicial review was not premature because the exhaustion requirement was excused. Finally, the lower court did not err in reversing the board's order because res judicata barred the relitigation of the election agreement's continuing validity.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the lower court's order that had enjoined the board from holding an election to determine teacher representation.

PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the dismissal of appellant's complaint for an injunction and a declaratory judgment against the Medical Licensing Board for failure to exhaust administrative remedies.

From the Marion Superior Court, Room Seven, Gerald Zore, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant physician sought review of an order from the Marion Superior Court (Indiana) dismissing his complaint for an injunction to delay a hearing and for a declaratory judgment that the scheduled hearing would violate several constitutional guarantees. Appellees, medical licensing board and its members (board), scheduled the hearing to determine whether the physician's medical license should be revoked.


OVERVIEW: The board alleged that the physician violated Ind. Code § 25-22.5-6-2 by illegally issuing controlled substances prescriptions to female patients in exchange for sexual acts. The physician argued that he should be allowed to bypass the normal review procedure because he raised pure questions of law concerning constitutional issues. The court affirmed the order, concluding that the trial court properly dismissed the action on the basis that the physician did not exhaust his administrative remedies. The court found that the Administrative Adjudication Act (Act), Ind. Code § 4-22-1-1 et seq., afforded the physician an adequate administrative procedure and review. The court determined that the Act restricted access to the courts even where the administrative agency took action affecting constitutional rights. Because the physician could not demonstrate that he was entitled to equitable or extraordinary relief, he was limited by the Act to the prescribed procedure for judicial review. The court found that the physician was not entitled to either injunctive relief or the remedy of declaratory judgment.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order that dismissed the physician's complaint for an injunction to delay a hearing and for a declaratory judgment that the scheduled hearing would violate several constitutional guarantees.
