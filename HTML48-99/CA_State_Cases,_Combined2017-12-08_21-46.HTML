PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Superior Court of Fresno County, No. 28737, Denver C. Peckinpah, Judge.



DISPOSITION: The order denying the petition for writ of prohibition is affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of a judgment rendered by the Superior Court of Fresno County (California) that denied appellant's peremptory writ seeking to prevent the municipal court from taking action in a case that charged him with disorderly conduct under Cal. Penal Code § 647.


OVERVIEW: Appellant was arrested and charged with disorderly conduct in violation of Cal. Penal Code § 647(b). Appellant demurred to the complaint on the grounds that the facts alleged failed to state a public offense. At the hearing on the demurrer, it was agreed between appellant and the district attorney that appellant was not a prostitute and had not engaged in an act of prostitution. The municipal court overruled the demurrer. Appellant filed a petition for writ of prohibition in the Fresno County Superior Court asking it to restrain the municipal court from taking any further action in the case other than to order a dismissal. The superior court denied the writ. Appellant sought review. The words in § 647 stated that "every person" who committed certain actions were guilty of disorderly conduct. Despite these words, appellant contended that the legislative history and prior judicial interpretation applied the statute to prostitutes and not customers. The reviewing court concluded that the legislative history was not helpful in deciding the matter. The court affirmed the judgment denying the writ. Under the ordinary meaning of the statute, a customer could be charged with the offense.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment denying appellant's preemptory writ seeking dismissal of his disorderly conduct charge because a customer of a prostitute could be charged with disorderly conduct under the ordinary meaning of the statute.

Civ. No. 35697


Court of Appeal of California, First Appellate District, Division One


54 Cal. App. 3d 488; 126 Cal. Rptr. 659; 1976 Cal. App. LEXIS 1148



January 19, 1976 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Superior Court of Alameda County, No. 427902, John P. Sparrow, Judge.



DISPOSITION: The judgment is reversed. The superior court will enter judgment in favor of the City of Fremont in a manner not inconsistent with our opinion.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant city sought review of a judgment of the Superior Court of Alameda County (California), which found in favor of respondent housing complex owner that in-lieu fees levied under Fremont, Cal., Municipal Code § 8-1716 were unlawfully applied by appellant to respondent.


OVERVIEW: Respondent housing complex owner filed an application with appellant city for approval of a subdivision map to convert its apartments into condominiums. While it was pending, appellant enacted Fremont, Cal., Municipal Code § 8-1716, an ordinance authorized under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 11546. Appellant eventually approved the plan, subject to the condition that respondent pay in-lieu fees as required by § 8-1716. The trial court found that § 8-1716 had been unlawfully applied. On appeal, the court reversed and ordered the trial court to enter judgment for appellant. When § 8-1716 was enacted, § 11546 provided that subdivisions were subject to the fees, subdivisions included condominiums, and appellant was presumed to have understood this in enacting the ordinance. Section 11546 was a revenue statute designed for the acquisition of municipal parklands; thus § 8-1716 could not be considered a local zoning ordinance subject to the provisions of Cal. Civ. Code § 1370. There was no constitutional requirement of uniform treatment and the legislative classification was not erroneous or arbitrary. The proper amount of levied fees was the amount equal to the value of the unimproved land.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and ordered the trial court to enter judgment in favor of appellant city because the in-lieu fees applied to respondent housing complex owner were valid. The conversion of respondent's apartments to condominiums was considered to be a subdivision, subject to the fees. The legislative classification was not arbitrary and the proper amount of the fees should be the unimproved land's value.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a resident of Arizona, challenged a determination by the Justice Court for the Needles-Calzona Judicial District of San Bernardino County (California), that found him guilty of operating a vehicle with its front side windows tinted in violation of Cal. OVERVIEW: Appellant, a resident of Arizona, was found guilty of violating Cal. Veh. Code § 26708(a) because the front side windows on the car he drove from Arizona to California were tinted. On review, appellant claimed that § 26708(a) was unconstitutional because it restricted his freedom to travel, and its burden on interstate commerce was excessive in relation to any local putative benefits. Appellant also argued that the statute violated the privileges and immunities clause of the U.S. Constitution. The court agreed, and held that § 26708(a) was unconstitutional. The statute that appellant Arizona resident was found guilty of violating was therefore unconstitutional.
