Short v. Birmingham


No. 6 Div. 400


Court of Criminal Appeals of Alabama


393 So. 2d 518; 1981 Ala. Crim. App. LEXIS 2116



January 20, 1981 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from Jefferson Circuit Court.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the judgment of the Jefferson Circuit Court (Alabama), which convicted him of soliciting for the purpose of prostitution in violation of City of Birmingham, Ala., Gen. Code § 11-7-33(b) (1980). The trial court rejected the argument that the ordinance violated defendant's due process rights under the Fifth Amendment or was overbroad in violation of the First Amendment.


OVERVIEW: The city ordinance made it unlawful to loiter for the purpose of prostitution and allowed police officers to consider prior convictions and repeated conduct. On appeal, the court followed decisions from other jurisdictions and held that the ordinance was not so vague as to violate the Due Process clause of the Fifth Amendment because it outlined specific conduct, in addition to loitering, which the officer was required to observe, and limited the extent of the statute to loitering for the purpose of perpetrating a specific crime. The court also found that the ordinance required proof of intent that could be inferred by conduct but did not violate due process by shifting the burden of proof to defendant or creating a presumption of guilt. The court noted that police officers could not make arrests arbitrarily or on a whim. The court further held that the ordinance was not overbroad in violation of the First Amendment because it was aimed at prostitution, which was not a protected activity. The court also found that evidence that defendant was dressed as a female and flagging down cars late at night in an area known for prostitution was sufficient to support the conviction.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction of soliciting for the purpose of prostitution.

State v. Spurlock

Court of Criminal Appeals of Alabama


393 So. 2d 1052; 1981 Ala. Crim. App. LEXIS 2120



January 20, 1981 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from Etowah Circuit Court.



DISPOSITION: REVERSED AND REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Etowah Circuit Court (Alabama) granted defendant's motion to quash seven separate indictments charging him with violations of Ala. Code § 32-8-87(c) (1975), concluding that the statute was arbitrary and capricious in nature, an abuse of the police power of the state, and violative of defendant's rights of due process of law and the equal protection of the laws. The state appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was the owner of a junk or salvage yard. The indictments charged defendant with the failure to remove the manufacturer's identification number plates from either junk, salvage, or total loss motor vehicles in his possession. The appellate court reversed the trial court's judgments quashing the indictments and remanded the cause. Ala. Code § 32-8-87(c) (1975) did not deny defendant equal protection of the laws and was not arbitrary and capricious because the statute applied to all junkyard owners equally and the class defined by the statute was reasonable. The statute was not unconstitutional because it imposed strict liability without a requirement of intent or knowledge. The statute was not aimed at penalizing the possession of stolen property and could not be invalidated on the basis that it permitted convictions even where the vehicles at issue were not stolen. The statute did not place vicarious liability upon junkyard owners for negligent acts of their agents, servants, or employees. Finally, the statute was a valid and constitutional exercise of the state's police power and the means of enforcement adopted thereunder were not unreasonable.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgments granting defendant's motion to quash the indictments and remanded the causes.
