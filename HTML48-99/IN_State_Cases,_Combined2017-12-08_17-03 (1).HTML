SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Rehearing Denied April 18, 1972. Transfer Denied September 15, 1972.


PRIOR HISTORY: From the Marion Superior Court, Room 5, Addison M. Dowling, Judge.

Appeal from an action by plaintiffs as remonstrators against an annexation of their property into the sewer district of the defendant city in which judgment went to the defendant.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff landowners appealed the judgment by the Marion Superior Court (Indiana), which found in favor of defendant city in the landowners' action to protest the annexation of their property into the sewer district.


OVERVIEW: The landowners' predecessors in title signed sewer service agreements with the city which included a provision that they would not protest their property's annexation into a sewer district. The city annexed the property. The landowners filed a protest. The trial court found in favor of the city. On the landowners' appeal, the court affirmed. Pursuant to 1953 Ind. Acts ch. 160, § 7, the landowners' predecessors waived the right to protest an annexation when they signed the sewer service agreements. The trial court was to permit the annexation to take place if the conditions listed in Ind. Stat. Ann. § 48-702 (1963) were demonstrated. Because the parties stipulated to the necessary conditions, the trial court did not err in not holding an evidentiary hearing on those conditions. The agreement executed by the predecessors was valid and binding even though the right to protest an annexation may not have been available at the time. The sewer service agreements did not have to be recorded pursuant to Tenn. Stat. Ann. § 48-4272 to be valid. 1953 Ind. Acts ch. 160, § 7 related to future annexations. Because the statute applied to everyone, there was no valid equal protection argument.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment in favor of the city in the landowners' action protesting the annexation of their property into a sewer district.

149 Ind. App. 283; 271 N.E.2d 475; 1971 Ind. App. LEXIS 411



July 27, 1971, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] From the Elkhart Circuit Court, Allen S. Rasor, Special Judge.

This is a case wherein appellants-plaintiffs as taxpayers and residents of the city filed a complaint for declaratory judgment to challenge annexation ordinance of the city council. On appeal the primary issue is whether resident taxpayers of a city who are neither residents nor owners of land in the area to be annexed may challenge an annexation ordinance by a taxpayer's suit in a form purporting to be a suit for declaratory judgment and injunctive relief.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, city residents and taxpayers, sought review of an order of the Elkhart Circuit Court (Indiana), which granted summary judgment to defendant city. The complaint for declaratory judgment challenged an annexation ordinance passed by the city. Plaintiffs contended that the trial court erred when it held it was without jurisdiction to conduct a hearing on the alleged illegality of the annexation ordinance.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, the court noted that the primary issue was whether persons who were not residents or owners of land in the area to be annexed could challenge an annexation ordinance by a taxpayer's suit for declaratory judgment and injunctive relief. The court held that to obtain relief under the Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act, plaintiffs had to have standing within the purview of Ind. Code § 34-4-10-2 (1971). Because the Act required a plaintiff to have a right affected by an ordinance or statute before declaratory relief could be granted, the right of a taxpayer as a personal right was insufficient to establish standing. Plaintiffs together had a right to bring an action, but their right was not unqualified. A lawsuit on behalf of the public had to involve a substantial interest or right of the public. Thus, the action could only proceed where the action of the city was clearly or patently illegal, where the city acted without jurisdiction, or where wastage of public funds was present or imminent. Because plaintiffs had made no such showing, the court held that they had not established a sufficient interest in the annexation to have standing.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
