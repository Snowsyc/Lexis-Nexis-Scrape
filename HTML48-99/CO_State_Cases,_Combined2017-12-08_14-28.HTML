GMC v. City & County of Denver


No. 98SA220


SUPREME COURT OF COLORADO


990 P.2d 59; 1999 Colo. LEXIS 1195; 1999 Colo. J. C.A.R. 6416



December 6, 1999, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:   [**1] Appeal from the District Court, City and County of Denver. Honorable John W. Coughlin, Judge.



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT AFFIRMED IN PART, REVERSED IN PART, AND CASE REMANDED WITH DIRECTIONS.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant city appealed from the District Court, City and County of Denver's (Colorado) decision striking down the manager of revenue's assessment against plaintiff corporation of Denver's municipal use tax, Denver, Colo. Rev. Munic. Code § 53-96(1) (1993).


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff corporation brought vehicles to its Denver lab each year, where engineers ran the vehicles through high altitude emissions testing, both in the lab and the surrounding mountains, before shipping them to other testing centers. On average, the vehicles spent two to four percent of their useful lives in Denver. Defendant audited plaintiff, and imposed a use tax, pursuant to Denver, Colo. Rev. Munic. Code § 53-96(1) (1993). Plaintiff contested the assessment. However, the hearing officer affirmed. On judicial review, the district court struck down the assessment. On appeal, the court reversed in part, concluding that the use tax did not offend the dormant Commerce Clause because it met the following four requirements: (1) it applied to an activity with a substantial nexus with Colorado; (2) it was fairly apportioned; (3) it did not discriminate against interstate commerce; and (4) it was fairly related to the services provided by Colorado.


OUTCOME: The trial court's ruling concerning the constitutionality of Denver's use tax assessment reversed. The tax properly applied to an activity with a substantial nexus with Colorado, and was externally consistent because it was apportioned to account for the fact that plaintiff's vehicles spent only part of their useful lives in Denver.

SUPREME COURT OF COLORADO


983 P.2d 5; 1999 Colo. LEXIS 625; 1999 Colo. J. C.A.R. 3920



June 28, 1999, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the District Court, Boulder County. Honorable Daniel C. Hale, Judge.



DISPOSITION: ORDER REVERSED AND CASE REMANDED. JUDGMENT AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant state government sought reversal of the ruling of the District Court, Boulder County (Colorado) dismissing a wiretapping charge against appellee on the grounds that Colo. Rev. Stat. § 18-9-303(1)(e) (1997) imposed greater punishment for interference with corded telephones than for cordless was facially overbroad and violative of appellee's equal protection rights.


OVERVIEW: Appellee was arrested and charged with violating the state wiretapping statute due to his having interfered with his girlfriend's attempted telephone call to the police by disabling the telephone. The trial court held that Colo. Rev. Stat. § 18-9-303(1)(e) (1997), which criminalized interference with cordless and corded telephones differently, was unconstitutionally overbroad and therefore violative of equal protection. On appeal, the ruling was reversed and the wiretapping charge reinstated. The court reasoned that, with respect to the equal protection challenge, the statutory distinction between interferences with corded and cordless telephones was rational inasmuch as the two types of telephones transmit via different mediums (i.e., telephone lines for corded telephones, radio waves for cordless), with corded telephones entitled to greater protection since their users have a greater expectation of privacy. Consequently, the statute was not facially overbroad since there was a rational basis for the classification.


OUTCOME: The trial court's order dismissing a wiretapping charge was reversed because the legislature had a rational basis for distinguishing between corded and cordless telephones since those using corded telephones have a greater expectation of privacy in their calls, and thus, statutory provision calling for greater punishment for interfering with corded telephone calls was not unconstitutional.
