DISPOSITION: Ordered that the judgment of the circuit court be reversed and that the case be remanded for entry of judgment in favor of Miike and against the plaintiffs.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant-appellant Director of the Department of Health appealed the decision of First Circuit Court (Hawai'i), which declared Haw. Rev. Stat. § 572-1 (1985) unconstitutional as violative of the equal protection clause of the state constitution.


OVERVIEW: The trial court entered judgment in favor of plaintiffs-appellees, ruling that the sex-based classification in Haw. Rev. Stat. § 572-1 (1985) was unconstitutional by virtue of being in violation of the equal protection clause of Haw. Const. art. I, § 5, and that the Department of Health was therefore enjoined from denying an application for a marriage license because applicants were of the same sex. Subsequently, the state legislature amended the state constitution, investing in the legislature the power to reserve marriage to opposite sex couples. On appeal, the court held that the marriage amendment validated § 572-1 by taking the statute out of the ambit of the equal protection clause of the Hawai'i Constitution, at least insofar as the statute, both on its face and as applied, purported to limit access to the marital status to opposite-sex couples.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the circuit court was reversed and the case remanded for entry of judgment in favor of appellant-defendant; the marriage amendment rendered the plaintiffs' complaint moot.

OVERVIEW: Defendant pled guilty to unauthorized entry into a motor vehicle in violation of Haw. Rev. Stat. § 708-836.5 (Supp. 1997). Defendant filed a motion in his criminal case to enjoin enforcement of a local ordinance which provided for collection of a fee from persons convicted of misdemeanors and felonies. The trial court granted the motion and enjoined collection of the fee saying that the local government lacked the authority to assess a tax. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the lower court's order enjoining the collection of a fee from defendant upon his conviction of a crime because the local government lacked the authority to collect such a charge. The charge was deemed to be a tax rather than a fee because the funds collected could benefit the city's general fund instead of being applied to defray the cost of a particular service.
