SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Certiorari Denied December 11, 2000, Reported at: 2000 U.S. LEXIS 8332.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] LC No. 95-501656 AA. Wayne Circuit Court.



DISPOSITION: Order of the lower court vacated, and the decision of the commissioner reinstated.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondents appealed by leave from an order of the Wayne Circuit Court (Michigan) that reversed respondent Michigan Racing Commissioner's decision to revoke petitioner's pari-mutuel horse-racing license.


OVERVIEW: Petitioner held an occupational license to participate in pari-mutuel harness racing. A postrace urine test of one of the horses stabled and trained at petitioner's place of business revealed the presence of a prohibited drug. The license of petitioner's father, then the horse's trainer, was suspended. Petitioner replaced his father as the trainer for other horses stabled and trained at petitioner's place of business. Prior to a race, representatives from the respondent commissioner's office searched a barn located on petitioner's property, turning up several unauthorized and illegal drugs. Petitioner's license was suspended. The lower court found that the search was unconstitutional, and thus it excluded the evidence obtained and reinstated petitioner's license. On appeal, the court found that a reasonable person would have believed that petitioner's consent to search the horses and their related tack included consent to search the barn and areas within it that might be able to hold the tack.


OUTCOME: Order of the lower court was vacated, and the decision of the commissioner was reinstated. Petitioner voluntarily consented both verbally and in his license application to the search conducted.

BLATT v. LYNN


No. 209686


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


1999 Mich. App. LEXIS 1150



June 22, 1999, Decided


NOTICE:   [*1] IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE MICHIGAN COURT OF APPEALS RULES, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS ARE NOT PRECEDENTIALLY BINDING UNDER THE RULES OF STARE DECISIS.


PRIOR HISTORY: Lapeer Circuit Court. LC No. 96-023291 NI.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, a traffic accident victim and his wife, sought review of an order of the Lapeer Circuit Court (Michigan), which granted defendant driver's motion for summary disposition in plaintiffs' third-party no-fault action for damages sustained in a rear-end collision. The circuit court held that a new amendment to Mich. Comp. Laws § 500.3135 (Act) required the victim to show that his injuries had caused serious impairment of body function.


OVERVIEW: After the accident, but before the victim filed suit, the Michigan no-fault automobile insurance statute, Mich. Comp. Laws § 500.3101, et seq., was amended. After the amendment, subsection seven of the Act required that in order to recover non-economic damages for serious impairment of body function, a claimant must show objectively manifested impairment of an important body function that affected his general ability to lead a normal life. On appeal, the court held that it was not error to apply the amendment to the Act retroactively to the victim's suit because the amendment had been procedural in nature, and the Act had specifically stated that it would apply to actions filed on or after 120 days after its effective date. The court found that the victim had not shown any objective manifestation of his injuries, other than a shoulder separation diagnosed a year after the accident, or impairment of an important body function and had been able to maintain his normal lifestyle, including exercise activities. Therefore, the court concluded that it was not error to grant the driver's motion for summary disposition. The court noted that the victim had not had a vested right to a trial.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the circuit court's order, which had granted the driver's motion for summary disposition of the third-party no-fault action filed by the victim and his wife.

AMERICAN EAGLE FIREWORKS v. CITY OF LANSING


No. 206713


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


1999 Mich. App. LEXIS 648



June 18, 1999, Decided


NOTICE:   [*1] IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE MICHIGAN COURT OF APPEALS RULES, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS ARE NOT PRECEDENTIALLY BINDING UNDER THE RULES OF STARE DECISIS.


PRIOR HISTORY: Ingham Circuit Court. LC No. 96-083849 CZ.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff fireworks retailer challenged the judgment of the Ingham County Circuit Court (Michigan), which granted summary disposition in favor of defendant city in the retailer's 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983 and malicious prosecution action. The trial court disposed of the retailer's claims on the grounds of governmental immunity and an earlier decision that found that the retailer's fireworks were illegal under Michigan's fireworks statute.


OVERVIEW: The city seized and destroyer certain fireworks sold by the retailer. The retailer pled no contest to the criminal charges. The retailer then filed suit, alleging malicious prosecution and § 1983 violation. The court affirmed the order that granted summary judgment in favor of the city. The court held that the fireworks were contraband per se and were therefore subject to immediate destruction. They were not legally possessed, and it therefore would have been improper to return them to the retailer. Because the fireworks were contraband per se, their seizure and immediate forfeiture was authorized by Mich. Comp. Laws § 780.652. The court held that the prosecuting attorney in the underlying case was absolutely immune from a 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983 claim based on the initiating of a prosecution and presenting the state's case. The retailer failed to state a claim for malicious prosecution because it did not demonstrate that the prior criminal proceedings terminated in its favor; the retailer pled no contest to violating the fireworks statute.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order that granted summary judgment in favor of the city in the retailer's suit for malicious prosecution and § 1983 violation.

PEOPLE v. HALL


No. 207062


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


1999 Mich. App. LEXIS 651



June 18, 1999, Decided


NOTICE:   [*1] IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE MICHIGAN COURT OF APPEALS RULES, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS ARE NOT PRECEDENTIALLY BINDING UNDER THE RULES OF STARE DECISIS.


PRIOR HISTORY: St. Clair Circuit Court. LC No. 97-001600 FH.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State appealed from a judgment of the St. Clair Circuit Court (Michigan), which ordered the suppression of evidence against defendant.


OVERVIEW: The trial court suppressed evidence seized from a hotel room because it found that the officers serving a search warrant did not sufficiently alert those inside the room and that they possibly acted improperly in using a key card to access the room. On review, the court reversed the order suppressing the evidence. In reaching its conclusion, the court relied on caselaw holding that officers serving a search warrant were not required to announce their presence when they believed that such an announcement could lead to the destruction of evidence. The court noted that the record established that officers believed that announcing their presence would lead to the disposal of the drugs on the property. Thus, the court held that the method by which the officers accessed the room was not unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's order that suppressed evidence against defendant.

Tolksdorf v. Griffith


No. 209240


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


1999 Mich. App. LEXIS 629



June 15, 1999, Decided


NOTICE:   [*1] IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE MICHIGAN COURT OF APPEALS RULES, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS ARE NOT PRECEDENTIALLY BINDING UNDER THE RULES OF STARE DECISIS.


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Appeal granted by Tolksdorf v. Griffith, 461 Mich. 1014, 611 N.W.2d 797, 2000 Mich. LEXIS 600 (2000)
Reversed by Tolksdorf v. Griffith, 464 Mich. 1, 626 N.W.2d 163, 2001 Mich. LEXIS 878 (2001)


PRIOR HISTORY: Keweenaw Circuit Court. LC No. 95-000304 CZ.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded for proceedings consistent with this opinion.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff claimants appealed the decision of the Keweenaw Circuit Court (Michigan), which ruled that the claimants did not prove the existence of a prescriptive easement over defendant landowner's property, and that they were not entitled to invoke Michigan's private roads statute, Mich. Comp. Laws § 229.1 et seq., to require defendant township supervisor to impanel a jury to determine whether roadway construction on the land was a necessity.


OVERVIEW: The claimants' asserted that they had an easement to use road on the landowners' property and sought to require the supervisor to impanel a jury to determine whether a road on the land was a necessity. The trial court ruled against the claimants. On appeal, the court affirmed the finding that there was no prescriptive easement, but remanded for a writ of mandamus compelling the supervisor to comply with the private roads act. The court ruled that evidence that the road was used by the public and that the claimants' use was not inconsistent with the landowners' rights indicated that the use was not open, notorious, and adverse, as required for a prescriptive easement. The court found that the presumption of a lost grant did not apply because the claimants did not assert that they had been granted an easement, but that their use of the road was continuous, adverse, open, and notorious. The court ruled that the trial court erred by failing to issue a writ of mandamus instructing the supervisor to impanel a jury on the claimants' application because he had a clear legal duty to begin proceedings to determine the necessity of the proposed road and had no discretion to do otherwise.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment that no prescriptive easement was established, reversed its ruling that the private roads statute did not apply, and remanded for issuance of a writ of mandamus compelling the township supervisor to impanel a jury to determine the necessity of opening a road on the landowners' property under the private roads act.
