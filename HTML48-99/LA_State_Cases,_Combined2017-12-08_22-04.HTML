Smith v. Cole


No. 89-C-1134


SUPREME COURT OF LOUISIANA


553 So. 2d 847; 1989 La. LEXIS 2961



December 11, 1989, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] ON WRIT OF CERTIORARI OR REVIEW TO THE COURT OF APPEAL, FIFTH CIRCUIT, PARISH OF JEFFERSON.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: This case involved a filiation and support action brought by plaintiff mother against defendant, the alleged biological father of a minor. Defendant sought review of a judgment from the Court of Appeal, Fifth Circuit, Parish of Jefferson (Louisiana), which reversed the trial court's judgment dismissing the case for failure to state a cause of action.


OVERVIEW: A child was born while the mother was physically separated from her husband, but not divorced. She was cohabitating with defendant, the alleged biological father, at the time. Several years later, the mother brought this filiation and support action against the alleged biological father. The father filed a peremptory exception raising the objections of no cause of action and no right of action. The trial court sustained the exception and dismissed the action, invoking La. Civ. Code art. 184, which provided that the husband of the mother was presumed to be the father of all children born or conceived during the marriage. The court of appeal reversed the judgment, applying the concept of dual paternity, and held that the biological father had an obligation to support his child. On further review, the court affirmed. The issue was whether the biological father was obligated to provide support for his child notwithstanding that the child was conceived or born during the mother's marriage to another person and thus the legitimate child of that other person. Answering in the affirmative, the court found that the mother stated a valid cause of action.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the appellate court reversal of the trial court's judgment dismissing the mother's action for filiation and support, and remanded the matter for further proceedings.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, Louisiana Public Service Commission (Commission), the State, and intervenors, various pilot members of pilot fee commissions, appealed from a decision of the Nineteenth Judicial District Court, Parish of East Baton Rouge (Louisiana). CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State of Louisiana appealed a judgment of the Court of Appeal, Second Circuit (Louisiana), which affirmed defendant's convictions for attempted aggravated rape, aggravated crime against nature, and attempted second degree murder. The appellate court determined that the evidence was insufficient to support defendant's conviction for aggravated kidnapping and it remanded the case for resentencing on simple kidnapping.


OVERVIEW: Defendant kidnapped the victim from a parking lot and drove her to a nearby apartment complex and raped her. The appellate court held that defendant was not guilty of aggravated kidnapping because it found no evidence that defendant forced the victim to engage in sexual intercourse in order to secure her release. The court held that the evidence was sufficient to convict defendant of aggravated kidnapping.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the appellate court's judgment insofar as it set aside defendant's conviction for aggravated kidnapping and remanded the case for resentencing for simple kidnapping. No. 88-C-2740


SUPREME COURT OF LOUISIANA


548 So. 2d 936; 1989 La. LEXIS 1966

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] On Writ of Certiorari, Court of Appeal, Fifth Circuit, Parish of St. Charles.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: On application of defendant Department of Public Safety and Corrections, Division of State Police, the court granted certiorari to review a judgment of the Court of Appeal of Louisiana, Fifth Circuit, Parish of St. Charles, which affirmed a district court's issuance of an injunction barring the enforcement of criminal statutes that made gambling illegal. The court considered the validity of such an injunction in general and in the case at bar.


OVERVIEW: As plaintiff charitable organization was holding a "Las Vegas Night," state police entered the premises, seized gambling machines, and threatened to arrest anyone conducting such activities again. On review, the court held that there were three requirements for the issuance of an injunction such as was issued: the statute had to be manifestly unconstitutional, irreparable injury was threatened, and existing property rights would be destroyed by enforcement. The most compelling basis for not issuing such injunctions was the constitutional requirement of separation of powers, La. Const. art. II, §§ 1, 2. Although the district court did not identify what statutes were not to be enforced, the organization failed to show that any statute was manifestly unconstitutional. It did not specifically identify any fatal ambiguity in any statute. Nor was any potentially applicable statute unconstitutional due to vagueness or ambiguity. The organization's allegation that statutes were being enforced arbitrarily and capriciously was frivolous.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgments of the court of appeal and the district court, vacated the injunction, and dismissed the suit.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was charged with the first degree murder of a man. Defendant pleaded guilty to the reduced charge of manslaughter and to the attempted first degree murder of the man's wife. Defendant's motion to withdraw her plea was denied and defendant was sentenced to the maximum sentences for each conviction, to be served consecutively. A divided court of appeal affirmed. The state supreme court granted certiorari.


OVERVIEW: When the prosecutor informed defense counsel on the day of defendant's trial that he filed a supplemental charge of attempted first degree murder, plea negotiation began anew, and defense counsel offered to accept a previous offer of a 21 year sentence, concurrent with 20 years on the new charge. The trial judge had the discretion to reject the plea bargain, even if the parties had been in complete agreement as to the terms. However, the judge did not have discretion both to reject the bargain and to refuse to allow defendant to withdraw her plea which was based on her reasonable belief that she would be sentenced to concurrent sentences in accordance with the bargain. Thus, the sentences were set aside.


OUTCOME: The sentences were set aside. The case was remanded to the district court for the original trial judge either to grant defendant's motion to withdraw the guilty plea or to sentence defendant to the concurrent sentences that she reasonably believed she would receive when she entered the guilty plea.
