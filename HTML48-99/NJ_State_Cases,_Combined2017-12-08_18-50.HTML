Berkley Condominium Asso. v. Berkley Condominium Residences, Inc.


C-290-76 & C-1191-76


Superior Court of New Jersey, Chancery Division, Atlantic County


185 N.J. Super. 313; 448 A.2d 510; 1982 N.J. Super. LEXIS 852



June 7, 1982, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff condominium association and defendant condominium developer filed cross motions for summary judgment in plaintiff's action to invalidate a right of first refusal in the sale of condominium units in favor of defendant that was contained in the condominium by laws and master deed, on the basis of N.J. Stat. Ann. § 46:8B-36. Defendant argued that § 46:8B-36 unconstitutionally impaired its contract.


OVERVIEW: N.J. Stat. Ann. § 46:8B-36 prohibited rights of first refusal in all new condominium master deeds and bylaws, and retroactively applied a rebuttable presumption that all such extant rights of first refusal were unconscionable and unenforceable. Noting that the provisions in the U.S. Const., art. I, § 10, and the N.J. Const. art. IV, § VII, par. 3 (1947), against laws impairing the obligation of contracts were not absolute, the court granted plaintiff condominium association's motion for summary judgment and denied defendant condominium developer's motion. The determination of whether a retroactive law unconstitutionally impaired a contract or deprived a remedy boiled down to a balancing of the extent of deprivation of the private right and the social good the law provided. The court noted the strong public policy favoring the alienation of private property, and held that this outweighed defendant's essentially inchoate right to purchase condominium units to be sold in the future. Section 46:8B-36 also was not unconstitutionally vague with respect to the type of evidence required to overcome the presumption.


OUTCOME: The court granted plaintiff condominium association's motion for summary judgment. The court held that a law creating a presumption that defendant condominium developer's right of refusal was unconscionable was not an unconstitutional impairment of contract, and that the law was not void for vagueness.

L-54994-81


Superior Court of New Jersey, Law Division, Hudson County


185 N.J. Super. 172; 447 A.2d 937; 1982 N.J. Super. LEXIS 838



June 1, 1982, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff independent candidate filed suit against defendants, secretary of state, county clerk, superintendent of elections, and other candidates, to obtain a place on the ballot in a special election to be held to fill a vacancy in the state senate. Plaintiff alleged that the deadlines for nominating candidates violated his right to equal protection of the laws.


OVERVIEW: When a seated legislator was removed from office, a special election was ordered. Pursuant to N.J. Stat. Ann. § 19:27-11, political parties had up to seven days from the issuance of the writ of election to nominate their candidates. Facially, however, § 19:27-11 made no provision for the filing of petitions by independent candidates for special elections, but provided only that their petitions had to be filed at least 34 days before a general election. Defendant secretary of state construed § 19:27-11 to require plaintiff independent candidate to file at least 34 days before the special election, which resulted in plaintiff having four less days in which to file than major party candidates. The court held that this construction patently violated plaintiff's right to equal protection of the laws, as there was no compelling state interest for the variance between candidates. Indeed, there was no legislative statement of findings or purpose at all relating to § 19:27-11. The court therefore construed the statute to permit plaintiff to file in the same time frame as party candidates, noting that this did no violence to the statute's purpose.


OUTCOME: The court found for plaintiff independent candidate, ruling that as it construed the statute that established the time frame for filing election petitions by independent candidates, plaintiff's petition was timely filed. So that the statute did not violate plaintiff's right to equal protection, the court held that independent candidates could file their petitions in the same time frame as applied to nominations by political parties.
