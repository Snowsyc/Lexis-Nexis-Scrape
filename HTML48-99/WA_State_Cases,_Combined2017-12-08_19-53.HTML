OVERVIEW: The landowners were assessed charges by respondents to finance respondents' water management department. The court also found that the charges were not an illegal tax.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed a decision that granted summary judgment for respondents in the landowners' declaratory judgment action. The landowners had alleged that an ordinance that assessed charges against them to finance respondents' water management department was unconstitutional.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The state appealed a decision of the Superior Court for King County (Washington), which dismissed murder charges against defendants. The charges stemmed from a robbery and shooting that caused the victim's death one year and eight months after the crime was committed.


OVERVIEW: The trial court dismissed the murder charges because the common law rule in effect at the time of the crime required death to occur within a year and a day. The victim died one year and eight months after the crime. As a result, although the law was changed prior to the victim's death to permit murder charges if death occurred within three years and a day, application of the new law would violate the constitutional prohibition against ex post facto legislation. The amendment escalated and aggravated defendants' crime because an offense not chargeable as murder when committed became murder. The consequences of a murder conviction were potentially far more severe than the consequences of an assault conviction.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the dismissal of the murder charges against defendants.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant business owners filed an action against respondents, the state, the governor, and others. Appellants alleged damages caused by the governor's declaring a state of emergency due to volcanic activity too soon, including a town in the restricted "red zone," and failing to remove the town from that zone soon enough. The Superior Court for Cowlitz County (Washington) granted respondents' motion for summary judgment, dismissing the complaint.


OVERVIEW: Appellants alleged that respondents' actions were tortious and constituted a taking of property without compensation. Appellants also sought damages under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983. The court affirmed the dismissal, holding that the governor's actions were authorized by statute and were entirely discretionary because: 1) in an area affected by a natural disaster, the preservation and maintenance of life, health, property, and the public peace was a basic governmental policy; 2) in the case of a volcano, the establishment of a restricted zone during a period of uncertain eruptions was essential to accomplish that policy; 3) the decision of whether a particular area should be included within the zone required the exercise of basic policy evaluation, judgment, and expertise; 4) the governor possessed the requisite constitutional and statutory authority to restrict access to certain localities for the protection of the public; and 5) the governor made a conscious decision to include the town because of her concern that it was exposed to danger from the volcano. Moreover, the governor's actions were proper exercises of her police power and thus did not constitute an unconstitutional taking.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the dismissal of appellants' action for damages based upon the state of emergency declared due to volcanic activity.

OUTCOME: The ruling of the trial court as to the residential placement of the daughter was affirmed.

92 Wn.2d 905; 602 P.2d 1177; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1462; 14 ERC (BNA) 1302



November 21, 1979 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied February 14, 1980.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant city sought review of the grant of summary judgment in favor of respondent billboard owners (owners) in the Superior Court for King County (Washington) in consolidated declaratory judgment actions by the owners challenging a sign removal ordinance, Seattle, Wash., Ordinance No. 97025 (ordinance), as unconstitutional and in conflict with state and the Highway Beautification Act and seeking an injunction against its enforcement.


OVERVIEW: The owners were required by the ordinance to bring their signs into compliance with the ordinance requirements or to remove the signs, without compensation. The owners filed a suit against the city, seeking declaratory and injunctive relief. Summary judgment was granted for the owners on the basis that the ordinance conflicted with the compensation provisions of Wash. Rev. Code § 47.42.107, which was enacted while the instant action was pending, and that it was unconstitutional as applied. The city was enjoined from requiring removal of the signs without compensation. On appeal, the court reversed, holding (1) the owners lacked standing to maintain the suit because they failed to pursue the available administrative remedy of a variance; (2) 23 U.S.C.S. § 131(g) of the Highway Beautification Act (Act) did not require compensation for the removals because, among other things, the signs were outside the scope of the Act's compensation provisions; (3) the retroactive application of § 47.42.107 constituted a gift of public funds in violation of Wash. Const. art. 8, § 5; and (4) the ordinance did not effect an unconstitutional taking and was not otherwise unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The trial court's grant of summary judgment in favor of the billboard owners in a declaratory judgment action seeking to enjoin the enforcement of the city's sign removal ordinance was reversed, and the case was dismissed.

State v. Crown Zellerbach Corp.


No. 45353


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


92 Wn.2d 894; 602 P.2d 1172; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1458; 10 ELR 20259



November 15, 1979 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The state sought review of a decision of the Superior Court for Pacific County (Washington), which dismissed a criminal information filed against respondent corporation, owner of a logging site, for noncompliance with conditions contained in a permit for a hydraulic project issued by the Department of Fisheries and the Department of Game (departments) pursuant to Wash. Rev. Code § 75.20.100.


OVERVIEW: The corporation received a permit for a hydraulic project by the departments for a logging operation on the corporation's property. The state filed a criminal information based on alleged noncompliance by the corporation with the permit's conditions. The trial court granted the corporation's motion to dismiss on the grounds that § 75.20.100 constituted an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power, and the conditions contained in the permit were not properly adopted and promulgated by the departments. On appeal, the court reversed the trial court's order and held that the delegation of legislative power was justified under § 75.20.100 because the legislature provided standards that in general terms defined what was to be done and what administrative body was to do it, and the statute provided procedural safeguards that existed to control arbitrary administrative action and abuse of discretionary power. The court ruled that the legislature did not delegate its authority to define crimes by its enactment of § 75.20.100. The court held that the corporation did not challenge the reasonableness of the terms of the permit. The court remanded the case for trial.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's order that dismissed the state's criminal information filed against the corporation for noncompliance with conditions contained in a permit for a hydraulic project.

92 Wn.2d 882; 602 P.2d 711; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1459

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff petitioner filed a personal restraint petition that challenged the legality of her detention based on a conviction for possession of a controlled substance. The petitioner also sought to have her probation that arose from a previous conviction for forgery reinstated.


OVERVIEW: The petitioner was arrested and charged with possession of Dalmane during the pendency of certain emergency regulations adopted under Wash. Rev. Code § 34.04.030. The petitioner was convicted of possession of a controlled substance and her probation arising from a previous forgery conviction was revoked. The petitioner filed a personal restraint petition that challenged the legality of her detention based on her conviction of possession of a controlled substance and also sought to have her probation that arose from the previous conviction for forgery reinstated. The court granted the petition as to the petitioner's drug possession conviction and denied as to her forgery conviction. The court held that the drug possession conviction was unconstitutional because the procedure by which Dalmane was designated as a controlled substance afforded inadequate notice. The court ruled that the delegation of authority under Wash. Rev. Code § 69.50.201(a) was unlawful because of the inadequacy of procedural safeguards. The court held that probation was revoked for reasons independent of her conviction on the drug charge.


OUTCOME: The court granted the petition as to the petitioner's drug possession conviction but denied relief as to her forgery conviction.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants sought review of a judgment from the Superior Court for Yakima County (Washington), which held that certain restrictive language in the Public Employees' Collective Bargaining Act, Wash. Rev. Code § 41.56, was invalid and applied the act to appellee county deputy sheriff's association.


OVERVIEW: The deputy sheriff's brought a declaratory action to challenge as unconstitutionally underinclusive the portion of the Act's classification of uniformed personnel by population, Wash. Rev. Code § 41.56.030(6). The trial court found that the deputy sheriff's equal protection rights were violated by the population classification. However, there was some basis in reality for reasonably distinguishing between those within and without the designated class. Finally, the deputy sheriff's failed to show conclusively why the classification did not serve the Act's purpose of promoting a healthy relationship between public employers and public employees.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment.

Clallam County Deputy Sheriff's Guild v. Bd. of Clallam County Comm'Rs


No. 46025


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


92 Wn.2d 844; 601 P.2d 943; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1449



October 25, 1979 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant county board of commissioners (board) appealed the decision from the Superior Court for Clallam County (Washington), which found that the personnel system in a county ordinance, Clallam County Code §§ 3.08.010-.060, conflicted with Wash. Rev. Code § 41.14 and violated Wash. Const. art. 11, § 11. The trial court enjoined the use of the ordinance in its entirety in a declaratory action by respondent deputy sheriffs' guild (guild).


OVERVIEW: After the appeal was filed the trial court ordered binding arbitration. The board argued that the dispute over the alleged conflict between the county ordinance and Wash. Rev. Code § 41.14 was not a justiciable controversy. The court reversed the injunction and remanded for further proceedings. The order of binding arbitration was reversed. The court found that an actual dispute existed due to the overlapping personnel systems of the county ordinance and the § 41.14. The court held that under the state supremacy clause where the county ordinance and § 41.14 conflicted, the merit system of employment for deputy sheriffs under § 41.14 preempted the application of the system under the county ordinance. However, to the extent that the county ordinance purported to regulate the fixing of salaries for deputy sheriffs, it neither conflicted with § 41.14 nor offended the state supremacy clause. The court remanded for a determination of which provisions of the county ordinance conflicted with § 41.14. The court ruled that the trial court had no authority to order binding arbitration because appellate review was accepted before the guild filed its motion for binding arbitration.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's issuance of an injunction to enjoin the county ordinance in its entirety and remanded for a determination as to the extent of the conflict between the county ordinance and the state statute. The court reversed the trial court's order of binding arbitration.

Sheffield v. State


No. 45920


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


92 Wn.2d 807; 601 P.2d 163; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1450



October 18, 1979 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied December 12, 1979.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner litigants appealed in forma pauperis a decision of the Superior Court for Spokane County (Washington), which denied their motion to waive the bond required by Wash. Rev. Code § 4.92.010 in their action against respondent, Washington Department of Social and Health Services. The court accepted certification of the question of whether Wash. Rev. Code § 4.92.010 violated equal protection principles.


OVERVIEW: Petitioners sued the Department for negligence in its administration of foster homes. Petitioners unsuccessfully moved to waive application of Wash. Rev. Code § 4.92.010, which required anyone suing the State to file a surety bond to indemnify the State for its costs. In reviewing the statute, the court held that § 4.92.010 was unconstitutional. The court indicated that, once a governmental entity had waived sovereign immunity pursuant to Wash. Rev. Code § 4.92.090, it could not subsequently hinder claimants through such indirect means. Differentiating governmental and non-governmental claimants in that manner violated the Equal Protection Clause of Fourteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution and U.S. Const. art. 1, § 12. The court therefore held that Wash. Rev. Code § 4.92.010 violated the U.S. and Washington constitutional guaranties of equal protection.


OUTCOME: The court held that the requirement for a cost bond violated equal protection. The court remanded the case to the superior court for further action consistent with its opinion.

SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


92 Wn.2d 823; 601 P.2d 527; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1452



October 18, 1979 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff administrator appealed from a judgment of the Superior Court for King County (Washington), for defendants, driver and owner, in the administrator's action for the deceased passenger's wrongful death in an automobile accident. The trial court instructed that gross negligence was a prerequisite for recovery. After the entry of judgment, the appellate court had changed the common-law rule to a requirement of ordinary negligence.


OVERVIEW: The administrator's decedent was a passenger in a car involved in an accident that killed the passenger. The passenger's administrator brought a wrongful death action against the driver and owner. The trial court rendered judgment for the driver and owner. The host-guest statute, Wash. Rev. Code § 46.08.080 (1974) was then repealed, and on remand the trial court was instructed that the common-law rule, which required gross negligence for recovery, was in effect. The trial court then entered judgment for the driver and owner, and then, in Roberts, the court abandoned the rule that required proof of gross negligence and held that the duty of a host driver was that of the exercise of ordinary care. The administrator appealed. The court held that: (1) the parties and the court had a reasonable expectation that the law as it existed applied to the proceedings, and could govern their actions accordingly; (2) interests involved in the administration of justice outweighed the interests of the administrator in enjoying the new rights accorded in Roberts; and (3) the trial court's decision in admitting expert testimony would not be disturbed in the absence of manifest abuse.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment for the driver and owner in the administrator's wrongful death action.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied March 10, 1980.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Superior Court for Cowlitz County (Washington), which committed him to a period exceeding the standard disposition range after defendant pleaded guilty to stealing a motorcycle.


OVERVIEW: Defendant, a juvenile, was arrested on suspicion of theft of a motorcycle. Defendant was not represented by counsel at his hearing, but the trial court informed him that counsel could have been appointed if he or his parents could not afford to hire an attorney. Defendant was asked twice if he wished to be represented by counsel, but refused. Defendant pleaded guilty and was sentenced for a period exceeding the standard disposition range. Defendant appealed and the court affirmed. The court held that the trial court met the requirements of Wash. Rev. Code § 13.40.230, and that its decision to depart from the standard disposition range was supported by the record. The court noted that the sentence imposed on defendant was neither too excessive nor too lenient and was appropriate in light of his past offenses, parole violations and the circumstances under which the motorcycle was stolen. The court the fact that defendant committed the crime within hours of meeting with his parole officer clearly and convincingly supported the trial court's conclusion that to impose the standard sentencing range would have effectuated a manifest injustice.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

Asarco, Inc. v. Air Quality Coalition


No. 45508


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


92 Wn.2d 685; 601 P.2d 501; 1979 Wash. LEXIS 1445; 10 ELR 20203



October 11, 1979 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied December 12, 1979.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, an organization and associated individuals, sought review of an order by the Superior Court for Pierce County (Washington), which vacated an order of a pollution control hearings board (hearings board) which had, in turn, vacated a resolution by respondent Puget Sound Air Pollution Control Agency (pollution control agency) granting respondent applicant a variance from the pollution control agency's rules and regulations.


OVERVIEW: The applicant contended that jurisdiction to hear and to decide appeals from the pollution control agency's orders or decisions granting variances rested exclusively with the superior courts and that the sole effect of Wash. Rev. Code § 43.21B was to give the hearings board jurisdiction over the pollution control agency in regard to appeals from violation orders only. Contrary to the trial court's holding, the hearings board had jurisdiction to hear and to decide appeals from the pollution control agency's decisions granting variances from its rules and regulations. The trial court's holding that the hearings board lacked jurisdiction to grant an organization's and associated individuals' motion for summary judgment by means of the hearings board's order also was in error. Further, the trial court erred by vacating the hearings board's order that revised and remanded the pollution control agency's resolution for further proceedings pursuant to State Environmental Policy Act before acting on the applicant's application for a variance. Thus, the court reversed the trial court, reinstated the hearings board's order, and vacated the pollution control agency's resolution.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the order of the trial court, reinstated the hearings board's order, and remanded the cause to the pollution control agency for further proceedings consistent with the opinion of the court.
