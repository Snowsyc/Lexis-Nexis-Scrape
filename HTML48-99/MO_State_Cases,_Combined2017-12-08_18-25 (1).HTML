Ellis v. State Dep't of Public Health & Welfare


No. 7310


SPRINGFIELD COURT OF APPEALS, MISSOURI


277 S.W.2d 331; 1955 Mo. App. LEXIS 73



February 24, 1955 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff benefits claimant sought review of a decision by defendant Department of Public Health and Welfare, which removed him from the rolls of those receiving aid to dependent children. A circuit court (Missouri) found that the claimant did not receive a fair hearing and remanded the matter for redetermination pursuant to Mo. Rev. Stat. § 208.100(5), for which the Department sought review.


OVERVIEW: When the Department's Division of Welfare removed the claimant as a benefits recipient, the claimant appealed to the Department. The Division's action was based on the medical review team report that, in turn, was based on the medical report and certification of one doctor. A referee conducted a hearing and the hearing record was the basis for the Department's decision. Neither the doctor nor the medical team testified at the hearing and the medical review team report was admitted into evidence after it was "identified" by a county director. The court held that: (1) the manner in which the documentary evidence was admitted at the hearing constituted hearsay; (2) the Department's view of Mo. Rev. Stat. § 208.040(2) failed to differentiate between the validity of the requirement that physical incapacity be certified and the competency of that certification as evidence; (3) the certification requirement could not elevate hearsay evidence to the status of "competent and substantial evidence"; and (4) because the claimant's counsel objected to the documentary evidence, it could not be considered and, as a result, the record did not support the Department's action.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the result of the trial court's judgment.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff county hospital trustees filed an action against defendants, associations and medical and osteopathic physicians, for declaratory judgment on whether a rule excluding doctors of osteopathy from practicing in the hospital violated the laws of Missouri. OVERVIEW: The trustees and medical physicians maintained that the rule excluding osteopaths was not in violation of Mo. Rev. Thus, the employee was given four consecutive hours away from work on election day and the purpose of the statute was met. However, if defendant penalized the employee by deducting from his salary for the hour and a half he was absent from his work to vote, it was clearly a violation of Mo. Rev. Stat. § 11785.


OUTCOME: The court reversed defendant's conviction as to its refusal to let the employee have four hours away from work to vote. It also reversed the grant of defendant's motion to quash the second count as to the deduction of wages from the employee for the time he was absent from work. The court remanded for a trial on that count.
