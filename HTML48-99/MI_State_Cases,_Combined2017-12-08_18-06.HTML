PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulation Board of Medicine, appealed as of right from an order of the Wayne County Circuit Court (Michigan), which reversed the Department's order suspending petitioner physician's license to practice medicine.


OVERVIEW: A limited autopsy was actually performed by the physician but he prepared an autopsy report indicating a much more extensive examination was done. The Michigan Attorney General filed a complaint with the Board charging a violation of Mich. Comp. Laws § 333.16221(a), (b)(i) (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 14.15(a), (b)(i)). OUTCOME: The court reversed the circuit court's judgment and reinstated the Board's initial order suspending the physician.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Leave to appeal applied for.
Appeal granted by Edmond v. Dep't of Corrs., 418 Mich. 877, 1983 Mich. LEXIS 1814 (1983)
Reversed by, Remanded by Luttrell v. Department of Corrections, 421 Mich. 93, 365 N.W.2d 74, 1984 Mich. LEXIS 1308 (1984)



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In consolidated appeals, both sides challenged decisions of the Ingham County Circuit Court (Michigan). Plaintiff prisoners sought review of the determination that the exclusion of "drug traffickers" from the community placement program was not arbitrary and capricious under Mich. Comp. Laws § 791.265(a). Defendant Michigan Department of Corrections challenged the circuit court's decision against its blanket exclusion for drug traffickers.


OVERVIEW: The prisoners filed suit because the department refused to consider them for the community placement program. On appeal, the prisoners argued that the department exceeded its rule-making authority by precluding "drug traffickers," as it defined the term, from consideration for eligibility in community placement programs. The court agreed, reversed the decision against the prisoners, and affirmed the decision against the department. The court held that the legislation authorizing community placement neither evinced an intent to exclude drug traffickers from consideration nor gave the department unfettered discretion to consider or fail to consider any class of offenders it deemed appropriate. The court held that the department could not have excluded an entire class of prisoners from consideration in light of the restrictions and exclusions explicitly adopted by the Michigan Legislature in Mich. Comp. Laws § 791.265(a). The court stressed that its holding in no way suggested that participation in the community placement program by drug traffickers or anybody else was a matter of right. The department remained free to choose to exclude individual prisoners on a case-by-case basis.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the circuit court's determination against the department regarding the blanket preclusion of consideration of "drug traffickers" from community placement programs. The court reversed the circuit court's order insofar as it held that a prisoner's classification as a "drug trafficker" automatically precluded his consideration for community placement. The action was remanded for further proceedings.

Moore v. Fragatos


Docket No. 55332


Court of Appeals of Michigan


116 Mich. App. 179; 321 N.W.2d 781; 1982 Mich. App. LEXIS 3118


November 9, 1981, Submitted  
April 30, 1982, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Leave to appeal applied for.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff injured party brought a medical malpractice suit against defendant doctors and hospital. The trial court dismissed the suit after finding that the injured party knowingly and voluntarily signed an arbitration agreement. The injured party appealed as of right.


OVERVIEW: The injured party contended that the trial court erred in finding that he knowingly and voluntarily signed an arbitration agreement. The court held that a party in a civil proceeding seeking to assert a waiver of the constitutional right to access to the courts must establish that the waiver was made knowingly, voluntarily, and intelligently. The court found that the doctors and the hospital failed to present sufficient evidence of a knowing waiver of the injured party's right to access to the courts. The evidence did not affirmatively show that the injured party was sufficiently informed of the arbitration provision. The injured party was never informed that he did not have to sign the arbitration agreement; therefore, the court held that the injured party did not voluntarily sign the agreement. Accordingly, the trial court erred in its conclusion that the injured party's waiver was effective.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment and remanded the case for further proceedings.

People v. Thompson


Docket No. 53296


Court of Appeals of Michigan


117 Mich. App. 522; 324 N.W.2d 22; 1982 Mich. App. LEXIS 3282


January 6, 1982, Submitted  
April 27, 1982, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Reversed and remanded with instructions.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of an order by a trial court (Michigan), which convicted defendant of kidnapping, a violation of Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.349, and first-degree criminal sexual conduct, a violation of Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.520b.


OVERVIEW: The court stated that the trial court's instruction to the jury on asportation was insufficient to convey the essential point that the asportation could not be merely incidental to the charged criminal sexual conduct. Defendant's right to a jury determination on all essential elements of the offense required that the trial court's instructions include all of the essential elements of the offense charged and not exclude from jury consideration material issues, defenses, or theories if there was evidence to support them. Failure to adequately instruct the jury on the essential element of asportation was reversible error. It was also reversible error to instruct the jury on the defense of consent to first-degree criminal sexual conduct. Defendant was charged with having committed first-degree criminal sexual conduct by sexual penetration under circumstances involving commission of a felony. The felony relied on was the charged kidnapping. Since there was no clear legislative intent to authorize multiple convictions for a single criminal act, defendant's conviction of both kidnapping and first-degree criminal sexual conduct violated the constitutional prohibition of double jeopardy.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the matter for further proceedings.

Altman v. Lansing


Docket No. 57874


Court of Appeals of Michigan


115 Mich. App. 495; 321 N.W.2d 707; 1982 Mich. App. LEXIS 3113


November 5, 1981, Submitted  
April 22, 1982, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Leave to appeal applied for.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, alternate developers and taxpayers, appealed an order from the lower court (Michigan) dismissing their suit to enjoin defendants, developer and city, from working on a project on the grounds of laches and lack of standing.


OVERVIEW: The city selected the developer to develop a building for a project involving federal grant money. The taxpayers challenged both the legality of the project and selection of the developer. Realizing their suit might be dismissed, the taxpayers filed a second suit on behalf of the alternative developers. The suits were consolidated and the alternate developers and taxpayers sought, but the court denied, injunctive relief on the grounds of laches and lack of standing. On appeal, the court affirmed. The taxpayers' complaint was fatally flawed for failure to show their present or prospective damages and that the money could be used for general expenditures. They also failed to allege exposure to taxation because of the challenged expenditure of tax funds. Because they failed to support their proposition that the city would save money if the contract was cancelled and failed to allege unlawful or fraudulent conduct, they lacked standing to challenge the city's actions regarding the federal grant money.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the dismissal of the alternate developers' and taxpayers' suit to enjoin the developer from working on a project.

Tahash v. Flint Dodge Co.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Leave to appeal denied by Tahash v. Flint Dodge Co., 418 Mich. 878, 1983 Mich. LEXIS 1818 (1983)



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant driver's insurer sought review of an order entered in the Genesee County Circuit Court (Michigan) that denied the driver's insurer's motion to quash garnishment proceedings and that found liability in favor of plaintiff vehicle owner's insurer.


OVERVIEW: The vehicle owner's insurer argued that the escape clause in its policy relieved it of liability. The driver's insurer argued that the escape clause contained in the vehicle owner's insurance policy could not be enforced because the escape clause was void as a matter of public policy as it violated the financial responsibility act, Mich. Comp. Laws § 257.520 (b) (2) (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 9.2220(b)(2)). The driver's insurer also argued that its excess insurance clause that limited its liability where, as in this case, its insured was driving a non-owned automobile. The court agreed with the driver's insurer and reversed the trial court's judgment. The court found that the escape clause contained in the vehicle owner's policy was contrary to the financial responsibility act because it excluded a class of persons from insurance coverage.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court that denied the driver's insurer's motion to quash the garnishment proceedings filed by the vehicle owner's insurer.

People v. Campbell


Docket No. 52861


Court of Appeals of Michigan


115 Mich. App. 369; 320 N.W.2d 381; 1982 Mich. App. LEXIS 3097


June 4, 1981, Submitted  
April 21, 1982, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment of the trial court (Michigan), which convicted him based on his guilty plea of possession of 50 to 225 grams of a mixture containing cocaine, a violation of Mich. Comp. Laws § 333.7403(2)(a)(iii). Defendant was sentenced to lifetime probation.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, defendant raised various constitutional challenges to the statutory provisions under which he was convicted and sentenced. Basically defendant was challenging the fact that a person convicted of possessing 51 grams of a mixture containing 15 percent cocaine was treated far more severely than a person convicted of possessing 49.9 grams of pure cocaine. He argued that the criminal classification scheme, which determined the severity of the crime solely by the weight of the mixture, which included the substance and not the substance's purity, offended constitutional guarantees of equal protection of the law. The court concluded that the classification did not deny equal protection of the law, although it believed that the legislature had adopted a bad policy. The court held that the policy was not irrational in part because an argument could be made that street pushers were at least as harmful as dealers were. Thus, the court concluded that the classification that effectively treated street dealers the same as real drug traffickers was rationally related to the purpose of eradicating illegal drug use in society.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

Bacon v. Michigan Dep't of State Highways


Docket No. 52958


Court of Appeals of Michigan


115 Mich. App. 382; 320 N.W.2d 681; 1982 Mich. App. LEXIS 3098


December 4, 1981, Submitted  
April 21, 1982, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant claimant sought review of the decision of the court of claims (Michigan), which entered an order limiting the claimant's recovery against defendant highway department to the degree of negligence attributable to the department, in the claimant's negligence action alleging that the department had failed to maintain a roadway in a condition reasonably safe and convenient for public travel.


OVERVIEW: The claimant's vehicle collided with a truck driver's vehicle on a newly opened highway lane. The claimant filed suit against the driver and the driver's employer. Prior to trial, the claims were settled. The claimant then filed the instant action against the department, alleging that the department failed to maintain the roads in a reasonably safe condition. After a bench trial, the court of claims determined that the claimant was 60 percent negligent, that the driver was 30 percent negligent, and that the department was 10 percent negligent. The court of claims, applying the principles of comparative negligence, limited the claimant's damages against the department to 10 percent of the damages determined by the court of claims. The court noted that the contribution statute, Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. § 600.2925d, permitted a nonsettling joint tortfeasor to subtract the amount of a settlement from a judgment, but did not permit contribution from the settling joint tortfeasor. The court concluded that the statutory settlement provision applicable to joint tort liability survived the adoption of comparative negligence and the court of claims improperly limited the claimant's recovery.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the court of claims.
