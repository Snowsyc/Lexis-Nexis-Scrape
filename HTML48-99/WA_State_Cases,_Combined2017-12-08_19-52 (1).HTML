105 Wn.2d 175; 713 P.2d 719; 1986 Wash. LEXIS 1063



January 30, 1986 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] As Amended by Order of the Supreme Court May 13, 1986. After Modification, Further Reconsideration Denied May 13, 1986. Order changing Opinion May 13, 1986.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants challenged their sentences imposed by the Superior Court for King County (Washington). Defendants challenged the constitutionality of the Sentencing Reform Act of 1981 (SRA), Wash. Rev. Code § 9.94A, pursuant to which they were sentenced.


OVERVIEW: The court found no violation of the separation of powers doctrine by the legislature's enactment of the SRA because it was within the legislature's power. The court also found that the legislature's structuring of the trial court's discretion did not infringe upon a judicial power. The court found that Wash. Rev. Code § 9.94A.100 did not violate defendant's constitutional rights by requiring defendant to disclose any prior convictions when defendant entered a plea of guilty pursuant to a plea agreement. The court found that the beyond a reasonable doubt standard was not required but only the SRA's requirement that the existence of prior convictions be proved by a preponderance of the evidence. The court found that the State did not have the affirmative burden of proving the constitutional validity of a prior conviction before it could be used in a sentencing proceeding and that the identity of names was sufficient proof to show that defendant and the person named in the prior conviction were the same person. The court affirmed the sentences and held that the SRA was constitutional.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's sentences that were given to defendants.

In re Ayers


Nos. 51181-1, 51601-4


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


105 Wn.2d 161; 713 P.2d 88; 1986 Wash. LEXIS 1053



January 23, 1986 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner prisoners filed personal restraint petitions, claiming that they were mentally ill and/or developmentally disabled and that they were unconstitutionally denied parole pursuant to Wash. Rev. Code § 9.95.100.


OVERVIEW: Personal restraint petitions were brought on behalf of the prisoners, alleging that the prisoners were mentally ill and/or developmentally disabled and that parol was unconstitutionally denied pursuant to § 9.95.100. In denying the petitions, the court rejected the contention that § 9.95.100 was unconstitutionally vague, holding that the principle of adequate specificity in creating a crime was different from the discretionary exercise of statutory authority by the parole board. The court also held that the prisoners were not denied due process because the prisoners did not have a liberty interest in the potential of parole. Likewise, the court rejected the contention that the prisoners were denied equal protection, holding that while equal protection required equal treatment, it did not make people equal. Finally, the court held that even though there was not a constitutional basis for a protected liberty interest, the parole board, by Wash. Admin. Code § 381-50-170, had created such an expectation which was to be recognized and followed. In remanding to the parole board, the court held that the record was woefully lacking in compliance with the regulation.


OUTCOME: The court denied the petitions and remanded the matters to the parole board for procedural corrections.

State v. Bergeron


No. 50960-3


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


105 Wn.2d 1; 711 P.2d 1000; 1985 Wash. LEXIS 1301



December 12, 1985 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the affirmance by the Court of Appeals (Washington) of his juvenile conviction of the crime of attempted burglary in the second degree. The basis for defendant's appeal was that the information was defective because it did not allege a particular crime that he intended to commit within the dwelling.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was charged with attempted burglary, and the juvenile court found him guilty in part because defendant had entered a dwelling with the intent to commit a crime therein. In upholding defendant's conviction, the court overruled the case of State v. Johnson, 100 Wash. 2d 607, 674 P.2d 145 (1983), only to the extent that it held that the state was required to allege and prove that defendant intended to commit a specifically named crime inside the burglarized dwelling as an element of that crime. The court's reasons were that the statutory offense of burglary was drastically different from the common law crime of burglary, that it would have been impossible for the state to prove beyond a reasonable doubt what specific crime or crimes a defendant intended to commit, and that if the state were required to prove any of several possible crimes, in which whether a defendant intended to commit one was solely within that defendant's mind, a jury would have been required to engage in blatant speculation as to that defendant's intent. To the extent it was necessary to defendant's defense that the state prove a specific crime, he could have requested a bill of particulars.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's juvenile conviction for attempted burglary.

People's Org. for Wash. Energy Res. v. Utils. & Transp. Comm'n


No. 50264-1


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


104 Wn.2d 798; 711 P.2d 319; 1985 Wash. LEXIS 1303



December 12, 1985 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied February 24, 1986.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners citizens group and the attorney general's public counsel section sought extraordinary review of an electricity rate increase approved by respondent Washington Utilities and Transportation Commission (WUTC).


OVERVIEW: Petitioners challenged the authority of the WUTC to include in rates, as an operating expense, costs prudently incurred by a power company for the planning and designing of a subsequently canceled nuclear electrical generating project. The court found that the WUTC, as the regulatory agency charged by law with the setting of just, reasonable and sufficient public utility rates, was empowered to allow a utility to amortize to expense costs incurred in an abandoned electrical generating project, where the project was prudently undertaken and terminated by the utility, and where the WUTC fairly weighed both consumer and investor interests in arriving at its decision, as established by the record. The proceeding was fair and provided opportunity to be heard. Because the WUTC only allowed the power company to amortize abandoned plant costs, and did not include those costs within rate base or otherwise enable it to earn a return thereon, the "used and useful" concept was not involved. The court held that the WUTC acted within its statutory power and did not abuse its discretion.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed respondent WUTC's determinations and rate increase.

State v. Bartholomew


No. 51357-1


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


104 Wn.2d 844; 710 P.2d 196; 1985 Wash. LEXIS 1304



December 12, 1985 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied February 19, 1986.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner state sought review of an order of respondent Superior Court for Pierce County (Washington), which entered an order requiring the prosecutor to proceed with a new sentencing hearing to decide whether to impose the death penalty against defendant.


OVERVIEW: Previously, defendant was convicted of aggravated first degree murder and sentenced to death. On review, the court remanded for resentencing based on the unconstitutional admission of evidence. The prosecutor decided not to seek the death penalty and decided to have the trial court sentence defendant to life imprisonment without parole. The trial court determined that a sentencing hearing must take place. On further review, the court found that the jury was better suited for determining defendant's sentence in a capital case. Wash. Rev. Code § 10.95.050(4) required that, after remand, a new jury must be impaneled to determine an appropriate sentence. The court again remanded with instructions that the prosecutor present the case to a sentencing jury for trial of the penalty phase. The prosecution had no right, statutory or constitutional, to usurp the jury's functions to determine mitigation and make the decision whether defendant should live or die. The prosecutor did have the right to appear before the jury and ask for mitigation and the sparing of defendant's life. The final decision, however, had to rest with the jury, subject to review by the court.


OUTCOME: The court remanded with instructions that the prosecutor present the case to a sentencing jury for trial of the penalty phase against defendant.

Seattle-King County Council of Camp Fire v. State Dep't of Revenue


No. 50599-3


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


105 Wn.2d 55; 711 P.2d 300; 1985 Wash. LEXIS 1310



December 12, 1985 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant taxpayers sought review of the judgment of the Superior Court for King County (Washington), which ruled that Wash. Rev. Code § 84.36.810 and the 1983 amendments to that provision were valid, which was in favor of appellee state revenue agencies.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayers filed suit, seeking a declaratory judgment. The taxpayers asked the court to determine the construction and validity of Wash. Rev. Code § 84.36.810 and the 1993 amendments to that provision. The lower court upheld the statute's rollback provisions, and concluded that the statute should be interpreted to require payment of interest only from the due date of the new obligation imposed under section 810. The taxpayers and the state revenue agencies appealed. The court held that (1) the statute did not constitute a gift of public credit; (2) the statute was not unconstitutionally vague because it was sufficiently clear to apprise persons of ordinary intelligence that they will be liable for back taxes if they cease to use tax exempt property for an exempt purpose; (3) that retroactive application of the amendment did not destroy a vested right to collect taxes; and (4) that the interest due on taxes owed under the statute accrued from the date the taxes would have been delinquent if the exemption never had been in effect. The court affirmed the judgment except as to the trial court's determination of the time interest on the taxes accrued, which was reversed.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the lower court's judgment in part and reversed in part.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed an order of the Superior Court for Kitsap County (Washington), which convicted him of two counts of cocaine delivery and sentenced him to two concurrent life sentences pursuant to the habitual criminal statute, Wash. Rev. Code § 9.92.090.


OVERVIEW: The police placed defendant under surveillance in his hotel room, entered the room without a warrant, and arrested defendant and others. The State and defendant agreed that evidence seized in the room was unconstitutionally obtained and had to be suppressed. The court affirmed defendant's sentence as an habitual offender as supported by substantial evidence. The court held that defendant could not challenge for the first time on appeal, the voluntariness of a guilty plea to a prior conviction used to establish his habitual criminal status. The court held that the issue of voluntariness of the guilty plea pertained to the proof of the habitual criminal status and did not relate to his constitutional rights. The court held that defendant failed to establish a violation of his right to a speedy trial, that the information was not unconstitutionally vague, that evidence from certain witnesses was not suppressible, that defendant was not denied his right to a fair trial, that the knowledge element of the jury instruction was not improper, and that defense counsel was not incompetent.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for cocaine delivery and his sentence as a habitual criminal.

State v. Svenson


No. 51149-7


SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


104 Wn.2d 533; 707 P.2d 120; 1985 Wash. LEXIS 1273



October 3, 1985 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner state appealed the judgment of the Superior Court for Pacific County (Washington), which found defendants not guilty of violating Wash. Admin. Code § 220-32-031, unlawful operation of a salmon gill net in a closed area.


OVERVIEW: The state appealed the dismissal of the cases against defendants for unlawful operation of a salmon gill net in a closed area. The trial court found defendants guilty. On review, the superior court reversed. On further review, the court found that for a person to be convicted of a Washington crime on the Oregon side of the river, Oregon must have similar legislation. The proof of Oregon law was the prosecution's burden. The state failed to prove that Oregon has a similar criminal statute prohibiting the act prohibited by Washington or that Oregon has not acted or that Washington and Oregon always act in conjunction when enacting legislation with respect to fishing on the river. The Columbia River Compact, Wash. Rev. Code § 75.40.010, permitted the states to enact legislation that limited fishing activity, but it did not permit enforcement by one state of its own laws in the physical territory of the other absent similar legislation by the other state. The court affirmed the dismissal of the charges.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's dismissal of the cases against defendants for violating unlawful operation of a salmon gill net in a closed area.

State v. Hall

SUPREME COURT OF WASHINGTON


104 Wn.2d 486; 706 P.2d 1074; 1985 Wash. LEXIS 1269



September 26, 1985 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied November 26, 1985.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from the judgment of the Superior Court for Clark County (Washington), which convicted defendant of escaping from work release under Wash. Rev. Code § 9A.76.110(1), after refusing to allow defendant to withdraw his guilty plea to a controlled substance crime for which he had been placed in the work release program.


OVERVIEW: Defendant pleaded guilty to possession of a controlled substance and was placed in a work release program. When defendant was charged with first degree escape, the trial court refused to allow defendant to withdraw his guilty plea to the controlled substance crime and convicted him of first degree escape. On appeal the court held that the prosecutor did not breach the plea bargain agreement because the prosecutor promptly complied with the terms of the plea agreement upon being notified of the state's failure to inform the press that defendant had no connection with a drug conspiracy ring and the prior conviction was properly used as an element for the first degree escape charge because in a prosecution for escape the state was not required to prove the defendant was being detained pursuant to a constitutionally valid conviction. However, the court reversed, holding that the first degree escape charge violated defendant's right to equal protection because different mental elements existed for state and nonstate prisoners for the crime of escaping from work release and no rational basis existed for the difference in treatment of the similarly situated groups.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's denial of defendant's motion to withdraw his plea of guilty and the trial court's use of the prior conviction as an element of first degree escape; the court reversed defendant's first degree escape conviction and remanded the matter for a new trial.
