Wright v. State


Supreme Court of Florida, Special Division B.


87 So. 2d 104; 1956 Fla. LEXIS 3713



May 2, 1956.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of a judgment by the trial court (Florida) convicting appellant of concealing intoxicating liquor on which no tax had been paid in violation of Fla. Stat. ch. 562.32, contending that the statute contravened Fla. Const., § 8 prohibiting excessive fines and cruel and unusual punishment.


OVERVIEW: Appellant contended that Fla. Stat. ch. 562.32 unconstitutionally imposed excessive fines and cruel and unusual punishment because fine and imprisonment could be imposed for failure to pay what appellant called a non-existent tax. Appellant argued that the state could not punish him for not paying a tax on what was commonly known as moonshine whiskey because it was impossible to pay such a tax. The court disagreed, finding that while it may have been impossible to pay a tax on moonshine, it was certainly possible to obey the law by simply manufacturing, removing, depositing, and concealing none of it. The court found that the gravamen of the statute was intent to defraud, which was proven by the state. The court found that the trial judge properly admitted evidence of the alcohol seized by police from appellant because due to appellant's driving conduct, his behavior in attempting to break a jug against the window when he was pulled over, and the fact that the jug was visibly dripping a liquid that could be smelled by the pursuers, there was a sound basis for belief by the officers that a felony was being committed in their presence.


OUTCOME: The court upheld appellant's conviction and held that the statute was not unconstitutional because while it may have been impossible to pay a tax on the alcohol possessed by appellant, appellant could have obeyed the law by refraining from manufacturing, removing, depositing, or concealing the alcohol.

Summary procedure by which default and final judgment was entered was proper because statute under which plaintiff proceeded was a special statutory proceeding and Florida Rules of Civil Procedure provided that procedure in such proceedings should be as prescribed by such statutes.

Broward County v. Administration Com.


No. X-287


Court of Appeal of Florida, First District


321 So. 2d 605; 1975 Fla. App. LEXIS 15567



May 6, 1975 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner county sought certiorari review of a final action of respondent Administration Commission (Florida), which modified petitioner's budget for respondent sheriff.


OVERVIEW: Petitioner sought certiorari from a final action of respondent Administration Commission that issued an order modifying petitioner county's budget for respondent sheriff. The court found that the action of respondent Administration Commission was executive, quasi-executive, and quasi-legislative in nature rather than quasi-judicial. The court held that the last sentence of Fla. Stat. ch. 30.49(5) (1974) showed the legislative intent that decisions made by respondent Administration Commission were final. The court also held that it would only exercise a more limited review of quasi-executive or quasi-legislative action than of quasi-judicial action. The court affirmed because there was no departure by respondent Administration Commission from the essential requirements of law.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order setting respondent sheriff's budget because respondent Administration Commission did not depart from essential requirements of law in reaching its decision.
