OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs alleged that the reapportionment and redistricting of the House of Representatives violated the U.S. and Rhode Island constitutions. The court dismissed the complaint and denied the injunctive relief sought by plaintiffs because they failed to bear the burden of proof imposed on them by law to demonstrate beyond a reasonable doubt that the Reapportionment Act of 1982, insofar as it reapportioned and redistricted the House of Representatives, violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the Constitution of the United States, or the Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution of Rhode Island, or the provisions of 42 U.S.C.S. § 1982. The court found that the deviations in population size within the new districts was not prima facie unconstitutional, that the strength of racial minorities within new districts was maintained, and that the territorial compactness of the districts was reasonable.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the complaint and denied the injunctive relief sought by plaintiffs.

LICHT v. QUATTROCCHI


C.A. No. 82-1494, C.A. No. 82-1727


SUPERIOR COURT OF RHODE ISLAND, PROVIDENCE


1982 R.I. Super. LEXIS 61



June 3, 1982, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff registered voters, individually and on behalf of all persons registered to vote in Rhode Island, filed a declaratory judgment action against defendants, state officials and members of the Republic Party. The voters sought injunctive relief and declaration that the House and Senate Redistricting Statute, R.I. Gen. Laws §§ 22-1-1, 2 and 22-2-1, 2, violated provisions of both the Rhode Island and the U.S. Constitutions.


OVERVIEW: The action was a consolidation of the actions filed by plaintiffs. The court considered the evidence presented and entered judgment in favor of plaintiffs because they proved their case beyond a reasonable doubt. The reapportionment plan overstepped political propriety and violated constitutional and statutory mandates. The plan crossed 33 municipality boundaries of which 22 were not necessary. The legislative structuring of the Senate districts violated the compactness requirement in that it was without a rational or legitimate basis, was not enacted in good faith, and was not free from the taint of arbitrariness or discrimination. The new Senate districts were not territorially compact, and violated the 19th Amendment to the Rhode Island Constitution. The court also found that the constituents of two black communities were moved into the new districts, which were not contiguous and diminished their political strength. This vote dilution constituted invidious discrimination and a denial of the equal protection of laws. Based on the foregoing, the court concluded that the redistricting statute was unconstitutional, invalid, and void.


OUTCOME: The court entered judgment in favor of plaintiffs.
