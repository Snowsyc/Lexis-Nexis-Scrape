Rosenberg v. Dep't of Prof'l Regulation


No. 85-2002


Court of Appeal of Florida, Third District


488 So. 2d 153; 1986 Fla. App. LEXIS 7791; 11 Fla. L. Weekly 1125



May 13, 1986 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] An Appeal from the State of Florida, Department of Professional Regulation, Board of Pharmacy.



DISPOSITION:  Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant chiropractors challenged a decision from the State of Florida, Department of Professional Regulation, Board of Chiropractic, which found appellants guilty of violating department regulations, imposed a fine, and placed appellants on probation for one year.


OVERVIEW: Appellant chiropractors were found guilty of violating Fla. Stat. ch. 460.413(1)(d) (e) (w), and certain regulations of appellee, the Department of Chiropractic, and were fined, and placed on one-year probation. Appellants were found guilty of those violations because of a "tasteless ad" they ran and because of their failure to have the initials D.C. after their names properly identifying themselves as chiropractors. Appellants challenged the decision. The court held that appellee had no regulation regarding how a doctor of chiropractic medicine should identify himself. However, it was clear from the ad that ordinary people would not be misled by the advertisement, but would know that the appellants and their clinic practiced chiropractic. Therefore, the court found no basis for the alleged violation in failing to have the words "D.C." following their names. The court further found that the regulation relating to advertising was overly broad and restricted, unlawfully, free speech, no matter how "tasteless" the ad, and the regulation, therefore, was found to be unconstitutional. The fines and probation were reversed.


OUTCOME: The reviewing court reversed the order of the appellee department of chiropractic which found that appellants violated certain regulations, fined them and placed them on probation of one year. It held that appellee had no regulation relative to how a doctor of chiropractic medicine identified himself. Further the regulation relating to advertising was overly board and restricted, unlawfully, fee speech, which was unconstitutional.

On review, however, the circuit court held that pursuant to the Brevard County Sheriff's Civil Service Act, 1967 Fla. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The state sought review of the order from the county court (Florida), which dismissed the driver's license suspension proceeding for driving under the influence of alcohol against appellee driver and declared Fla. Stat. ch. 322.261(2),(3) & (4) (Supp. 1982) unconstitutional under the Florida Constitution, which provided that only the state supreme court could promulgate court rules of practice and procedure.


OVERVIEW: The trial court found that Fla. Stat. ch. 322.261(2),(3), and (4), which mandated the suspension of appellee's license when a continuance requested by appellee delayed the hearing on the suspension beyond the statutorily prescribed time, constituted an unlawful encroachment upon the judicial rule-making authority. However, the court held that ch. 322.261(4) did not attempt to deny to the trial court power to grant a continuance; it merely limited the privileges of appellee once such a continuance was sought and granted. Chapter 322.261(4) did not preclude nor require that the trial court grant a continuance. Furthermore, the court noted that the purpose of the statute was to preclude appellee from delaying the hearing indeterminably while allowing appellee, who refused to take a breathalyzer test, back on the public highways. Thus, the court found the statute constitutional.


OUTCOME: Lower court judgment was reversed and remanded. There was a compelling state interest in keeping public highways safe, which justified legislature in allowing suspension of appellee's driver's license for failing to take breathalyzer or blood alcohol test. The statute which limited appellee's driving privileges to 20 days if appellee sought a continuance of the suspension hearing after she refused to take a breathalyzer test was constitutional.
