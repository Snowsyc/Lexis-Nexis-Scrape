SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Stay Granted and Mandate Recalled October 11, 1988, Reported at: 488 U.S. 884; Certiorari Granted October 17, 1988, Reported at: 488 U.S. 907.


PRIOR HISTORY: Petition for Discretionary Review from the Court of Appeals, Fifth Supreme Judicial District of Texas, Dallas County.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of the decision of the Court of Appeals, Fifth Supreme Judicial District of Texas, Dallas County, that convicted him of desecration of a venerated object in violation of Tex. Penal Code § 42.09(a)(3) when, during a political protest, he destroyed a United States flag.


OVERVIEW: Appellant sought review after he was convicted of desecrating the United States flag in violation of Tex. Penal Code Ann. § 42.09(a)(3). The court reversed and remanded the case for dismissal finding that appellant's activity was symbolic speech protected by U.S. Const. amend. I, and that § 42.09(a)(3) was too broad and overly vague. The court found that the state's interests in preventing breaches of the peace and preserving the flag as a symbol of national unity were inadequate to support the statute. It reasoned that § 42.09(a)(3) was so broad that it could be used to punish protected conduct which had no propensity to result in breaches of the peace. Further, the court found that the existence of Tex. Penal Code Ann. § 42.01, which addressed the same basic interest as § 42.09, but in a less restrictive manner, indicated that the legislature was aware of and able to implement an alternative to the language of § 42.09. This, the court found, proved that § 42.09, as it related to breaches of the peace, was too broad for U. S. Const. amend. I purposes.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded the case for dismissal holding that the burning of the United States flag was symbolic speech protected by the First Amendment and that the state statute was broad and overly vague because it punished speech that would not result in breaches of the peace and because other less restrictive statutory mechanisms were available to address the state's interest in preserving the flag as a symbol of unity.

The court denied appellee's motion as to appellant whose conviction was reversed for riot by criminal mischief holding that the jury charge in his case was defective because the charge did not require the jury to find beyond a reasonable doubt that he knowingly participated in the riot.


OUTCOME: The court granted appellee state's motion for rehearing and affirmed appellants' conviction for riot by arson holding that the evidence was sufficient to support their participation and conviction in the riot. The court denied appellee's motion for rehearing as to appellant convicted of riot by criminal mischief because the charge to the jury was defective in not requiring proof beyond reasonable doubt he knowingly participated in the riot.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of the decision by the trial court (Texas) to revoke his probation after it found that he had committed aggravated perjury in violation of Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 37.04(a).


OVERVIEW: Appellant was convicted of delivery of marihuana, and his probation was revoked for allegedly committing an offense against the law. Appellant contended that the statute defining aggravated perjury, Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 37.04(a) was unconstitutionally vague. The testimony could have effected the credibility of state's witness, so it was material. It was not error for the trial court to deny appellant's motion to quash the state's motion to revoke probation because the motion did give him full and fair notice of that which he would be called to defend. Finally, it was not an abuse of discretion for the trial court to infer the requisite intent to commit perjury from the circumstances. As a result of the above findings, the court affirmed the revocation of probation.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's decision to revoke appellant's probation because the aggravated perjury statute was not unconstitutional, appellant's testimony was material, appellant had adequate notice of the allegations against him, and the trial court was permitted to infer the intent to commit perjury.
