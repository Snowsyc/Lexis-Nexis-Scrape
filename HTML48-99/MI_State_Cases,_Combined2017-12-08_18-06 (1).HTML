October 9, 1980, Submitted  
April 5, 1982, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Leave to appeal applied for.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, neighborhood association and landowners, brought an action under the Michigan Environmental Protection Act (MEPA), Mich. Comp. Laws § 691.1201 et seq. (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 14.528(201) et seq.), against defendant developer to enjoin the construction of homes on vacant land. The developer appealed from the Michigan trial court's injunction.


OVERVIEW: A neighborhood association and landowners objected to the construction of homes on nearby vacant land. They brought an action under the MEPA, and the trial court granted an injunction barring such development. The developer appealed and the court reversed. The court held that although the natural resources on the land were the type subject to the MEPA, the developer's proposed construction did not rise to the level proscribed by statute and Mich. Const. art. 4, § 52. The court held that the proper standard to be used was a balancing of the rarity of the resources against the magnitude of the harm likely to result. Because the plant and animal life was not rare or unique, the land was only marginally suitable for their purposes, and there were many other locations better suited, the court held that the MEPA did not apply.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's injunctions against the construction of homes by the developer in the environmental protection action.

OVERVIEW: Defendant stole two hams from a store. A jury found him guilty of larceny in a building, and the trial court sentenced him. The prosecution filed a supplemental information, charging defendant as a fifth offender after the larceny conviction. Defendant pled guilty to the charge. The fact that defendant was forced to go to trial without proper notice of the subsequent supplemental information and the attendant increased sentence resulted in substantial prejudice.


OUTCOME: The court vacated defendant's sentence under the habitual offender statute and reinstated the original sentence of two years and eight months to four years.

The circuit court then determined that no statute specifically required a child to attend schools. The court reversed, finding that § 712A.2(a)(4) gave the juvenile division of the probate court exclusive jurisdiction over any child under 17 years of age who was wilfully and repeatedly absent from school. The compulsory education statute that was in effect at the time was Mich. Comp. Laws § 340.731, which required that parents send their children, between the ages of six and 16, to school. The court noted that it would be unreasonable to hold that the legislature intended to require parents to send their children to school without a requirement that the children must attend. Accordingly, the student's attendance in school was required by law.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the circuit court's order and remanded to the juvenile division of the probate court for appropriate proceedings.
