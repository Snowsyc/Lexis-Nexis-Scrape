April 30, 1991, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from the District Court of Washita County, Oklahoma; Honorable Ralph W. Emerson, Judge.



DISPOSITION: REVERSED AND REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant judgment debtor sought review of a judgment of the District Court of Washita County, Oklahoma, which was in favor of appellee, the receiver of the judgment creditor. The proceeding concerned notice of a sheriff's sale.


OVERVIEW: The judgment debtor's property was sold at a sheriff's sale to satisfy the judgment in this case. Before the sale, notice was printed in a newspaper. The judgment debtor argued that the judgment creditor never gave her notice of the sale though she had lived at the same address for 15 years and was a 40-year customer of the judgment creditor. In an affidavit, the judgment creditor's counsel stated that he sent the judgment creditor notice of the hearing to confirm the sale. The trial court confirmed the sale by order. The judgment debtor filed unsuccessful motions to set aside the sale, to deny a writ of assistance, to dismiss, and to reconsider. The court reversed the judgment and remanded the case. Though Okla. Stat. tit. 12, § 764 (1981), controlled when the sale occurred and only called for notice by publication, such notice violated due process because the judgment debtor's whereabouts were known. Consequently, the order confirming the sale was void and the trial court lacked jurisdiction to enter it. In addition, an Oklahoma Supreme Court holding applicable to the sale date mandated personal notice for judicial sales to which § 764 applied.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded the district court's entry of a judgment for the receiver.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant father sought review of a judgment from the District Court of Washington County, Oklahoma, which adjudicated the father's children deprived and made them wards of the court. OVERVIEW: On the day of the dispositional hearing, the father filed a demand for a jury trial. He contended that Okla. Const. art. PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant former wife sought review of a judgment of the District Court, Cleveland County (Oklahoma) that held a trial for the divorce of the wife from respondent former husband after a settlement agreement entered into by the parties had been revoked. The court held that the wife received more than an equitable and fair share of the jointly acquired property. She was not entitled to share in her husband's earning capacity nor was she entitled to alimony.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court, as modified.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant landowners appealed a judgment of the District Court of Love County (Oklahoma), which overruled the landowners motion to vacate the trial court's order that plaintiff had properly condemned the landowners' property so that a road could be built.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff commenced an eminent domain proceeding against the landowners to build a road. The appraiser's report was submitted and the trial court condemned the property. Then the landowners sought to vacate the order. The trial court overruled the landowners' motion. The landowners appealed, contending that the trial court erred in taking the property without a hearing and that the taking was unconstitutional. The court held that a condemnation decision that adjudicated the right to condemn was a final order. Neither the order giving the plaintiff occupation of the property nor the denial of the motion to vacate constituted an adjudication of the plaintiff's right to condemn the property. If the landowners had raised the objection properly, there would have been a right to a hearing on all aspects of the plaintiff's right of condemnation. Having failed to object, the landowners were in no position to claim that failure to hold a hearing before the property was taken was unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

OVERVIEW: Following plaintiff's arrest for an unpaid traffic ticket, plaintiff brought a false imprisonment action against the city pursuant to the Act. PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from the District Court of Osage County; Don Hampton, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff landowners challenged an order of the District Court of Osage County (Oklahoma), which sustained a demurrer by defendants, a City and a zoning board, and dismissed the landowners' action under the Oklahoma Declaratory Judgment Act, Okla. Stat. tit. 12, §§ 1651-1657 (1971), on the ground that there was no actual controversy. The landowners had sought a declaration that an airport zoning ordinance was invalid.


OVERVIEW: The landowners had property in the zoned area. The court found that, even though they had not yet used the property in any manner inconsistent with the airport zoning ordinance, the ordinance operated as a legal restriction upon the property in the same manner as an easement would. The court concluded that landowners who claimed a decreased value in their property due to a zoning ordinance had a present, not a potential, dispute with defendants as to the general invalidity of the ordinance. Defendants also claimed that there was a nonjoinder of parties. The court held that, despite the word "shall" in Okla. It was only necessary to join those directly affected by the action. Thus, the beneficial owner of the airport property was not a necessary party because the City was a sufficient protagonist of the validity of the ordinance. Further, the landowners purported to bring a class action, and the averments were sufficient to withstand the demurrer.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the matter for further proceedings.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner, a judicial officer, filed an application for a writ of prohibition asking the court to assume jurisdiction and to prohibit respondent, the Oklahoma Trial Division of the Court on the Judiciary, from proceeding further in the suit filed by the State of Oklahoma to remove the officer from office.


OVERVIEW: On the basis of the findings of the Council on Judicial Complaints submitted to the Attorney General, the State filed a petition seeking the officer's removal. The court found that Okla. Const. art. VII-A, § 4(a) provided that the trial court's jurisdiction could be invoked by a petition filed by any of six different officers or bodies and that it gave the named officers, including the Attorney General, discretionary authority to invoke the trial court's authority. The court further found that Okla. The court, therefore, concluded that § 1659 was unconstitutional insofar as it imposed a mandatory duty on the Attorney General to file a petition invoking the jurisdiction of the trial court when directed to do so by the Council.


OUTCOME: The court granted the writ of prohibition.
