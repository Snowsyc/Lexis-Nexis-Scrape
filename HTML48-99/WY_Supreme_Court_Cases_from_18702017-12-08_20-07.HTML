Doles v. State


No. 98-273


SUPREME COURT OF WYOMING


994 P.2d 315; 1999 Wyo. LEXIS 197



December 22, 1999, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the District Court of Campbell County. Honorable Dan R. Price II, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of District Court of Campbell County (Wyoming) order compelling appellant, as convicted felon, to submit deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) samples as part of DNA Identification Record System established by Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 7-19-401 through 7-19-406 (LEXIS 1999), contending it violated U.S. Const. amend. IV.


OVERVIEW: The Act establishing state deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) database identification system, Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 7-19-401 through 7-19-406 (LEXIS 1999) (Act), required that every person who was in custody because of felony conviction, provide DNA tissue sample. Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 7-19-403(a) (LEXIS 1999). Appellant was convicted for conspiracy to commit larceny. In addition to imprisonment and fine, court ordered he supply DNA sample. Appellant objected Act violated U.S. Const. amend. IV's guarantee against unreasonable searches and seizures. On appeal, court affirmed, holding Act was constitutional. Collecting deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) samples either from blood, saliva, or hair, through routine, unobtrusive procedures was minimally intrusive. Parolees and juvenile probationers had lesser U.S. Const. amend. IV protections than those of law abiding citizens. Convicted felons had diminished privacy rights. Act's purpose in collecting DNA identification information was to advance legitimate state interest in criminal law enforcement.


OUTCOME: Order was affirmed because once person was convicted of felonies, his identity became matter of state interest and he lost any legitimate expectation of privacy in identifying information derived from blood sampling.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a consolidated appeal, defendants sought review of judgments from the District Courts of Laramie and Natrona Counties (Wyoming), which convicted them of various crimes for which the State refused to consent to the probation established by Wyo. OVERVIEW: Under § 7-13-301, if both the defendant and the State consented, the court could defer further prosecution and place the defendant on probation without entry of a judgment of guilt or conviction. On appeal, the court found that § 7-13-301 did not violate the separation of powers doctrine, and was a constitutional recognition of the executive department's power to initiate, control, and terminate criminal prosecutions before the judicial department exercised its power to enter a final judgment. OUTCOME: The court held that the statute did not violate the separation of powers doctrine and was constitutionally enacted. The State's refusal to consent to probation without entry of judgment against defendants was not an abuse of discretion and was not arbitrary.
