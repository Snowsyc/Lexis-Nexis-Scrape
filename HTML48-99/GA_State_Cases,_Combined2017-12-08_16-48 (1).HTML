PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Interpleader. Clayton Superior Court. Before Judge Emeritus Kemp.



DISPOSITION: Judgment reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Clayton Superior Court (Georgia) entered judgment for appellee county board of education in an action in interpleader brought by the county tax commissioner to determine who was entitled to a portion of educational funds that the commissioner had paid to appellant county's general fund. The county challenged the judgment.


OVERVIEW: Under O.C.G.A. § 32-1106, the tax collector was entitled to a commission for collecting school taxes, which was to be paid over to the proper fiscal authorities if the collector was on a salary basis. The tax collector had been paying the commission to the county's general fund. When the board of education claimed the commission, the tax collector filed an interpleader action. The lower court found for the board of education, but the court reversed. The court rejected the board of education's argument that the cost of collecting the educational funds was not "necessary and incidental" to public education because the tax commissioner was on a salary basis of compensation. A ruling that the commissions were only "necessary and incidental" when the tax commissioner was compensated on a fee basis would be totally inconsistent with § 32-1106.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment for the county board of education and ordered the disputed portion of the collected funds to be paid to the county's general fund.

PRIOR HISTORY: Murder. Bibb Superior Court. Before Judge Bell.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant challenged a decision of the Superior Court of Bibb County (Georgia), which convicted him of three counts of murder and sentenced him to death on each count.


OVERVIEW: Defendant brutally attacked six elderly people in their homes. Three of the victims identified defendant, and the other three victims died. Defendant was connected to the crimes by items that he stole, by a fingerprint, and by statements that he made to his friends. The trial court convicted defendant of three counts of murder and sentenced him to death for each count. Defendant argued, among other things, that the trial court erred in failing to grant a pre-trial motion for change of venue, in failing and refusing to give defendant's requested jury charge on intoxication, and in failing to command the jury to render a unanimous verdict following the trial's sentencing phase. The court affirmed. The court held that defendant was not entitled to a change of venue based on pre-trial publicity because the jurors did not have preconceived opinions as to defendant's guilt or innocence and there was a large period of time between the media coverage and the trial. Defendant's request for a jury instruction on intoxication was properly denied because it was untimely. The jurors' reservations to imposing the death penalty did not prevent their verdicts from being unanimous.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's three murder convictions and his sentences to the death penalty for each count.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Validity of Consumers' Utility Counsel Act; injunction, etc. Fulton Superior Court. Before Judge Langford.



DISPOSITION: Judgment reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, Consumer's Utility Counsel (counsel), sought review of a judgment of the Superior Court of Fulton County (Georgia), which declared the Consumers' Utility Counsel Act (Act), Ga. Code Ann. ch. 93-3A, inoperative and void for uncertainty and indefiniteness in meaning. The trial court also dismissed counsel's rate increase action against appellees, the Georgia Public Service Commission (commission), its members, and electric company.


OVERVIEW: Counsel was appointed pursuant to the Act to act in consumers' and the public's interest in virtually all matters involving Georgia's regulated public utilities. Counsel filed an action against the commission, its members, and the electric company regarding a rate increase. The electric company filed a counterclaim contending that the Act was unconstitutional, void, and unenforceable. The trial court declared the Act inoperative and void for uncertainty and indefiniteness in meaning and dismissed counsel's action. On appeal, the court reversed and remanded the case. The court held that the Georgia General Assembly had the authority to provide for such an advocate to challenge actions taken by the commission so long as the legislation was not unconstitutional. Although counsel was given discretion in choosing which matters to pursue, such discretionary decisions, which were not unlike those faced by other governmental entities, did not make the Act void for vagueness.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's declaration that the Act was inoperative and void for uncertainty. The court remanded counsel's rate increase action against the commission, its members, and the electric company to the trial court for consideration of any other issues raised by the electric company on its counterclaim.

OVERVIEW: After he was convicted by the recorder's court of public drunkenness and disorderly conduct, petitioner was sentenced to 60 days in jail. OUTCOME: The court granted the petition for certiorari, reversed, and directed that petitioner was entitled to a hearing on whether he had been denied his constitutional right to counsel.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Contempt; constitutional question. Cherokee Superior Court. Before Judge Pope.



DISPOSITION: Judgment vacated with direction.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant witness sought review of a judgment of the Cherokee Superior Court (Georgia), which held him in contempt of court pursuant to Georgia's Immunity From Prosecution Statute, Ga. Code Ann. §§ 38-1715, 38-1716.


OVERVIEW: The trial court entered an order requiring the witness to give testimony in a criminal case in which he was allegedly involved. Paragraph 3 of the order directed that the witness "not be required to produce evidence that could be used in any other courts against himself." The witness, on advice of counsel, refused to testify as ordered on the ground that the trial court's order did not provide him with "use and derivative use" immunity. The court vacated the judgment of the trial court holding the witness in contempt. The court determined that it was the intention of the legislature to grant "use and derivative use" immunity under the statute. The court found that the compelled evidence or any other evidence derived from the compelled evidence could not be used against the witness in proceedings in any other forum. The court concluded that with such an interpretation placed upon the statute, it was not unconstitutional as violative of the Fifth Amendment as contended.


OUTCOME: The court vacated the judgment and remanded with direction for deletion of paragraph 3 of the trial court's order and for proceedings consistent with the court's opinion.
