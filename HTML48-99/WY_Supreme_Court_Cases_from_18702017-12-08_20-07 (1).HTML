OVERVIEW: Defendant contended that the trial court erred in denying his motion for a sentence reduction and that his sentence was void because it did not comply with the 30-day notice requirements of Wyo. P. 33 did not extend to the mere fixing of an execution date after a stay.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the denial of sentence reduction and stayed execution pending outcome of defendant's petition for certiorari in the U.S. Supreme Court.

OVERVIEW: The parties owned adjoining ranches. Plaintiffs had a right of way across defendants' lands. DISPOSITION: Consistently with our admonition in State ex rel. Pape v. Hockett (1944), 61 Wyo. 145, 168, 156 P.2d 299, 307, that "public office should not be vacated by judicial decree unless the disqualification be clear and imperative", we hold that the disqualification is that and that the judgment of the district court must be affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a former board member, challenged a decision of the District Court of Park County (Wyoming), which granted the motion for summary judgment by respondents, other board members, and ousted the former board member from office as a member of the board of trustees of the county school district.


OVERVIEW: The former board member was a contract teacher in the district when he ran for, was elected, and qualified as a member of the board of trustees. Claiming that such service as a member of the board was incompatible and inconsistent with the former board member's service as a teacher in the school district, the other board members filed a complaint in quo warranto. Affirming the district court, the court held that the former board member was properly ousted. The court found that there was no constitutionally protected right to hold incompatible offices or employments. Where a personal interest came in conflict with a compelling state policy or interest, the person's right had to be considered subordinated to the public interest. Application of the rule against incompatibility was not an unreasonable interference with any of the former board member's political rights. The court held that employment as teacher and office as member of the board of trustees of the school district were incompatible within the meaning and intent of the common-law rule. There was a conflict that constituted an incompatibility that was not in the best interest of the school district.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the district court.
