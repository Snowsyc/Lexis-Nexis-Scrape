395 So. 2d 1258; 1981 Fla. App. LEXIS 18963



March 27, 1981 


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the Circuit Court for Polk County; William A. Norris, Jr., Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of the judgment of the Circuit Court for Polk County (Florida), which convicted him on a charge of manufacturing cannabis. Appellant argued that the trial court erred when it denied his motion to suppress the marijuana evidence.


OVERVIEW: After his motion to suppress was denied by the court below, appellant entered a plea of nolo contendere to a charge of manufacturing cannabis. The facts showed that two uniformed sheriff's deputies went to appellant's home to talk about misdemeanors that had previously occurred. One deputy went into the fenced backyard and there observed (1) marijuana plants growing near a shed and (2) other marijuana plants growing in pots inside the house in the dining room window. The other deputy went to the front door, knocked, and was invited to enter. Once inside, he saw the plants on the windowsill and recognized them as marijuana. All the plants were then seized. The lower court's judgment and sentence were affirmed on appeal, even though the activities of the first deputy may have been unconstitutional, because the first officer's actions in no way altered the fact that the plants inside the house were in plain view of the deputy who was lawfully invited to enter. The court noted that even if the plants taken from the backyard should have been suppressed, that any error in that regard was harmless because that evidence was merely cumulative and clearly unnecessary to the judgment.


OUTCOME: Appellant's conviction for manufacturing cannabis was affirmed. Even though the court found that it was possible that the court below should have suppressed marijuana evidence obtained by a police officer from appellant's back yard, the court found that any error in that regard was harmless because that evidence was merely cumulative to plain view evidence that another officer seized following his lawful invitation into appellant's home.

When the mediation panel met to consider the cause, appellants refused to offer any evidence before the panel. Appellants asserted that a full hearing would be wasteful, because they intended to file an action in a state court regardless of the outcome of the mediation proceedings. The panel found that appellees were not actionably negligent. The court below dismissed a subsequent medical malpractice action filed by appellants against appellees because it found that appellants' failure to offer any evidence at the mediation proceeding constituted non-performance of a condition precedent to the right to sue. The lower court's judgment was reversed and remanded based on the court's determination that under Fla. Stat. ch.768.44, a plaintiff, such as appellants, did not have to present evidence in support of his claim at the required mediation hearing as a condition precedent to his right to sue.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the court below, which dismissed a medical malpractice action appellant parent's had filed against appellee hospital and appellee doctor because of appellants' failure to offer evidence at a mediation proceeding, was reversed and remanded because the statute that required mediation did not require appellants to present evidence at the mediation proceeding in support of their claim as a condition precedent to their right to sue.

Harrell v. State, Dep't of Health & Rehabilitative Servs., Div. of Soc. & Econ. Servs.


No. 78-33


Court of Appeal of Florida, Fourth District


361 So. 2d 715; 1978 Fla. App. LEXIS 14959



February 14, 1978 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner indigents filed a motion to compel respondent, Department of Health and Rehabilitative services (Florida), to furnish a copy of the certified transcript of proceedings before respondent to petitioners' legal representative, and to file the original with the court, at no cost to petitioners. Petitioners argued that the right of indigents to no-cost transcripts was secured under state law and the state and federal constitutions.


OVERVIEW: Indigent petitioners filed a motion to compel respondent, the department of health and rehabilitative services, to furnish a copy of a transcript of proceedings before respondent to petitioners' counsel and to file the original with the court, at no cost to petitioners. The court denied the motion. The court did not have the statutory authority to order respondent to furnish a transcript at its own expense. Fla. Stat. ch. 120.57(1)(b)(6) (statute) required an agency to make a transcript available at no more than actual cost, with no exception for an indigent. The statute as applied to indigents was constitutional because it bore a rational relationship to the purpose of offsetting agency expenses. Fla. Const. Art. I, § 21, regarding open access to courts, was never intended to create new causes of action. Section 21 only applied if access to the courts for redress of a particular injury was provided by statutory law predating the adoption of the Declaration of Rights in the Florida Constitution or where such right became a part of the common law of the state pursuant to Fla. Stat. ch. 2.01.


OUTCOME: The court denied petitioner indigents' motion to compel respondent department of health and rehabilitative services to furnish a free transcript of proceedings before respondent. The court concluded that it was not authorized under relevant statutes to issue such an order and that the state and federal constitutions did not impair the validity of the statute requiring petitioners to pay for a transcript.
