PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal of the trial court's declaratory judgment invalidating a City of East Chicago ordinance which contained a residency requirement for members of the city fire force.

From the Lake Superior Court, Room No. 3, James Danikolas, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, the City of East Chicago, sought review of a judgment of the Lake Superior Court (Indiana) holding that East Chicago, Ind., Ordinance No. 1-2123 (14-36), which contained a residency requirement for members of the city fire force, was unconstitutional because it was superseded and preempted by state law and granting appellee firefighters' request for an injunction on the enforcement thereof.


OVERVIEW: In granting the relief requested by the firefighters, the trial court found that the ordinance was unconstitutional on its face and as applied due to the enactment of Ind. Code § 19-1-2-1 (1971), which contained specific residence requirements for municipal firemen, and Ind. Code § 19-1-37.5-10 (1971), which listed the criteria the City was required to follow in promoting firemen and did not include residency. The court held that the trial court acted properly in invalidating East Chicago, Ind., Ordinance No. 1-2123 (14-36) and in enjoining its enforcement because: (1) a city could not impose regulations that were in conflict with rights granted or reserved by the General Assembly; (2) such a conflict existed when an ordinance sought to prohibit that which a statute expressly permitted; (3) the power to regulate the residence of municipal firemen vested in the State when it enacted a statute setting forth specific residence requirements; and (4) Ind. Code § 19-1-2-1 (1971), which expressly permitted firemen employed by the City to live within 15 miles thereof, could not be read to permit the City to apply a more stringent residence requirement.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant company, the owner of a peninsula that extended into a lake, sought review of the judgment of Noble Circuit Court (Indiana), which found for appellee individual, the owner of the only unsubmerged land adjoining the peninsula, in the company's suit to acquire an easement over the individual's land. The peninsula was formed in 1838 when the state dammed a river. The company based its claim to an easement on Indiana Code § 32-5-3-1.


OVERVIEW: Ind. Code § 32-5-3-1 provided that where, heretofore or hereafter, a landowner's lands were shut off from a public highway by the state's erection of a dam, if the landowner could not secure an easement over adjacent lands, he was to have a right of easement by necessity. The trial court held that the relief provided by this statute was available only to those who held title to affected land at the time it was deprived of access by the state, and that to construe the statute to allow a taking by a subsequent owner would sanction a taking for a private purpose in violation of the Indiana Constitution. The trial court found the company sought the easement for a private use. The court affirmed the trial court. The company wished easy access to its land. That was not sufficient to establish a public purpose. Although the state had a duty to compensate those whom it deprived of access, that duty ran to the landowner at the time of the taking, not to each subsequent owner.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
