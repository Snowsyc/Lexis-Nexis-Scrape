PRIOR HISTORY: From the Third Circuit Court of Davidson County, Honorable Matthew J. Sweeney, III, Judge.



DISPOSITION: VACATED IN PART, AFFIRMED AND REMANDED


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff child filed a complaint alleging that defendants, physician and others, were guilty of negligence in the prenatal care of his mother and that, as a proximate result, he suffered serious and permanent injuries. The Third Circuit Court of Davidson County (Tennessee) denied the physician's motion for an order precluding expert testimony and held that Tenn. Code Ann. § 29-26-115(b) did not apply to causation experts. The physician appealed.


OVERVIEW: In response to interrogatories, the child notified defendants that he expected to call two expert witnesses from New York and Washington D.C. to testify as to the causation of the child's injuries. The physician contended that the testimony should be excluded because the experts did not meet the geographical limitations of Tenn. Code Ann. § 29-26-115(b) and the child contended that § 29-26-115 did not apply to causation experts. The court reversed and remanded in part and affirmed in part. The court held that under § 29-26-115 the child had the burden of proving all three elements of his malpractice cause of action specifically set out in § 29-16-115(a) and that the evidence to establish each of these elements, including causation, had to come from witnesses meeting the geographical limitations of § 29-16-115(b). Thus, the trial court erred in ruling that § 29-26-115 did not apply to causation experts. However, it did not follow that the testimony of the child's experts should be excluded because nothing in § 29-26-115 prohibited the use of relevant medical expert testimony.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was vacated to the extent that it held that the statutory geographic limitations for expert witnesses was not applicable to a causation expert, but the order denying the physician's motion to exclude the child's expert testimony was affirmed.

The court stated that because the tax was an ad valorem tax measured by the value of property, and was not a privilege tax on the company's right to conduct an interstate business, it was not proscribed by the commerce clause and did not violate the due process clauses of the Federal and State Constitutions. Further, the company received the benefit of Tennessee's laws, its roads, and its other facilities thus enabling it to carry on its business in Tennessee. Although the court could not determine a fixed situs for those properties at any specific place, nevertheless, the property so used in Tennessee was properly deemed to have a situs in Tennessee.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
