PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State sought review of a judgment from the Huron County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio), which held Ohio Rev. The trial court noted that the nearly identical vehicular manslaughter statute, Ohio Rev. OVERVIEW: After unintentionally running a stop sign, defendant hit another car and killed the other driver. Thereafter, defendant was charged with involuntary manslaughter under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2903.04(B), and in dismissing the indictment, the trial court held § 2903.04(B) violated defendant's equal protection rights. In so holding, the trial court noted that: (1) § 2903.04(B) imposed a harsher penalty than the nearly identical vehicular manslaughter statute, Ohio Rev. McKenzie v. Registrar


CASE NO. CA95-01-005


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, TWELFTH APPELLATE DISTRICT, CLERMONT COUNTY


1996 Ohio App. LEXIS 854



March 5, 1996, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The court sua sponte reconsidered its prior unreported decision interpreting Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4507.169 in pari materia with Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4507.60 to allow plaintiff driver to petition for occupational driving privileges under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4507.169. Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4507.60 enacted the Driver's License Compact in Ohio, and the court's decision was based upon the mistaken belief that Kentucky was also a Compact state.


OVERVIEW: The driver was convicted in Kentucky for driving under the influence of alcohol (DUI). Because Kentucky was not in fact a member of the Driver's License Compact, the court held that its previous decision improperly applied Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4507.60 in this case. The driver had initiated a complaint below to enjoin imposition of his administrative license suspension in Ohio based on his Kentucky DUI conviction. The trial court held that Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4507.169 violated the Equal Protection Clause, concluding that there was no rational basis for providing occupational driving privileges to Ohio residents who had been cited for DUI in Compact states, while not providing Ohio residents cited in non-Compact states with the same opportunity. The State failed to point out any rational basis to the court for treating an Ohio resident convicted of DUI in a state not a member of the Compact any differently from an Ohio resident convicted of DUI in Ohio or in another Compact state. The court concluded that § 4507.169 was unconstitutional as applied to the driver in this case because it could not be interpreted so as to allow him to petition for occupational driving privileges.


OUTCOME: This court remanded the cause to the trial court. If the driver had yet to serve or was currently serving an administrative license suspension, the trial court was instructed to permanently vacate the suspension.
