Court of Appeal of Florida, First District


570 So. 2d 377; 1990 Fla. App. LEXIS 8763; 15 Fla. L. Weekly D 2805



November 15, 1990, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:   [**1] An Appeal from the Circuit Court for Escambia County. Lacey Collier, Judge.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED in part, and REVERSED and REMANDED in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed his criminal conviction and sentence from the Circuit Court, Escambia County (Florida). The trial court sentenced defendant as an habitual offender under Fla. Stat. ch. 775.084(4) (1988) without making any finding that it was necessary for public protection and without giving defendant notice. The trial court also ordered defendant to make restitution as determined by probation and parole.


OVERVIEW: Defendant appealed his conviction and sentence, raising five issues. The court affirmed on five of the four issues. First, the court held that defendant failed to preserve for appeal the issue of the sufficiency of the evidence because defendant's motion for judgment of acquittal made below was deficient. Second, Fla. Stat. ch. 775.084 (1988) was not unconstitutional on the arguments raised in defendant's brief. Third, the trial court did not err in sentencing defendant as a habitual felony offender without making certain findings or providing notice to defendant. The court held that under ch. 775.084, a court that sentenced a defendant as an habitual offender was neither required to make any finding that it was necessary for public protection nor required to give a defendant notice pending the sentencing. Fourth, the court held that there was no requirement to give written reasons for imposing a guideline departure sentence once it was determined that defendant fit the definition of an habitual offender. On the fifth issue, the court reversed the restitution imposed on defendant because the amount had to be set by a court and not a probation or parole officer.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction and sentence except that it reversed that part of the sentence that ordered restitution as determined by a probation and parole officer, because the amount of restitution had to be determined by the trial court. The court held that the habitual offender statute did not require a finding of necessity of a sentence for public protection or notice of intention to seek habitual offender sentencing.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of the order of the Circuit Court for Hillsborough County (Florida), which denied appellant's motion for post-conviction relief. The case had been remanded because the trial court imposed a guideline sentence without an affirmative request from appellant; and after remand, the trial court deleted any reference to sentencing guidelines but did not alter the length of the six sentences themselves.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, the court held that because six of the nine offenses had occurred prior to October 1, 1983, it was error to impose a guideline sentence without an affirmative request from appellant. After remand, the trial court deleted any reference to sentencing guidelines but did not alter the length of the six sentences. Appellant was therefore entitled to correction of these sentences. Also, the court concluded that should appellant opt for a guideline sentence, and the trial court was considering another upward departure, the trial court should look only to the reasons originally provided and could not craft new reasons. If subsequent case law invalidated any of those reasons, they were no longer to be considered. However, if any one of the departure criteria remained valid, the court could again impose a departure sentence.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed in part and reversed in part the order of the trial court, which denied to re-sentence appellant. It was improper for the trial court to impose a guideline sentence without an affirmative request from appellant; also, if appellant opted for a guideline sentence and the trial court was to consider another upward departure the trial court could not craft new reasons to support the departure.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a decision of the Circuit Court for Polk County (Florida), which applied the habitual offender statute Fla. OVERVIEW: Defendant appealed from the sentence imposed under the habitual offender statute Fla. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the sentence imposed under the habitual offender statute, because there was no arbitrary application of the habitual offender criteria to violate equal protection and the judge's discretion to determine that defendant was not a danger to society, did not shift any burden to defendant.

OVERVIEW: Appellant, the prosecution in a criminal case, appealed an order granting appellee's motion for a mitigated sentence. Appellant contended that the trial court erred in reducing appellee's sentence because the record did not show that appellant's refusal to move for a sentence reduction pursuant to Fla. Appellee cross-appealed, arguing that his sentence should have been reduced or mitigated to the same sentence as that imposed on a codefendant. The court found no merit in appellee's argument. However, the court agreed with appellant's contention. Specifically, the court found that appellant had not acted in bad faith or overreached by refusing to move for a mitigated sentence for appellee while seeking such a sentence for appellee's codefendant. OUTCOME: The order mitigating appellee's sentence was reversed, and the cause was remanded. PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant corporations sought review of an order of the Circuit Court for Indian River County (Florida), entering a final summary judgment in favor of appellee city upholding the validity of an amendment to the town charter concerning beach restoration.


OVERVIEW: Appellant corporations challenged an amendment to a town charter concerning beach restoration implemented by appellee city. The trial court granted summary judgment in favor of appellee and upheld the validity of the amendment. Appellant challenged the order, and the court affirmed the summary judgment. The court found that the amendment did not interfere or conflict with activities that were exclusively within the state's power, but instead spoke to activities that were exclusively or concurrently within appellee's power, and therefore the amendment was not preempted by state laws regarding beach restoration. The court further held that the overbreadth doctrine was not applicable because appellant's U.S. Const. amend. I rights were not in jeopardy and that the amendment was not vague when the ordinary meaning and common understanding of the terms were applied.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the circuit court's grant of summary judgment in favor of appellee city, upholding an amendment to the town charter in appellant corporations' challenge of the amendment because the amendment was not preempted by state law, nor was it impermissibly vague or overbroad.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant extradition challenger sought review of a decision by the Circuit Court, Leon County (Florida), which dismissed his complaint, holding that no justiciable controversy existed in his constitutional challenge of Fla. Stat. ch. 856.04 (1987) while his habeas corpus petition was pending.


OVERVIEW: Appellant extradition challenger was charged under Fla. Stat. ch. 856.04 (1987), which subjected all males who willfully withheld support from their wives or children to punishment as third degree felons. While resisting extradition from North Carolina, he brought a complaint in Florida, arguing that the statute was unconstitutional. The trial court ruled that there was no justiciable controversy because appellant's petition for habeas corpus was pending. During the pendency of the appeal, the petition for habeas was granted. The appellate court reversed the dismissal of appellant's statutory challenge and remanded, holding that the existence of an outstanding arrest warrant made appellant a proper party to challenge the constitutionality of the statute he had been charged with violating. An actual arrest was not necessary in order to sharpen the dispute.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded the decision of the trial court dismissing appellant extradition challenger's complaint arguing that the statute he had been charged with violating was unconstitutional. The court found that the existence of an outstanding arrest warrant made him a proper party to challenge the statute, and that appellant's actual arrest was not necessary in order to sharpen the dispute.

Glisson v. Alachua County


Case No. 88-2111


Court of Appeal of Florida, First District


558 So. 2d 1030; 1990 Fla. App. LEXIS 227; 15 Fla. L. Weekly D 187



January 12, 1990, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Review Denied October 5, 1990, Reported at 1990 Fla. LEXIS 1256. Rehearing Denied April 18, 1990.


PRIOR HISTORY: An Appeal from the Circuit Court for Alachua County. Chester B. Chance, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant landowners sought review of a final judgment of the Circuit Court, Alachua County (Florida), which granted appellee county's motions for partial summary judgment and involuntary dismissal of appellants' complaint, which challenged a land use amendment to appellee's comprehensive plan.


OVERVIEW: Appellant landowners owned real property in an environmentally sensitive area of appellee county. Appellee enacted land use regulations that restricted appellants' use of their property, by amending the appellee's comprehensive plan (plan). The amendments were challenged as beyond the police powers of appellee, or, as an enactment of those powers that required compensation. The trial court granted partial summary judgment for appellee, and dismissed part of the complaint, finding that the challenge was mooted by a subsequent amendment to the plan. On review, the court affirmed, holding that appellants' challenge to the plan was facial in nature, and further amendment to the plan made the challenge moot. Because no individual appellant had applied for or been denied a permit under the amendment, the amendment also allowed appellants most existing uses of their property. As appellants could not demonstrate that they had been denied all or most of the beneficial uses of the property, no compensable taking had occurred. Finally, the court held that the plan and its amendments properly addressed conservation concerns and were a proper use of appellee's police powers.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's grant of partial summary judgment and involuntary dismissal of appellants' claims, holding that the challenged land use amendments and regulations of appellee county's comprehensive plan were a valid exercise of appellee's police power, and did not, on their face, constitute a compensable taking. The court held that appellants failed to show that they were denied all or most beneficial uses of their property.
