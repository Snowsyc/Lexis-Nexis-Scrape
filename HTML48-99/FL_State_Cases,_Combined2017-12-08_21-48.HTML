Finally, the statute was not constitutionally deficient, as its title sufficiently gave notice of its contents for purposes of Fla. Const. art. III, § 6.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed upholding the constitutionality of statute charging appellant with producing, and procuring a minor to perform in, obscene photos, as statute was not void for vagueness, as information filed by appellee state sufficiently described the photos, as appellant lacked standing to attack for overbreadth a statute proscribing conduct related to minors, and as law's title gave sufficient notice of its contents.

Construing § 925.036 to allow stacking, the circuit court awarded appellees the requested compensation and held the statute to be unconstitutional. OUTCOME: The court affirmed that part of the circuit court's decision holding that the fee limits regarding compensation of court-appointed counsel could be stacked and awarding appellees, attorneys, the requested compensation, since statutory construction required the award of reasonable attorneys' fees; however, the court reversed that part of the order finding the statute unconstitutional, since that ruling was unnecessary.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a correctional officer at a penal institution, sought review of a judgment of the Circuit Court in and for Polk County (Florida), which convicted appellant of introducing contraband into a correctional institution in violation of Fla. Stat. ch. 944.47 (1978), and of possession of marijuana in violation of Fla. Stat. ch. 893.13 (1977).


OVERVIEW: Appellant, a correctional officer convicted of introducing marijuana into a penal institution in violation of Fla. Stat. ch. 944.47 (1978), argued that § 944.47 was constitutionally vague and overbroad. The court disagreed, noting first that the contraband was detected during a pat-down search of officers entering the facility. Explaining, the court found the illegal conduct proscribed in understandable terms and that no selective enforcement was shown to result from "overbreadth." Nor was the statute an unreasonable delegation of legislative power to the executive branch, the court added, as direct legislative supervision of prison administrators was impractical and as the administrator kept within prescribed guidelines in keeping marijuana out of the prison. Moreover, no error occurred by not suppressing the evidence recovered during the search, as the inspection -- at the functional equivalent of an entry "border" -- was reasonable, providing minimal intrusion of appellant's rights as compared to the state's interest in keeping the prison drug free. Appellant effectively consented to the search by being alerted to periodic search procedures by the rule book provided to him.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed convicting appellant, a correctional officer, of introducing marijuana into a penal institution, because statutory prohibition was not constitutionally vague, and because drug evidence was not suppressible, as pat-down search of officers entering facility reasonably promoted state's interest in keeping prison drug free and only minimally intruded on appellant's expectation of privacy guaranteed by the Fourth Amendment.
