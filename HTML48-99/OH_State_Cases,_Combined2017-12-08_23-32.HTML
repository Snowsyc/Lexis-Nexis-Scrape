PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL: Court of Appeals for Seneca County



DISPOSITION: Judgment Reversed 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant mother sought review of a judgment from the Juvenile Court of Seneca County (Ohio), ordering the mother's child, by a previous order having been found a dependent child, to be placed in the permanent legal care and custody of the Seneca County Welfare Department and divesting the mother and father of all parental rights and privileges.


OVERVIEW: The mother argued that the complaint violated Ohio Rev. Code §§ 2151.27, 2151.23, 2151.04 and Ohio R. Juv. P. 10, the trial court did not appoint a guardian ad litem, grandparents were not summoned, the entire procedure was unconstitutionally vague, the trial court improperly accepted evidence, and there was insufficient evidence to support the finding of dependency. The court reversed based on an error in jurisdiction. The trial court initially acquired jurisdiction in a proceeding filed for and resulting in a temporary change of custody and retained jurisdiction. Later, a motion for a change of permanent custody was filed that failed to sufficiently allege that the infant was in fact a dependent child. Under Ohio Rev. Code § 2151.353, the filing of a complaint containing a prayer requesting permanent custody, sufficiently apprising the parents of the grounds, and the service of summons upon the parents explaining permanent divestiture of parental rights were prerequisite to a valid adjudication that a child was neglected or dependent for the purpose of obtaining an order for permanent custody divesting parental rights. The remaining assertions of error lacked merit.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded the judgment.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL FROM Common Pleas COURT, No. 878,708



DISPOSITION: Judgment Affirmed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, a reversionary heir, brought an action to quiet title against defendants, a city, the city's grantee, and other reversionary heirs. Defendant heirs filed a cross-petition in ejectment and for damages against the grantee. A common pleas court (Ohio) entered judgment for the city under the Ohio Marketable Title Act and against defendant heirs on their cross-petition. Plaintiff and defendant heirs appealed.


OVERVIEW: The grantors conveyed property to a village, stipulating that the property was to be used only for public purposes; otherwise it would revert to the grantors or their heirs. The village was annexed by a city. The annexation instrument provided that the property was to be used for public purposes, but no mention was made of the possibilities of reverter. Over 40 years later, the city conveyed the property to the grantee, who used the property for private business ventures. The court held that the reversionary interests of plaintiff and defendant heirs were extinguished by the Act. The annexation constituted root of title under Ohio Rev Code Ann. § 5301.47(E) and (F) because it was the most recent transaction affecting title. Because the root of tile was determined over 40 years earlier by the annexation, plaintiff and defendant heirs were required by Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 5301.49(A) to file a preserving notice to preserve their reversionary interests. They failed to do so. The Act was not unconstitutional. It was rationally related to the legitimate state interest of simplifying land transactions, and persons with future interests were able to preserve their interests.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment that was in favor of the city on plaintiff's claim and against defendant heirs on their cross-petition.

State v. Manly

1978 Ohio App. LEXIS 9585

PURSUANT TO RULE 2(G) OF THE OHIO SUPREME COURT RULES FOR THE REPORTING OF OPINIONS, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS MAY BE CITED SUBJECT TO CERTAIN RESTRAINTS, LIMITATIONS, AND EXCEPTIONS.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] APPEAL FROM Juvenile COURT, No. 312,744


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant attorney challenged a decision from the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas, Juvenile Division (Ohio), which, after the attorney obtained a writ of mandamus against the juvenile court judge to order prepaid blood tests for an indigent putative father in paternity proceedings, held the attorney in contempt for alleging, in his application for the writ, that the juvenile court judge "threatened" the attorney in pretrial hearings.


OVERVIEW: The attorney represented an indigent putative father in paternity proceedings. He requested that the costs of a blood test be prepaid by the State, which motion was denied by the trial court. The attorney then filed a petition for a writ of mandamus, which was granted. As part of his petition, the attorney alleged that the juvenile court judge "threatened" that, if the paternity case was not settled and if the indigent client was found guilty at trial, then the client would be assessed significant monies; and, if they were not immediately paid, he would be incarcerated. The court reversed the judgment and vacated the citation of contempt. It was impossible to determine conclusively and beyond a reasonable doubt that the juvenile court judge did not threaten the attorney, and it was clear to the court that the use of the word "threaten" in the mandamus petition was descriptive in nature, intending to aid the attorney in establishing the primary allegation of the petition. Further, attorneys had a right, in the legitimate pursuant of justice, to urge all matters of fact and law in support of their position, so long as it was not done for an illegitimate or improper purpose.


OUTCOME: The judgment was reversed, and the court voided the conviction of contempt entered against the attorney.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, the Ohio Ethics Commission and its officials, appealed the decision from the Court of Common Pleas, Franklin County, Ohio, which entered judgment for plaintiff lawyers, who were also professors, in their declaratory judgment action challenging the advisory opinion by the Commission applying Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 102.04(A) to the lawyers. The lawyers contended that Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 102.01(B) made the other statute inapplicable.


OVERVIEW: Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 102.04(A) barred state employees from receiving compensation except for payment from the employing agency in matters before state agencies and departments. The opinion applied the statute to the lawyers. The trial court entered judgment for the lawyers by way of granting a motion for judgment on the pleadings. On appeal, the court agreed with the decision. The lawyers were exempted from the statute by § 102.01(B), which specifically exempted educators who did not perform administrative or supervisory functions, from the definition of "public official or employee" subject to the former statute. Further, one lawyer was also excluded because he was on an unpaid leave of absence. Thus, the lawyers could have obtained payment when working for clients.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment on the pleadings in favor of the lawyers.

59 Ohio App. 2d 31; 392 N.E.2d 574; 1978 Ohio App. LEXIS 7579; 13 Ohio Op. 3d 99

PRIOR HISTORY: APPEAL: Court of Appeals for Lucas County.



DISPOSITION: Judgment reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant building owner sought review of a decision of the Common Pleas Court of Lucas County (Ohio), which affirmed the order and final decision of appellee zoning board of appeals (board) that denied the building owner approval for the reconstruction of a building damaged by fire under Jerusalem Township, Ohio, Zoning Resolution §§ 7.5-1, 7.5-2 (1964).


OVERVIEW: The building had been used to store fireworks, a use allowed as a continuance of use under the township's zoning ordinance. The board denied approval for reconstruction on grounds that the building and its use did not meet present zoning requirements. On appeal, the court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the case. Prior to adoption of § 7.5-1, the owner's use of the building as a fireworks warehouse was not prohibited by law and was a legal non-conforming use under § 7.5-1. Jerusalem Township, Ohio, Zoning Resolution § 7.5-5 (1964) limited reconstruction of non-conforming buildings when damaged to the extent of more than 75 percent of value at the time of the damage. The owner's building had never been a non-conforming building, and its non-conforming use was allowed under the zoning ordinance. The owner's due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment of the United States Constitution and Ohio Const. art. I, § 16 were violated by the taking of the owner's pre-existing non-conforming use of his property and notice of hearing requirements of Jerusalem Township, Ohio, Zoning Resolution § 8.3-3 (1964) were not met by the board.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court, remanded the case, and ordered the board to issue the building owner a certificate of occupancy for the damaged building.

DISPOSITION:  [*1] Judgment Affirmed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Court of Common Pleas of Portage County (Ohio), which had convicted him of kidnapping, felonious assault, and aggravated burglary. He claimed in part that his conviction had violated double jeopardy and that the kidnapping statute, Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2905.01, had been improperly applied to his case.


OVERVIEW: During defendant's trial, the trial court had dismissed several counts, permitted amendment of the felonious assault charge, and then held further trial before a second jury after a continuance. Defendant argued that it had been error to permit amendment of the indictment after the prosecution had rested its case, that he had been placed in double jeopardy, that the kidnapping statute did not apply, and that it had been improper to refuse to charge on lesser included offenses of aggravated assault, aggravated menacing, and trespassing. The court found that: (1) under Ohio R. Crim. P. 7(D), the amendment of the felonious assault charge had been proper where it had not changed the name or identity of the crime charged; (2) jeopardy did not attach to the further prosecution after the dismissal of the first jury; (3) it had been proper to refuse to charge on the lesser included offenses where there was no reasonable trier of fact that could find defendant guilty of the included offenses and not guilty of the charges; and (4) the attack on the kidnapping statute's constitutionality was waived as not raised in the trial court.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment convicting defendant of kidnapping, felonious assault, and aggravated burglary.

PURSUANT TO RULE 2(G) OF THE OHIO SUPREME COURT RULES FOR THE REPORTING OF OPINIONS, UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS MAY BE CITED SUBJECT TO CERTAIN RESTRAINTS, LIMITATIONS, AND EXCEPTIONS.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff injured husband filed a claim for personal injury against defendant State of Ohio. Plaintiff wife filed a claim for loss of services and consortium. The Court of Claims of Ohio dismissed plaintiffs' complaints under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2743.16, finding that plaintiffs failed to timely file a notice of intent to file a claim.


OVERVIEW: The husband suffered injury to his arm and shoulder when he nearly fell in an interior stairwell of a State-owned building. Neither the husband nor the wife filed a notice of intent to file a claim against the State within 180 days under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 2743.16; however, their claims were filed within two years. The claims court gave the husband and the wife an opportunity to prove they had good cause for failing to file the notice; however, no good cause was shown. On appeal, the court concluded that § 2743.16 applied to the husband's claim. The husband's failure to show good cause warranted dismissal of his complaint. The court ruled, however, that § 2743.16 did not apply to the wife's claim and that a four-year statute of limitations applied to the wife's complaint. Because the wife was not required to file the notice and because her complaint was filed timely, the claims court erred in dismissing the wife's complaint. The court rejected argument of the husband and the wife that § 2743.16 was unconstitutional because there was no discrimination in the constitutional sense. The classifications in § 2743.16 were reasonable and did not result in a denial of equal protection.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the claims court's judgment as to the husband, but reversed the claims court's judgment as to the wife and remanded the action for further proceedings.
