170 Vt. 194; 744 A.2d 864; 1999 Vt. LEXIS 406



December 20, 1999, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] As Revised January 31, 2000.


PRIOR HISTORY: APPEALED FROM: Chittenden Superior Court. DOCKET NO. 1009-97CnC. Linda Levitt, J.



DISPOSITION:  The court reversed the trial court's judgment and retained jurisdiction pending legislative action because defendant State was constitutionally required to extend to same-sex couples the common benefits and protections that flowed from marriage under Vermont law.



CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, three same-sex couples, appealed the Chittenden Superior Court's (Vermont) denial of their cross-motion for judgment on the pleadings and the granting of the motion to dismiss filed by defendants, the State of Vermont and towns, in a suit involving defendant town's refusal to grant marriage licenses to plaintiffs.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff, three same-sex couples, were denied marriage licenses from their respective town clerks. Plaintiffs brought suit against defendants to compel the issuance of the licenses. The trial court denied plaintiffs' cross-motion for judgment on the pleadings and granted defendants' motions to dismiss. Plaintiffs appealed. The court reversed the trial court's judgment and ruled that under the Common Benefits Clause of the Vermont Constitution, Vt. Const. art. VII, ch. 1, defendant State was constitutionally required to extend to same-sex couples the common benefits and protections that flowed from marriage under Vermont law. The court stated that it did not rule that plaintiffs were entitled to the marriage licenses, but that they were entitled to the same benefits and protections afforded opposite-sex, married couples. The court retained jurisdiction to give the legislature a reasonable time to remedy the constitutional imbalance.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment and retained jurisdiction pending legislative action because defendant State was constitutionally required to extend to same-sex couples the common benefits and protections that flowed from marriage under Vermont law.

OVERVIEW: Plaintiff former dog owners lost their dog, which was found loose, without tags; dog was delivered to animal control, which placed notices throughout town, in accordance with the local ordinance, and then placed dog with defendant animal league, which adopted dog out to defendant present dog owners. This Opinion Substituted on Denial of Reargument for Withdrawn Opinion of October 11, 1996, Previously Reported at: 1996 Vt. LEXIS 98.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant State sought review of a decision by the Vermont Labor Relations Board, which granted reinstatement with back pay to appellee employee after she was dismissed as a state employee. The Board found that the employee's dismissal violated her constitutional right to due process of law and a contract right under a collective bargaining agreement because she was not given explicit notice that she might be discharged.


OVERVIEW: The employee's primary duty as the most senior compliance officer for the Department of Employment and Training (DET) was to collect delinquent unemployment tax contributions. She was also an officer with her husband in a corporation that operated an automobile repair shop. She was discharged after the corporation was assigned to her for collection purposes and she failed to report the assignment, a violation of DET's conflicts of interest policy. The State challenged the Board's holding that the employee was entitled to specific notice that dismissal was contemplated. Reversing the Board's decision, the court held that, under Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, the employee's knowledge that her behavior was prohibited, coupled with notice of the charges, an opportunity to respond, and a hearing, satisfied the requirements of due process. The court also found that the labor contract did not require specific notice of contemplated dismissal. The court reinstated the dismissal because the Board could only impose a lesser discipline if it found that there was no just cause for that imposed by the DET and because the evidence and the Board's findings clearly showed just cause.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the Board's decision.

139 Vt. 37; 422 A.2d 255; 1980 Vt. LEXIS 1391



September 9, 1980, Opinion filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Department of Taxes appealed order reversing its determination that use taxes were owed. Bennington Superior Court, Morrissey, J., presiding.



DISPOSITION: The judgment of the Bennington Superior Court is vacated. The September 20, 1976, assessment of the Commissioner of Taxes is reinstated. To be certified to the Bennington Superior Court and to the Commissioner of Taxes.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Bennington Superior Court (Vermont) reversed a determination of appellant Department of Taxes assessing sales taxes against appellee taxpayer. The department challenged the judgment.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayer published a weekly publication that was mainly devoted to advertisements. The department determined that the publication was not exempt from sales taxes and imposed taxes on the ink, newsprint, and other supplies used in the publication. The court vacated the judgment of the lower court and reinstated the department's determination that the publication was subject to taxes. The court found that the publication was a newspaper because it was both periodic and of public interest. However, the court rejected the taxpayer's argument that the newspaper publication process was exempt from the sales and use tax. 32 Vt. Stat. Ann. § 9741(14) provided for an exemption from taxes for tangible personal property which became an ingredient of tangible personal property for later sale. Thus, the exemption was conditioned on the ultimate sale of the product made from the goods sought to be exempted. The taxpayer did not sell its publication. Therefore the sale to the taxpayer of the ink and other supplies was the ultimate retail sale. Imposition of the tax was rationally related to the purpose of the sales tax in limiting multiple taxation.


OUTCOME: The court vacated the judgment and reinstated the assessment.

Catlin v. Town of Hartland


No. 128-79


Supreme Court of Vermont


138 Vt. 1; 409 A.2d 596; 1979 Vt. LEXIS 1090



December 4, 1979, Opinion filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Town appealed reclassification of Class 4 road as Class 3. Windsor Superior Court, Morrissey, J., presiding.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed. To be certified forthwith.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant town sought review of a judgment of the Windsor Superior Court (Vermont), in favor of plaintiffs property owners in an action seeking to reclassify a section of highway from class 4 to class 3.


OVERVIEW: The property owners commenced the instant action to have a section of a highway reclassified from class 4 to class 3. The trial court reclassified the highway as requested. On appeal, the town claimed that the evidence did not support the result, and the road at issue could not be found to be identical to other town highways either classified or maintained as class 3. The court found that the trial court justified its reclassification on both discrimination and the compliance with statutory standards for reclassification, even in the absence of discrimination. The parties stipulated that the road at issue and other roads classified as class 3 had similar characteristics as to grade, ledge, and narrowness. The court found that discrimination in their maintenance was correctly inferred from these similarities and conceded unevenness of treatment.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment in favor of the property owners.

Potter v. Hartford Zoning Bd. of Adjustment


No. 68-79


Supreme Court of Vermont


137 Vt. 445; 407 A.2d 170; 1979 Vt. LEXIS 1010



September 17, 1979, Opinion filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Board appealed from order that landowner be issued a zoning variance. Windsor Superior Court, Morrissey, J., presiding.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant zoning board sought review of a judgment of the Windsor Superior Court (Vermont), which found that the board had failed to comply with the mandate of Vt. Stat. Ann. tit. 24, § 4470(a) and determined that plaintiff applicant was entitled to a zoning variance.


OVERVIEW: After the applicant had started to build a home, it was discovered that the home was only set back 13 feet, instead of the required 25 feet. The applicant sought a zoning variance, which was denied. The trial court found that the board's fact findings contained only conclusions reached by the majority of the board. It therefore ruled that the board had not complied with the mandate of Vt. Stat. Ann. tit. 24, § 4470(a), which stated that the board should render its decision, including findings of fact within 45 days. Section 4470(a) also provided that if the requirements were not met, then the variance was deemed approved. The trial court approved the variance. On appeal, the board contended that the formal error of the conclusory statements should not undermine its decision and that the trial court erred in granting a variance. On appeal, the court held that § 4470(a) required findings of fact, including reasons for the denial of the variance. Because the board failed to provide substantive findings of fact, it failed to comply with the statute. The court further found that it was not necessary for the trial court to approve the variance since it was approved by operation of law.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

Governor Clinton Council v. Koslowski


No. 202-77


Supreme Court of Vermont


137 Vt. 240; 403 A.2d 689; 1979 Vt. LEXIS 974



May 3, 1979, Opinion filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Motion for Enlargement of Time Pursuant to Rule 40 Granted May 15, 1979.


PRIOR HISTORY: Listers and tax collector appealed from order upholding tax exemption claim. Windham Superior Court, Hayes, J., presiding.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part and remanded for entry of an order consistent with the views expressed herein.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, town listers and tax collector, appealed from an order of the Windham Superior Court (Vermont) in favor of plaintiff boy scout council upholding a tax exemption claim with regard to the council's real property in the council's action for declaratory and injunctive relief.


OVERVIEW: After defendants began appraising a tax on timberland and wilderness land owned by the council in the town, the council filed a complaint seeking a declaration that all its land in the town was exempt from taxation pursuant to Vt. Stat. Ann. tit. 32, § 3802(2) and an injunction preventing the levying and collecting of taxes on such property. The trial court entered judgment for the council and defendants appealed. On appeal, the court reversed the judgment in part. The court held that Vt. Const. art. 9 did not abridge the power of the legislature to declare what property should be exempt from taxation. Also, Vt. Stat. Ann. tit. 32, § 3802(2) did not violate the Equal Protection Clause because the classification in § 3802(2) was reasonable. The court found that the council satisfied their burden of showing that the use of the timberland was directly and substantially utilized in furtherance of its organizational purposes. The council demonstrated that it enhanced its ability to provide merit badge programs of an outpost camping nature. However, the council failed to satisfy is burden as to the wilderness because it failed to show its use of the land was substantial.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court judgment's with respect to the wilderness lands and affirmed the trial court's order as to the timberland in the council's action for declaratory and injunctive relief against defendants. However, the court held that the trial court could not grant relief beyond June of 1976.
