Baird v. State


No. 14984


Supreme Court of Utah


574 P.2d 713; 1978 Utah LEXIS 1198



January 9, 1978, Filed


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, State of Utah, State Industrial Commission, Occupational Safety and Health Division, and Utah Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission, appealed from the judgment of a Utah district court, which in a declaratory judgment action brought by plaintiff individual, declared the Utah Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1973 (Act), Utah Code Ann. tit. 35, ch. 9 unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: The court held that the trial court should have dismissed this action on the ground it lacked jurisdiction to render an advisory opinion. The allegations concerning the unconstitutionality of the Act were all pleaded in the abstract. There were no concrete facts pleaded indicating any specific injury sustained or threatened to the individual personally. There were no allegations that he had sustained a particularized injury that set him apart from the public generally and would give him standing to challenge the constitutionality of the Act. While statutes authorizing courts to render declaratory relief were to be liberally construed, a court must, nevertheless, operate within the constitutional and statutory powers and duties imposed upon it. To maintain an action for declaratory relief, a plaintiff must show that the justiciable and jurisdictional elements requisite in ordinary actions were present, for a judgment could be rendered only in a real controversy between adverse parties. The individual in this case failed to meet these conditions.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In appellant city's action for a declaratory judgment, the 2nd Judicial District Court (Utah) determined that, under § 20-4-2, Utah Code Annotated 1943, as amended by Chap. 35, Laws of Utah 1943, as amended by Chap. 26, Laws of Utah 1951, § 104-4-2, there should be two judges on the Ogden City Court and that respondent candidate was lawfully elected a judge of that court. The city appealed.


OVERVIEW: Pursuant to the official 1940 United States Census, the city had a population of 43,688. On May 8, 1951, Chap. 26, Laws of Utah 1951, amending Sec. 20-4-2, U.C.A. 1943, went into effect and provided that, at the 1951 municipal election, cities with a population between 50,000 to 100,000, would have two city judges. On June 17, 1951, the Director of the Census issued his final report for the 1950 census in which the city's population was established as 57,112. On July 31, 1951, the candidate filed his declaration as a candidate for the office of the second city judge. The incumbent city judge filed for re-election for the first division. Other candidates filed for the first division. No one else filed for the second city judge position. The candidate's name was not on the ballot. The city alleged that the next census after the effective date of sec. 20-4-2 was 1960, and that the city had only one judge in 1951. The court held that the city improperly interpreted the legislature's intent. The court affirmed the trial court's finding that the city had two city judges and that the candidate, who was unopposed and received a certificate of election was the second city judge.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

State v. Packard


No. 7773


Supreme Court of Utah


122 Utah 369; 250 P.2d 561; 1952 Utah LEXIS 212; 31 L.R.R.M. 2122; 22 Lab. Cas. (CCH) P67,283



November 17, 1952, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was convicted of failing to register with the Utah Industrial Commission before commencing work for company whose employees were on strike. A Utah district court dismissed the proceedings on the ground that the statutes requiring such registration, §§ 49-1-29 to 49-1-32, U.C.A. 1943, were unconstitutional. The State appealed the dismissal.


OVERVIEW: The subject statutes required a person to register before he commenced work with a company whose employees were on a strike called by a national labor union. Affirming the dismissal, the court held that the statutes were unconstitutional and void because they were vague. The court stated that a statute that either forbade or required the doing of an act in terms so vague that men of common intelligence must necessarily guess at its meaning and differ as to its application violated the first essential of due process of law. The court noted that in regard to a vagueness challenge, the test a statute was required to meet to be valid was that it had to be sufficiently definite (a) to inform persons of ordinary intelligence, who would be law abiding, what their conduct had to be to conform to its requirements; (b) to advise a defendant accused of violating it just what constituted the offense with which he was charged; and (c) to be susceptible of uniform interpretation and application by those charged with responsibility of applying and enforcing it. The subject statues were vague because a person was unable to determine whether he was in fact required to register with the Commission.


OUTCOME: The judgment that dismissed defendant's conviction for failing to register with the Utah Industrial Commission before commencing work was affirmed.
