SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Dissenting Opinion December 3, 1963. Rehearing Denied January 20, 1964.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant river authority brought a proceeding in conformity with La. Rev. Stat. Ann. §§ 19:141-160, to expropriate appellee owners' land. The owners asserted that §§ 141-148 were unconstitutional, null and void, as the legislation authorized a taking of property by ex parte order prior to the payment of just and adequate compensation.The district court (Louisiana) declared the statutes unconstitutional. The authority questioned the judgment.


OVERVIEW: The words "after" as used in La. Const. art. I, § 2 and "previously paid" as used in La. Const. art. IV, § 15 were not meaningless, nor inserted without design. The clear import of their meaning was that payment of just and adequate compensation must precede a taking. Therefore, the legislature was powerless to enact legislation that permitted a taking until payment of such compensation had been made or at least tendered and deposited to the owner's court registry account. Aside from constitutional provisions rendering the legislation invalid, null and void, the very language of La. Const. art. XIV, § 45, which created the authority, provided that it should exercise its eminent domain power as provided for under general law. The general expropriation laws were embodied La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 19:1-14 and La. Civ. Code Ann. arts. 2626-2641. The law which the legislature made applicable to the authority was special in character and the authority's use of it transcended the scope of the constitutional limitations that required the authority to expropriate under the general law. Therefore, the authority could not avail itself of §§ 19:141-19:160, even if they were not otherwise invalid.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the district court was affirmed.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Rehearing Denied May 31, 1954.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment of the trial court (Louisiana), which convicted defendant of attempting to possess narcotics and of being a double offender, and sentenced him to seven years at hard labor in the state penitentiary.


OVERVIEW: Defendant, convicted of attempting to possess narcotics and of being a double offender, argued trial court error in overruling his motion to quash the bill of information on grounds that § La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 40:961 et seq., the Uniform Narcotic Drug Law, was unconstitutional by inflicting cruel and unusual punishment on persons convicted thereunder. The court disagreed, ruling that the issue was previously considered and held to be without merit. Moreover, the trial court did not err in refusing defendant's requested instruction to the jury that it could only convict him if the narcotics were found to be in his "exclusive possession." Noting that joint ownership or control was sufficient to prove defendant's guilt, the court ruled that the trial court properly allowed the greater part of the requested instructions to go to the jury and denied only the "exclusive possession" portion. Under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 15:390, a trial judge was only obligated to give requested charges that were wholly correct, and not to give misleading charges. And the denial of a new trial could not be considered, as the alleged newly discovered evidence was not attached to defendant's exceptions.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's convictions and sentence for attempting to possess narcotics and for being a double offender.

225 La. 495; 73 So. 2d 437; 1954 La. LEXIS 1241



April 26, 1954 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Rehearing Denied May 31, 1954.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was charged, tried, and convicted with violation of the voter registration law, La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 18:222. The trial court sustained a motion in arrest of judgment, based on the overruling of defendant's demurrer. The State appealed to the Appellate Division No. 2 of the Criminal District Court (Louisiana), which reversed the trial court. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: The information alleged that defendant "did wilfully and unlawfully cause himself to be registered as a voter for Ward 3, Precinct 2, by submitting false information in his application for registration." Reversing the appellate court and affirming the trial court, the court held that the information was insufficient to give defendant notice of the charges as required by the Due Process Clause of the state constitution, La. Const. art. 1, § 10. Under the information, nothing was revealed; the false information was concealed. There was no way for defendant to know on what point to defend or what set of witnesses to summon to rebut the charge.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the appellate court and affirmed the judgment of the trial court, which had granted defendant's motion in arrest of judgment.

Succession of Gambino


No. 41391


Supreme Court of Louisiana


225 La. 674; 73 So. 2d 800; 1954 La. LEXIS 1260



April 26, 1954 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Rehearing Denied May 31, 1954.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A decedent's will was filed, registered, and executed. Plaintiff, allegedly the decedent's adopted son, sought a reduction of the legacy to the widow, contending that he was entitled to his legitime as the adopted child and forced heir of the decedent. The district court (Louisiana) recognized the son as the decedent's forced heir and awarded him one-third of the separate property and one sixth of the community property. Defendant widow appealed.


OVERVIEW: The allegedly adopted son was born to the decedent's brother and sister-in-law, who were then living in the decedent's home with the decedent and his wife. The decedent and his wife adopted the child by notarial act when he was seven years old. When he was 15, the son's natural parents instituted adoption proceedings under 1938 La. Acts 428, and the child was decreed to be the adopted child of his natural parents. Affirming, the court held that when the status of parent and child was created by the decedent's adoption of the son, certain legal rights and obligations ensued, including the right to his parents' estates under La. Civ. Code Ann. art. 214. There was nothing in article 214 which provided for any way to undo these rights. The statement in 1938 La. Acts 428 that, upon adoption, the "child shall cease to be heir of its parents," if effective, was a substantive change in the law and was not reflected in the title of the act, in violation of La. Const. art. 3, § 16.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the district court.

Arkansas Fuel Oil Corp. v. Fontenot


No. 40988


Supreme Court of Louisiana


225 La. 166; 72 So. 2d 465; 1954 La. LEXIS 1203



March 22, 1954 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Rehearing Denied April 26, 1954.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff taxpayer, a foreign corporation, appealed from the judgment of the district court (Louisiana), which dismissed its suit. The taxpayer had filed suit against defendant state tax collector to recover corporate franchise taxes, which it paid in protest. The district court upheld the tax collector's interpretation of the Corporation Franchise Tax Act, La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 47:601 et seq., and its method of applying the tax thereunder.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayer, a holding company in the pipeline business, contended that certain intangible property, which it owned, including revenues, investments, and bank deposits, was improperly allocated to Louisiana under the Corporation Franchise Tax Act (Act), La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 47:601 et seq., as it was domiciled in Delaware. It urged that the movables followed the owner. Yet it maintained its principal office in Louisiana, where its officers resided. From such office it collected its revenues and kept deposits in Louisiana banks during the years in question, from which it paid dividends to its stockholders, as well as salaries and expenses. The court affirmed the judgment of the district court, dismissing the taxpayer's suit against the state tax collector, as the allocation of the aforementioned intangibles was in accordance with the Act. The court found that the taxpayer had established a commercial domicile in Louisiana. Also, in applying the allocation formula under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 47:606(B), the tax collector had not attempted to allocate all of the taxpayer's intangibles, but only a portion equal to the percentage of capital employed in Louisiana by its subsidiaries.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the district court, which upheld the state tax collector's method of applying the Corporation Franchise Tax Act. Costs of the appeal were assessed to the taxpayer.
