Wood v. Good Shield


No. 10863


Supreme Court of South Dakota


85 S.D. 625; 188 N.W.2d 757; 1971 S.D. LEXIS 112



July 14, 1971, Opinion Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from Circuit Court, Mellette County; Hon. John B. Jones, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of the judgment from the Circuit Court of Mellette County (South Dakota) that affirmed the decision from the police magistrate that convicted defendant of violating a municipal ordinance of the town.


OVERVIEW: Defendant challenged his conviction on the ground that his constitutional right to a fair and impartial trial was violated because the police magistrate had a direct, substantial, pecuniary interest in his conviction by virtue of S.D. Codified Laws § 9-29-22. In affirming, the court disagreed, holding that the effect and meaning of S.D. Codified Laws § 9-29-22 was that in any action prosecuted by a city attorney for the violation of a municipal ordinance defendant had to pay the costs of such action if found guilty. But if defendant was acquitted or the prosecution was otherwise unsuccessful, the municipality had to pay the costs. In such event defendant was also entitled to tax costs against the municipality, but in cases tried to a police magistrate the taxable costs would not exceed the sum of two dollars. Such construction of the statute removed it from the condemnation of previous cases and from all doubt as to its constitutionality.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment from the trial court.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged the judgment of conviction entered by the Stanley County Circuit Court (South Dakota) after a jury verdict finding appellant guilty of violating state blue sky laws, in connection with his high-pressure securities sales.


OVERVIEW: Appellant was the organizer of a fast food corporation. Appellant and a licensed agent went to the complainant's home to sell corporate stock to her. During their visit, the complainant revealed that she was having tax difficulties. Appellant promised to help the woman with her tax matters if she purchased his stocks, and stayed at her house all night until she finally purchased $ 6,000 worth of shares. Appellant was later indicted under 1961 S.D. Laws 270, § 23(5) for selling securities which worked a fraud upon the public. Appellant challenged his conviction. The court affirmed the conviction because appellant's failure to make a disclosure by withholding material facts, was as serious a misrepresentation as an oral or written misrepresentation. The court ruled that appellant's failure to give the complainant a prospectus, or disclose material information regarding corporate finances, was evidence of guilt under state blue sky laws.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the conviction because appellant's failure to make a disclosure by withholding material facts, was as serious a misrepresentation as an oral or written misrepresentation.
