Veal v. Interstate Fire & Casualty Co.


No. 56,176


Supreme Court of Louisiana


325 So. 2d 795; 1975 La. LEXIS 4185



December 8, 1975 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Dissenting Opinion January 27, 1976.


PRIOR HISTORY: Writ of Review to the Court of Appeal, Fourth Circuit, Parish of Jefferson.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff sought review of a Court of Appeal, Fourth Circuit, Parish of Jefferson (Louisiana) summary judgment dismissing plaintiff's uninsured motorist claim under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 22:1406(D)(1). Plaintiff's insurer was a foreign insurer not authorized to transact rate-regulated business in the state; plaintiff's policy was procured through a surplus-line broker.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff sued defendant, his automobile liability insurer, to recover damages caused through the negligence of an uninsured motorist. The lower court granted judgment to defendant, holding that La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 22:1406(D)(1), which required policies issued for delivery in Louisiana to contain uninsured motorist coverage unless expressly waived, did not apply to unauthorized insurers like defendant, who in accordance with the law issued policies through surplus line brokers. On appeal, the court reversed and remanded. The broad and unambiguous language of the statute expressly provided that all policies issued for delivery in Louisiana must afford uninsured motorist coverage. The court found no intent to exempt from this requirement policies issued through Louisiana licensed brokers for coverage of Louisiana based automobiles and Louisiana policyholders.


OUTCOME: Summary judgment was reversed and remanded because surplus line insurers were subject to same requirement to provide uninsured motorist coverage as authorized insurers.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] On Writ of Certiorari, or Review, to the Court of Appeal, First Circuit, Parish of Terrebonne, Louisiana.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, a father and a son, sued defendants, Louisiana High School Athletic Association (LHSAA) and a Catholic high school, to enjoin defendants from declaring the son had forfeited one year's athletic eligibility pursuant to the LHSAA's transfer rule. 324 So. 2d 389; 1975 La. LEXIS 5188

PRIOR HISTORY: On Application for Writs of Certiorari, Prohibition, Mandamus and Stay Order to the First Judicial District Court in and for Caddo Parish, Louisiana. Honorable John F. Fant, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Certiorari was granted to the First Judicial District Court of Caddo Parish, Louisiana, to review an order denying a motion to stay prosecution and revoke defense counsels' appointments, or to order reasonable compensation. The lawyers argued that La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 15:141 violated La. Const. art. I, § 13, and defendants were deprived of effective assistance of counsel under La. Const. art. I, §§ 2, 13, and the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments.


OVERVIEW: There was no showing that the defendants would not be effectively represented by skilled counsel. The trial court had the inherent power to appoint lawyers not on the volunteer panel under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 15:14. Section 15:141 did not limit payment to lawyers on the panel. If the district court denied compensation because the lawyers were not on the panel, an application for relief could be had. The motion was premature since no services had yet been rendered. In the absence of any legislation, and regardless of any provisions of the Louisiana Constitution, the United States Constitution and decisions of the United States Supreme Court would require the appointment of counsel for indigents accused of crime. The request to stay prosecution until further legislative action was taken was not taken seriously. The court could not say, even in the absence of the Louisiana Convention debates, that § 15:141 so lacked uniformity that it violated La. Const. art. I, § 13. Nor was the lack of uniformity so clear to the legislature that it capriciously abdicated its responsibility under art. I, § 13. There were no exigent emergent conditions requiring extraordinary measures.


OUTCOME: The order was affirmed.

323 So. 2d 120; 1975 La. LEXIS 4734



November 3, 1975 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Dissenting Opinion December 18, 1975.


PRIOR HISTORY: On Writ of Review to the Court of Appeal, First Circuit, Parish of East Baton Rouge, Louisiana.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant wife sought certiorari review of a judgment from the Court of Appeal, First Circuit, Parish of East Baton Rouge (Louisiana). The court of appeal reversed the trial court's decision and held that the consent of the wife's father was not necessary to uphold the validity of a prenuptial contract entered into by the wife and plaintiff husband.


OVERVIEW: The husband and the wife executed a prenuptial contract and stipulated that there would not be a community of acquets and gains. When the husband filed an action for divorce, the wife filed a reconventional demand, alleged the existence of a community, and requested a partition. The trial court held that the prenuptial contract was null because the wife, a minor at the time it was executed, did not have her father's consent. The court of appeal reversed. The court reversed the court of appeal's judgment. Under La. Civ. Code Ann. art. 2330, a minor had to be assisted in an agreement by those persons whose consent was necessary to the marriage. La. Civ. Code Ann. art. 112, however, prevented nonconsenting parents from annulling a marriage on the grounds of lack of consent. The two statutes could both be given effect by recognizing that each reflected two different, yet equally important, public policies. A marriage contract entered into by a minor with the assistance of his parents was valid; however, if entered into without the assistance of both parents, it was null. Thus, a marriage contract entered into by an unemancipated minor without the assistance of her father was invalid.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the court of appeal and reinstated the trial court's judgment.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, the Board of Supervisors of the Louisiana State University and Agricultural and Mechanical College, appealed a declaratory judgment from the court of appeal (Louisiana) in favor of plaintiff, the Student Government Association (SGA) of the university's Baton Rouge campus, holding that La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 17:803 was binding upon the Board and prohibiting the Board from imposing parking fines of more than one dollar.


OVERVIEW: The SGA sought a declaratory judgment that § 17:803, providing for a maximum fine of one dollar for the violation of any parking regulation on the campus of a state supported college or university, be recognized as applying to the university and therefore, overriding a university regulation providing for a parking fine of five dollars. The lower courts held that § 17:1803 did not infringe upon the Board's constitutional authority to administer the university. On appeal, the court reversed and held that La. Const. art. XII, § 7 unambiguously granted to the Board full administrative control over the university. The power to regulate student parking and to enforce reasonable parking regulations by administrative penalties, including fines, was clearly within the grant to the Board of exclusive administrative authority over students in their relationship with the university and in their use of the university campus. Section 17:803 was a legislative infringement on the Board's exclusive administrative power over university affairs and was therefore, unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment, dismissed the action, and rejected the SGA's demand that it declare the university parking regulations invalid insofar as they imposed a fine exceeding the maximum permitted by the statute.

OVERVIEW: Defendant was charged with and convicted of possession of a controlled dangerous substance known as Tuinol under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 40:971. Before defendant was sentenced, however, the Supreme Court of Louisiana declared unconstitutional the act that regulated certain drugs, including Tuinol. Defense counsel filed a motion in arrest of judgment, which was granted by the district court. A second information was thereafter filed charging defendant with possession of barbiturates under La. As such, the law remained in force for the purpose of sustaining defendant's conviction and for the enforcement of a penalty.


OUTCOME: The district court order granting the motion to quash was reversed, and the case was remanded to the district court for sentencing.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Pursuant to a grant of certiorari, the State sought review of an order of the Criminal District Court, Parish of Orleans, Section C (Louisiana), which granted a motion to suppress by defendant, a theater corporation, which was charged with the crime of obscenity by the exhibition of lewd, and sexually indecent motion picture films, in violation of La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:106(A)(2), on the basis that the seizure of the films was unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: After viewing three allegedly obscene films, the police filed applications for search warrants with affidavits containing descriptions of the sexual activities performed on the films. The police obtained search warrants for defendant's theaters and seized the films. Defendant was charged with the intentional exhibition of lewd, lascivious obscene films in violation of La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:106(A)(2). The trial court granted defendant's motion to suppress on the basis that the seizure of the films without a prior adversary hearing violated the First, Fourth, and Fourteenth Amendments. The court reversed, holding that the seizure complied with the Fourth Amendment and that no adversary hearing was required for the seizure of a single copy of an allegedly obscene motion picture under a valid search warrant, which was based upon an affidavit with factual statements for a determination of probable cause. The court held that the only lawful means for producing the evidence for an obscenity offense was to seize the film unless the material was readily available for purchase and that to insure protection of First Amendment rights, an immediate hearing within 48 hours was necessary.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the order of the trial court, which granted defendant's motion to suppress three films seized as allegedly obscene material.

PRIOR HISTORY: ON WRIT OF REVIEW TO THE COURT OF APPEAL, SECOND CIRCUIT, PARISH OF CADDO.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State of Louisiana and a Parish challenged an order of the Court of Appeal, Second Circuit, Parish of Caddo (Louisiana), which reversed a trial court and recalled its injunction that restrained and enjoined defendant theatre owners and operators from exhibiting an obscene motion picture in the Parish. The appellate court declared unconstitutional La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 13:4712 of the Abatement of Public Nuisances Act, §§ 13:4711-17.


OVERVIEW: Section 13:4712 provided that an obscene motion picture, as defined by the criminal laws of Louisiana, and premises displaying it were nuisances to be enjoined. The principal attack upon the constitutionality of § 13:4712 was aimed at its declaration that the judge "shall" grant a temporary injunction. The court found that § 13:4712 did not require that the judge sign every petition. Rather, the judge was to ascertain whether the petition and affidavit alleged that a film was being exhibited with intent to primarily appeal to the prurient interest of the average person, and that it was lewd, lascivious, filthy, or sexually indecent. The court held that § 13:4712 set out a reasonable basis for restraint, providing for an adversary hearing within five days of the issuance of a temporary injunction. Further, in the court's view, La. Civ. Code Ann. art. 3606 required a decision on a preliminary injunction at the earliest possible time; the appellate procedure, arts. 3601, 2081-2167, was as expeditious as possible. Finally, the trial court correctly applied the statute, placing the burden on the State and the Parish, and the court agreed with its holding that the film was obscene.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the appellate court and reinstated the judgment of the trial court, making it the judgment of the court.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment of the lower court (Louisiana), which entered a conviction against him for murder and sentenced him to death. On appeal, defendant argued several assignments of error, including that his death sentence was cruel and unusual punishment.


OVERVIEW: After being sentenced to death for murder, defendant appealed on the basis that the death penalty was cruel and unusual punishment prohibited by the constitution. The court affirmed defendant's conviction and sentence. Specifically, the court held that defendant's death sentence was not cruel and unusual punishment because the cruelty against which La. Furthermore, the court found that certain statements made by the prosecutor during opening statements were not inculpatory statements, rather, the statements were part of the res gestae and were admissible under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 15:447 because defendant made the statements to co-defendant almost immediately after murdering the victims in an effort to conceal the victims' bodies.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's murder conviction and death sentence.
