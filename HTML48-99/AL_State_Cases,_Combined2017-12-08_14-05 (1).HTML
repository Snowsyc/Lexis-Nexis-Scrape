CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff taxpayers appealed an order of the Jefferson Circuit Court (Alabama), which granted defendant county's motion to dismiss and denied the taxpayers' motion for partial summary judgment, in the taxpayers' class action to recover alleged excess taxes paid to the county based upon the assessment ratios provided for in Ala. Code § 40-8-1 (1975), on the grounds that the taxes were unconstitutional, violating the Fourteenth Amendment.


OVERVIEW: Under Ala. Code § 40-8-1 (1975), the taxpayers, who were citizens of the county, were required to pay a higher rate of ad valorem tax on the same class of property than citizens in other counties. The taxpayers filed a class action challenging the constitutionality of § 40-8-1, asserting that the assessment violated the Fourteenth Amendment. OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's order granting the county's motion to dismiss and denying the taxpayers' motion for partial summary judgment and remanded for further proceedings.

Raines v. State


SC 989


Supreme Court of Alabama


294 Ala. 360; 317 So. 2d 559; 1975 Ala. LEXIS 1209



May 22, 1975 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] As Corrected on Denial of Rehearing July 10, 1975



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant claimed his benefits under the Youthful Offender Act, Ala. Code tit. 15, § 266 (1972) (Act), but he refused to waive his right to trial by jury. The court then ordered that he be tried as an adult and a jury found defendant guilty of selling marijuana. On appeal, the Court of Criminal Appeals (Alabama) affirmed and defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was indicted for unlawfully selling marijuana. At arraignment the trial court appointed counsel to represent defendant and advised him of his youthful offender rights under the Youthful Offender Act, Ala. Code tit. 15, § 266 (1972). Defendant refused to waive his right to trial by jury, was convicted as an adult, and appealed. Defendant contended the Act was unconstitutional because of the jury waiver. The court held that the juvenile proceedings under the Act were not criminal in nature and therefore were not subject to the constitutional mandate of trial by jury. Also, the adjudicatory procedures defined by the Youthful Offender Act did not violate due process of law, and any benefits which might have resulted from the infusion of a jury trial into youthful offender proceedings were greatly outweighed by the detrimental effects it would have had to the orderly functioning of the youthful offender system and to the interests of those persons the Act was designed to protect.


OUTCOME: The judgment of conviction was affirmed.

Mitchell v. Mobile County


SC 832


Supreme Court of Alabama


294 Ala. 130; 313 So. 2d 172; 1975 Ala. LEXIS 1158



May 8, 1975 



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, a county personnel board and its director, appealed a decision of an Alabama trial court, which entered a judgment in favor of defendants, Mobile County, a county sheriff, and other counties and sheriffs. Plaintiffs, by a declaratory action, alleged that 1973 Ala. Acts 1177, relating to compensation for sheriffs, was unconstitutional. The trial court severed two sentences from the Act and found the remaining portions constitutional.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs contended that the Act was unconstitutional because it was a local bill that did not comply with the advertising requirements required by Ala. Const. art. 4, § 106. Further, they alleged that the word "compensation" within the Act was vague. The trial court deleted provisions of the Act that made it a local law. Thus, the remaining provisions of the Act were constitutional and did not need to comply with § 106. Because the Act was stripped of its local amendments, Ala. Const. art. 4, § 111 did not apply because there was no reason to guard against any deception created by a local act. The plain language of the Act clearly indicated that the legislature intended the Act to be severable and that remaining valid provisions should survive. The trial court correctly construed "compensation" to mean the minimum basic salary, excluding overtime pay, benefits, and allowances. The purpose of the Act was to provide uniform minimum compensation of deputy sheriffs, and the inclusion of the additional variables would have defeated the uniformity. The remaining provisions established a reasonable population classification that bore a reasonable relationship to the minimum salary.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
