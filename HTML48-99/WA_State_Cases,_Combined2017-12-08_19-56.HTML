State v. Prater


No. 6130-5-I


COURT OF APPEALS OF WASHINGTON, Division One


30 Wn. App. 512; 635 P.2d 1104; 1981 Wash. App. LEXIS 2781



November 2, 1981 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied January 13, 1982. Review Denied by Supreme Court March 26, 1982.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants appealed judgments from the Superior Court for King County, Washington, entered upon findings that they were guilty of two counts of first degree robbery, two counts of first degree assault, and one count of first degree burglary, were armed with a deadly weapon and a firearm during the commission of each crime, and were habitual criminals.


OVERVIEW: Defendants broke into the victims' apartment, shot the husband victim, hit the wife victim in the head while she was looking for money, took the money, and shot a whole in the wall before leaving. Defendants were convicted, and on appeal, the court affirmed in part and reversed in part. The court struck the firearm findings as to the robbery and burglary because those crimes were committed with a firearm. Under the Washington burglary antimerger statute Wash. Rev. Code § 9A.52.050, defendants were subject to punishment for the robbery they committed while they were in the apartment and for the first degree burglary they committed in entering the apartment with the intent to commit a felony. The assault in shooting the husband victim was not part of the robbery and was punishable. However, the assault in hitting the wife victim was part of the force used to commit the robbery and was not punishable. The trial court erred in refusing to consider one defendant's argument that a prior guilty plea supporting a habitual criminal finding was invalid.


OUTCOME: The court struck the firearm findings as to the robbery and burglary and vacated the convictions for the assault upon the wife victim and the habitual criminal finding as to one defendant. The court remanded both cases for further proceedings and resentencing. In all other respects, the court affirmed the judgments.

30 Wn. App. 496; 636 P.2d 494; 1981 Wash. App. LEXIS 2790



October 27, 1981 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Reconsideration Denied November 10, 1981. Review Granted by Supreme Court January 8, 1982.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, an injured party and her husband, challenged a judgment of the Superior Court for Yakima County, Washington, which granted appellee county's motion to dismiss appellants' suit on the basis that the action was neither timely nor properly commenced. Appellants brought suit against the county and defendant trucking company to recover for damages to the injured party as a result of an automobile accident on a county road.


OVERVIEW: Appellants filed a complaint exactly 3 years after the collision. Over two months later, appellants served a claim upon the county board of commissioners and served them with a copy of the summons and complaint. The court affirmed the dismissal and held as follows: (1) the trial court properly dismissed the claim because before an action could be commenced against the county based on tortious conduct, a claim had to be filed pursuant to Wash. Rev. Code § 36.45; (2) Wash. Rev. Code § 4.16.170, the tolling statute, did not extend the time for filing the claim beyond the normal period of the statute of limitation because the 90-day tolling period related only to completion of tentative commencement; (3) Wash. Rev. Code § 36.45.030 required as a condition precedent to maintaining an action against the county that a claim be presented; (4) thus, until the claim was presented, filing a complaint or serving the county could not effectively commence a lawsuit; and (5) since the completion of commencement merely related back to the beginning of tentative commencement, any conditions precedent to maintaining the suit had to be satisfied before the original statute of limitation expired.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment dismissing appellants' complaint.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a judgment from the Superior Court for Clark County (Washington), which convicted defendant of burglary after requiring defendant's testimony and cross-examination before expert testimony on defendant's intoxication, which was based on defendant's hearsay statements, would be admitted.


OVERVIEW: During defendant's trial for burglary, defendant admitted to having broken into a liquor store but claimed to have been in an "alcohol blackout" at the time, thus having been incapable of forming a specific intent to commit the crime. In support of defendant's intoxication defense, an expert was called who planned to testify to the blackout and defendant's incapability of forming the requisite intent. Because the expert's findings were based on defendant's hearsay statements, the court felt the testimony would enable defendant to testify without taking the stand, thus shielding defendant from cross-examination. The court also held prejudicial error had been committed by requiring defendant's testimony because the additional evidence had probably affected the verdict.


OUTCOME: The court reversed a judgment which convicted defendant of burglary after requiring defendant's testimony and cross-examination before expert testimony on defendant's intoxication, which was based on defendant's hearsay statements, would be admitted.

19 Wn. App. 35; 573 P.2d 1347; 1978 Wash. App. LEXIS 2064

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants appealed from the decision of the Superior Court for Grays Harbor County (Washington), which convicted them of armed robbery. The state cross-appealed the trial court's finding that the state firearm statute, Wash. Rev. Code § 9.41.025, was unconstitutional.


OVERVIEW: Defendants were convicted at separate trials of armed robbery. They both appealed from their convictions, challenging the admissibility of their confessions. The state also cross-appealed from the trial court's refusal to sentence in accordance with the state's firearm statute. On a consolidated appeal due to similar factual contexts, the court affirmed their convictions. The court held that one defendant was not so incapacitated by the effects of smoking marijuana as to be unable to understand or knowingly and intelligently waive his constitutional rights. However, the court held that the trial court erred in failing to comply with the state's firearm statute and remanded the cause for resentencing.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendants' conviction for armed robbery. The court held that the trial court erred in finding that the state firearm statute was unconstitutional and remanded for resentencing in accordance with the state firearm statute.

The court held that the exclusion of jurors who could not impose the death penalty was not error because the jury was specifically instructed that the death penalty did not apply in the case and that it should not be considered, and because the exclusion of those potential jury members did not deprive defendant of a fair trial. The court held that the trial court did not abuse its discretion in failing to appoint an interpreter to show the seriousness of the victim's threat to defendant that was spoken in Spanish. The court found that defendant was able to adequately convey the nature of the victim's threat meant to him without the assistance of an expert interpreter.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of conviction.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Superior Court for King County, Washington, which convicted him of carnal knowledge and assault in the second degree after a bench trial and found defendant guilty of being a habitual criminal and sentenced him to life imprisonment.


OVERVIEW: On appeal, defendant argued that the trial court erred when it refused to allow him to cross-examine the prosecutrix regarding a complaint of rape which he claimed she had made two years before, that the prosecutor made a remark which was highly prejudicial, and that the State discriminated against him by charging him as a habitual criminal in a supplemental information. The court disagreed and affirmed defendant's conviction. The court held that: (1) the trial court did not abuse its discretion in limiting the scope of cross-examination; (2) there was no evidence that the trial judge who heard the evidence was influenced by a remark that the prosecuting attorney made about a spectator who was evicted by the judge for causing a disturbance; (3) the trial court properly refused to dismiss the supplemental information filed against defendant charging him to be a habitual criminal; (4) defendant was not denied the equal protection of the law because the prosecuting attorney filed charges against him as a habitual criminal; and (5) double jeopardy did not apply simply because the State of Oregon had already convicted defendant of being a habitual criminal.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.
