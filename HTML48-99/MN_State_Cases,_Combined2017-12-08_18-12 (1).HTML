SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Review denied by Seifert v. Erickson, 1988 Minn. LEXIS 595 (Minn., May 18, 1988)


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from District Court, Washington County; Hon. Esther Tomljanovich, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant inmate sought review of a decision of the District Court, Washington County (Minnesota), which denied, without an evidentiary hearing, his petition for writ of habeas corpus, challenging the good time statute, Minn. Stat. § 244 (1986).


OVERVIEW: The inmate was serving two concurrent sentences. He was given release dates that represented his sentence lengths less the maximum good-time credit he could earn, calculated as one day for every two days served, or one-third the sentence length. He filed a petition for writ of habeas corpus contending that the good-time statute, Minn. Stat. § 244 (1986), should have been construed to provide separate a good-time deduction to be served on supervised release. The district court denied his petition without an evidentiary hearing and the inmate appealed. The court affirmed. The court found that earned good time, which entitled an inmate only to serve his time on supervised release rather than in prison, was "used up" once the inmate obtain supervised release. Minn. Stat. § 244 was not unconstitutionally vague because it clearly provided that good time would accrue to the period of supervised release, Minn. Stat. § 244.04, subd. 1 (1986). Because an inmate was entitled to an evidentiary hearing only if a factual dispute was shown, the inmate's petition was properly denied without a hearing because it presented a purely legal question involving construction of a statute.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the district court's decision.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a decision from the District Court, Hennepin County (Minnesota), which convicted him of one count of assault in the first degree in violation of Minn. OVERVIEW: On appeal defendant challenged the sufficiency of the evidence to convict him and argued that the jury did not presume him to be innocent. J.L. Shiely Co. v. County of Stearns

MINNESOTA TAX COURT, COUNTY OF STEARNS, REGULAR DIVISION


1986 Minn. Tax LEXIS 135



February 13, 1986 


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Reversed by J.L. Shiely Co. v. County of Stearns, 395 N.W.2d 357, 1986 Minn. LEXIS 895 (Minn., 1986)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner taxpayer filed an action against respondent county, challenging the assessment of an aggregate production tax imposed pursuant to Minn. Stat. § 298.75. The Attorney General for the State of Minnesota intervened in the action.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayer operated a crushed rock plant for the production of aggregate material. The county assessed an aggregate production tax, as provided by Minn. Stat. § 298.75, on the taxpayer's production. The taxpayer filed a statement of objection, contending that the tax was unconstitutional. The court found that the taxpayer had shown beyond a reasonable doubt that § 298.75 did not meet the requirements of uniformity and equal protection under either the Minnesota Constitution or the United States Constitution. There was no rational basis for the State of Minnesota to levy the aggregate production tax in 22 specific counties and to exempt the levy of the tax in the rest of the counties of the State. Aggregate and rock suitable for crushing were found broadly across the State. Accordingly, the court held that § 298.75 violated the Uniformity Clause of the Minnesota Constitution and the Equal Protection Clauses of the Minnesota Constitution and the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.


OUTCOME: The court awarded judgment to the taxpayer.

Greens v. County of Hennepin


File Nos. TC-3207 and TC-4104


MINNESOTA TAX COURT COUNTY OF HENNEPIN, FOURTH JUDICIAL DISTRICT


1985 Minn. Tax LEXIS 17



November 22, 1985 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner property owner sought review of an assessment of fair market value of the owner's property by respondent County of Hennepin, Minnesota. The owner claimed, on the basis of unequal treatment, that there should have been an additional reduction in value.


OVERVIEW: The owner introduced unadjusted assessment/sales ratio studies prepared by the Minnesota Department of Revenue indicating median ratios for commercial/industrial property in Minneapolis of 88.9 percent for 1983 and 84.6 percent for 1984. The County countered with assessment/sales ratio studies using essentially the same sales as used in the Department study, but adjusting the sale prices for cash equivalency and inflation. The adjustments raised the median ratios to 93.5 percent for 1983 and to 92.8 percent for 1984. The court found that the general level of assessment at which other properties were taxed was 93 percent of market value for both 1983 and 1984. The court determined that a 93 percent level of assessment did not constitute an unconstitutional denial of equal protection because there had to be a substantial disparity in assessment ratios before an equal protection violation was found. Thus, in the absence of evidence of arbitrary or systematic substantial undervaluation of other property within the same taxing district, the court concluded that the owner was not denied equal protection.


OUTCOME: The court denied the owner's request for a reduction in the assessment of fair market value.
