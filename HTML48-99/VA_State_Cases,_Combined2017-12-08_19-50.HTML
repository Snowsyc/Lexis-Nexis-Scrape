PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] FROM THE COURT OF APPEALS OF VIRGINIA.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Criminal defendant appealed an order of the Court of Appeals of Virginia affirming the trial court's denial of his motion to dismiss his reindictment for the same offenses after the granting State's motion for nolle prosequi on the original indictment based on alleged violations of U.S. Const. amend. IV.


OVERVIEW: Criminal defendant was indicted for obtaining and attempting to obtain money by false pretenses. The prosecution later filed a continuance motion, which was denied, so the prosecution moved to nolle prosequi indictment. The trial court granted the motion. Later, criminal defendant was indicted again on the same charges. Criminal defendant's motion to dismiss indictment was denied and the lower court affirmed the denial. This appeal followed. The court noted that the granting of a motion for nolle prosequi would only be overturned if there was clear evidence that the decision to grant the motion was not judicially sound. The record supported the trial court's action in granting the nolle prosequi. The court evaluated four factors to determine whether criminal defendant had been deprived of his constitutional right to a speedy trial. The court concluded that the period between criminal defendant's appearance to accept service of the second indictment and his subsequent conviction did not constitute a violation of his constitutional right to a speedy trial under U.S. Const. amend. VI.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed dismissal of the reindictment because defendant's speedy trial right was not violated where the court considered the period between appearance to accept service of his reindictment and the subsequent conviction rather than the period from first indictment to the conviction.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] FROM THE CIRCUIT COURT OF MADISON COUNTY. Lloyd C. Sullenberger, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff landowner appealed a judgment of the Circuit Court of Madison County (Virginia), which affirmed the denial of plaintiff's application to approve his 191-acre subdivision plat that included a nine acre closed landfill.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff asserted trial court error in withholding approval of his 191-acre subdivision plat that included a closed landfill, because a "substantial surface drainage course" was located on the landfill area. The court disagreed, noting first that plaintiff was planning to build a road over the landfill but that he was unwilling to accept a drainage easement prohibiting any land disturbing activity in the landfill area that would affect the existing drainage course. Explaining, the court rejected plaintiff's assertion that the "course" -- as used in the applicable county ordinance barring land disturbing activity -- meant a creek bed or gully, ruling that the ordinance's use of "stream or drainage course" encompassed differing topographical land features. Moreover, the trial court did not err in finding a substantial drainage course on plaintiff's landfill, on the basis of testimony by a professional engineer. Finally, the court rejected the contention that rejection of plaintiff's application was justified only if the "course" was located in the area being subdivided, rather than the landfill, as the parcels encompassed by the subdivision application included the landfill.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed denying plaintiff's application to approve his subdivision plat that included a closed landfill, because denial was properly based on existence of "substantial surface drainage course" on the landfill, as "course" -- used in ordinance barring land disturbing activity -- not limited to creek bed, but included other land features, as substantial nature of drainage confirmed by engineer, and as application included landfill.

OVERVIEW: The inmate brought an action under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983 alleging that prison officials misapplied Virginia's parole eligibility statute, Va. Code Ann. § 53.1-151. Although convicted on more than one occasion, the inmate only received an active prison sentence on his most recent conviction. OUTCOME: The court responded in the negative to a federal court's certified question whether a Virginia inmate had to be convicted of a felony and committed to a Virginia correctional facility on three occasions before being rendered ineligible for parole.
