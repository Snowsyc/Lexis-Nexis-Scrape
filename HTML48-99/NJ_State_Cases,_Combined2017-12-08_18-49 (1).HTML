Imbrie v. Marsh


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


Superior Court of New Jersey, Appellate Division


5 N.J. Super. 239; 68 A.2d 761; 1949 N.J. Super. LEXIS 657


October 5, 1949, Argued  
October 19, 1949, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant political candidates sought review of a decision of a New Jersey trial court, which upheld statutes that added "belief" language to the oath of allegiance in N.J. Rev. Stat. § 41:1-1, and the oath pledging support to the constitution in N.J. Rev. Stat. § 41:1-3. Appellants contended the language exceeded and violated the oath requirements contained in Pa. Const. art. VII, § 1.1.


OVERVIEW: Chapters 21 to 25 of the Laws of 1949, N.J. Rev. Stat. §§ 41:1-1, 3, added language to the oath of allegiance and oath to uphold the constitution which office holders took prior to assuming office; the required language began with the phrase "I do not believe in." Appellant political candidates challenged the requirement that they state a belief as a condition of office. The lower court upheld the additional language. On appeal, the court reversed because the first rule of constitutional jurisprudence was that the legislation could not add to or subtract from the qualifications for public office established by the constitution under Pa. Const. art. VII, § 1.1. It held that the challenged phrase was an impermissible test intended to disqualify certain people from public office, and was an impermissible act by the legislature. The court held inviolate the right of citizens to elect anyone they choose to represent them, without legislative concern for their beliefs. Accordingly, the challenged statutes were held to be invalid as they pertained to the governor, senators and members of the general assembly, and candidates for those offices.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the lower court and held that legislative additions to the oath of allegiance and oath to uphold the constitution, which added language about elected officials' "belief" were invalid under the state constitution because the legislature had no authority to vary the constitutional qualifications for office and could not test the supposed fitness of state officials by such inquiry.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant candidate sought review of a decision of the superior court (New Jersey), which dismissed appellant's complaint against respondent election officials in a primary election dispute involving a tie vote. Appellant contended that under N.J. Rev. Stat. § 19:13-20, respondents had to select him because the other candidate had taken write-in votes from a rival party.


OVERVIEW: A primary election was held to nominate candidates for local office. Two candidates received the same number of votes. Under N.J. Rev. Stat. § 19:13-20, the selection of a candidate was submitted to respondent election officials. At the same time, one of the two candidates received the nomination of a rival party. He consented, pursuant to N.J. Rev. Stat. § 19:23-16, to stand for such election. Appellant candidate filed an action to compel respondent election officials to select him as his party's candidate on the ground that he was the only eligible person. He relied on § 19:13-20, which required that a certificate accompany a statement of selection that the candidate was not affiliated with any other party. Appellant maintained that the other candidate, by accepting the write-in vote of a rival party, could not sign the certificate. The trial court dismissed the complaint and the court affirmed because statutory disqualifications of candidates must be construed strictly, and nothing prohibited the other candidate from withdrawing his acceptance of the rival party nomination. The court had no authority to order respondents to select one candidate over another.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the decision of the trial court, which dismissed appellant candidate's complaint filed against respondent election officials because the requirement of an election eligibility certificate was not pre-empted in the case of a rival who accepted the nomination from another party where he could withdraw that acceptance and stand for election in his own party and make the required certification.
