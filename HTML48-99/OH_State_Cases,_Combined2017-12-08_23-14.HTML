State v. Cichowicz


CASE NO. 91-A-1613


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, ELEVENTH APPELLATE DISTRICT, ASHTABULA COUNTY


1992 Ohio App. LEXIS 1113



March 13, 1992, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:   [*1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDINGS: Criminal Appeal from Eastern County Court, Case No. 90 CRB 455



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT: Affirmed in part and reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of her conviction by the Ashtabula Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) on the State's charge that she violated Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 959.15, the animal fights statute.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of violating the Ohio animal fights statute, Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 959.15. She sought review of the conviction, arguing that the statute was unconstitutionally vague and overbroad. The court upheld the conviction. The court ruled that although one portion of the statute was overly vague and broad, that portion was severable for the remainder. The court also held that defendant did not demonstrate that the statute was unconstitutional as applied to her. The court noted that § 959.15 prohibited a person from knowingly engaging in cockfighting, bearbaiting, or pitting an animal against another. The statute also provided that any person who knowing purchased a ticket of admission to a place where animal fights were held or was "present thereat" or witnessed such a spectacle was an aider or abettor. The court found that the phrase "present thereat" was unconstitutionally vague because it could have been applied to a person who was at a place where animal fights were held when there was no fight in progress. Thus, the phrase potentially infringed a party's First Amendment right of free assembly. Therefore, the court said, the phrase was unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction for having knowingly witnessed an animal fight in violation of Ohio law. The court reversed the trial court's judgment to the extent that it found that a portion of the statute was not unconstitutional.

The county court also held that the applicable city ordinances were constitutional.


OVERVIEW: The resident was charged a minimum charge for garbage collection pursuant to a city ordinance. He objected to the charge, asserting that he did not generate garbage requiring collection. After failing to pay his bill, he was notified of a potential termination of service. He requested a hearing, which was granted pursuant to a city ordinance. The hearing officer found that the city was entitled to collect the minimum charge and that the resident was responsible for paying it. His failure to do so subjected him to a termination of service. Applying the rational basis test, the disconnection of the service was rationally related to a government purpose. The utility billing procedures were administered identically among residents and the charges were reasonable and lawful.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the county court's judgment affirming the hearing officer's finding that the resident was subject to termination for failing to pay his bill and holding that the city ordinances in issue were constitutional.
