PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] APPEAL FROM CIVIL DISTRICT COURT, PARISH OF ORLEANS, DIVISION L, NO. 88-8471, HONORABLE MAX N. TOBIAS, JR., JUDGE.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant school board appealed an order of the Civil District Court for the Parish of Orleans (Louisiana), which awarded plaintiff instructor back wages based on a retroactive Army promotion. The instructor cross-appealed and sought attorney fees under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. §§ 23:631, 632.


OVERVIEW: The instructor was employed by the board in a Junior Reserved Army Training Corps program at a high school under a contract between the high school and the United States Army. The instructor was paid on the basis of his rank as listed when he was first employed, despite the instructor's eligibility for a promotion in rank at the time of his employment. When the Army finally promoted the instructor, retroactive to a date preceding his employment with the school, the instructor filed an action to recover past due wages, as well as penalties, attorney fees and interest under the provisions of La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 23:631 et seq. The trial court awarded back pay, but denied the request for penalties and fees. The court affirmed and held that the claim for wages was timely because it was filed within three years of the time the payments became "exigible," regardless of whether the date the prescription period commenced was the date the judgment of promotion was rendered or the date the instructor was informed of the judgment. Attorney's fees were not due because the wages were not technically "due" at the time of the instructor's resignation.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order that awarded the instructor back wages, and the court affirmed the denial of attorney's fees to the instructor.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review from the 22nd Judicial District Court in and for the Parish of St. Tammanay (Louisiana), where he was convicted of aggravated oral sexual battery in violation of La. OVERVIEW: The victims, a five year old and a three year old, lived with defendant and their mother. Inappropriate sexual contact was discovered when an uncle reported possible physical abuse. OUTCOME: Defendant's convictions for aggravated oral sexual battery and sexual battery were affirmed. Defendant's sentences were also affirmed.

Moore v. Roemer


No. 89 CA 1496


Court of Appeal of Louisiana, First Circuit


560 So. 2d 927; 1990 La. App. LEXIS 975



April 10, 1990, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1]  

ON APPEAL FROM THE NINETEENTH JUDICIAL DISTRICT COURT (NO. 337,216), PARISH OF EAST BATON ROUGE, HONORABLE CARL GUIDRY, JUDGE PRESIDING.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a declaratory judgment action, plaintiff worker's compensation claimants sought review of a decision from the 19th Judicial District Court, Parish of East Baton Rouge (Louisiana), dismissing their claims that the legislature's 1988 amendments to the Worker's Compensation Law, La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 23:1310 et seq., violated La. Const. art. V, § 16, providing for original jurisdiction of worker's compensation appeals in the district courts.


OVERVIEW: The claimants contested the dismissal of their suit seeking a declaration that legislative amendments divesting the state's district courts of jurisdiction to hear appeals from the decisions of administrative worker's compensation panels were unconstitutional. The amendments excluded district courts from the worker's compensation adjudication system by granting aggrieved claimants or employers a direct appeal to the state's circuit courts of appeal. On appeal, the court held that worker's compensation claims were civil matters. Therefore, the amendments contravened La. Const. art. V, § 16(A), which invested the district courts with exclusive original jurisdiction over "all civil matters." Additionally, the legislature was powerless to change the nature of a worker's compensation claim from a private right to a public right. La. Const. art. V, § 10(B), did not provide for direct review by the circuit courts of appeal of administrative determinations of private rights. The court declined to decide the question of whether judicial review of an administrative agency's determination was an exercise of a district court's original jurisdiction or of its appellate jurisdiction.


OUTCOME: The court reversed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a jury decision from the 12th Judicial District Court, Parish of Avoyelles (Louisiana), convicting her of attempted theft of property having a value of $ 100 or more, a violation of La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:67 and 14:27.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of signing checks on a checking account held in the name of the victim, defendant's live-in boyfriend. The victim had a practice of leaving defendant in possession of signed blank checks, which he permitted her to use for household expenses while the victim was away traveling for his job. After the parties split up, the victim accused defendant of making out his pre-signed checks for more than the purchase amount or making out the checks to herself and cashing them, all without the victim's authorization. The victim admitted that he freely gave defendant pre-signed blank checks for household use, that they had lived in a marriage-like relationship, and that he never filed a complaint until they had parted. On these facts, the State failed its burden of proving that defendant committed a crime.


OUTCOME: The court reversed defendant's conviction.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of a jury verdict from the 22nd Judicial District Court, Parish of St. Tammany (Louisiana), convicting him of armed robbery and attempted armed robbery, violations of La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:64, §14:27, and §14:64, respectively, and his concurrent second felony offender sentences of 33 and 23 years at hard labor without benefit of parole, probation, or suspension.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of robbing two food stores on consecutive evenings. Both store cashiers described defendant except for his face, which was covered by a red ski mask. A high-speed chase ensued after the second robbery. Defendant bailed out of the getaway car, ran between police cars, and escaped. When defendant was arrested three weeks later for a different offense, the officer who saw him during the high-speed chase recognized him. On appeal, the court held that defendant's inculpatory statements were admissible because defendant's story that police had brutalized him during questioning at the police station was not corroborated. A gun found in a park near defendant's escape route was admissible even though no direct evidence tied defendant to the gun. Defendant's sentence was not excessive even though his co-defendant was sentenced to only five years in prison on both counts because due process did not mandate sentencing parity for co-defendants.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, the employer of the decedent, and plaintiff wife, individually and on behalf of the decedent's estate and their two minor children, appealed from a judgment of the Civil District Court for the Parish of Orleans (Louisiana) awarding the wife damages for her survival claim, loss of support and services, and funeral expenses, and reversing the jury's award for punitive damages.


OVERVIEW: The wife's claims were brought under general maritime law, the Jones Act, 46 U.S.C.S. § 688, and Death on the High Seas Act, 46 U.S.C.S. § 761. In addition, the decedent was treated in a foreign land and was not administered sufficient pain medication. Finally, the court held that prejudgment interest was not appropriate because the employer was found liable under the Jones Act and general maritime law.
