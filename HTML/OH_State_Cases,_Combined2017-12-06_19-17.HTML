CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a decedent's estate administratrix in her administrative and individual capacities, sought review of a judgment from the Lawrence County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio), which dismissed her claims against appellees, asbestos-related (AR) entities, in the administratrix's multi-count action, seeking recovery for alleged AR injuries sustained by the decedent. They were dismissed based on failure to meet the requirement of R.C. § 2307.92.


OVERVIEW: The trial court denied the administratrix's motions to prove a prima facie case under R.C. ch. 2307 and to set the matter for trial. Upon finding that she had not met the prima facie standard of R.C. § 2307.92, the trial court administratively dismissed the complaint. On appeal, the court found that the trial court had properly determined that H.B. 292, Gen. Assem. (Ohio 2004) (codified at R.C. ch. 2307) was not unconstitutionally retroactive generally, as it prohibited its application if such would result in unconstitutional retroactivity in violation of Ohio Const. art. II, § 28, based on its own savings provision under R.C. § 2307.93(A)(3)(a). In determining whether application of R.C. ch. 2307 to the administratrix's case was unconstitutionally retroactive, the court noted that the legislature had expressly intended retroactive application. Further, it found that as ch. 2307 imposed a new, more difficult statutory standard upon the administratrix's ability to maintain the claims compared with the prior, potentially viable common law cause of action, it was a substantive rather than remedial law. The court concluded that its application would be unconstitutionally retroactive.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the case for an evaluation of the adminstratrix's causes of action under Ohio common law.

DISPOSITION: Judgment of the Court of Common Pleas affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants taxpayers sued appellee taxing official, and others, in the Summit Count Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) for declaratory judgment, permanent injunction, and recovery of illegally collected real estate taxes. The trial court granted the official's motion to dismiss the taxpayers' complaint for failure to state a claim under Ohio R. Civ. P. 12(B)(6). The taxpayers appealed.


OVERVIEW: The Ohio Supreme Court had held certain school finance statutes unconstitutional for violating the mandate of a "thorough and efficient" public school system, in Ohio Const. art. VI, § 2, and the legislature was given a year to enact corrective legislation, which was extended. The appellate court held this decision did not intend to reduce or eliminate property taxes for public schools, although reliance on these taxes was criticized. The state had to look to additional funding sources in addition to, and not instead of, these taxes. The Supreme Court held, on reconsideration, property taxes could fund a school system, but could not be the primary financing means. The year allowed to enact corrective legislation did not mean property taxes were lowered if such legislation was not enacted within that period. Voted property taxes for schools, under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. ch. 5707, were not implicitly found unconstitutional as the statute was presumed constitutional, and the Supreme Court did not explicitly find it unconstitutional. There was no support for the argument that, if corrective legislation was not enacted, their property taxes for schools were reduced to 10 mills.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Court of Common Pleas, Portage County (Ohio), convicting him of improper handling of a firearm in a motor vehicle, in violation of R.C. 2923.16(B). Defendant alleged that the trial court should have granted his motion to dismiss the indictment because § 2923.16(B) violated his right to bear arms embodied in U.S. Const. amend. OVERVIEW: Defendant, a West Virginia resident, was operating a motor vehicle in Ohio when he was stopped by a police officer for a traffic violation. October 27, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] 
CIVIL APPEAL FROM WARREN COUNTY COURT OF COMMON PLEAS. Case No. 05CV64483.



DISPOSITION: 


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellees, a deceased worker's family members, sued, inter alia, appellant manufacturer, in the Warren County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) for products liability. The trial court denied the manufacturer's summary judgment motion, after finding R.C. 2125.02(D)(2)(a), a statute of repose, violated the right to remedy clause of Ohio Const. art. I, § 16. The manufacturer appealed.


OVERVIEW: The manufacturer said it was error to find R.C. 2125.02(D)(2)(a) unconstitutional under Ohio Const. art. I, § 16. The appellate court agreed. The trial court held, absent further analysis, that the statute violated the right to remedy clause. The court's opinion did not show the statute and the constitutional provision were incompatible beyond a reasonable doubt, nor did it give the general assembly legislative deference before finding the statute unconstitutional. In addition, after it issued its decision, the Ohio Supreme Court, in another case, overruled an argument that another products liability statute of repose was unconstitutional for the same reason. The right to remedy provision in art. I, § 16 only applied to existing, vested, rights, and state law determined what remedies were available. When the legislature created a remedy in products liability cases, it also had the power to impose limitations on the remedy, including a requirement that an action be brought within ten years of the delivery of the manufacturer's allegedly defective product, and this did not leave the family members without any remedy, as redress could be sought from the employer.


OUTCOME: The trial court's decision was reversed, and the matter was remanded for further consideration of the merits of the manufacturer's summary judgment motion.

This case concerns the legal effect of two actions taken by a decedent prior to the effective date of Ohio's Transfer-on-Death Security Registration Act. First, in 1983, Chester S. In the "Adoption Agreement" that he signed to open this account, Chester named his sister, Stella, as the beneficiary of the account's balance upon his death. Shortly thereafter, Chester made a will containing a clause giving all of his property to his wife, Dorothy, upon his death.

In 1993, three years before Chester's death, the General Assembly codified Ohio's version of the Uniform Transfer-on-Death Security Registration Act ("Act"), R.C. 1709.01 et seq., 145 Ohio Laws, Part II, 2858. The Act provides, inter alia, that "any transfer-on-death resulting from a registration in beneficiary form * * * is not testamentary." (Emphasis added.) R.C. 1709.09(A). Accordingly, the Act removes such transfers on death from the decedent's testamentary estate, and also from the purview of Ohio's Statute [***2] of Wills, which outlines the formalities that apply to testamentary dispositions. R.C. 2107.03. R.C. 1709.11(D) makes the entire Act applicable to registrations in beneficiary form made "prior to, on, or after the effective date of this section, by decedents dying prior to, on, or after that date." R.C. 1709.11(D).

Soon after Chester's death in 1996, Dorothy discovered that Chester had named Stella the beneficiary of his IRA. Dorothy filed a complaint in the Summit County Probate Court, seeking a declaratory judgment that she, not Stella, was entitled to the IRA proceeds. Dorothy's argument to the probate court consisted of four steps. First, Dorothy argued that Chester's IRA beneficiary clause constituted testamentary language when it designated Stella as the pay-on-death beneficiary of the account. Second, Dorothy argued that the beneficiary clause was, therefore, null and void, since the Adoption Agreement in which the clause appeared did not comply with the signature and attestation requirements of our Statute of Wills. Third, even though R.C. 1709.09(A), supra, defines beneficiary [***3] registrations such as this one as "not testamentary," Dorothy argued that the Act could not constitutionally apply retroactively to Chester's IRA beneficiary clause, which was signed a decade prior to the effective date of the Act. Finally, Dorothy averred that without the Act to validate the beneficiary clause in the IRA, the IRA account balance would be transferred not to Stella, but rather to Chester's probate estate, where it would pass to Dorothy under the terms of Chester's will.

After considering Dorothy's declaratory judgment action and Stella's motion to dismiss, the probate court concluded that the Act validated the non-testamentary transfer-on-death clause in Chester's IRA even though the beneficiary designation in the IRA was created before the effective date of the Act. Therefore, the probate court ordered the balance of the IRA to pass to Stella under the terms of the beneficiary clause that Mr. Bielat had signed in 1983.

Dorothy appealed this judgment to the Ninth District Court of Appeals. She argued that the application of the Act to the pay-on-death beneficiary registration that Chester executed prior to the Act's effective date constituted a retroactive application [***4] of the law in violation of Section 28, Article II of the Ohio Constitution. The court of appeals affirmed the probate court's decision in favor of Stella. The court held that although the Act was being applied in this case to a transfer-on-death beneficiary clause executed before the Act's effective date, the Act did not impair a vested right belonging to Dorothy, and thus did not violate the Ohio Constitution's prohibition against retroactive laws.

The cause is now before this court upon the allowance of a discretionary appeal.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant filed an appeal from an affirmance entered in the Court of Appeals for Summit County (Ohio), which affirmed the probate court's decision in favor of appellee in a declaratory judgment action.


OVERVIEW: Deceased opened an Individual Retirement Account (IRA), and named his sister, appellee, as the beneficiary of the account. The Supreme Court affirmed, and held that the Uniform Transfer-on-Death Security Registration Act, Ohio Rev. Code Ann. §§ 1709.09(A), 1709.11(D), constituted remedial, curative statutes that provided a framework by which parties to certain investment accounts could more readily enforce their intent to designate a pay-on-death beneficiary. OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed, because the Uniform Transfer-on-Death Security Registration Act was remedial in nature, and therefore could not be construed as an unconstitutional prohibition against retroactive laws.

OVERVIEW: The inmate argued that the trial court erred when it found that the amended sections of R.C. Chapter 2950, known as the Adam Walsh Act, were constitutional as applied to him. State v. Phipps

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDINGS: Criminal Appeal from Municipal Court.



DISPOSITION: Judgment of the Municipal Court reversed; cause remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant charged, in the Auglaize County Municipal Court (Ohio) with driving under the influence of alcohol, contrary to Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4511.19, moved to suppress field sobriety test results. The trial court held Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4511.19(D)(4)(b), allowing admission of field sobriety tests conducted in "substantial" as opposed to "strict" compliance with testing standards was unconstitutional. The State appealed.


OVERVIEW: An amendment to Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4511.19, found at § 4511.19(D)(4)(b), allowed field sobriety tests conducted in substantial compliance with standards of the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration to be admitted in evidence against defendant. Before the amendment, strict compliance with these standards was required, pursuant to an Ohio Supreme Court decision. The trial court found the amendment violated Ohio Const. art. IV, § 5 by conflicting with a previous rule of practice and procedure. The appellate court held it had to find the amendment constitutional unless it was shown to be unconstitutional beyond a reasonable doubt. Under art. IV, § 5, only statutes conflicting with a formally proposed and adopted rule of evidence were invalid. No provision of the Ohio Rules of Evidence had been adopted regarding the standard of admissibility for field sobriety tests. Therefore, the amendment did not infringe on the Ohio judicial branch's exclusive authority to promulgate rules of procedure for its courts. A statute could govern the admissibility of evidence, and this amendment simply replaced the common law standard of admissibility.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment was reversed, and the matter was remanded.
