DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of a decision from the Ingham Circuit Court (Michigan), which dismissed his constitutional and civil rights claims, finding it lacked subject matter over the zoning claims because appellant had failed to file an administrative appeal.


OVERVIEW: Appellant owned an interest in land zoned agricultural. He filed an application with appellee township to have the property rezoned to mobile home park district. The planning commission voted unanimously to recommend to the township board that the rezoning be denied, which the board did. OUTCOME: Judgment reversed because appellant's law suit did not involve a challenge to the administrative activities of a municipal body acting in the capacity of a zoning board of appeals but involved constitutional challenges to their legislative actions.

OVERVIEW: Charged with murder, defendant filed a notice of intent to assert an insanity defense. PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff's medical malpractice case was remanded from the Michigan Supreme Court for reconsideration, in light of recent precedent, of a prior decision to reverse the trial court's ruling that a cardiologist was not qualified to render expert testimony on the issue of the negligence of defendant cardiovascular surgeon.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff patient sued defendants, cardiovascular surgeon and medical group, for malpractice. The trial court granted summary disposition to defendants. Defendant appealed his convictions. He contended that the trial court wrongfully denied his motion to suppress, erroneously admitted hearsay testimony, and refused to issue particular jury instructions.


OVERVIEW: Arguing that his confession, out-of-court statements of one of the victims, and evidence obtained during three separate searches of defendant's hotel room should not have been admitted into evidence at trial, defendant appealed his convictions of murder and a firearms violation. He also alleged ineffective assistance of counsel. The appellate court affirmed. Exigent circumstances justified two of the searches of defendant's hotel room. In both instances, defendant's possible presence in the room endangered the lives of other guests and threatened the destruction of evidence. The second search was executed pursuant to a search warrant supported by probable cause. Defendant's confession was voluntary, it was elicited after a proper arrest, and even if the arrest was not proper, the confession was sufficiently an act of free will to purge any primary taint. The victim's statements were admissible and, therefore, failure to object to their admission did not constitute ineffective assistance of counsel.


OUTCOME: Conviction affirmed; the officer's warrantless reentry onto the premises, after executing a valid search warrant and leaving, was justified under the exigent circumstances exception to the warrant requirement, because defendant's armed presence at the premises endangered the lives of others.

Citizens for Unif. Taxation v. Northport Pub. Sch. Dist.


No. 209901


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


239 Mich. App. 284; 608 N.W.2d 480; 2000 Mich. App. LEXIS 18


July 8, 1999, Submitted  
January 7, 2000, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Certiorari Denied November 13, 2000, Reported at: 2000 U.S. LEXIS 7518.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] LC No. 96-003865 CZ. Leelanau Circuit Court.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a school property taxation challenge, plaintiff appealed the order of the Leelanau Circuit Court (Michigan) granting summary disposition pursuant to Mich. Ct. R. 2.116(C)(10) in favor of defendants, the school districts and state tax commission.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff challenged the taxation under Mich. Comp. Laws § 380.1211(1) (Mich. Stat. Ann. § 15.41211) stating that it violated the Privileges & Immunities Clause, U.S. Const. art. IV, § 2, and the Equal Protection Clauses, U.S. Const. amend. XIV and Mich. Const. art. 1, § 2. The statute distinguished between homestead property and property that was not a homestead. The statute treated nonresidents and Michigan residents who owned property that did not qualify as homestead property exactly the same. Because nonresidents and residents were not treated differently, the Privileges and Immunities Clause was not implicated. The legislature's interest in granting an exemption to homestead property was to protect and promote homestead property, which was a legitimate state interest. Decreasing the burden of property taxes on homesteads by granting an exemption from the property tax mills was rationally related to legitimate state interest. Therefore, there was no equal protection violation.


OUTCOME: Order affirmed; court held that the school taxation statute did not violate the Privileges and Immunities Clause because it did not treat nonresidents and residents differently; statute did not violate the Equal Protection Clauses because granting homestead exemption from the property tax mills was rationally related to legitimate state interest.
