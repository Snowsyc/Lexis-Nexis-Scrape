DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of the Circuit Court in and for Duval County's (Florida) judgment of conviction and death sentence for first-degree felony murder and aggravated child abuse in the death of his girlfriend's five-month-old daughter.


OVERVIEW: Appellant was convicted of first-degree murder during aggravated child abuse of his girlfriend's infant daughter, and sentenced to death. Appellant hid the baby's body in a pond and claimed she had been kidnapped but later confessed, stating he had dropped her while changing her diaper and shaken her trying to revive her, and then panicked. Death sentence was proportionate; prior felony child abuse was significant aggravator.

Delgado v. State, 2000 Fla. LEXIS 75 (Fla., Feb. 3, 2000)



DISPOSITION: Case remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged a judgment, based upon a jury verdict, of the Circuit Court in and for Dade County (Florida) convicting him on two counts of first-degree murder and one count of armed burglary. Appellant was sentenced to death on each murder count and to life in prison on the burglary count.


OVERVIEW: Appellant was convicted of burglary and two counts of murder after killing two victims in their own home. The State prosecuted the case on the premise that appellant's entry into the victims' home was consensual but that at some point, this consent was withdrawn. Appellant was a business acquaintance of the victims. On appeal, appellant claimed the trial erred in denying his motion for judgment of acquittal on the charges of first-degree murder. Specifically, appellant argued the State's theory of felony murder should not have been presented to the jury. The murder convictions were set aside and the burglary conviction was reversed. In the context of an occupied dwelling, burglary was not intended to cover the situation where an invited guest turns criminal or violent. Rather, burglary was intended to criminalize the conduct of a suspect who terrorizes, shocks, or surprises the unknowing occupants. The conduct of appellant, who's entry into victims' home was consensual, did not amount to burglary.


OUTCOME: Judgment was reversed and case remanded; appellant's actions were not the type of conduct which the crime of burglary was intended to punish. The theory of burglary, and felony murder, offered by the State was legally inadequate. Appellant, an invited guest into victims' home, could not be found guilty of burglary even though victims' later withdrew their consent.
