CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-The tribe's payment in lieu of tax (PILT) was not a tax at all, but rather, a charge that tribes paid to compensate municipalities for public services provided to the exempt property; [2]-Because the PILT was not a tax, it was not subject to Wash. OUTCOME: Judgment reversed.

State v. Baird, 331 P.3d 1172, 2014 Wash. LEXIS 630 (Wash., 2014)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: By a plurality, [1]-Evidence of the defendants' refusals to submit to breath alcohol tests under the implied consent statute, Wash. Rev. Code § 46.20.308(2), could be admitted in their trials on the charges of driving while under the influence of an intoxicant because the right not to submit to the test is statutory, not constitutional; [2]-The implied consent statute does not authorize a warrantless search, and a driver has no constitutional right to refuse a breath test because such a search falls under the search incident to arrest exception to the warrant requirement; [3]-Although the implied consent statute gave the defendants a statutory right to refuse a breath alcohol test, by exercising the privilege to drive, the defendants consented to admission in evidence of their refusals to take a breath test.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners, residents and Kittitas County, challenged respondent governor's exercise of authority to site an energy facility that exclusively used wind power under the Energy Facilities Site Locations Act (EFSLA), Wash. Rev. Code ch. 80.50.


OVERVIEW: The wind energy company filed an application with Energy Facility Site Evaluation Council (EFSEC) for site certification of a wind power project. The court first determined that it had appellate jurisdiction under the certification procedures established in the Washington Administrative Procedure Act (APA), Wash. Rev. Code § 80.50.140, and that Wash. Const. art. IV was not violated by conferring unauthorized original jurisdiction. The petitions for review met the conditions under Wash. Rev. Code § 80.50.140(1), and therefore the court accepted certification from the superior court. Because EFSLA provided no procedures for how the governor could exercise her discretion to approve or reject the application, the court looked to the APA, Wash. Rev. Code § 34.05.570(3), for the scope of review. EFSEC's preemption power did not violate the Growth Management Act, Wash. Rev. Code ch. 36.70A. EFSEC members did not violate the appearance of fairness doctrine. And finally, substantial evidence supported EFSEC's decision to preempt the County's land use and zoning laws based on the criteria under former Wash. Admin. Code § 463-28-040.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the governor's final decision to approve the site certification application.
