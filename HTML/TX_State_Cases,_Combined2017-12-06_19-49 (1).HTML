SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Petition for discretionary review refused by In re Florance, 2010 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 578 (Tex. Crim. App., May 5, 2010)
US Supreme Court certiorari denied by Florance v. Texas, 2010 U.S. LEXIS 6637 (U.S., Oct. 4, 2010)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] 
On Appeal from the County Court at Law No. 3, Collin County, Texas. Trial Court Cause No. 003-81453-06.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from two judgments of the County Court at Law No. 3, Collin County, Texas, one which acquitted her of the offense of failure to appear and one convicting her of consumption of alcohol by a minor in violation of Tex. Alco. Bev. Code Ann. § 106.04 (2007) following a jury trial. The county court assessed her punishment at a fine of $ 75.


OVERVIEW: The court held that it did not have jurisdiction to review the appeal acquitting defendant in the failure to appear case because the judgment was not adverse as required by Tex. R. App P. 25.2(a)(2). Additionally, because the county court assessed her punishment at a fine in the amount of $ 75, the court did not have jurisdiction to review any issues arising out of her alcohol consumption conviction other than her challenge to the constitutionality of § 106.04, pursuant to Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 4.03 (2005). The court determined that § 106.04 was not unconstitutional. It was not void for vagueness or overbroad. It was specific in its terms. It did not substantially implicate constitutionally protected conduct or speech. Also, it was not defined in such a manner that permitted arbitrary and discriminatory enforcement. The court rejected defendant's argument that because her offenses occurred in a private residence, their enforcement was an unconstitutional invasion of the right to privacy. Additionally, § 106.04 was not unconstitutional because it is codified outside of the Texas Penal Code. The court held that § 106.04 did not require a culpable mental state.


OUTCOME: This court reclassified the appeal as an appeal of a criminal conviction. The appeal in the failure to appear case was dismissed. The court directed the clerk to change the style of the case to reflect the correct spelling of defendant's name and to identify the appeal as a criminal appeal. The county court's judgment on the alcohol charge was modified to correctly spell defendant's last name and was affirmed as modified.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] 
On Appeal from the 160th Judicial District Court, Dallas County, Texas. Trial Court Cause No. 06-04868-H.
In re TCI West End, Inc., 2007 Tex. App. LEXIS 2190 (Tex. App. Dallas, Mar. 22, 2007)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant property owner challenged orders of the 160th Judicial District Court, Dallas County, Texas, granting the first amended pleas to the jurisdiction of appellees, a city and the Texas Historical Commission, to the property owner's counterclaims alleging inverse condemnation under Tex. Const. art. I, § 17, a violation of the Texas Private Real Property Rights Preservation Act, and a conspiracy to take its property.


OVERVIEW: The property owner owned land in the city's historic district that included a building. When the property owner demolished the building, appellees filed suit, seeking the rebuilding of the building and civil penalties and damages for violating city ordinances. On appeal, the court held that the trial court properly granted the Commission's plea to the jurisdiction because the property owner failed to establish that the Commission committed a regulatory taking under Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ann. § 315.006, as neither § 315.006 nor the Commission's enforcement claim actually regulated the property. The trial court also properly granted the city's plea to the jurisdiction because the property owner's inverse condemnation claim was not ripe, as there was no final decision regarding the revocation of the property owner's demolition permit and thus the city was not given the opportunity to exercise its full discretion in considering plans for the building before the property owner demolished it. Appellees were immune from liability as to the property owner's conspiracy to take claim pursuant to Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Ann. § 101.057 (2005).


OUTCOME: The orders were affirmed.

Ex parte Weise


NO. 01-99-01195-CR


COURT OF APPEALS OF TEXAS, FIRST DISTRICT, HOUSTON


23 S.W.3d 449; 2000 Tex. App. LEXIS 2453



April 13, 2000, Opinion Issued


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Petition for Discretionary Review Granted on Court's Own Motion With Written Order November 15, 2000.
Reversed by Weise v. State, 55 S.W.3d 617, 2001 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 73 (Tex. Crim. App., 2001)


PRIOR HISTORY: On Appeal from the County Criminal Court at Law No. 5. Harris County, Texas. Trial Court Cause No. 994827. Trial Judge: E. J. Love.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged the County Criminal Court at Law No. 5, Harris County, Texas, denial of his motion to quash the information that charged him and his pretrial application for habeas corpus relief on grounds of unconstitutionality arising from his alleged violation of an anti-trash-dumping statute, Tex. Health & Safety Code Ann. § 365.012.


OVERVIEW: Appellant challenged the denial of his motion to quash the information charging him with violating Tex. Health & Safety Code Ann. § 365.012, and denial of his pretrial habeas corpus application, arguing that the anti-trash-dumping statute was unconstitutional for lack of a culpable mental state. The court reversed, ordering the information dismissed after considering the six Aguirre factors to determine if the legislature meant to impose strict liability. The fact that the statute was a public health and safety law, which normally impose strict liability, was diminished by the relatively minor danger posed by illegal dumping. However, the severity of the punishment imposed, which could be as high as a Class A misdemeanor bearing up to one year's confinement in jail, weighed against strict liability. The court held that a culpable mental state of at least "recklessly" was required, and denying appellant's habeas corpus petition was an abuse of discretion.


OUTCOME: Denial of application was reversed and the information dismissed. Appellant's information was unconstitutional because it lacked a culpable mental state. Although the anti-trash-dumping statute was a public health measure, which normally impose strict liability, the severity of the potential punishment required at least a "reckless" culpable mental state.

Sturgeon v. Sul Ross State Univ.


NO. 03-01-00716-CV


COURT OF APPEALS OF TEXAS, THIRD DISTRICT, AUSTIN


2003 Tex. App. LEXIS 331



January 16, 2003, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Rehearing Petition Overruled February 13, 2003 Released for Publication March 20, 2003.
Petition for review denied by N. C. Sturgeon, L.P. v. Sul Ross State Univ., 2004 Tex. LEXIS 24 (Tex., Jan. 16, 2004)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] FROM THE DISTRICT COURT OF TRAVIS COUNTY, 261ST JUDICIAL DISTRICT. NO. GN101480, HONORABLE MARGARET A. COOPER, JUDGE PRESIDING.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant contractor sued appellee state university for breach of contract and governmental taking when the university withheld funds and then terminated the parties' construction contract. The university filed a plea to the jurisdiction, pleading sovereign immunity from suit. The District Court of Travis County, 261st Judicial District, Texas, granted the university's plea and dismissed the contractor's lawsuit. The contractor appealed.


OVERVIEW: On review, the contractor contended the trial court erred in dismissing its suit. The appellate court found that the supreme court had already explicitly rejected the waiver by conduct exception to sovereign immunity. Further, the instant disputes were inherently related to the provisions of the parties' contract as they related to the quality of the contractor's work; attempting to evade the reach of sovereign immunity by casting the dispute in terms of a takings claim did not change the inherent character of the contract dispute. Even if the claim was analyzed under the takings doctrine, the appellate court found that the university lacked an intent to take and the contractor consented to the university's possession of the disputed property. Thus, granting the university's plea to the jurisdiction was proper. Finally, documentary evidence the contractor attempted to admit only further established that the dispute was over the contractor's performance and rights under the contract; their exclusion did not harm the contractor and did not demonstrate that the university's conduct qualified as any unconstitutional taking of property, as the contractor contended.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

COURT OF APPEALS OF TEXAS, FIFTH DISTRICT, DALLAS


2003 Tex. App. LEXIS 799



January 28, 2003, Opinion Filed


NOTICE:   [*1] PLEASE CONSULT THE TEXAS RULES OF APPELLATE PROCEDURE FOR CITATION OF UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS.


PRIOR HISTORY: On Appeal from the Criminal District Court No. 2. Dallas County, Texas. Trial Court Cause No. F01-73300-PI.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed as Modified.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant pleaded guilty in the Criminal District Court No. 2, Dallas County, Texas, to aggravated assault with a deadly weapon. The trial court deferred adjudicating guilt, placed him on five years' probation, and assessed a $ 1000 fine. Subsequently, the trial court revoked his probation and sentenced him to three years' confinement. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued the trial court's judgment warranted modification to delete a fine that was not orally pronounced at adjudication. He further contended that Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 42.12, which prohibited an appellant from challenging a trial court's determination to proceed with adjudication of guilt, violated the Texas Constitution. The appellate court initially noted defendant did not object in the trial court about the constitutionality of Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 42.12; therefore, he had not preserved his complaint on appeal. However, the appellate court held the denial of a deferred adjudication probationer's right to appeal the trial court's determination to proceed with adjudication was rationally related to a legitimate State interest and was not unconstitutional. The appellate court concluded that, under Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann. art. 42.03, § 1(a) (Vernon Supp. 2003), the trial court's judgment incorrectly included a $ 1000 fine that was not orally pronounced as part of defendant's sentence after the trial judge adjudicated guilt.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed, as modified to delete the fine imposed.

In the Interest of S.N.


NO. 14-07-00161-CV


COURT OF APPEALS OF TEXAS, FOURTEENTH DISTRICT, HOUSTON


287 S.W.3d 183; 2009 Tex. App. LEXIS 3368



March 5, 2009, Opinion on Rehearing Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] 
On Appeal from the 314th District Court, Harris County, Texas. Trial Court Cause No. 2005-10415J.
In the Interest of S.N., 2008 Tex. App. LEXIS 7772 (Tex. App. Houston 14th Dist., Oct. 14, 2008)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant father appealed the judgment of the 314th District Court, Harris County (Texas) that terminated his parental rights.


OVERVIEW: The father argued, inter alia, that the evidence was legally and factually insufficient to support the trial court's finding that the children were removed for neglect. The court of appeals disagreed. A case worker stated that when officers arrived at the home at 2:15 p.m., they found the children home alone, as well as two cat litter boxes overflowing with cat feces, cat feces on a bed, a soiled mattress in the children's room, dirty dishes in the sink, and spoiled milk in the refrigerator. The children told the officers that their mother had left at 2:00 a.m., returned to leave doughnuts, and then left again. After waiting forty-five minutes for the mother, the officers left with the children. The mother arrived later at the police department and was arrested for abandonment of the children. When questioned as to her whereabouts, she told the officer that she went to the store to buy milk. However, the officer informed the case worker that there was a corner store that sold milk 300 yards from the house, and that she had not been in there. Therefore, the evidence is legally and factually sufficient to support the trial court's finding that the children were removed for neglect.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.
