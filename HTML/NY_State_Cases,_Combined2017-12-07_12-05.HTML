Crimes -- Possession of Weapon -- Self-Defense

The information charging defendant with criminal possession of a weapon in the fourth degree based upon allegations that he possessed a box cutter in his right front pants pocket and that when asked about the box cutter, the defendant told a police officer that he possessed the box cutter "to protect myself," was facially insufficient. CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, QUEENS COUNTY


54 Misc. 3d 978; 41 N.Y.S.3d 864; 2016 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4317; 2016 NY Slip Op 26384

HEADNOTES

Local Laws -- Preemption by State -- Field Preemption

1. Administrative Code of the City of New York § 19-190, which requires a motorist to yield to a pedestrian or cyclist with the right-of-way, is not preempted by the Vehicle and Traffic Law under the doctrine of field preemption. Field preemption occurs when the state legislature has explicitly or implicitly stated its intention to be the sole arbiter in a certain area of law. While the Vehicle and Traffic Law imposes consistent rules and regulations on drivers and pedestrians throughout the state and restricts and invalidates enactment or enforcement of inconsistent laws, it was not intended to be the sole governing body in the area. The New York State Constitution's home rule provision confers broad power upon local government relating to the welfare of its citizens. Cities having a population in excess of one million, like New York City, are authorized, under Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1642, to restrict and regulate traffic and pedestrian use of any highway by local law, ordinance, order, rule, regulation or health code provision. Moreover, Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1642 expressly allows local rules to supersede the state law (Vehicle and Traffic Law), if in conflict. That deference to local authorities explicitly shows that the state legislature did not intend the Vehicle and Traffic Law to preempt the field.

Local Laws -- Preemption by State -- Conflict Preemption

2. Administrative Code of the City of New York § 19-190, which requires a motorist to yield to a pedestrian or cyclist with the right-of-way, is not preempted by Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1146 under the doctrine of conflict preemption. Administrative Code § 19-190 and Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1146 both punish motorists who fail to exercise due care and fail to yield the right-of-way. Under Administrative Code § 19-190, a motorist who fails to yield without causing injury can be charged with a traffic infraction. If the motorist injures a pedestrian or bicyclist while failing to yield, the motorist is charged with a misdemeanor. Under Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1146, a motorist is not charged unless a pedestrian, bicyclist or animal is injured. If there is an injury, the motorist is charged with a traffic infraction. Repeat offenders, within the preceding five years, would be charged with a misdemeanor. While it is clear that the difference in penalty could result in inconsistencies in whether a motorist is charged, what the motorist is charged with and the potential penalty a motorist faces, preemption is only required if Administrative Code § 19-190 conflicts with conduct prohibited by the state, prohibits conduct specifically permitted by the state, or imposes restrictions on rights granted by the state. Moreover, even if a conflict existed or a due process violation was found, Administrative Code § 19-190 would supercede Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1146 as Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1642 (a) (10) expressly permits local law to supercede state law in the area of pedestrian and vehicle right-of-way.

Crimes -- Violation of Municipal Code -- Failure to Yield Right-of-Way -- Constitutionality of Code Provision

3. Administrative Code of the City of New York § 19-190, which requires a motorist to yield to a pedestrian or cyclist with the right-of-way using the "due care" standard, does not unjustifiably restrict defendant's state constitutional rights by not requiring a more stringent standard of care and/or mens rea of defendant's wrongdoing. The statute allows for an affirmative defense that the motorist exercised due care. In the context of Administrative Code § 19-190 (and related Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1146), due care has been defined as the care of a reasonably prudent and competent driver to avoid colliding with pedestrians and cyclists. Given that a due care-reasonably prudent standard is recognized in several New York criminal statutes and has been upheld by the Court of Appeals, it is clear that the standard is accepted, and therefore does not unjustifiably restrict defendant's state constitutional rights.

Constitutional Law--Right to Bear Arms--Validity of Penal Law § 265.01 (1), Possession of Gravity Knife

Penal Law § 265.01 (1), to the extent it criminalizes possession of gravity knives, does not violate the Second Amendment because, narrowly tailored as such, it was designed as a valid and constitutionally permissible exercise of the state's police power. The statute, like all legislative enactments, is supported by a presumption of validity demanding a demonstration of invalidity beyond a reasonable doubt and will be struck down only as a last un-avoidable result. Particular enactments that are fundamentally intended to protect public safety, in the form of a specific weapon prohibition, are subject to yet another factor weighing against a declaration of constitutional invalidity--the Second Amendment right to bear arms is subject to reasonable regulation pursuant to a state's police power. New York's prohibition against gravity knives appears to have been intended as such an exercise of reasonable regulation. When it was enacted in 1958, the legislature narrowly defined gravity knife to refer to a particular type of bladed instrument that was found to be nearly exclusively used as a weapon of choice of criminals, a practical replacement for the previously outlawed switchblade knife. Given that legislative intent, as applied to the cultural circumstances prevalent at the time of the law's enactment, the current version of Penal Law § 265.01 (1) prohibiting possession of gravity knives is not violative of the Second Amendment and defendant did not overcome the statute's strong presumption of constitutionality.

HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law -- Validity of Statute -- Criminality of "Lack of Due Care" Statute -- Failure to Yield Right-of-Way to Pedestrian

Administrative Code of the City of New York § 19-190 (b), which provides that a driver of a motor vehicle that makes contact with a pedestrian or bicyclist who has the right-of-way and thereby causes physical injury to that person shall be guilty of a misdemeanor, if the driver failed to use due care (Administrative Code § 19-190 [c]), is impermissibly vague and, therefore, facially unconstitutional. The statute turns on a purported culpable mental state of due care. However, except for specifically identified strict liability crimes, intentionality in some form such as purposely, knowingly, or recklessly, is required for criminality. Moreover, proof of criminal conduct must be beyond a reasonable doubt, as opposed to the civil preponderance of the evidence standard. The familiar civil liability feature of the due care standard of a reasonable person is inconsistent with the conventional requirement of criminal wrongdoing--awareness of some wrongdoing. Thus, Administrative Code § 19-190 (b)'s use of a civil tort liability standard of negligence instead of the higher criminal standard of mens rea violates the defendant's rights and protections under the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments to the United States Constitution and the New York State Constitution. The introduction of criminal guideline elements into civil negligence liability standards and the resulting vagueness--which offers no guidance to the judiciary, the People, or law enforcement, and can lead to arbitrary application of the law--violate the defendant's due process rights.

Crimes -- Violation of Municipal Code -- Failure to Yield Right-of-Way to Pedestrian -- Constitutionality of Code Provision

An accusatory instrument charging defendant with an unclassified misdemeanor for violating the Right-of-Way Law (Administrative Code of City of NY § 19-190) by striking and causing physical injuries to a pedestrian in a crosswalk with a motor vehicle was dismissed because the law is facially unconstitutional under both the State and Federal Constitutions. Under the Right-of-Way Law, "any driver of a motor vehicle who fails to yield to a pedestrian . . . when such pedestrian . . . has the right of way . . . and whose motor vehicle causes contact with a pedestrian . . . and thereby causes physical injury" is guilty of a misdemeanor, punishable by a fine and imprisonment for not more than 30 days, unless "the failure to yield and/or physical injury was not caused by the driver's failure to exercise due care" (Administrative Code § 19-190 [a]-[c]). CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-Defendants, a city, police department, and officer, were not entitled to summary judgment on plaintiff's false arrest and false imprisonment claims; although the officer had probable cause to arrest plaintiff once he admitted money found on his person was counterfeit, his testimony, if credited, negated probable cause to initially stop him, which was fatal to defendants' claim of qualified immunity for his subsequent arrest; [2]-As there was a fact issue as to whether the officer had probable cause for his initial stop of plaintiff's vehicle, defendants were not entitled to summary judgment on plaintiff's malicious prosecution claim; [3]-Plaintiff's negligent hiring, retention, and supervision claims had to be dismissed because defendants admitted that at all relevant times, the officer had acted with the scope of his employment with the city and department.


OUTCOME: Defendants' motion was granted in part and denied in part.


HEADNOTES

Torts -- False Imprisonment -- False Arrest -- Probable Cause

1. In a civil action arising out of plaintiff's arrest for criminally possessing a forged instrument, defendants were not entitled to summary judgment dismissing the claims for false arrest and false imprisonment because they failed to establish, beyond a factual dispute, that plaintiff's arrest was based on probable cause. When confronted with proof of false arrest and/or imprisonment, a defendant can nevertheless prevail if he or she proves legal justification for the arrest and imprisonment, which may be established by showing that the arrest was based on probable cause. According to plaintiff, defendant police officer stopped plaintiff's vehicle after plaintiff made a legal turn, ordered plaintiff to exit the vehicle, handcuffed him and proceeded to search the vehicle, during which the officer found money he alleged to be counterfeit. The officer, however, testified that he stopped plaintiff's vehicle after he saw plaintiff make an illegal U-turn and noticed a $100 bill with smeared ink on the center console, and that plaintiff admitted the bill was fake and that he had more fake money in his glove compartment. The evidence thus raised an issue of fact as to whether the stop of plaintiff's vehicle was lawful, and as such whether the arrest thereafter was lawful.

Torts -- Malicious Prosecution -- Probable Cause

2. Defendants were not entitled to summary judgment dismissing plaintiff's malicious prosecution claim arising out of his arrest for criminally possessing a forged instrument following a police stop of his vehicle, where issues of fact existed regarding whether the police had probable cause to stop plaintiff's vehicle. The elements of the cause of action for malicious prosecution stemming from a prior criminal proceeding are the commencement or continuation of a prior criminal proceeding by the defendant, the termination of the prior proceeding in favor of the plaintiff, the absence of probable cause for the initiation of the prior criminal proceeding, and actual malice. Whether there is probable cause to initiate a prosecution hinges on whether the defendant's conduct at the time he or she commenced the prior proceeding would have led a reasonably prudent person to initiate the proceeding. Plaintiff testified that his vehicle was stopped after he made a legal turn at a traffic light, while defendant police officer testified that he stopped plaintiff's vehicle after he saw plaintiff make an illegal U-turn.

Employment Relationships -- Respondent Superior -- Police Officer Acting within Scope of Employment

3. Plaintiff's cause of action alleging negligent hiring, retention and training of defendant police officer who stopped his vehicle and subsequently arrested him for criminally possessing a forged instrument was dismissed, where defendant municipality and its police department conceded that the officer's actions were within the scope of his employment. Generally, where an employee is acting within the scope of his or her employment, thereby rendering the employer liable for any damages caused by the employee's negligence under a theory of respondeat superior, no claim may proceed against the employer for negligent hiring or retention. Within their amended answer, defendants answered for the police officer, thereby admitting that at all relevant times he was acting within the scope of his employment.

Civil Rights -- Federal Civil Rights Claim -- Failure to Allege Municipal Policy or Custom

4. In a civil action arising out of plaintiff's arrest for criminally possessing a forged instrument following a police stop of his vehicle, plaintiff's cause of action pursuant to 42 USC § 1983 against defendant municipality and its police department was dismissed because plaintiff failed to plead the existence of an official policy or custom which deprived him of a constitutional right. A municipality bears liability under 42 USC § 1983 only where the action by its agent alleged to be unconstitutional implements or executes a policy statement, ordinance, regulation, or decision officially adopted and promulgated by that body's officers. Accordingly, municipal liability only lies if the municipal policy or custom actually caused the constitutional tort and not merely because the municipality employs a tortfeasor who perpetrated a constitutional tort. Plaintiff failed to plead specific facts establishing that his arrest, assault, imprisonment and prosecution were the result of a municipal custom or practice, and failed to identify the municipal custom and practice which he alleged existed and which resulted in his arrest, imprisonment and prosecution.

OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-The State was entitled to permanently enjoin the independent subscription agents from soliciting business in violation of General Business Law § 335-a by sending magazine renewal subscriptions to New York subscribers without disclosing the month and year in which the subscription expired because jurisdiction was appropriate under CPLR 302(a) where a servicer and its owner reviewed and processed the solicitations, derived substantial revenue from interstate commerce, and should have anticipated that the solicitations would have consequences within New York, the fact that the regulation might preclude the agents from sending solicitations to New York did not render it unconstitutional, and the disclaimer on the back of the solicitations raised a question of fact as to whether the agents' solicitations amounted to deceptive acts under General Business Law §§ 349 and 350.

HEADNOTES

Courts--Jurisdiction--Long-Arm Jurisdiction--Out-of-State Consulting Firm

1. In a special proceeding commenced by petitioner Attorney General alleging that respondent independent subscription agents in the business of soliciting magazine subscriptions and renewals sent misleading solicitations to New York consumers in violation of the General Business Law, respondent out-of-state consulting firm--providing bookkeeping, data management, consumer mail processing and consumer refund processing support for the solicitation business--was subject to long-arm jurisdiction in New York. Though the firm was careful not to project itself into New York or to transact business there, it was subject to jurisdiction under CPLR 302 (a) (1) where it had, albeit indirectly, availed itself of the benefit of New York consumers and laws. The firm's reason for being was to support and facilitate the solicitations sent to New York consumers, it had a role in formulating the content thereof and its profits flowed therefrom. Moreover, the firm's clients--other respondents providing the firm's revenue--were owned by a New York limited liability company. The firm was also subject to jurisdiction under CPLR 302 (a) (3) as it allegedly committed the tortious act of reviewing and processing solicitations that violated New York law, and it clearly derived substantial revenue from interstate commerce and should have anticipated that the solicitations would have consequences in New York. 2. The owner herself conducted all of the activity ascribed to the firm in the petition, and any benefit that accrued to the firm also accrued to the owner. The legislature's legitimate purpose in passing the statute was consumer protection, and that interest was rationally related to the statutory obligation, which applies to for-profit entities engaged in soliciting magazine subscriptions. Similarly, the statute was not rendered unconstitutional because independent subscription agents, which typically have no relationship to magazine publishers and thus no way of knowing whether subscriptions exist or when they expire, might be precluded from sending solicitations to New York. 4. In a special proceeding commenced by petitioner Attorney General alleging that respondent independent subscription agents in the business of soliciting magazine subscriptions and renewals sent misleading solicitations to New York consumers, questions of fact existed to be determined at a hearing before a factfinder on the claims under General Business Law § 349, prohibiting deceptive business practices, and its companion, General Business Law § 350, prohibiting false advertising. HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Confession -- Noncustodial Statements -- Statement Made by Police Officer Relating to Official Duties

1. Defendant police officer, whose statement that she observed an assault formed the basis of a complaint that she signed under penalty of perjury, but who later admitted in a statement to an assistant district attorney (ADA) that she arrived at the scene "after the assault was over," was not in custody for purposes of Miranda at the time she made the statement in the ADA's office. A reasonable person in defendant's position, innocent of any wrongdoing, would not have believed he or she was in custody. Defendant, who was the arresting officer and a key witness in the assault case, was notified by the ADA to report to his office for the purpose of preparing the case for trial. She arrived at the District Attorney's office by her own transportation, her movements were not restricted and there was no discussion of her being detained for her conduct. It would be absurd to consider that a police officer is in custody for purposes of Miranda each time the officer is directed to appear at the District Attorney's office in furtherance of the officer's job duties.

Crimes -- Immunity from Prosecution -- Statement Made by Police Officer Relating to Official Duties

2. A statement made by defendant police officer to an assistant district attorney (ADA)--that, despite having previously stated that she observed an assault, she arrived at the scene "after the assault was over"--was not immunized from use in a criminal proceeding against defendant. Here, defendant was not told that she would be dismissed if she refused to answer the ADA's questions; she was simply notified by the ADA to come to his office to further discuss the assault case in which she was the arresting officer and a key witness. Moreover, the District Attorney's office was not defendant's employer, and neither that office nor the ADA had the authority to terminate her employment. Finally, defendant's subjective fears as to what might have happened if she failed to appear at the District Attorney's office were insufficient to immunize her statement.

3. Defendant police officer, whose statement that she observed an assault formed the basis of a complaint that she signed under penalty of perjury, but who later admitted in a statement to an assistant district attorney that she arrived at the scene "after the assault was over," was granted a Huntley hearing to determine the admissibility of her statement, notwithstanding that she was not in custody for purposes of Miranda at the time she gave the statement and that the statement was not immunized from use in a criminal proceeding against defendant. JUSTICE COURT OF NEW YORK, VILLAGE OF MASSAPEQUA PARK, NASSAU COUNTY


45 Misc. 3d 458; 994 N.Y.S.2d 256; 2014 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3882; 2014 NY Slip Op 24245



July 30, 2014


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: ISSUE: Whether an appearance summons charging defendant with violating Village of Massapequa Park, N.Y., Code § 279-3, by residing as a registered sex offender within one mile of schools and parks, was to be dismissed. HOLDINGS: [1]-The court was unable to determine whether defendant resided at the subject premises pursuant to an order of a county court issued on the date that he was alleged to have violated the provision; [2]-The requirement of written notice to defendant and an opportunity to relocate contemplated by the provision rendered the regulation prospective; [3]-The court found that Village of Massapequa Park, N.Y., Code § 279-3, as applied to defendant, was preempted by the New York Sex Offender Registration Act, Correction Law § 168 et seq., because he was serving a court imposed sentence of probation under the supervision of a county probation department.


OUTCOME: Appearance summons and information dismissed.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Sex Offenders -- Residency Restrictions -- Residing at Premises Pursuant to Court Order Approving Conditions of Probation

1. Defendant, a level one sex offender under the Sex Offender Registration Act (Correction Law art 6-C), was not entitled to dismissal of an information charging him with violating Code of the Village of Massapequa Park § 279-3, which prohibited registered sex offenders from residing within one mile of a school, on the ground that defendant resided at the premises pursuant to a County Court order approving the conditions of his probation and thus permitting him to remain at the residence. Absent from the record was proof of the date on which defendant was first permitted by the Probation Department to remain at the premises, or of when that condition was first approved by County Court. Thus, it could not be held, as a matter of fact, that defendant resided at the premises pursuant to County Court's order on the date of the alleged violation.

Crimes -- Sex Offenders -- Residency Restrictions -- Establishment of Residence Prior to Enactment of Local Law Restricting Residency

2. The information charging defendant, a level one sex offender under the Sex Offender Registration Act (Correction Law art 6-C), with violating Code of the Village of Massapequa Park § 279-3, which prohibited registered sex offenders from "establish[ing] a residence" within one mile of a school, based on the allegation that defendant "resid[ed]" within a restricted location, was facially sufficient. Even if defendant had established his residence before the enactment of Village Code § 279-3, the Village Board, prior to defendant's alleged violation, repealed the local law which made the residency restrictions of section 279-3 inapplicable to offenders who had established their residences prior to its enactment. Moreover, pursuant to Village Code § 279-3, each day an offender continued to reside within a restricted area constituted a separate violation subject to a fine. Thus, although the regulation expressly restricted the establishment of a residence, the scheme, history and purpose of Village Code chapter 279 reflected an intent to also prohibit registered sex offenders from continuing to reside within a restricted area.

Crimes -- Sex Offenders -- Residency Restrictions -- Local Law Preempted

3. Defendant, a level one sex offender under the Sex Offender Registration Act (SORA) (Correction Law art 6-C) who lived 1,360 feet from a school, was entitled to dismissal of an information charging him with violating Code of the Village of Massapequa Park § 279-3, which prohibited registered sex offenders from residing within one mile of a school, as that local law was preempted by state law precluding certain sex offenders on probation from residing within 1,000 feet of a school (Penal Law §§ 65.10 [4-a]; 220.00 [14]). New York's preemption doctrine precludes local legislation where an express conflict exists between state and local laws, or where the state has clearly evinced a desire to preempt an entire field. By way of SORA, the state legislature enacted a comprehensive and detailed regulatory scheme regarding the registration and regulation of sex offenders who are on probation, thus demonstrating an intent to preempt more restrictive local laws regulating the same subject matter. As defendant was on probation, SORA's residency restrictions preempted those imposed by Village Code § 279-3.

401875/13

44 Misc. 3d 695; 992 N.Y.S.2d 393; 2014 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2779; 2014 NY Slip Op 24170

CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-An agency's demand for payment of a $ 1,000 fine, as a condition of the business license renewal, was in excess of its authority as Administrative Code of the City of NY §§ 20-101 and 20-104(e)(3) were interpreted in a manner not supported by their plain language as the city had not bestowed the agency with the power to decline to renew a pedicab business license based on the owner's failure to pay fines assessed in connection with a pedicab driver's license.


OUTCOME: Petition granted, in part; agency determination denying the driver a renewal of his business license was annulled; matter was remitted to the agency for further proceedings.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Licenses -- Pedicabs -- Renewal of Pedicab Business License Conditioned on Payment of Fine Associated with Pedicab Driver's License

Following the receipt by petitioner, a pedicab owner and driver, of a violation in connection with his pedicab driver's license and the imposition of a fine relating thereto, the demand by respondent Department of Consumer Affairs for payment of the fine as a condition of renewing petitioner's pedicab business license was in excess of its authority as that agency is not bestowed with the power to decline to renew a pedicab business license based on the owner's failure to pay fines assessed in connection with a pedicab driver's license. The agency cited no case law to suggest that petitioner's refusal to pay the fine implicated his honesty and integrity under Administrative Code of City of NY § 20-101, especially where his defiance was based on a concern that payment of the fine would "moot out" his opportunity to vindicate his constitutional rights relating to the underlying violation. Moreover, while the agency has authority to "suspend the license of any person pending payment of such fine or civil penalty" under Administrative Code § 20-104 (e) (3), that section ties the fine to the particular license under which the fine was issued [***2] by its reference to "the license" and "such" fine, and that section, by its terms, relates only to a suspension of a license without any indication that it applies to a renewal. Finally, a review of other Administrative Code provisions relating specifically to violations by pedicab drivers, which place certain obligations but not others on pedicab owners, indicated that the legislature did not intend to hold a pedicab owner responsible for fines incurred by a pedicab driver, even when the business is operated by the driver.

Matter of Allen v. New York State Dept. of Motor Vehicles


01-13-ST4610


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, ALBANY COUNTY


45 Misc. 3d 475; 2014 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2772; 2014 NY Slip Op 24166



May 21, 2014


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed by Matter of Joy v New York State Dept. of Motor Vehicles, 133 AD3d 1167, 20 NYS3d 475, 2015 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 8770 (N.Y. App. Div. 3d Dep't, Nov. 25, 2015)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-Petitioner, whose application for a new driver's license was denied by the DMV, filed suit challenging regulations adopted by the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles regarding drivers, like her, who had multiple DUIs. There was no conflict between Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1193(2)(b)(12)(b) (regulating DUI-related license revocations) and 15 NYCRR pt. 136 (same), as no legislative policy required the Commissioner to abandon her discretion with respect to the decision to reissue a revoked license; [2]-In adopting pt. 136, the Commissioner did not invade the province of the legislature, as it had explicitly granted her exclusive administrative authority over the revocation and issuance of driver licenses; [3]-The Commission's temporary suspension of the review of new license applications until after pt. 136 took effect was within her inherent discretionary authority.


OUTCOME: The Commissioner's motion for summary judgment was granted.


HEADNOTES

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Waiver of Permanent License Revocation

1. Revised 15 NYCRR part 136 (eff Sept. 25, 2012), imposing an additional, five-year waiting period before a relicensing application will be granted where a driver's license was revoked for an alcohol- or drug-related driving offense and the driver had three or four such offenses within a 25-year look-back period (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b] [3]), does not conflict with Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1193 (2) (b) (12) (b), directing waiver after five years of the "permanent" license revocation required by section 1193 (2) (b) (12) (a) for certain alcohol- and drug-related driving offenses, or section 1193 (2) (b) (12) (e), permitting waiver after eight years of the "permanent" license revocation required by section 1193 (2) (b) (12) (d) for certain alcohol- or drug-related driving offenses. Under section 1193 (2) (b) (12) (b), the waiver of a "permanent" license revocation is subject to a case by case review by the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles in the interest of public safety and welfare, and section 1193 (2) (b) (12) (e) likewise does not require restoration of a permanently revoked license. Thus, waiver is not mandated so long as the Commissioner exercises his or her discretion. Moreover, the Commissioner has been delegated broad authority to oversee the relicensing of revoked driver's licenses, and the revocation periods set forth in section 1193 (2) (b) are expressly described as "minimum periods." Finally, section 1193 (2) (b) (12) (c) provides for the conditional or restricted operation of a motor vehicle after certain mandatory revocation periods subject to such criteria, terms and conditions as established by the Commissioner, and 15 NYCRR 136.5 (b) (3)'s waiting period merely delays issuance of a conditional or restricted license.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Ignition Interlock Device

2. Revised 15 NYCRR part 136's (eff Sept. 25, 2012) requirement, upon the relicensing of a driver whose license was revoked for an alcohol- or drug-related driving offense, for installation of an ignition interlock device (IID), in connection with issuance of an A2 restricted license, on vehicles owned or operated by the driver (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b] [3]) does not conflict with Penal Law § 65.10 (2) (k-1) or Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1198 (2), which, respectively, expressly authorize and direct a court, when imposing a sentence of probation or conditional discharge in connection with certain alcohol-related driving offenses, to require installation of an IID. Given the legislative policy to uniformly require installation of an IID after conviction of most alcohol-related driving offenses (see Penal Law § 60.21), the administrative imposition of an IID requirement in connection with the issuance of a conditional or restrictive license following the revocation of a driver's license for an alcohol- or drug-related driving offense is merely a rational extension of such policy, and the legislature conferred sufficient authority upon the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles to adopt revised part 136's IID requirement.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Lifetime Review and Look-Back Periods

3. Revised 15 NYCRR part 136's (eff Sept. 25, 2012) directive that, upon an application for relicensing following the revocation of a driver's license for an alcohol- or drug-related driving offense, the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles conduct a lifetime review of the driver's driving record (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b]) and its imposition of lifetime (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b] [1]) and 25-year (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b] [2], [3]) look-back periods in determining whether the Commissioner should grant or deny the relicensing application do not conflict with numerous provisions of the Vehicle and Traffic Law imposing lesser look-back periods. The mere fact that the legislature, in limited circumstances and unrelated contexts, imposed its own look-back periods does not prohibit the Commissioner from administratively imposing different ones for other purposes.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Lifetime Denial of Relicensing Application

4. Revised 15 NYCRR part 136 (eff Sept. 25, 2012), permitting the lifetime denial of an application for relicensing where the driver has five or more alcohol- or drug-related driving offenses in his or her lifetime (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b] [1]) or three or four such offenses and one or more serious driving offenses within a 25-year look-back period (15 NYCRR 136.5 [b] [2]), does not conflict with Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1193 (2) (c) (3), imposing a lifetime prohibition with respect to the issuance of a new license where the driver has been twice convicted of certain alcohol- or drug-related driving offenses and physical injury resulted from each incident. Although the legislature, through section 1193 (2) (c) (3), spoke with regard to how a relicensing application should be handled in one specific situation, the language of that section does not establish a legislative intent to prevent the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles, in the exercise of his or her broad discretion, from imposing a lifetime restriction on relicensing in other appropriate circumstances.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Separation of Powers

5. The adoption by the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles of revised 15 NYCRR part 136 (eff Sept. 25, 2012), which governs relicensing following the revocation of a driver's license for, among other things, alcohol- or drug-related driving offenses, did not overstep the line between administrative rulemaking and legislative policymaking so as to violate the separation of powers doctrine. In determining whether administrative action is in contravention of the separation of powers doctrine, courts consider whether the agency acted within its legislatively delegated policy goals, whether the agency was merely filling in the details of broad legislation describing the overall policies to be implemented, whether the legislature repeatedly tried but failed to adopt legislation in the area and whether the agency has special expertise in the area. Here, revised part 136 falls squarely within the policy and purpose of the Vehicle and Traffic Law, which is to protect public safety and welfare, and it was not adopted pursuant to extraneous social or economic policy issues. The legislature properly delegated the Commissioner exclusive administrative authority over the issuance of driver's licenses, and revised part 136 merely carries out that legislatively delegated authority. Despite a single, prior attempt, the legislature has not repeatedly failed to legislate in the area of relicensing, and public safety and welfare with regard to the operation of motor vehicles upon state roadways is a matter within the Commissioner's technical competence.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Legislative Preemption

6. The adoption by the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles of revised 15 NYCRR part 136 (eff Sept. 25, 2012), which governs relicensing following the revocation of a driver's license for, among other things, alcohol- or drug-related driving offenses, did not violate the principle of legislative preemption. No provision of the Vehicle and Traffic Law expressly precludes or limits the promulgation of rules or regulations with regard to the reissuance of previously revoked driver's licenses, and, given that the legislature granted the Commissioner broad powers with regard to rulemaking and discretionary review in that area, revised part 136 is not inconsistent or out of harmony with the Vehicle and Traffic Law.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Ex Post Facto Law

7. Revised 15 NYCRR part 136 (eff Sept. 25, 2012), which governs relicensing following the revocation of a driver's license for, among other things, alcohol- or drug-related driving offenses, did not violate the Ex Post Facto Clause, which prohibits legislation making criminal an act not criminal when committed or increasing the punishment for a previously committed offense, as the purpose of revised part 136 is to protect the public safety and welfare, not to impose punitive sanctions. Incidental thereto, although in anticipation of revised part 136's promulgation all pending relicensing applications were temporarily held by the Commissioner of Motor Vehicles to be reviewed in a uniform fashion under the new regulations, petitioner's relicensing application, which ultimately was denied pursuant to 15 NYCRR 136.5 (b) (3), was subject to review under revised part 136 as it had not been finally determined as of the time revised part 136 became effective.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Department of Motor Vehicles Relicensure Regulations -- Due Process

8. Upon the revocation of her driver's license for an alcohol-related driving offense, petitioner's due process rights were not implicated by the denial of her application for relicensing pursuant to revised 15 NYCRR part 136. Although a driver's license is a substantial property interest that may not be deprived without due process of law, that due process right arises only where the applicant has a legitimate claim of entitlement to the license, not merely a unilateral expectation. Here, given that her license previously was revoked, petitioner possessed, at best, a unilateral expectation that she would receive a new license, not a bona fide right thereto. Further, while there is no due process right to a particular state-created review procedure, sufficient protections were made available to petitioner to fulfill the requirements of procedural due process concerning review of her relicensing application by way of an administrative appeal and CPLR article 78 review of the adverse administrative determination.

Airbnb, Inc. v. Schneiderman


5393-13


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, ALBANY COUNTY


44 Misc. 3d 351; 989 N.Y.S.2d 786; 2014 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2270; 2014 NY Slip Op 24129



May 13, 2014


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-The internet platform's assertion was without merit that a factual predicate had not been established as there was evidence that a substantial number of their clients or hosts may be in violation of the Multiple Dwelling Law and/or New York State and/or New York City tax provisions; [2]-The subpoena was not limited to New York City Hosts or those who resided in cities, towns or villages that had adopted the Multiple Dwelling Law, nor was it limited to rentals of less than 30 days; [3]-With respect to the New York City Hotel Room Occupancy Tax, the subpoena was not limited to New York City Hosts, nor did it take into account the exceptions that existed; [4]-The subpoena was overbroad; [5]-The subpoena was not unduly burdensome; [6]-Any arguments concerning the constitutional vagueness of statutes which might be applied were not ripe for review.


OUTCOME: The application to quash was granted, and the cross-motion to compel was denied.


HEADNOTES

Attorney General -- Subpoena Power -- Factual Basis for Issuance of Subpoena

1. Upon petitioner's application to quash a subpoena served pursuant to an investigation by the Attorney General relating to petitioner's clients, individuals who rent their apartments located in New York City and State as lodging accommodations via petitioner's Internet platform, a factual predicate for issuance of the subpoena, which sought information identifying petitioner's clients, their accommodations, the dates and durations of guest stay, the rates charged and payment methods, their revenues through petitioner's website and their tax-related communications with the website, was established by evidence that a substantial number of petitioner's clients might be in violation of the Multiple Dwelling Law and/or New York City and/or State tax provisions. Under Multiple Dwelling Law § 4 (8) (a), a class A multiple dwelling can only be used for permanent residence purposes, which requires occupancy of the unit by the same person or family for at least 30 consecutive days. Nevertheless, an investigator with the Attorney General's Internet Bureau, who conducted searches of petitioner's website, averred that petitioner's clients were violating the Multiple Dwelling Law by renting their class A multiple dwelling residences to guests for periods of less than 30 days while not also residing therein. OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-In a landlord's summary holdover proceeding, alleging that a rent-controlled tenant had sublet the rental premises without permission, the tenant established excusable default pursuant to CPLR 5015(a)(1) because his failure to appear was due to his incarceration, which was not deemed willful due to his many attempts to respond to the proceeding and the failures of various notices; [2]-The tenant made a showing of meritorious defenses because another occupant of the premises was a roommate and not a subletter, it was unconstitutional to extinguish his tenancy rights due to his incarceration under N.Y. Const. art. II, § 4, and there were multiple procedural defects in the pleadings; [3]-Laches did not bar the tenant's day in court, as his default was not willful and the delay in seeking to vacate the default was beyond his control.


OUTCOME: Motion to vacate default granted.


HEADNOTES

Judgments -- Default Judgment -- Vacatur -- Excusable Default 

1. Upon entry of a default judgment of possession in favor of petitioner landlord following an inquest conducted in a summary holdover proceeding brought by petitioner against respondent tenant, who was incarcerated at the time the proceeding was commenced, based on allegations that respondent sublet his rent-controlled apartment without petitioner's permission, respondent demonstrated in support of his application to vacate the default judgment under CPLR 5015 (a) (1) that his incarceration constituted a reasonable excuse for his failure to appear at the inquest. Respondent made many and varied attempts to respond to the proceeding, thereby establishing that his failure to appear was not willful; he attempted to retain counsel to represent him in the proceeding; he repeatedly made inquiries at the prison about being produced for the inquest; he sent his common-law wife to court to speak on his behalf; and he sent letters to the court after the inquest explaining why he failed to appear. Moreover, the orders to produce issued by the inquest court, which failed to result in respondent's production at the inquest, did not comply with the statutory requirement that they be signed by a Supreme Court Justice, and the orders used an outdated form requesting respondent's physical production at the inquest rather than his production via videoconference.

Guardian and Ward -- Guardian Ad Litem -- Appointment of Guardian Ad Litem to Represent Incarcerated Respondent in Summary Holdover Proceeding

2. Upon entry of a default judgment of possession in favor of petitioner landlord following an inquest conducted in a summary holdover proceeding brought by petitioner against respondent tenant, who was incarcerated at the time the proceeding was commenced, based on allegations that respondent sublet his rent-controlled apartment without petitioner's permission, respondent was denied due process as a result of the inquest court's failure to appoint a guardian ad litem to represent him throughout the proceeding. Trial courts must insure that incarcerated litigants have a meaningful opportunity to be heard, and a litigant who fails to appear in a summary proceeding due to incarceration and whom the court is unable to produce via videoconference or otherwise is unable to adequately defend his or her rights. Here, despite his many and varied attempts to respond to the proceeding, respondent was unable to appear at the inquest due to his incarceration, and the orders to produce issued by the inquest court failed to result in respondent's production at the inquest. Nevertheless, there was nothing in the record to indicate that the inquest court even considered the possibility of appointing a guardian ad litem to represent respondent.

Judgments -- Default Judgment -- Vacatur -- Meritorious Defense

3. Upon entry of a default judgment of possession in favor of petitioner landlord following an inquest conducted in a summary holdover proceeding brought by petitioner against respondent tenant, who was incarcerated at the time the proceeding was commenced, based on allegations that respondent sublet his rent-controlled apartment without petitioner's permission, respondent sufficiently articulated the existence of meritorious defenses to the proceeding in support of his application to vacate the default judgment under CPLR 5015 (a) (1). Respondent asserted that he had a roommate, not subletting, arrangement with an individual who resided in the apartment both before and after his incarceration but who vacated the apartment prior to the inquest, and he further asserted that, at the time of the inquest, the only alleged occupants of the apartment were his minor child and the child's mother, who could not be considered subtenants given their familial relationship with respondent. Moreover, absence from a rent regulated apartment due to incarceration is excusable and does not preclude the assertion of the tenant's rights. Finally, the pleadings on their face contained numerous defects, including defects in the petition as well as the predicate notices, which were acknowledged by petitioner.

Equity -- Laches -- Delay in Seeking Vacatur of Default Judgment

4. Upon entry of a default judgment of possession in favor of petitioner landlord following an inquest conducted in a summary holdover proceeding brought by petitioner against respondent tenant, who was incarcerated at the time the proceeding was commenced, based on allegations that respondent sublet his rent-controlled apartment without petitioner's permission, laches did not bar vacatur of the default judgment. Respondent's failure to appear at the inquest was not willful; respondent's three-year delay in seeking vacatur of the default judgment was beyond his control; and the prejudice to petitioner, which spent funds renovating the apartment following entry of the default judgment, was outweighed by the potential prejudice to respondent, who was in danger of losing the rent-controlled apartment that he had occupied for approximately 50 years. Mere delay, however long, is in and of itself insufficient to establish laches.

Matter of Carniol v. New York City Taxi & Limousine Commn.


114029/2011


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


42 Misc. 3d 199; 975 N.Y.S.2d 842; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4716; 2013 NY Slip Op 23358



September 26, 2013, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed by Matter of Carniol v New York City Taxi & Limousine Commn., 126 AD3d 409, 2 NYS3d 337, 2015 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 1733 (N.Y. App. Div. 1st Dep't, Mar. 3, 2015)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-The New York City Taxi and Limousine Commission's (TLC's) mandate that all medallion cabs be equipped with GPS, text messaging capabilities, and a monitor displaying certain rate and fare information for the passenger pursuant to 35 RCNY 3-06 [b], did not violate a taxicab driver's privacy rights under US Const. amend. IV and N.Y. Const. art. I, § 12; [2]-The taxicab driver failed to demonstrate that he had a legitimate expectation of privacy in the information obtained as a result of the use of the GPS or that the information was collected in derogation of his privacy rights; [3]-Where a petition charged the taxicab driver with 90 counts of violating 35 RCNY 2-34 [a], the TLC did not err by revoking his license and imposing an $850 fine pursuant to 35 RCNY 8-03 (b)(ii).


OUTCOME: Motion granted, and proceeding dismissed.


HEADNOTES

Licenses -- Taxicab License -- Challenge to Discretionary Revocation -- Failure to Exhaust Administrative Remedies 

1. Upon discovery that petitioner had overcharged passengers using the higher out of town rate for trips that were entirely within the city according to records generated by a Taxi Technology System, which included a Global Positioning System, that was installed in every city taxicab pursuant to the rules and regulations of respondent New York City Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) to electronically transmit location, trip and fare information to the TLC, the revocation of petitioner's taxicab driver's license by respondent TLC Chairperson was a discretionary revocation that petitioner was required, but failed, to appeal to the full TLC. Petitioner thus failed to exhaust his administrative remedies, thereby precluding judicial review of his nonconstitutional claims. TLC Rules (35 RCNY) § 68-18 provides for an appeal to the full TLC from the Chairperson's final decision revoking a taxicab driver's license where the revocation is discretionary. Under TLC Rules (35 RCNY) §§ 2-87 (a) (1) and 8-03 (b) (ii), the TLC may exercise its discretion to revoke a taxicab driver's license if the driver is found guilty of overcharging passengers once or twice within 24 months but the TLC must revoke the license if the driver is found guilty of overcharging passengers three times within a 36-month period. Here, the revocation of petitioner's license was identified as a discretionary revocation from the initiation of the charges against him, and the charges against petitioner were for his first offense.

Searches and Seizures -- Administrative Searches -- Installation of Global Positioning System in Taxicabs 

2. Upon the revocation of petitioner's taxicab driver's license based on discovery that petitioner had overcharged passengers using the higher out of town rate for trips that were entirely within the city according to records generated from a Taxi Technology System (TTS), which included a Global Positioning System, that was installed in every city taxicab pursuant to the rules and regulations of respondent New York City Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) to electronically transmit location, trip and fare information to the TLC, there was no violation of petitioner's privacy rights under the Fourth Amendment of the United States Constitution or article I, § 12 of the New York Constitution. Petitioner did not have a legitimate expectation of privacy in the data generated by the TTS as he knew that the TTS was installed in his taxicab, he was required to use the TTS pursuant to the TLC rules and regulations and the data collected by the TTS related solely to his employment as a taxicab driver. In any event, any search resulting from the TLC's use of the TTS data was reasonable. Petitioner's privacy interest and the degree of governmental intrusion, which were minimal as the TTS only gathered information when petitioner was on duty and pertaining to the taxicab industry, were outweighed by the substantial governmental interests in insuring the safety of taxicab drivers and passengers and in generating information to improve taxicab customer service.

People v. Schiavone


2012BX044603


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, BRONX COUNTY


41 Misc. 3d 845; 971 N.Y.S.2d 431; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4145; 2013 NY Slip Op 23312



September 18, 2013, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-The charge of loitering for the purpose of engaging in a prostitution offense under Penal Law § 240.37 was dismissed because only one single instance of approaching a police officer (presumably not in uniform) and offering to engage in sexual conduct in exchange for money was alleged to have occurred, and, at no time was defendant alleged to have engaged in repeated conduct; [2]-Pursuant to CPL § 30.30, defendant's motion to dismiss the patronizing a prostitute in the third degree charge under Penal Law § 230.04 was denied because the People were charged with a total of only six days to date, and the People's time to prosecute the matter had not yet expired.


OUTCOME: Motion to dismiss granted in part and denied in part.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Loitering -- For Purposes of Prostitution -- Repeated Conduct Required 

1. An information charging loitering for the purpose of engaging in a prostitution offense, alleging that defendant approached a police officer and said, "I want sex. I'll give you 40 dollars," was facially insufficient because only one single instance of the conduct complained of is alleged to have occurred. A person is guilty of loitering for the purpose of engaging in a prostitution offense under Penal Law § 240.37 (2) when he or she "remains or wanders about in a public place and repeatedly beckons to, or repeatedly stops, or repeatedly attempts to stop, or repeatedly attempts to engage passers-by in conversation, or repeatedly stops or attempts to stop motor vehicles, or repeatedly interferes with the free passage of other persons, for the purpose of prostitution, or of patronizing a prostitute." The conduct sought to be proscribed is repeated conduct, not a single act.

Crimes -- Right to Speedy Trial -- Effect of Superceding Accusatory Instrument 

2. Defendant's criminal action was not dismissed on speedy trial grounds, even though the offense initially charged, loitering for the purpose of engaging in a prostitution offense, a violation, was facially insufficient and a superceding information charging that offense and patronizing a prostitute in the third degree, a class A misdemeanor, was filed eight months after the first accusatory instrument. Replacement of one accusatory instrument that is defective with another involving the same crime does not affect time computations or automatically render the entire period prior thereto includable, and the People's initial statement of readiness was not rendered illusory simply because the initial charge was facially insufficient on both accusatory instruments. Further, any perceived pleading defect related to the subsequently dismissed charge did not serve to vitiate the People's otherwise valid readiness statement relating to the properly pleaded charge. CPL 30.30 time periods are generally calculated based on the most serious offense charged in the accusatory instrument and are measured from the date of commencement of the action. The determinative factor is not the initial charge, but the level of crime with which the defendant is ultimately accused and for which he or she is prosecuted. Because a class A misdemeanor was ultimately charged, a 90-day time period applied. The entire period between commencement of the action and the filing of the superceding information was excludable for various reasons and the People were chargeable with only six days after filing the superceding information, after which they timely announced readiness.

Dugan v. London Terrace Gardens, L.P.


603468/2009


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


45 Misc. 3d 362; 986 N.Y.S.2d 740; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 6550; 2013 NY Slip Op 23467



September 9, 2013


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: ISSUE: Whether a landlord unlawfully removed apartment units from rent stabilization or control and charged tenants excessive rents under the New York City Rent Control Law and Rent Stabilization Law. HOLDINGS: [1]-Because a forfeiture of rent increases statutory provision was not a penalty it did not bar class certification under CPLR 901(b); [2]-Certification of a class of past and current tenants was appropriate under CPLR 902 and 903; [3]-Dismissal of the landlord's alternative third-party actions against the New York State Division of Housing and Community Renewal (DHCR) was appropriate because there was no genuine dispute between the DHCR and the landlord, and the DHCR's inclusion in one action as a third party defendant would not affect the determination of the rights and obligations between the landlord and the tenant class, while the other action was not ripe for adjudication.


OUTCOME: Motion to consolidate granted, motion for class certification granted, and motion to dismiss granted.


HEADNOTES

Actions -- Class Actions -- Recovery of Penalty -- Claim for Rent Overcharge -- Waiver of Treble Damages

1. In consolidated class actions in which plaintiffs, former and current tenants of defendant's building complex, sought rent overcharges based on defendant's unlawful deregulation of their apartments under the luxury decontrol provisions of the New York City Rent and Rehabilitation Law and the Rent Stabilization Law of 1969 while receiving J-51 tax benefits, plaintiffs' waiver of treble damages under those statutes and their implementing regulations (Administrative Code of City of NY §§ 26-413 [d] [2]; 26-516 [a]; 9 NYCRR 2206.8 [b] [1]; 2526.1 [a]) and allowance for class members to opt out of the class to pursue such damages permitted plaintiffs to proceed as a class action despite CPLR 901 (b)'s prohibition against a class action that seeks a statutory penalty. Waiver of a statutory penalty is permitted as long as the penalty is neither mandatory nor the sole measure of recovery, and class members retain the right to opt out of the class to pursue the punitive relief. Here, although the rent-stabilization and rent-control laws provide for treble damages as a penalty for a willful rent overcharge, they supplement the recovery of overcharges and may be awarded only upon a finding of willfulness or bad faith. Thus, treble damages were neither the sole means of recovery nor mandatory and could be waived on behalf of the class.

Actions -- Class Actions -- Recovery of Penalty -- Claim for Rent Overcharge -- Forfeiture of Lawful Rent Increases

2. In consolidated class actions in which plaintiffs, former and current tenants of defendant's building complex, sought rent overcharges based on defendant's unlawful deregulation of their apartments under the luxury decontrol provisions of the New York City Rent and Rehabilitation Law and the Rent Stabilization Law of 1969 while receiving J-51 tax benefits, plaintiffs' claim that, in calculating their damages, defendant should be deemed to have forfeited lawful rent increases as a result of its noncompliance with rent-stabilization and rent-control registration requirements (Administrative Code of City of NY § 26-517 [e]; 9 NYCRR 2203.7, 2528.4 [a]) did not bar plaintiffs from proceeding as a class under CPLR 901 (b) as such forfeiture, even if applicable, did not amount to a statutory penalty. The forfeiture of rent increases equates to nonentitlement to a statutory benefit, and the forfeiture provisions do not provide for the recovery of a penalty as redress for noncompliance. Moreover, unlike the treble damages recovery available as a penalty for willful rent overcharges, the forfeiture of lawful rent increases would enhance an overcharge award by comparatively little and thus would not amount to a punitive measure or provide litigation incentive or deterrent effect. In any event, class certification was not to be denied at such an early stage of the litigation solely on the possibility that forfeiture was both a penalty in this context and applicable to a landlord that, like defendant, assumed it was exempted from the registration requirements in reliance on the Division of Housing and Community Renewal's long-standing but erroneous interpretation of the luxury decontrol provisions.

Actions -- Class Actions -- Class Certification

3. In consolidated class actions in which plaintiffs, former and current tenants of defendant's building complex, sought rent overcharges based on defendant's unlawful deregulation of their apartments under the luxury decontrol provisions of the New York City Rent and Rehabilitation Law and the Rent Stabilization Law of 1969 while receiving J-51 tax benefits, the CPLR 901 (a) prerequisites for class certification were satisfied, and the factors enumerated in CPLR 902 weighed in favor of proceeding as a class action. The proposed class--all tenants formerly and currently residing in defendant's 558 deregulated apartments--was so numerous that it would be impracticable to join the potential plaintiffs individually. Moreover, the common, predominate questions were whether class members paid deregulated rent during defendant's participation in the J-51 program and, if so, the period of defendant's liability and the formula for calculating rent overcharges--questions requiring minimal individualized disputed evidence and to which no conceivable individualized defenses pertained. The named plaintiffs' claims were typical of the classwide claims as they sought the same declaratory and injunctive relief based on defendant's illegal deregulation of their apartments and damages equal to the excessive rents charged as a result thereof according to the same formula, and the named plaintiffs were adequate representatives of the class as the proposed class definition included both former and current tenants whose apartments were deregulated through various means. Finally, the class members' numerosity and the expense of bringing separate, individual actions outweighed any anticipated difficulties in managing a class action.

Courts -- Justiciable Questions -- Third-Party Action for Declaratory and Injunctive Relief -- Lack of Controversy between Parties

4. Upon the commencement by plaintiffs, former and current tenants of defendant's building complex, of consolidated class actions seeking rent overcharges based on defendant's unlawful deregulation of their apartments under the luxury decontrol provisions of the New York City Rent and Rehabilitation Law and the Rent Stabilization Law of 1969 while receiving J-51 tax benefits, third-party actions commenced by defendant against the Division of Housing and Community Renewal (DHCR) were dismissed in the absence of a viable controversy between the parties. The claims upon which defendant sought declaratory and injunctive relief against DHCR--that Roberts v Tishman Speyer Props., L.P. (13 NY3d 270, 918 NE2d 900, 890 NYS2d 388 [2009]), holding that luxury decontrol did not apply to buildings receiving J-51 tax benefits, should not be applied retroactively; that retroactive application of Roberts constituted an un-constitutional regulatory taking; and that defendant could rescind its participation in the J-51 program--had already been decided, and defendant's alternative claim for declaratory relief--that, upon the expiration of its J-51 tax benefits, it should no longer be subject to rent stabilization or control--was premature for judicial adjudication because the effect of any such declaration would be contingent upon future events that might not occur. Defendant's remaining claims regarding the regulatory status of its apartments and its liability for deregulation while receiving J-51 tax benefits were disputed is sues in which only defendant and the plaintiff class held a genuine stake. The third-party actions thus presented no factual or legal basis to grant defendant any of the relief sought against DHCR.
