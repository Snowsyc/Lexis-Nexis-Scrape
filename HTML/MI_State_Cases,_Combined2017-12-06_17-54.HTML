Fluor Enters. v. Dep't of Treasury


No. 251005


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


265 Mich. App. 711; 697 N.W.2d 539; 2005 Mich. App. LEXIS 909


April 6, 2005, Submitted at Lansing  
April 14, 2005, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Appeal granted by Fluor Enters. v. Dep't of Treasury, 474 Mich. 1097, 711 N.W.2d 74, 2006 Mich. LEXIS 587 (2006)
Affirmed in part and reversed in part by, Remanded by Fluor Enters. v. Revenue Div., Dep't of Treasury, 2007 Mich. LEXIS 976 (Mich., May 2, 2007)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Court of Claims. LC No. 02-000027-MT.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant Department of Treasury treated receipts from services performed on out-of-state for projects constructed in Michigan as Michigan receipts under Mich. Comp. Laws § 208.53(c) of the Single Business Tax Act, Mich. Comp. Laws § 208.1 et seq. Rejecting this interpretation of § 208.53(c), the Court of Claims (Michigan) ordered the Department to refund plaintiff, a California corporation, taxes paid under protest. The Department appealed.


OVERVIEW: The receipts at issue were received by the corporation for engineering and architectural services related to real estate improvement projects constructed in Michigan. The services were performed by the corporation's employees at its out-of-state facilities. The trial court held that receipts from the services performed for out-of-state projects constructed in Michigan were not "in this state" for purposes of calculating the sales factor to be used in apportioning the corporation's tax base. On appeal, the court determined that the trial court incorrectly interpreted Mich. Comp. Laws § 208.53(c) and that the correct construction of the section was that receipts derived from services performed for construction activities within the state should be deemed Michigan receipts for the purposes of the sales factor. The court determined further that § 208.53(c), as properly construed, violated the Commerce Clause, U.S. Const. art I, § 8, cl. 3, because the tax was not fairly apportioned; if other states used the same apportionment formula, there were situations where more than one state would tax business activity performed in one state for construction activities in another.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the matter for further proceedings.

Wheeler v. Charter Twp. of Shelby


No. 249116


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


265 Mich. App. 657; 697 N.W.2d 180; 2005 Mich. App. LEXIS 891


April 5, 2005, Submitted at Detroit  
April 12, 2005, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Macomb Circuit Court. LC No. 2001-001173-CE.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part and reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff resident brought suit against defendants, the township and its board of trustees, challenging the constitutionality of Shelby Mich., Township Ordinance 211, § 9.126. The Macomb Circuit Court (Michigan) granted the resident summary judgment as to that part of the ordinance which authorized certain fees, but granted summary judgment to the township upholding the constitutionality of the ordinance. The parties all appealed.


OVERVIEW: On review, the township and the board challenged the trial court's finding that the provisions of Shelby Mich., Township Ordinance 211, §§ 9.126(e-f), which required single-family residences to use a single waste hauler approved by the township for the disposal of household solid waste and to pay a fee to the hauler for the services, created a tax in violation of the Headlee Amendment, Mich. Const. art. IX, § 31. The resident challenged the trial court's finding that the township's renewal of its contract with the waste hauler without opening the process to competitive rebidding did not constitute a violation of the dormant Commerce Clause of U.S. Const. art. I, § 8, ch., 3. The appellate court held that because the waste hauler fees were fees for services rather than taxes, the imposition of user fees did not offend Mich. Const. art. IX, § 31. As such, summary disposition in favor of the resident as to this issue was improper. However, the process the township and the board employed for selecting a waste hauler regulated commerce even-handedly and only incidentally and permissibly affected interstate commerce so as to be in accord with the dormant Commerce Clause.


OUTCOME: That portion of the judgment finding that the fees were an unauthorized tax was reversed; otherwise, the judgment was affirmed.

DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded for proceedings consistent with this opinion. We do not retain jurisdiction.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In an interlocutory appeal, defendant appealed orders of the Barry Circuit Court, Michigan, denying his motion to quash an information with respect to a charge of driving with a revoked license and causing death, in violation of Mich. Comp. Laws § 257.904(4).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was driving a pickup truck, with snowplowing equipment attached to its front, even though his driver's license had been revoked. He was traveling at apparently normal speeds when the victim crossed the road in front of him while riding a snowmobile. The snowmobile and the truck collided. The instant court declined to reach such a harsh result.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Wayne Circuit Court. LC No. 01-120001-PZ.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner, a county treasurer, sought review of a judgment from the Circuit Court of Wayne County (Michigan), which, in a postjudgment proceeding brought by respondents, property owner and mortgagee, vacated a foreclosure order and declared the certificate of forfeiture in favor of the treasurer, as well as the subsequent quitclaim deed purporting to transfer the property from the treasurer to a foreclosure sale purchaser, to be null and void.


OVERVIEW: The treasurer filed a foreclosure action based on unpaid special assessments. The circuit court ordered foreclosure. After the statutory redemption period expired, the treasurer sold the property at public auction. In a postjudgment proceeding, the owner and mortgagee sought to vacate the foreclosure judgment, claiming that statutory notice errors occurred and that their due process rights were violated. The circuit court granted relief from the foreclosure judgment under Mich. Ct. R. 2.612(C). The circuit court erred, however, in requiring strict compliance with the notice requirements of Mich. Comp. Laws § 211.78i.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the circuit court's order and remanded for a determination of whether the constitutional minimal due process rights of the owner and the mortgagee were violated.

COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


265 Mich. App. 236; 694 N.W.2d 761; 2005 Mich. App. LEXIS 348


November 10, 2004, Submitted

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Ingham Circuit Court. LC No. 01-093038-CZ.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded for entry of an order consistent with this opinion. We do not retain jurisdiction.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff workers' compensation self-insurer group appealed a decision of the Ingham Circuit Court (Michigan), which denied plaintiff's motion for summary disposition in plaintiff's action challenging the constitutionality of a portion of Mich. Comp. Laws § 500.2016(1)(a) and (3) precluding a workers' compensation self-insurer group from conditioning the payment of a dividend on continued participation in the group.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff sought to prohibit defendants, the workers' compensation bureau, and consumer department, from enforcing the provisions in regards to an employer who decided to discontinue participation with plaintiff. The court held that the pertinent language of § 500.2016 served the purpose of fostering competition in the market for workers' compensation coverage by precluding a self-insurer group from conditioning receipt of a dividend. Also the language did not violate the federal or Michigan Contract Clause because there was a legitimate public purpose for such an impairment of contract and the means were reasonably related to the public purpose. OUTCOME: The court reversed in part holding that the statute could not be applied retroactively. The court otherwise affirmed.

No. 232260


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


256 Mich. App. 387; 663 N.W.2d 522; 2003 Mich. App. LEXIS 1045


April 8, 2003, Submitted  
April 29, 2003, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:   [***1] Oakland Circuit Court. LC No. 91-405744-DP.
Hall v. Novik, 456 Mich. 898, 456 Mich. 900, 572 N.W.2d 3, 1997 Mich. LEXIS 2732 (1997)



DISPOSITION: Remanded this case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. We do not retain jurisdiction.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant father sought review of the decision of the Oakland Circuit Court (Michigan), which denied his motion for modification of previous child support orders, in an action involving former Mich. Comp. Laws § 722.713 (repealed by 1996 Mich. Pub. Acts 308, § 2).


OVERVIEW: The mother filed a complaint for paternity against the father. The parties entered into a process of negotiation, and an agreement was reached providing that the father did not acknowledge that he was the father of the mother's child, but that he would make payments to the mother for the child's support and education at a set level, former Mich. Comp. Laws § 722.713 (repealed by 1996 Mich. Pub. Acts 308, § 2). The statute was repealed, and the mother returned to the circuit court seeking a modification of the original support order. The circuit court granted that relief and entered an order increasing the monthly payments. The Supreme Court of Michigan determined that § 722.713 did not violate the equal protection clauses and that non-modifiable support agreements entered into pursuant to § 722.713 were enforceable. The court stated that the original agreement was premised on a constitutional statute and should have been enforced throughout. Having freely negotiated and accepted that agreement, the court could not rightfully claim that it was unfair to impose its limitations upon her in the future. The Supreme Court's ruling was entitled to prospective application only.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment failing to give prospective effect to a Michigan Supreme Court decision but affirmed the ruling that the order should not be applied retroactively to require that the mother reimburse the father any amounts paid previously to her in excess of those required by the original order. The judgement was remanded for further proceedings.

Houghton v. Keller


No. 232524


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


256 Mich. App. 336; 662 N.W.2d 854; 2003 Mich. App. LEXIS 1014


April 15, 2003, Submitted  
April 22, 2003, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Appeal denied by Houghton v. Keller, 2003 Mich. LEXIS 2423 (Mich., Oct. 31, 2003)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Oakland Circuit Court. LC No. 98-601932-DO.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Oakland Circuit Court, Michigan, granted plaintiff wife and defendant husband a judgment of divorce. The husband appealed.


OVERVIEW: The husband argued that the trial court erred in ruling that a guardian could initiate a divorce action on behalf of an incompetent spouse. The appellate court found that, based on Mich. Ct. R. 3.202(A) and 2.201(E), a guardian could bring an action for divorce on behalf of an incompetent spouse. The husband did not explain the statutory requirements that he suggested were changed by Mich. Ct. R. 3.202. Mich. Comp. Laws § 552.6 required that the plaintiff allege that there had been a breakdown of the marriage relationship to the extent that the objects of matrimony were destroyed and there remained no reasonable likelihood that the marriage could be preserved. Nothing in the statute expressly prohibited guardians from filing a complaint for divorce on behalf of a party to the marriage. Mich. Ct. R. 3.202(A) did not change the statutory provisions pertaining to divorce. The husband's failure to properly address the merits of his assertion of error constituted abandonment of the issue.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

People v. Haynes


No. 244327


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


256 Mich. App. 341; 664 N.W.2d 225; 2003 Mich. App. LEXIS 1015


April 8, 2003, Submitted  
April 22, 2003, Decided


NOTICE:

 [***1]


PRIOR HISTORY: St. Clair Circuit Court. LC No. 02-001637-FH.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. We do not retain jurisdiction.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a prosecution of defendant for operating a vehicle while under the influence of intoxicating liquor (OUIL), third offense, the State appealed an order of the St. Clair Circuit Court, Michigan, granting defendant's motion to strike a prior conviction for operating a vehicle by a minor with a bodily alcohol content that was used to enhance the OUIL charge.


OVERVIEW: The State argued that the trial court erroneously prevented the use of defendant's prior zero tolerance conviction, Mich. Comp. Laws § 257.625(6), to enhance his subsequent OUIL conviction, Mich. Comp. Laws § 257.625(1), on due process and equal protection grounds. The instant court agreed with the State. Defendant's fundamental liberty interests were adequately protected by the right to a trial concerning the instant offense. The instant court was not persuaded that the application of Mich. Comp. Laws § 257.625(23) in this case violated equal protection guarantees. It found no evidence that Mich. Comp. Laws § 257.625(23) treated underage drivers differently from those over 21. Regarding defendant's due process challenge, the instant court did not find that it was "fundamentally unfair" to use his prior uncounseled juvenile conviction to enhance his subsequent OUIL charge. Thus, the trial court erred in ruling that the use of defendant's prior zero tolerance conviction to enhance his subsequent OUIL charge violated his due process rights. The instant court also found no violation of the constitutional prohibitions against ex post facto laws.


OUTCOME: The trial court's order was reversed, and the case was remanded for further proceedings consistent with the instant court's opinion.

People v. Abraham

COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


256 Mich. App. 265; 662 N.W.2d 836; 2003 Mich. App. LEXIS 886


March 18, 2003, Submitted  
April 10, 2003, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Oakland Circuit Court Family Division. LC No. 97-063787-FC.
People v. Abraham (In re Abraham), 234 Mich. App. 640, 599 N.W.2d 736, 1999 Mich. App. LEXIS 67 (1999)



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Oakland Circuit Court, Family Division, Michigan, convicted juvenile defendant of second-degree murder pursuant to Mich. Comp. Laws §§ 750.317 and 712A.2d. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that a new trial was warranted because the prosecution failed to establish that he acted maliciously when he shot a gun at trees when people were nearby. The appellate court noted that a child psychologist who personally examined defendant indicated that defendant stated that he was fearful that he might hit someone when shooting. This testimony qualified as showing defendant had the intent to create a very high risk of death with the knowledge that the act probably would cause death or great bodily harm. Severance was not mandatory because the two incidents were related under Mich. Ct. R. 6.210(B). The prosecutor's statements were not plainly erroneous and the prosecution did not attempt to shift the burden of proof to defendant. Any prejudicial effects were cured by proper jury instructions, and did not violate defendant's right to a fair trial. Mich. Comp. Laws § 712A.2d was not unconstitutional as defendant received all due process protections he was entitled to. It was not within the court's authority to determine where children had no constitutional right to juvenile prosecution. Defendant's competency was evaluated and confirmed three times.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

McDonald v. Grand Traverse County Election Comm'n


Docket No. 237984


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


255 Mich. App. 674; 662 N.W.2d 804; 2003 Mich. App. LEXIS 662


March 5, 2003, Submitted  
March 18, 2003, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Appeal denied by, Motion denied by McDonald v. Grand Traverse County Election Comm'n, 469 Mich. 946; 671 N.W.2d 46; 2003 Mich. LEXIS 2367 (Mich., Oct. 31, 2003)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Grand Traverse Circuit. Court LC No. 00-020835-AW.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff candidate challenged the constitutionality of Mich. Comp. Laws § 168.737. The Grand Traverse Circuit Court (Michigan) granted summary disposition pursuant to Mich. Ct. R. 2.116(C)(10) to defendants and denied the candidate's motion for summary disposition. The candidate appealed.


OVERVIEW: The candidate's complaint alleged that the straight-ticket ballot was unconstitutional. The appellate court concluded that the straight-ticket ballot option was constitutional and did not deprive the candidate of his First Amendment associational rights because it was justified by the state's compelling interest in the integrity of the election process and its strong interest in the stability of its political systems. Also, the ballot did not violate his equal protection rights because the election law did not prevent him from receiving votes for the trustee position. Further, the straight-ticket ballot option did not violate the Michigan Constitution's purity of elections requirement because the ballot option did not provide an unfair advantage to major party candidates. Accordingly, defendants were entitled to judgment as a matter of law, and the trial court did not err in granting their motion for summary disposition. Additionally, the trial court did not abuse its discretion in denying the candidate's motion for discovery sanctions. Finally, the trial court did not clearly err in awarding attorney fees and costs to defendant, Michigan Secretary of State.


OUTCOME: The appellate court affirmed the trial court's judgment.
