DISPOSITION: Judgments reversed and cases remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Habersham and Rockdale Counties Superior Courts (Georgia) awarded appellees non-custodial fathers custody of their children after holding that the "best-interest-of-the-child" standard of Ga. Code Ann. § 19-7-1(b.1) was unconstitutional. In a consolidated action, appellants maternal grandparents challenged the judgments.


OVERVIEW: In both actions in the consolidated appeal, the minor children were living with appellants when appellees, single, noncustodial fathers, sought custody. In both cases, the trial courts determined that it would be in the children's best interest to remain with appellants, but awarded custody to appellees on the ground that the "best interest of the child" standard of Ga. Code Ann. § 19-7-1(b.1) was unconstitutional. On appeal, the instant court reversed, holding that § 19-7-1(b.1) was constitutional as applied to custody disputes between a noncustodial parent and a third party. The court interpreted the best interest standard to mean that the third party had to prove by clear and convincing evidence that the child would suffer physical or emotional harm if custody were awarded to the biological parent. Once the showing was made, the third party then had to show that a custody award to him would promote the child's welfare and happiness.


OUTCOME: Judgments reversed and cases remanded. Under the court's interpretation of the parent-third party custody statute, the best interest of the child standard was constitutional.

May 12, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: Sexual offender registry. Troup Superior Court. Before Judge Baldwin.


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the order of the trial court (Georgia), which denied his petition for release from the requirement that he register as a sexual offender for life, pursuant to O.C.G.A. § 42-1-12(f)(7). Defendant had been convicted of child molestation with regard to actions involving his three daughters. Defendant was sentenced to 10 years confinement and served three years. He was released to serve the remainder of the sentence on probation.


OVERVIEW: Following the 10th anniversary of his release from prison, defendant filed the underlying petition. On appeal, defendant argued that the trial court erred in failing to release him from the requirement to register under § 42-1-12(g) because the State failed to meet its burden of showing that he posed a substantial risk of reoffense in opposition to his prima facie case to the contrary. The court held that the trial court did not abuse its discretion by denying defendant's petition since he characterized his previous criminal conduct toward his children as a mistake and he acknowledged that an open Department of Family and Children Services case existed as to one of his daughters and grandchildren wherein one condition barred his daughter from bringing children around him. Based on that evidence, the court held that defendant failed to present prima facie evidence demonstrating that he no longer posed a substantial risk of reoffending. The court also held that defendant's failure to abide by § 42-1-12 would result in a new crime based on his status as a child molester and, therefore, the statute was not retrospective and, thus, not an ex post facto law.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order of the trial court denying defendant's petition.

Forde v. State


A08A0602.


COURT OF APPEALS OF GEORGIA


289 Ga. App. 805; 658 S.E.2d 410; 2008 Ga. App. LEXIS 206; 2008 Fulton County D. Rep. 685



February 28, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: Sexual battery. DeKalb Superior Court. Before Judge Castellani.


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed in part and vacated in part, and case remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed his conviction, his sentence, and the denial of his motion for a new trial by a trial court (Georgia) with regard to his conviction on one count of sexual battery against a child under the age of 16 years, in violation of O.C.G.A. § 16-6-22.1(b) and (d). Defendant was sentenced to serve five years in prison.


OVERVIEW: Defendant's conviction arose with regard to actions he took toward his daughter. On appeal, defendant asserted that his trial counsel provided ineffective assistance by failing to object to inadmissible hearsay evidence involving his daughter's videotaped testimony while being interviewed by a psychologist. The court did find that by failing to raise an objection to the inadmissible hearsay evidence of the victim's statements made during the videotaped interview, defendant's trial counsel's performance fell below an objective standard of reasonableness under the first prong of the Strickland test. However, defendant failed to show that his trial court's error prejudiced his defense since the statements made by the victim during the videotaped interview were merely cumulative of the testimony she had offered at trial. The court did find that the trial court erred in sentencing defendant for the commission of a felony pursuant to the amended sexual battery statute despite the fact that it was unclear from the jury's verdict whether he was found guilty of pre-amendment misdemeanor or post-amendment felony conduct.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed defendant's conviction but vacated the felony sentence and remanded the case to the trial court for resentencing.

State v. Morgan


A07A1873.


COURT OF APPEALS OF GEORGIA


289 Ga. App. 706; 658 S.E.2d 237; 2008 Ga. App. LEXIS 189; 2008 Fulton County D. Rep. 707



February 22, 2008, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Cert. applied for.
Writ of certiorari denied State v. Morgan, 2008 Ga. LEXIS 504 (Ga., June 2, 2008)


PRIOR HISTORY: Implied consent. Dade Superior Court. Before Judge Van Pelt.


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State appealed the order of the trial court (Georgia), which granted defendant's motion to suppress the results of a state-administered blood test. Defendant was charged with two counts of homicide by vehicle, driving while under the influence of drugs/marijuana; two counts of homicide by vehicle, reckless driving; four counts of serious injury by vehicle; and other crimes.


OVERVIEW: The charges against defendant arose after he was involved in a two-car collision in which two people were killed and several others were injured. A state trooper who arrived on the scene did not find any signs that defendant was impaired. As defendant's wife was preparing to drive him away from the hospital, an officer approached them and asked defendant to take a blood test. Defendant was never given a consent form nor was advised of his constitutional rights at any time. Defendant took the test, and the test results showed the presence of marijuana. Defendant successfully obtained the suppression of the test results by asserting that he had not been advised of his implied consent rights. The court upheld the suppression, finding that if it condoned the conduct of the officer, the court would effectively eviscerate the implied consent statute, O.C.G.A. § 40-5-55(a). The court held that it was the clear intent of the Georgia Legislature that an officer seeking to obtain a chemical test of a person's bodily substances following an accident involving serious injuries or fatalities must do so via the implied consent statute.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the order granting defendant's motion to suppress.

Wilshin v. State


A07A1696.


COURT OF APPEALS OF GEORGIA


289 Ga. App. 683; 658 S.E.2d 224; 2008 Ga. App. LEXIS 178; 2008 Fulton County D. Rep. 628



February 21, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: Speeding. Hall State Court. Before Judge Wynne.


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Following a bench trial in a Georgia court, defendant stood convicted of speeding. Thus, he appealed, acting pro se, challenging the sufficiency of the evidence. Specifically, he contended that the arresting officer's in-court identification of him was tainted and that the trial court should have dismissed his case, as the traffic stop violated O.C.G.A. § 40-14-6.


OVERVIEW: Upon a review of the evidence in light of defendant's claims, the appeals court found no reason for reversal. First, the trooper's testimony, coupled with the readout of the laser speed detection device, which measured defendant's speed at 61 miles-per-hour, in excess of the posted speed limit of 45 miles-per-hour, was sufficient to support the speeding conviction. Second, defendant's identity as the perpetrator was not at issue. To the contrary, after the trooper made the stop, defendant produced his driver's license and when he was given a traffic citation, he signed it. This signature matched the one on his pro se pleadings. And, defendant presumably sat at defense table during trial, allowing the trooper to ascertain who was on trial for speeding. Third, the appeals court lacked original jurisdiction to address defendant's motions to suppress and for a mistrial. Finally, because the trooper was employed by the State, and not a county, municipality, college, or university, O.C.G.A. § 40-14-6 did not apply to him.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

COURT OF APPEALS OF GEORGIA


289 Ga. App. 540; 657 S.E.2d 600; 2008 Ga. App. LEXIS 139; 2008 Fulton County D. Rep. 462



February 7, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: Medical malpractice. Fulton State Court. Before Judge Thompson.


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a medical negligence action filed by a patient, a jury in a Georgia trial court granted judgment to a doctor. Thus, the patient appealed, contending that the trial court erred in refusing to admit statements conceding negligence made by the doctor on the date of the second surgery and erred in allowing two witnesses to testify as experts without properly establishing their credentials.


OVERVIEW: Upon a review of the evidence in light of the patient's claims, the appeals court first found that the trial court properly refused the doctor's statements of regret following the second surgery, pursuant to O.C.G.A. § 24-3-37.1(c). Further, despite the fact that the trial court did not expressly rule upon the constitutionality of the statute, retroactive application was not improper, as the General Assembly intended that the law be applied to cases pending at the time it was passed. As to the issue relating to the two experts, the appeals court found that the patient failed to timely challenge the expert evidence via O.C.G.A. § 24-9-67.1(d), by way of a pretrial hearing. Moreover, even assuming that the untimeliness of the patient's request for a hearing did not waive the particular grounds asserted in his motion to strike brought at the close of evidence, he nevertheless waived all remaining objections to the expert testimony by failing to object contemporaneously to the same.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

Kromer v. Bechtel


A07A2490.


COURT OF APPEALS OF GEORGIA


289 Ga. App. 306; 656 S.E.2d 910; 2008 Ga. App. LEXIS 63; 2008 Fulton County D. Rep. 256



January 24, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: Settlement offer; attorney fees. Bryan State Court. Before Judge Carney.


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: After a jury awarded a judgment to an offeree after his offer was deemed rejected when the offeree did not respond, the offeror moved for attorney's fees and expenses, under O.C.G.A. § 9-11-68, because the judgment obtained by the offeree was not at least 25 percent more favorable than the offer of judgment. After the offeree did not respond, the motion was granted. Thus, the offeree appealed.


OVERVIEW: For the first time on appeal, the offeree alleged that it was unconstitutional to apply O.C.G.A. § 9-11-68 retroactively and that it violated the fundamental right of access to the courts, due process, and equal protection. But, because she never raised these constitutional issues in the trial court, they could not be considered when asserted for the first time on appeal but had to be clearly raised in the trial court and distinctly ruled upon there. Further, had the constitutional challenge been raised and ruled on in the trial court, and if it presented an unresolved question, jurisdiction would lie in the Supreme Court of Georgia. Second, the offeree argued that under the amended version of the statute, she would not have been required to pay any fees or costs. But, applying the current statute retroactively in would impair the offeror's rights to recover attorney's fees and costs under the statute in effect at the time the offer of settlement was made. Hence, there was no error.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

Padgett v. State of Ga.


A07A2233.


COURT OF APPEALS OF GEORGIA


289 Ga. App. 95; 656 S.E.2d 233; 2008 Ga. App. LEXIS 6; 2008 Fulton County D. Rep. 32



January 4, 2008, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY: Forfeiture. Bartow Superior Court. Before Judge Smith.
Padgett v. State, 287 Ga. App. 789, 653 S.E.2d 102, 2007 Ga. App. LEXIS 1096 (2007)


DISPOSITION:  [***1] Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant landowner challenged the judgment of the trial court (Georgia), which resulted in the forfeiture of his real property where he grew marijuana. Appellee, the State of Georgia, had filed a complaint seeking forfeiture of the real property and the items listed in a request for condemnation after the landowner was found guilty of trafficking in and manufacturing marijuana in August 2006. The landowner's motion for a new trial was denied.


OVERVIEW: On October 22, 2004, law enforcement officers executed a search warrant at real property owned, in part, by the landowner and, as a result, the criminal charges against him arose. The same day, the local sheriff's office filed a request for condemnation that listed personal property seized by it, but the real property was not included. On appeal, the landowner contended that the trial court erred in forfeiting the real property because the State failed to report the seizure of the real property within 20 days, in accordance with O.C.G.A. § 16-13-49(h). The court held that the landowner was not entitled to a reversal of the forfeiture. The court determined that even if the 20 day reporting requirement was applicable, the landowner's remedy under the statute for a violation of the requirement would have been the release of the real property to him pending further forfeiture proceedings and not the dismissal of the entire action. Since the landowner remained in possession of the property until the resolution of the forfeiture proceeding, no error occurred.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's forfeiture of the real property.
