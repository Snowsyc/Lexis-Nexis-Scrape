SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed by Bristow v. Del. Bd. of Examiners in Optometry, 2005 Del. LEXIS 386 (Del., Oct. 11, 2005)



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part; reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff optometrist sued defendant Delaware Board of Examiners in Optometry for a declaration that the conditions of her employment and, in particular, her relationship with a commercial optician, did not violate Del. Code Ann. tit. 24, § 2113, as the Board found. She also sought an injunction to enjoin the Board from taking any action to suspend or revoke her license to practice optometry. The parties filed cross-motions for summary judgment.


OVERVIEW: The optometrist, in her practice, shared a building with a commercial optician, from which her employer subleased office space at a monthly rate far below market value. Her practice shared a common entrance with the optician, and shared open spaces and facilities. The widely publicized address of the optometrist's practice was identical to the optician's, and the practice's phone number was prominently associated with other of the optician's locations. Further, the optician's practice engaged in mutual advertising and marketing strategies with the optician. The entities shared conspicuous signage and advertising, both at the practice location and in the Internet, through the optician's own website and other informational sites such as medical and vision plan insurance, as well as promotional discounts offered at the optometrist's office location offered at the conclusion of an examination. The court found that substantial evidence existed to support the Board's findings as to the violations of Del. Code Ann. tit. 24, §§ 2113(a)(5) and 2113(a)(6). However, the Board erred as a matter of law when it concluded that the optometrist violated Del. Code Ann. tit. 24, § 2113(a)(2)


OUTCOME: The decision was affirmed in part as to finding that the optometrist advertised with and practiced in conjunction with the optician; however the decision was reversed in part as to the finding that the optometrist's office did not conform to that used by the majority of professional optometrists in the area.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Summary judgment granted by, Summary judgment denied by Lynch v. City of Rehoboth Beach, 2005 Del. Ch. LEXIS 222 (Del. Ch., Feb. 15, 2005)


PRIOR HISTORY: Lynch v. City of Rehoboth, 2004 Del. Ch. LEXIS 216 (Del. Ch., May 17, 2004)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, the owners of real property, brought an action against defendants, the city and its commissioners, to enjoin the enforcement of an ordinance which purported to rezone the owners' property from a commercial to a residential designation. The city and its commissioner filed a motion to dismiss the complaint.


OVERVIEW: The owners challenged the validity of the commissioner's actions in adopting an ordinance which purported to rezone the owners' properties. The city and its commissioners argued that Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 8126(a), the statute of repose, imposed a period during which suit could be filed commencing on the date of publication of the ordinance and ending 60 days thereafter. Because the owners filed suit after the rezoning was adopted, but before publication, the city and its commissioners argued that the suit was not filed within the period called for by statute and was therefore untimely. Additionally, the city and its commissioners argued that the owners failed to join a party in the action who was the holder of a contingent remainder interest. The court found that the complaint was not void as premature. In addition, there was no indispensable party then not joined pursuant Del. Ch. Ct. R. 19(a). The case did not have to be dismissed based upon the failure to join the holder of a remainder interest in the affected property whose interested never vested and which was never transferred to the party.


OUTCOME: The motion was denied.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs landowners sued defendants, a city and its commissioners, for allegedly acting unlawfully, arbitrarily, and capriciously in approving a rezoning. The landowners sought an order enjoining the approval of the rezoning. Defendants moved to dismiss.


OVERVIEW: The court found, orally, that its equitable jurisdiction, under Del. Code Ann. tit. 10, § 342, did not encompass the landowners' suit. On reconsideration, it found the landowners' request for injunctive relief invoked equity sufficient to establish equitable jurisdiction because (1) they alleged the ordinance in question was unconstitutionally and unlawfully created, (2) if they were correct, the unlawful zoning was having an immediate and ongoing impact on their right to use their land, (3) prior challenges to zoning ordinances impliedly determined equitable jurisdiction was present, (4) it had been found that a request to enjoin enforcement of a zoning ordinance on the grounds that the ordinance was unconstitutional and arbitrary sufficiently invoked the jurisdiction of the court of chancery, and (5) the landowners alleged the ordinance of which they complained was enacted arbitrarily and capriciously and in derogation of their right to enjoy their land.


OUTCOME: The court held it had jurisdiction, withdrawing its oral draft report in favor of its written report.

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [*1] Revised June 1, 2004.
Related proceeding at Christiana Town Ctr. v. New Castle County, 2004 Del. Super. LEXIS 209 (Del. Super. Ct., July 7, 2004)
Motion denied by, Stay granted by, in part Sterling Prop. Holdings v. New Castle County, 2013 Del. Ch. LEXIS 107 (Del. Ch., Apr. 30, 2013)
Later proceeding at Sterling Prop. Holdings v. New Castle County, 2014 Del. Ch. LEXIS 145 (Del. Ch., Aug. 5, 2014)


PRIOR HISTORY: Christiana Town Ctr., LLC v. New Castle County, 841 A.2d 307, 2004 Del. LEXIS 26 (Del., 2004)



DISPOSITION: Defendant County's motion for judgment on the pleadings granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff developer sued defendants, a county, its planning board, and the county department of land use, for a permanent injunction preventing application of the Sunsetting Law, New Castle County, Del., Code § 40.01.130, to the developer's proposed plan. Defendants moved for judgment on the pleadings as to certain paragraphs challenging the validity of the law because they were beyond the time allowed by the land use statute of repose.


OVERVIEW: In 1997, the county revised its land use regulations and adopted what was known as the Unified Development Code (UDC), a compilation of all county development regulations, including regulations on zoning, subdivisions, design, concurrency, impact fees, and signs. As part of the UDC, the county adopted the Sunsetting Law, which provided that if the owner of a major land development project, which had been approved and recorded by December 31, 1997, did not commence construction before December 31, 2002, the approval would expire and any subsequent development effort would be governed by the land use regulations then in effect. When the developer did not begin construction before December 31, 2002, defendants voided the developer's plan. The developer then sought to enjoin application of the Sunsetting Law. The court granted defendants' motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the challenge to the Sunsetting Law, finding that the developer had a 60-day period in which to challenge the ordinance after notice of its enactment was properly published, and that because the challenge was commenced more than five years after the publication, the challenge was time barred.


OUTCOME: The court granted defendants' motion for judgment on the pleadings with respect to the disputed paragraphs of the complaint.

Newark Landlord Ass'n v. City of Newark


C.A. No. 17583-NC


COURT OF CHANCERY OF DELAWARE, NEW CASTLE


2003 Del. Ch. LEXIS 124


July 16, 2003, Submitted  
November 17, 2003, Decided  
November 17, 2003, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY: Newark Landlord Ass'n v. City of Newark, 2003 Del. Ch. LEXIS 66 (Del. Ch., June 13, 2003)



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Judgment entered.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In an action by plaintiffs, landlords to students within defendant city and the landlords' representative association, the court had previously concluded that City of Newark, Del., Ord. §§ 99-10, 99-14 violated the Delaware Fair Housing Act (DFHA), Del. Code Ann. tit. 6, ch. 46, and the Delaware Residential Landlord-Tenant Code, Del. Code Ann. tit. 25, ch. 51-50. Before the court was the reserved issue of severability.


OVERVIEW: In its prior decision, the court had found that the definition of a living arrangement in City of Newark, Del. Ord. § 99-14 as a single-family detached dwelling comprised of post-secondary students, unrelated by marriage, operated to discriminate on the basis of marital status as prohibited by the DFHA. The city contended that the ordinance should be severed because, by striking the offending language, the discriminatory effect would be eliminated, while leaving intact the intent of the city council. Plaintiffs argued that it was not severable because removing the offending language would broaden the scope of the ordinance beyond the legislative intent. Determining that the discriminatory language could not be severed, the court held that: (1) it would violate the will of the city council, as evidenced by its clearly expressed decision to exempt married students from the restrictions, if the exclusion for married students was severed from the ordinance. This provision of the law was intended to rise and fall with the rest of the law. In addition, the severability provisions in the city ordinances did not result in severance because it would contravene the legislative intent.


OUTCOME: The court denied the city's request to sever the discriminatory ordinance, and it ordered that both ordinances were invalidated.

Mell v. New Castle County


Civil Action No. 20003-NC


COURT OF CHANCERY OF DELAWARE, NEW CASTLE


2003 Del. Ch. LEXIS 40


March 17, 2003, Submitted  
April 11, 2003, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Related proceeding at Mell v. New Castle County, 835 A.2d 141, 2003 Del. Super. LEXIS 315 (Del. Super. Ct., 2003)



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Defendants' cross-motion for summary judgment granted. Plaintiffs' motion for summary judgment granted in part and motion stayed in part. Permanent injunction granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff taxpayers moved for summary judgment in an action against defendants, a county and its county executive and county administrator, seeking to enjoin the county from paying attorneys' fees and from conducting defense work in county office space and interviewing county employees during work hours. Defendants filed a cross-motion for summary judgment. A temporary restraining order had been issued by the court.


OVERVIEW: The investigation concerned the use of county employees for political campaign purposes. The court held that the amendment to New Castle County, Del., Code § 2.03.201, providing indemnification in some circumstances for legal fees of county officials, was properly retroactive. The statute was constitutional under Del. Const. art. VIII, § 8. Indemnification furthered, inter alia, the public purpose of encouraging the recruitment and retention of high-risk officers. Defendants were permanently enjoined from interviewing county employees during working hours and using county office space. It was premature to enjoin the payment of the legal fees, as the county attorney had not made a determination as to whether the fees would be paid. Relief was not available at law under a writ of certiorari, mandamus, or prohibition, providing jurisdiction to the Court of Chancery of Delaware. Further, the chancery court had jurisdiction through the injunctive relief already granted. Maintaining jurisdiction over the entirety of the action met the factors for chancery court jurisdiction.


OUTCOME: Defendants' cross-motion for summary judgment were granted. The taxpayers' motion for summary judgment was granted as to the request to enjoin the use of county office space and the interviewing of county employees during working hours. The request to enjoin the payment of legal fees for legal representation of the county executive and the county administrator was stayed.
