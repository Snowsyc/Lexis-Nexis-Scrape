CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a declaratory relief action involving the certification of nonpublic, nonsectarian schools, the Los Angeles County Superior Court, California, entered a judgment of dismissal in favor of defendants, the California Superintendent of Public Instruction and the California Department of Education. Plaintiffs, an association that represented 182 schools and a school that alleged it was adversely affected by the Department's practices, appealed.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs argued that the Superintendent could not suspend or revoke the certification of a nonpublic, nonsectarian school providing educational services to disabled children without providing a hearing with proper notice before any adverse administrative action could be taken. OUTCOME: The judgment of dismissal was affirmed.

DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was convicted, among other things, of manufacturing methamphetamine, Cal. Health & Saf. The Superior Court of the County of Lake, California, imposed a sentence that included a three-year term for the volume enhancement. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: The trial court gave a jury instruction that permitted a true finding even if the substance containing methamphetamine, a solvent, had not yet been used in the manufacturing process. Defendant argued that the instruction exceeded the scope of § 11379.8 and that the volume enhancement applied only to a substance produced during manufacture. The court disagreed and concluded that the volume enhancement applied to any substance containing methamphetamine that was produced, used, or to be used in the process of manufacturing methamphetamine. Further, § 11379.8 was not unconstitutionally vague; the plain language, statutory intent, and cases interpreting the underlying offense provided notice. The court held, however, that the trial court mistakenly believed it lacked discretion in imposing the enhancement.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the sentence and remanded to permit the trial court to determine whether or not to exercise its discretion.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant city sought review of a judgment from the Superior Court of Alameda County (California), which, in an action brought by respondent landlords' association, issued a peremptory writ of mandate enjoining the city from enforcing a mandatory inspection program for rental housing units as set forth in Hayward Mun. OVERVIEW: As originally written, the ordinance forced landlords to grant city inspectors access to occupied units without tenant consent. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner housing corporation sought a writ of mandate and challenged a decision of respondent Superior Court of San Joaquin County (California), which denied the housing corporation's motion to compel reference of an action against the corporation for defective home construction damages brought by real parties in interest, both original and non-original home purchasers. The court issued an alternative writ of mandate and stayed all proceedings.


OVERVIEW: The original and non-original purchasers sought damages for alleged defects in their homes. The housing corporation sought to have the matter heard by a referee, as the purchase agreements between the housing corporation and the original purchasers required all disputes to be determined by judicial reference. The superior court denied the motion to compel, and the housing corporation sought a writ of mandate. The court refused to issue a writ with regard to the non-original purchasers because they never consented to judicial reference. The court did issue a writ with regard to the original purchasers. The court found that the reference provision in the agreements was not unconscionable under Cal. Civ. Code § 1670.5. The reference provision was clear and conspicuously placed in the agreements, and the provision was not one-sided or oppressive. Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 645.1(a) required that a referee's fees be paid as agreed by the parties, and the court found no harshness in the parties' agreement to share referee fees equally. Under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, the superior court and the court lacked authority to invalidate the valid contractual reference provision.


OUTCOME: The court denied a writ of mandate against the superior court's order denying the housing corporation's motion to compel reference of those claims brought by the non-original purchasers. The court granted the petition as to the claims brought by the original purchasers. The court vacated the stay.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff contractors challenged defendant city's law requiring preferential treatment to women and minorities in the awarding of city contracts. The trial court held that the ordinance violated Cal. Const., art. I, § 31. The California Court of Appeal, First District, Division Four, affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded the matter to determine whether the federal equal protection clause required the ordinance. Review was granted.


OVERVIEW: The court held that Cal. Const., art. I, § 31, which forbid a public entity awarding public contracts to discriminate or grant preferential treatment based on race or gender, did not violate the political structure doctrine. The doctrine did not invalidate state laws that broadly forbid preferences and discrimination based on race, gender and other similar classifications. Section 31 prohibited race- and gender-conscious programs the federal equal protection clause permitted but did not require. Instead of burdening the right to equal treatment, § 31 directly served the principle that all governmental use of race had to have a logical end point. A core purpose of the equal protection clause was to do away with all governmentally imposed discrimination based on race, ultimately creating a political system in which race no longer mattered. The federal funding exception in § 31, subd. (e), did not exempt the ordinance from § 31's general prohibition of racial preferences. The court agreed with the appellate court that remand was necessary for the limited purpose of adjudicating whether the equal protection clause required the ordinance as a remedy for the city's own discrimination.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the appellate court was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner school district sought review of a decision by the California Workers' Compensation Appeals Board, which found that respondent teacher had sustained a work-related injury, the injury caused permanent partial disability after adjustment for age and occupation of 34 percent, and the teacher needed further medical treatment.


OVERVIEW: The issue was whether new laws enacted by S.B. 899, 2003-2004 Reg. Sess. (Cal. 2004), specifically Cal. Lab. Code §§ 4663, 4664, requiring apportionment based on causation, applied retroactively in this case, which was submitted to a workers' compensation judge for decision prior to the effective date of the legislation, but on which an award and findings were not issued until four days after the effective date of the legislation. The court held the repeal of former Cal. Lab. Code § 4663 was effective immediately on enactment of S.B. 899 and that Cal. Lab. Code §§ 4663, 4664, were applicable to any case still pending, except those cases that were finally concluded subject only to the WCAB's continuing jurisdiction under Cal. Lab. Code §§ 5803, 5804. The use of the language "regardless of the date of injury" in S.B. 899 clearly reflected the understanding and intent of the legislature that S.B. 899 would be applicable to pending workers' compensation cases and not just to new compensable injuries occurring after its effective date. The WCAB was incorrect in holding the apportionment provisions of S.B. 899 did not apply.


OUTCOME: The decision of the WCAB was annulled, and the matter was remanded for further proceedings.

DISPOSITION: Writ Granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a dependency case, an attorney on behalf of petitioners, two children who were being home schooled, filed a petition for a writ of mandate. Counsel sought an order directing respondent, the Los Angeles County Superior Court, California, to order that the children be enrolled in a public or private school for their safety.


OVERVIEW: In an opinion on rehearing, the court concluded that California statutes permit home schooling. Although the legislature had not amended the statutory scheme so as to expressly permit home schooling, the legislature had apparently accepted the proposition that home schools were permissible in California when conducted as private schools. Several statutory enactments indicated a legislative approval of home schooling, by exempting home schools from requirements otherwise applicable to private schools. However, the court also concluded that parents did not possess an absolute right to home school their children. Although parents possessed a constitutional liberty interest in directing the education of their children, that right had to yield to state interests in certain circumstances. The statutory permission to home school could constitutionally be overridden in order to protect the safety of a child who had been declared dependent. An order that would require the children to attend school for their safety would not be an unconstitutional violation of their parents' right to direct the children's education because the parents had already been judicially determined not to be fit.


OUTCOME: The petition for writ of mandate was granted, and the matter was remanded to the trial court with directions to vacate its order denying the motion to require the children to attend public or traditional private school, and to reconsider the motion. The trial court was directed to consider whether the children's safety necessitated removing them from home schooling.
