Mumm v. Mornson, 2004 Minn. App. LEXIS 1359 (Minn. Ct. App., Dec. 7, 2004).



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, a city and city police officers, appealed a decision of the Minnesota Court of Appeals affirming the district court's denial of their motion for summary judgment in an action brought by respondents, a pedestrian's surviving spouse, the driver, and the owner of the vehicle, alleging constitutional violations and state tort claims for the officers' conduct during their pursuit of the driver.


OVERVIEW: A police pursuit of the reportedly suicidal driver ended when officers rammed their car into the driver's vehicle resulting in the driver's vehicle killing the pedestrian. The court held that the driver adequately asserted a deprivation of her Fourth Amendment right to be free from the unreasonable use of deadly force because she had committed no crime when they first began following her. Because the officers should have known that the prerequisites for the constitutional use of deadly force had not been met, they were not entitled to summary judgment on qualified immunity grounds as to the driver's Fourth Amendment claim. Analyzing under the Lewis intent-to-injure standard, the court found that the spouse had not sufficiently asserted a deprivation of the pedestrian's substantive due process rights as the officers' poor judgment did not automatically convert their acts into conscience-shocking conduct. Because the officers failed to perform the ministerial duty to discontinue their pursuit in accordance with police policy because they knew the driver's identity, the officers were not entitled to common law official immunity for their decision to initiate and continue the pursuit.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the denial of summary judgment as to the spouse's substantive due process claim. The court otherwise affirmed the judgment of the appellate court.

Mendota Golf, LLP v. City of Mendota Heights

708 N.W.2d 162; 2006 Minn. LEXIS 4

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Court of Appeals.
Mendota Golf, L.L.P. v. City of Mendota Heights, 2004 Minn. App. LEXIS 1108 (Minn. Ct. App., Sept. 28, 2004).



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.



CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant city challenged a decision of the Minnesota Court of Appeals affirming a district court writ of mandamus compelling the city to amend its comprehensive plan to allow respondent golf course company to turn its golf course property into a residential development. The golf course property was zoned residential.


OVERVIEW: After the city council denied the company's proposed comprehensive plan amendment, the company's mandamus action claimed that the "golf course" designation in the comprehensive plan had no direct corresponding zoning classification. The court held that the presence of alternative ways to reconcile the conflict between the comprehensive plan and the zoning ordinance indicated that the city did not have a clear duty to amend the comprehensive plan to conform to the zoning ordinance. The district court exceeded the scope of its authority by interfering with the exercise of legislative discretion and ordering the city to reconcile the conflict by amending the plan. Under the Metropolitan Land Planning Act, Minn. Stat. § 473.859, subd. 1 (2004), the comprehensive municipal plan guided land use in cities within the metropolitan area. While a declaratory action would have been a proper procedure for reviewing the matter, the court nevertheless considered the parties' arguments and found that legitimate objectives supported the city's denial of an amendment to the comprehensive plan. The company failed to show that the city lacked a rational basis for the decision.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded to the district court to issue a writ of mandamus directing the city to reconcile the comprehensive plan and zoning ordinance provisions.

State v. Henderson

SUPREME COURT OF MINNESOTA


706 N.W.2d 758; 2005 Minn. LEXIS 751



December 1, 2005, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Court of Appeals.
State v. Henderson, 2004 Minn. App. LEXIS 991 (Minn. Ct. App., Aug. 31, 2004).



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded for resentencing.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Minnesota Court of Appeals entered an order affirming defendant's 144-month sentence for first-degree burglary as a career offender under Minn. Stat. § 609.1095 (2004). Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: The trial court departed and imposed a sentence of 144 months under the career offender statute, Minn. Stat. § 609.1095, after finding that defendant had five or more prior felony convictions and that the present offense was committed as part of a pattern of criminal conduct. Defendant appealed the durational departure to the court of appeals, arguing that his prior convictions did not establish a pattern of criminal conduct sufficient to sentence him as a career criminal. The court of appeals affirmed the sentence, holding that the Blakely rule did not apply to upward departures under the career offender statute because the determination of a pattern of criminal conduct was a legal conclusion, not a finding of fact. The supreme court held that recidivism was a narrow exception to the general rule that all facts going to punishment had to be found by a jury; therefore, the determination of a pattern of criminal conduct under Minn. Stat. § 609.1095 (2004) went beyond solely the fact of a prior conviction, and the imposition of an enhanced sentence based on the trial court's finding of a pattern of criminal conduct violated defendant's Sixth Amendment right to trial by jury.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the court of appeals was reversed, and the matter remanded to the trial court for resentencing.
