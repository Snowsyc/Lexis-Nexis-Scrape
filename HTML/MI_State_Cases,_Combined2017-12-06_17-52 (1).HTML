CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The People challenged a decision from the Oakland Circuit Court, Michigan, which concluded that a charge under MCL 750.237 had been properly dismissed. Defendant filed a cross-appeal.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was arrested for being in possession of a firearm while intoxicated. The trial court dismissed the charge primarily based on U.S. Const. amend. II. This appeal followed. In affirming, the appellate court determined that both U.S. Const. amend. II and Const. 1963, art 1, § 6 granted individuals the right to keep and bear arms for self-defense. However, this right was not unlimited, and the categorical regulation of gun possession by certain classes of person, such as felons and the mentally ill, was permitted. There was a two-prong test used to determine if MCL 750.237 was constitutional as applied to defendant. The prosecution failed to show that defendant's conduct fell outside of the protections of U.S. Const. amend. II. Next, the State failed to show that there was a reasonable fit between the asserted governmental objective and the burden placed on the individual. Here, defendant's possession was constructive, rather than actual. Based on MCL 28.425k(2), the government's legitimate concern was not that a person who had consumed alcohol was in the vicinity of the firearm, but that he actually had it in his physical possession.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant city challenged a decision from the Livingston Circuit Court, Michigan, which granted partial summary disposition in favor of plaintiff owners in a dispute relating to the constitutionality of Brighton, Mich., Code of Ordinances § 18-59.


OVERVIEW: The city informed the owners that their properties constituted unsafe structures and public nuisances. They were informed that it was unreasonable to repair the structures under Brighton, Mich., Code of Ordinances § 18-59. Partial summary disposition was granted for the owners, and this appeal followed. In affirming, the court of appeals held that the ordinance at issue only allowed the exercise of an option to repair when the owners overcame or rebutted the presumption of unreasonableness by proving that it was economical to do so, regardless of whether the owners were otherwise willing and able to timely make the necessary repairs. The ordinance's exclusion of a repair option when city officials deemed repairs unreasonable on the basis of expense the owner was able and willing to incur bore no reasonable relationship to the legislative objective. Moreover, there was no safeguard to an owner's right to retain the property. As such, the ordinance violated substantive and procedural due process.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a judgment of the Kent Circuit Court (Michigan) that convicted him of possession of ketamine under MCL 333.7403(2)(b)(ii), arguing that the prosecution failed to establish, as an element of the charged offense, that the ketamine was not in a proportion or concentration to vitiate the potential for abuse, as such diluted substances were excluded from the Controlled Substances Act, MCL 333.7101 et seq.


OVERVIEW: Defendant's probation officer went to defendant's home for an unscheduled visit. When defendant came to the door, his pupils were dilated, his face was flushed, and he was confused and disoriented. He consented to a search of his residence, during which search probation officers found drugs. At defendant's trial on a charge of possession of ketamine, defense counsel moved for a directed verdict on the ground that the prosecution failed to establish that the drug was not in a concentration to vitiate the potential for abuse. The trial court denied the motion, and defendant raised the identical issue on appeal. On review, the court held that the elements of possession under MCL 333.7403 were limited to knowing or intentional possession of a controlled substance. Once the prosecution presented a prima facie case of those elements, defendant bore the burden to affirmatively establish the exception to the Controlled Substance Act (CSA) under MCL 333.7227 as an affirmative defense. Defendant had the burden of proof in relation to exceptions to the CSA, as exceptions were affirmative defenses, not elements of the underlying offense.


OUTCOME: The judgment of conviction was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant insurer sought review of a judgment from the Oakland Circuit Court (Michigan), which partially granted summary disposition to plaintiff insureds in their action regarding disputed coverage for fire damage. The trial court held that the insureds' adjuster was "competent" and "independent" under MCL 500.2833(l)(m), such that he could serve as their appraiser.


OVERVIEW: The insureds' residence was severely damaged by a fire. They hired a public adjusting firm to help them present their claim to the insurer. When a dispute developed between the adjuster and the insurer, the adjuster sent an appraisal demand pursuant to § 500.2833(l)(m). However, when the insurer refused to allow the adjuster to be the insureds' appraiser, they filed suit. The trial court granted partial summary disposition to the insureds upon finding that the adjuster was "competent" and "independent," despite having a contingency-fee contract with the insureds for the adjusting. Accordingly, the trial court concluded he was qualified to be their appraiser. On appeal, the court agreed with the trial court ruling. It found that the fact that the adjuster had a contingency-fee contract did not prohibit him from being "independent," as he was capable of exercising his own judgment regarding the value of the loss in the proceeding. The use of the adjuster as an appraiser did not violate the insurer's due process rights, as he was not a quasi-judge.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: On motions for summary disposition by appellant Department of Treasury and appellee corporation, the Court of Claims ruled for the corporation that giving retroactive effect to 2007 PA 103 violated the corporation's right to due process, violated Const. 1963, art 4, § 29, and that the corporation's program vehicles were exempt from use tax under MCL 205.94(1)(c), as amended by 2007 PA 103. Treasury appealed and the corporation cross-appealed.


OVERVIEW: The appellate court found that the corporation had not overcome the presumption that 2007 PA 103 was constitutional and the Court of Claims erred by concluding otherwise because 2007 PA 103 did not violate due process since the act was rationally related to a legitimate legislative purpose, the limiting of an interpretation of the Use Tax Act that might have caused significant and unanticipated loss of tax revenue that had been collected in good faith. To the extent the Due Process Clause limited the reach of retroactive legislation to only a "modest" time frame, that limitation was not exceeded. 2007 PA 103 did not readjust rights and burdens or upset settled expectations such that the corporation's interest in finality exceeded the state's interest in revising the Use Tax Act to protect the precarious public fisc from refund claims that were as much as 11 years old. Nothing on the face of 2007 PA 103 supported the conclusion that it was "special" legislation governed by Const 1963, art 4, § 29.


OUTCOME: The court reversed and remanded for entry of judgment in favor of the Department of Treasury. The court did not retain jurisdiction. No taxable costs were assessed because questions of public policy were involved.

Blue Harvest, Inc. v. DOT


No. 281595


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


288 Mich. App. 267; 792 N.W.2d 798; 2010 Mich. App. LEXIS 787



April 29, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Leave to appeal denied by Blue Harvest v. DOT & Ottawa County Rd. Comm'n, 789 N.W.2d 439; 2010 Mich. LEXIS 2105 (Mich., Oct. 20, 2010)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Ottawa Circuit Court. LC Nos. 04-051026-CZ, 04-002340-MZ.
Blue Harvest, Inc. v. DOT, 483 Mich. 917, 762 N.W.2d 482, 2009 Mich. LEXIS 464 (2009)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Ottawa Circuit Court (Michigan), in plaintiff commercial blueberry growers' action against defendants, a state transportation agency and a county road commission, denied the agency's summary disposition motion asserting governmental immunity barred the growers' trespass-nuisance claim. It also awarded summary disposition to the agency and commission on the growers' inverse-condemnation claim. The agency appealed; the growers' cross-appealed.


OVERVIEW: The growers were engaged in the commercial production of blueberries. Their properties were adjacent to highways or primary county roads. The agency contracted with the commission to maintain the highways and county roads during the winter, when salt was used to prevent ice from forming on the highways and roads. The growers sued the agency and commission, and alleged that salt blown on to their properties by passing vehicles destroyed their crops. The growers sought recovery under trespass-nuisance and inverse condemnation theories. The agency alleged as to the trespass-nuisance claim that it had governmental immunity, but the trial court denied the agency's corresponding MCR 2.116(C)(7) summary disposition motion. However, it did grant summary disposition to the agency and commission on the inverse condemnation claim. The appellate court found that there was no trespass-nuisance exception to the sovereign immunity doctine and, thus, the agency's summary disposition motion alleging governmental immunity should have been granted. It also held, as to the inverse condemnation claim, that the growers had not shown they suffered a harm differing from similarly-situated landowners.


OUTCOME: The appellate court reversed the trial court's judgment that denied the agency's summary disposition motion claiming governmental immunity on the trespass-nuisance claim. It otherwise affirmed the trial court's judgment. It also remanded the case to the trial court for entry of a judgment in favor of the agency.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Oakland Circuit Court, Michigan, convicted defendant of failing to pay child support in violation of MCL 750.165, for which she was sentenced to probation for one year. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that she was entitled to a new trial because her rights under Michigan's due process clause were denied by the trial court's order prohibiting her from presenting as a defense her inability to pay ordered child support. The appellate court noted that defendant was prosecuted for failing to comply with an order that was entered after a judicial determination was made that she had the financial means to comply with the court order, i.e., the duty imposed upon defendant was adjudged possible for her to perform. Defendant's attempt to challenge the child support order by claiming an inability to pay the amount order was properly denied. Defendant was afforded numerous opportunities in the civil proceedings to establish her inability to pay the ordered amount of child support. She was not denied due process on the ground that, because the offense imposed strict liability, she was prevented from proving that her failure to pay child support in compliance with the court order was involuntary. Evidence of the inability to pay was not relevant. The prosecutor's remarks were an appropriate response to defense counsel's argument and not a personal attack on defense counsel.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Washtenaw Circuit Court. LC No. 05-001221-FH.
People v. Mungo, 483 Mich. 1091, 792 N.W.2d 686, 2009 Mich. LEXIS 1343 (2009)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: After the Washtenaw Circuit Court (Michigan) granted defendant's motion to suppress, the State appealed. On remand from the Michigan Supreme Court, the present court reconsidered its prior decision in light of the United States Supreme Court's holding in Gant that a vehicle could not be searched incident to a recent occupant's arrest after the arrestee had been secured and could not access the interior of the vehicle.


OVERVIEW: After a traffic stop, defendant's passenger was arrested on two outstanding warrants for traffic violations. The deputy searched defendant's vehicle incident to the passenger's arrest. The court held that under Gant, the warrantless search of defendant's car violated U.S. Const. amend. IV. Neither defendant nor the passenger would have been able to reach into the passenger compartment of the vehicle; thus, concern for officer safety was not at issue. Furthermore, because the passenger was arrested for traffic violations, there would have been no reasonable basis for the officer to conclude that evidence of those offenses could be found in a search of defendant's vehicle. Next, the search did not fall within the good faith exception to the exclusionary rule. Because the law in Michigan regarding a vehicle search solely incident to a passenger's arrest was not established and clear, the search and seizure of evidence from defendant's vehicle could not, as a matter of law, have been premised on law enforcement's good-faith reliance on case law. Thus, pursuant to the retroactivity doctrine, defendant was entitled to have the rule of law announced in Gant applied to his case.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's order granting defendant's motion to suppress evidence and quashing the information.
