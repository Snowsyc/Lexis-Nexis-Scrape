November 21, 2016, Submitted 
November 30, 2016, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Special Court Convened Under W. Va. Code § 3-7-3 [***1] .



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-In an election contest case, the record reflected that ten voters who failed to sign the poll book in violation of W. Va. Code § 3-1-34(a) (2016) (Supp. 2016) were qualified to vote and, in fact, voted; [2]-The Supreme Court of Appeals found no reason to disturb the special court's ruling on the votes cast at the precinct; [3]-Even if the record had shown that the poll workers in a second precinct failed to properly take the required oath, case law would not permit rejecting the ballots cast in the precinct merely because of this defect; [4]-Although poll workers at a third precinct erred in demarcating the "No Electioneering" zone at the polling place, the Supreme Court of Appeals held that the voters of West Virginia cannot be disenfranchised or criminally penalized because of such errors.


OUTCOME: Decision denying relief affirmed.

Appeal from the Circuit Court of Marion County. Honorable Fred L. Fox, II, Judge. Civil Action No. 98-F-167.
State v. Kent, 213 W. Va. 535, 584 S.E.2d 169, 2003 W. Va. LEXIS 62 (2003)



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A jury found defendant guilty of murder in the first degree, deliberate and premeditated. On appeal, defendant's conviction was reversed. Following a second trial, a jury found defendant guilty of murder of the first degree, felony murder. The Circuit Court of Marion County (West Virginia) denied defendant's motions for judgment of acquittal and for a new trial. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that the trial court erred in failing to conclude that the jury's consideration of felony murder at his second trial violated double jeopardy. The issue before the appellate court was whether double jeopardy was violated when defendant, who had been charged with first-degree murder under alternative theories of premeditation and felony murder, was first convicted of premeditated murder with no jury finding as to felony murder, but, upon retrial following reversal of the conviction, was found guilty under the alternative theory of felony murder. The appellate court held that there was no double jeopardy violation. At his first trial, the jury found defendant guilty of deliberate and premeditated first-degree murder, and remained silent as to the alternative theory of felony murder. Because premeditated murder and felony murder were but two alternative theories for establishing first-degree murder, the jury's election of one theory in defendant's first trial did not amount to an implied acquittal of the other theory. Consequently, double jeopardy did not bar the jury's consideration of felony murder in the second trial.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed.

Appeal from the Circuit Court of Boone County. Honorable William S. Thompson, Judge. Civil Action No. 06-C-15.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff former employee sued defendant former employer alleging retaliatory discharge under W. Va. Code § 23-5A-1 (2005) and refusal to reinstate employment under W. Va. Code § 23-5A-3(b) (2005). The trial court denied the employer's motion for summary judgment. As per a jury verdict, the Circuit Court of Boone County (West Virginia) awarded the employee $ 885,107 in compensatory and $ 1 million in punitive damages. The employer appealed.


OVERVIEW: The employee filed a workers' compensation claim after breaking his wrist at work. When the employee was cleared to return to work he notified a manager. It was not until a week later that the employee was contacted and told report to work in five hours. When the employee could not report that day, he was terminated. Contrary to the employer's contention, the supreme court of appeals concluded that the employee's claims under W. Va. Code §§ 23-5A-1 and 23-5A-3 were not preempted by federal labor law. Nor was the employee collaterally estopped from litigating whether his discharge constituted an unlawful retaliatory discharge, as a prior proceeding only addressed whether he had violated the collective bargaining agreement. The supreme court of appeals also found that the award of front pay was appropriate and that the employee was not required to mitigate damages given the employer's malicious conduct. Finally, the supreme appellate court concluded that the employee was entitled to punitive damages, noting that the employer placed him under surveillance when a doctor did not approve his return to work and only gave him five hours notice that he was required to report to work.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed.

PRIOR HISTORY: E. H. v. Matin, 201 W. Va. 463, 498 S.E.2d 35, 1997 W. Va. LEXIS 238 (1997)


DISPOSITION:  [***1] WRIT DENIED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner state health agency filed a writ of prohibition to bar the trial court from taking any action in a case involving the monitoring and care of individuals with traumatic brain injuries, as well as the issue of overcrowding and other potential statutory violations allegedly occurring at state psychiatric facilities. The state supreme court reviewed that request and the trial court's proposal to conduct an evidentiary hearing.


OVERVIEW: The state health agency filed a petition for writ of prohibition in which it sought to prevent the trial court from taking any action in an underlying mandamus action. The state health agency objected to the reopening of that underlying action for the purposes of conducting an evidentiary hearing on the progress in implementing care to individuals with traumatic brain injuries and examining the issue of overcrowding and other potential violations of W.Va. Code § 27-5-9 (2007) at state psychiatric facilities. The state supreme court found that the trial court's proposed order, to conduct an evidentiary hearing regarding the underlying matter so that the record regarding the alleged problems could be developed accordingly, fell within the trial court's power to ensure that a state agency complied with the legislative mandates. Indeed, the state supreme court specifically found that the trial court had the power to ensure that patients were receiving treatment guaranteed to them under W. Va. Code § 27-5-9 (2007). It also held that the trial court had the power to enforce a consent order it had previously issued regarding the matter.


OUTCOME: The state supreme court denied the petition for writ of prohibition.
