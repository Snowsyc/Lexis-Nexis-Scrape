PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent developer challenged the legality of two conditions imposed by petitioner city for approval of a preliminary plat for a residential subdivision. The trial court ruled that both conditions were unlawful. The Washington Court of Appeals affirmed as to the first condition, an open space requirement, but reversed as to the second condition, a secondary access road, and upheld the condition. The city sought discretionary review.


OVERVIEW: In this Washington Land Use Petition Act case, the challenged conditions included a 30 percent open space set aside and provision of a secondary limited access road into the proposed development for emergency vehicles. Regarding the open space requirement, the instant court noted that the court of appeals determined that the requirement constituted an unconstitutional taking. However, the instant court concluded that the open space set aside condition violated Wash. Rev. Code § 82.02.020, and it did not reach arguments respecting the constitutionality of this condition. The open space set aside condition did not fall within any exception in § 82.02.020. Regarding the secondary limited access road condition, the instant court held that, after balancing the various factors, this condition did not violate substantive due process and was not arbitrary or capricious.


OUTCOME: The decision of the court of appeals was affirmed, and the matter was remanded for further proceedings.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner mobile home park owners' association appealed from an order of the Washington Court of Appeals, which affirmed the judgment of the trial court dismissing petitioner's complaint alleging that Wash. Rev. Code § 59.23.005 violated the takings clause of Wash. Const. art. I, § 16.


OVERVIEW: Petitioner mobile home park owners' sought a declaration that the mobile home parks--resident ownership act (the Act), Wash. Rev. Stat. § 59.23.001 et seq., created an unconstitutional taking of property in violation of amended Wash. Const. art. I, § 16, as well as U.S. Const. amend. V. The superior court denied petitioner's motion for summary judgment and dismissed the complaint. The Court of Appeals affirmed, holding the Act did not amount to an unconstitutional taking of property. OUTCOME: The judgment was reversed and the statute was invalidated as unconstitutional; the statutory grant of a right of first refusal to mobile home park tenants amounted to a taking without just compensation having been first paid. Moreover, such transfer was legislatively granted to tenants who were private persons, not the public, an absolute prohibition with or without compensation.
