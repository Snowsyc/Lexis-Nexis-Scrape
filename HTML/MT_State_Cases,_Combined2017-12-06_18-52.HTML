DISPOSITION: District Court is affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff inmate filed a complaint, pursuant to 42 U.S.C. §§ 1983, 1985, and 1988, in the First Judicial District Court (Montana) seeking to address alleged deprivations of his rights by defendant prison officials. The trial court granted the officials' motion to dismiss the complaint. The inmate appealed, and the supreme court remanded the case to the trial court. On remand, the trial court dismissed his remaining claims. The inmate appealed.


OVERVIEW: The inmate argued the trial court erred in granting judgment to the prison officials without first conducting a hearing. The supreme court's direction to "remand for further proceedings," did not require the trial court to conduct a hearing. Accordingly, the supreme court held that the trial court did not err when declining to conduct a hearing on remand. The inmate next argued that the trial court erred in holding that he failed to state a cognizable claim under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983. The trial court held that his claims were barred by Heck v. Humphrey. The inmate argued that, unlike Heck, his claim did not implicate the validity of his continued incarceration. He stated that, as his sentence was for a total of 194 years, a favorable ruling on earned good time credits would only allow him an earlier parole appearance. The supreme court held that that the inmate's claims, if proven, would necessarily imply the invalidity of his conviction or sentence and were therefore not cognizable under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983, unless his conviction or sentence was invalidated. His claims were not barred by the doctrine of collateral estoppel and could have been brought in a habeas corpus proceeding.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment was affirmed.
