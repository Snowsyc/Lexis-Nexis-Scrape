287 Mich. App. 648; 792 N.W.2d 7; 2010 Mich. App. LEXIS 565



March 30, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Leave to appeal denied by, Motion denied by People v. Malone, 790 N.W.2d 675; 2010 Mich. LEXIS 2319 (Mich., Nov. 22, 2010)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Wayne Circuit Court. LC No. 08-003077-FH.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Following a jury trial in the Wayne Circuit Court (Michigan) defendant was convicted of stealing or retaining a financial transaction device without consent, MCL 750.157n(1), and she was sentenced to one year of probation. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was a county employee and several other employees reported identity theft. A search warrant was executed and in defendant's home the employees' personal and financial information was discovered. Defendant argued that the statute required an actual attempt to gain access to the accounts and that her possession of the information was inadvertent and innocent. Defendant's testimony that she had copied the information in the course of her employment was contradicted, and the jury believed the other testimony. MCL 750.157m did not require an attempt to gain access to the accounts and defendant's decision to copy the information without the authorization of the employees was conduct prohibited by the plain language of the statute, MCL 750.157n. Although the accounts of the employees had not been compromised, the statute punished retention or possession that could have been used to access proprietary accounts, MCL 750.157m(f). Accordingly, defendant's challenge to the constitutionality of the statute with regard to her conduct was without merit. The evidence complained of was properly admitted to show how the investigation was initiated and how it had come to focus on defendant.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed.

Practical Political Consulting, Inc. v. Sec'y of State


No. 291176


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


287 Mich. App. 434; 789 N.W.2d 178; 2010 Mich. App. LEXIS 451



March 9, 2010, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Ingham Circuit Court. LC No. 08-000706-CZ.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff group brought an action against defendants, the Michigan Secretary of State and the Michigan Department of State, under the Freedom of Information Act, MCL 15.231 et seq. Plaintiff sought to obtain the "separate records" created under § 615c(3) of 2007 PA 52 for the 2008 presidential primary. The Ingham Circuit Court (Michigan) ruled in favor of plaintiff. Defendants appealed.


OVERVIEW: The court first noted that MCL 168.495a(2) had been repealed by 2007 PA 52 but was now back in effect because § 615c had been ruled unconstitutional. The court stated that the "voter registration records" that MCL 168.495a(2) exempted from disclosure were distinct from the "separate records" kept under § 615c(3). The "separate records" were not voter registration records, but records of the participating political party ballots that electors selected at their polling places. Furthermore, the "information" kept under § 615c(3) was not an elector's "declaration of party preference" (or no preference), and it was only such declarations of party preference that MCL 168.495a(2) exempted from disclosure. Thus, the statutory exemption to disclosure of MCL 15.243(1)(d) applied neither to these "separate records" nor to the information contained therein. Finally, the records did not fall within the privacy exemption of MCL 15.243(1)(a). The indication of a ballot that an elector wished to vote in the 2008 presidential primary was not information of a personal nature, and even if it was, its disclosure would not be a clearly unwarranted invasion of that elector's privacy.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's decision.

Mich. Deferred Presentment Servs. Ass'n v. Ross


No. 292685


COURT OF APPEALS OF MICHIGAN


287 Mich. App. 326; 788 N.W.2d 842; 2010 Mich. App. LEXIS 331



February 18, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Leave to appeal denied by Mich. Deferred Presentment Servs. Ass'n v. Ross, 785 N.W.2d 156; 2010 Mich. LEXIS 1647 (Mich., July 26, 2010)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Oakland Circuit Court. LC No. 2008-091732-CZ.
Mich. Deferred Presentment Servs. Assoc. v. Ross, 485 Mich. 934, 773 N.W.2d 726, 2009 Mich. LEXIS 2579 (2009)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, an association of companies licensed under the Deferred Presentment Service Transactions Act (DPSTA), MCL 487.2121 et seq., brought an action under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983 against defendant Commissioner of the Office of Financial and Insurance Regulation. The Oakland Circuit Court (Michigan) struck down an administrative order issued by defendant as an unconstitutional infringement of the right of access to the courts. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Before passage of the DPSTA, payday lenders who received checks that were returned to them for nonsufficient funds (NSF) obtained judgments under MCL 600.2952(4), which allowed for treble damages. The administrative order directed licensees to conform to the limitations in MCL 487.2158 regarding checks returned due to insufficient funds. The court held that the administrative order was a correct interpretation of the applicable law. MCL 600.2952 and MCL 487.2185 were in pari materia and irreconcilably conflicted; thus, the more specific statute, MCL 487.2158, which applied only to payday lenders, controlled. Furthermore, under the principle of expressio unius est exclusio alterius, the DPSTA preserved legal remedies existing before its enactment for checks dishonored because of a closed account or a stop payment order, but not preexisting legal remedies against a person who gave a NSF check. Thus, the only thing that defendant was "ordering" the lenders to do was to abide by the provisions of the statute. Furthermore, the lenders were not being denied access to the courts under U.S. Const. amend. I. They were not prohibited from bringing causes of action to recover for NSF checks.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's decision. It remanded the case for proceedings consistent with its opinion.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Ingham Circuit Court. LC No. 02-000308-CZ. Ingham Circuit Court. LC No. 02-000308-CZ.
County Rd. Ass'n v. Governor of Mich., 474 Mich. 11, 705 N.W.2d 680, 2005 Mich. LEXIS 2248 (2005)



DISPOSITION: Vacated and remanded for dismissal.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, a nonprofit corporation and a county road commission, brought an action against defendants, state agents and agencies, in which plaintiffs challenged defendants' reallocation of certain revenues. The Ingham Circuit Court (Michigan) ordered the restoration of certain funds and required the state to perform a new study. Both parties appealed.


OVERVIEW: The nonprofit corporation represented the interests of county road commissions or public works departments. The court held that plaintiffs had failed to establish standing. The injury that they alleged--that the commissions received reduced distributions from the Michigan Transportation Fund (MTF) and related funds--did not constitute an injury distinct from that suffered by the public at large. Further, plaintiffs had failed to establish that a causal connection existed between the injury and the conduct complained of, namely, a reduction in the distribution of MTF revenues to the commissions. Even if plaintiffs' lack of standing were not at issue, the cause of action would still be barred by the doctrine of sovereign immunity. No constitutional or statutory provisions waived the state's sovereign immunity from suits concerning these types of budgetary appropriations. Furthermore, the court was not aware of any circumstance under which a constitutional or statutory right could be conferred upon a governmental body. It was individual taxpayers, not local governmental entities, who would have standing to challenge the violation of a right conferred by the state constitution.


OUTCOME: The court dismissed the appeal and vacated the cause of action.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Michigan Court of Claims entered a judgment that granted summary disposition in favor of defendant state treasury department in a tax dispute case after it found that plaintiff refund applicants were not entitled to a statutory bad debt deduction. The refund applicants appealed.


OVERVIEW: The refund applicants were in the business of financing transactions and believed that they overstated their gross receipts due to bad debts. Because of the DaimlerChrysler ruling, they filed sales tax refund claims. However, the relevant statute, MCL 205.54i, had been amended to place limitations on recovery under the bad debt provision. The amendment to MCL 205.54i also contained an enacting provision, which made it clear that the statute had been enacted to cure what the Legislature believed was the erroneous DaimlerChrysler ruling as to who could recover sales tax refunds. The refund applicants claimed that they were still entitled to a refund. The state treasury department claimed that the statute, as amended, expressed the Legislature's intent. The court of claims granted the state treasury department's summary disposition motion. The appellate court found that the plain language of the statute, not the ruling, governed the refund request regarding the bad debt provision. It also found that, constitutionally speaking, the refund applicants lacked a vested right in a tax refund and had no right to expect that the DaimlerChrysler ruling would continue to be the law.


OUTCOME: The appellate court affirmed the judgment of the court of claims.
