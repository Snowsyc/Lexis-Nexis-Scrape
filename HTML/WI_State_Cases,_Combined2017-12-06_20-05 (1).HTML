PRIOR HISTORY: APPEALS from judgments of the circuit court for Burnett County: JAMES H. TAYLOR, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part; reversed in part.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the judgments of the Circuit Court for Burnett County (Wisconsin), which determined that defendant constructed a dwelling without a permit and that he extended or enlarged a private sewage disposal system without a permit.


OVERVIEW: The trial court determined that defendant constructed a dwelling without a permit and that he extended or enlarged a private sewage disposal system without a permit. On appeal, the court found that the definition of dwelling contained in Burnett County, Wis., Land Use Ordinance § 2.1(8) did not encompass defendant's building, because the building was not designed or used exclusively as living quarters. It was inappropriate to find that defendant had constructed a dwelling without a permit. That judgment was reversed. Defendant did violate the sanitary ordinance, Burnett County, Wis., Sanitary Ordinance § I.A.5, by connecting the garage's plumbing to an existing septic system without a sanitary permit. The ordinance was not unconstitutionally vague because it gave fair notice of its requirements and provided an objective standard for its enforcement. That judgment was affirmed.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed in part; defendant needed a sanitary permit to connect the garage plumbing to the existing septic system, and the ordinance was not unconstitutionally vague. Judgment reversed in part; defendant's garage was not a dwelling, as such, defendant did not violate an ordinance by failing to obtain a permit to construct a dwelling.

PRIOR HISTORY:   [*1] APPEAL from an order of the circuit court for Dane County: MARYANNE SUMI, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from an order by the Circuit Court of Dane County (Wisconsin) denying her motion to dismiss her charge for operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was arrested in a parking lot of a four-unit apartment complex and charged with operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated. On a motion to dismiss, defendant challenged the constitutionality of Wis. Stat. § 346.61, claiming it violated the Equal Protection Clause. The trial court decided that § 346.61 did not violate the Equal Protection Clause and defendant appealed. The court agreed with the trial court and affirmed. The court found that § 346.61 met the rational basis test. The legislature could reasonably conclude that operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated was more of a safety hazard on a parking lot of rental housing with four or more units than on the parking lot of rental housing with fewer units, or on the parking lot for a residence that was not rental housing. The state had a legitimate interest in protecting persons from injury by intoxicated drivers.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's order. The statute at issue met the rational basis test under equal protection analysis. The statute treated persons differently only insofar as the persons were operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated on different types of premises.

PRIOR HISTORY:   [*1] APPEAL from a judgment of the circuit court for Milwaukee County: TIMOTHY G. DUGAN, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant sought review of his conviction for first-degree reckless homicide from the Circuit Court for Milwaukee County (Wisconsin), where polygraph evidence was excluded from a hearing, self-defense instructions were not allowed to be given to the jury, and an adult sentence was given to the juvenile appellant.


OVERVIEW: Appellant, a 15-year-old, shot and killed his father's roommate. Appellant contested several rulings of the trial court. The court found that polygraph evidence was properly excluded from a suppression hearing because appellant relied on authority that pertained to parole issues, not criminal proceedings. A custodial statement was properly admitted where appellant did nothing more than allege improper police conduct and later gave an inculpatory statement to other officers. A self-defense instruction was properly excluded where appellant confronted his intended robbery victim with a gun and announced that he wanted the victim's money. Appellant could not challenge statutes governing juvenile disposition on vagueness grounds because they governed procedure and did not prohibit specific conduct. Finally, appellant's adult sentence was proper where the trial court contemplated appellant's background, recognizing appellant was an "angry young boy" whose father had a drug problem and mother had psychological problems.


OUTCOME: Conviction affirmed because polygraph evidence was properly excluded, there was no factual basis to support giving a self-defense instructions to the jury, and an adult sentence for the juvenile appellant was properly considered and applied.

PRIOR HISTORY: APPEAL from a judgment and an order of the circuit court for Winnebago County: BRUCE K. SCHMIDT, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed the judgment and order of the circuit court for Winnebago County (Wisconsin), which convicted defendant for operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated and denied his motion to suppress evidence of the violation.


OVERVIEW: The trial court convicted defendant for operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated and denied his motion to suppress evidence of the violation. On appeal, defendant contended that his stop by the officer was unreasonable and unlawful. The court concluded that the U.S. Const. amend. IV "community caretaker" exception was applicable. A Fourth Amendment seizure of defendant did occur. The officer was involved in a bona fide community caretaker activity that could have focused on either assisting the vehicle's operator or, if necessary, protecting the marina property. The inconvenience of a motorist rolling down his or her vehicle window and answering an officer's inquiry under the circumstances described by the officer here was far outweighed by the benefit to the motorist who needed assistance or to the security of the area. The officer's actions leading to defendant's arrest and conviction did not violate defendant's Fourth Amendment protections against unlawful seizure.


OUTCOME: Judgment and order affirmed; the Fourth Amendment "community caretaker" exception was applicable, a Fourth Amendment seizure of defendant did occur, the officer was engaged in a bona fide community caretaker activity, and the public need outweighed the intrusion upon defendant's privacy.

PRIOR HISTORY: APPEAL and CROSS-APPEAL from a judgment of the circuit court for Dunn County: CONRAD A. RICHARDS, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a decision by the Circuit Court for Dunn County (Wisconsin) entering judgment for plaintiffs on their claims of negligent infliction of emotional distress arising from the wrongful death of their son, and plaintiffs cross-appealed the award for loss of society and companionship and for loss of pecuniary benefits.


OVERVIEW: The trial court entered a judgment in favor of plaintiffs for negligent infliction of emotional distress, loss of society and companionship, and loss of pecuniary benefits arising from the wrongful death of their son in an automobile accident with defendant driver, who was intoxicated. Defendants, driver and insurance company, appealed the judgment for negligent infliction of emotional distress, and plaintiff cross-appealed the amount of the award for loss of society and companionship. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The defendant appealed from a judgment of the circuit court for Marathon County (Wisconsin), convicting him of kidnapping and false imprisonment, while using a dangerous weapon, and denying defendant sentence credit for the time spent in custody on murder charges.


OVERVIEW: The defendant's prosecution for kidnapping and false imprisonment was not barred by double jeopardy due to his previous acquittal of murdering the victim because the jury in the murder trial had not necessarily found that he had not abducted the victim. Evidence from a previous murder trial was properly admitted, and admission of the evidence comported with due process guarantees. The defendant was not entitled to sentence credit for time spent in custody on murder charges.
