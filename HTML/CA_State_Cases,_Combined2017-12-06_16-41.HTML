Six Flags, Inc. v. Workers' Comp. Appeals Bd.


B184245


COURT OF APPEAL OF CALIFORNIA, SECOND APPELLATE DISTRICT, DIVISION THREE


145 Cal. App. 4th 91; 51 Cal. Rptr. 3d 377; 2006 Cal. App. LEXIS 1855; 2006 Cal. Daily Op. Service 10873; 2006 Daily Journal DAR 15485



November 27, 2006, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Petition for writ of review, WCAB No. VNO 0498554.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners, an employer and its workers' compensation insurer, challenged a decision from respondent Workers' Compensation Appeals Board (California), which awarded a death benefit under Lab. Code, § 4702, subd. (a)(6)(B), to the estate of a deceased worker and also made an award to the state pursuant to Lab. Code, § 4706.5.


OVERVIEW: The decedent, who had no dependents, sustained a fatal injury in the course and scope of her employment. The court held that because Cal. Const., art. XIV, § 4, did not identify estates as a class of beneficiaries entitled to workers' compensation death benefits, the Legislature was not empowered to promulgate Lab. Code, § 4702, subd. (a)(6)(B), which was therefore unconstitutional. The plenary power clause of Cal. Const., art. XIV, § 4, could not be used to expand the Legislature's power to award workers' compensation death benefits beyond the purpose of compensating dependents if a worker died in the course and scope of employment. Awarding death benefits to non-dependents did not further the underlying policy of the workers' compensation laws, which was to compensate workers and their dependents for a loss of earnings based upon death. Although death benefit awards to the state had been authorized by means of a state constitutional amendment, there was no such authority to award death benefits to deceased workers' estates. The term "dependents" in Cal. Const., art. XIV, § 4, did not include a deceased worker's estate.


OUTCOME: The court annulled the award to the decedent's estate and affirmed the award to the state.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff city appealed a judgment of the Stanislaus County Superior Court, California, denying the city relief on its complaint to recover an alleged business license tax deficiency from defendant health maintenance organization (HMO).


OVERVIEW: The trial court ruled that the city's business license tax ordinance that was operative for the tax years at issue was unconstitutional as applied to the HMO because it imposed a tax on business activities occurring outside of the city. PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner agricultural employer and real party in interest union failed to agree on the terms of a collective bargaining agreement, and a private mediator determined the terms of the contract by which the parties would be bound, pursuant to Lab. OVERVIEW: The employer argued that the statutory scheme of Lab. Code, § 1164 et seq., which provided for mandatory mediation and conciliation, violated due process and equal protection. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff county sheriff sought review of a summary judgment from the Superior Court of San Bernardino County (California), which found the removal provisions of San Bernardino County, Cal., Ord. No. 3875 to be constitutional and valid.


OVERVIEW: The ordinance dealt with removal of county officers, including the sheriff, and provided that removal for cause could be accomplished by a four-fifths vote of the board of supervisors. The ordinance stated that it could not be applied to interfere with the independent and constitutionally and statutorily designated investigative and prosecutorial functions of the sheriff and the district attorney. The court found the ordinance valid because the county had the right and duty to decide removal procedures for the sheriff. Pursuant to Cal. Const. art. Gov't Code § 25303 because it specified that there would be no interference with the sheriff's investigative and prosecutorial functions. Although sheriffs were elected officials, as provided in Cal. OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment that found the ordinance constitutional.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff State Controller sued defendants holders of escheated property in the Superior Court of Sacramento County (California), in two cases, under Cal. Code Civ. OVERVIEW: The holders remitted escheated property to the Controller before Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1577.5, forgiving interest due on such property that was not timely delivered to the Controller, was enacted. The appellate court held § 1577.5 satisfied the standard for retroactive legislation, as the legislature expressly provided it was retroactive. The statute was intended to motivate holders of unclaimed property to surrender escheated property to the state, conferring a monetary benefit on the state which benefitted the public in general, creating a public purpose rendering the statute constitutional under Cal. As to the holders, the statute did not primarily serve a public purpose and only incidentally benefit private individuals.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgments were reversed.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part and reversed in part; remanded with directions.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a juvenile offender, sought review of a judgment from the Superior Court of Tulare County (California), which placed the juvenile on probation for disturbing the peace and resisting, delaying, or obstructing a peace officer. The trial court concluded that the charges were gang related and ordered the juvenile to register as a gang member pursuant to Cal. Penal Code § 186.30(b).


OVERVIEW: The juvenile challenged § 186.30(b)(3) on vagueness grounds. The court rejected that argument, observing that a definition of "criminal street gang" was set forth in Cal. Penal Code § 186.22(e), (f). As part of the same statute, this definition provided a limiting construction of the word "gang" in Cal. Penal Code § 186.30(b)(3), thus avoiding vagueness. Although the juvenile's constitutional argument lacked merit, the court found no substantial evidence to support the trial court's finding that the juvenile's offenses were gang-related. The record contained evidence that the juvenile belonged to a gang and that he committed the offenses while in the company of other gang members. There was no showing of the requisite two predicate offenses, however, and the commission of enumerated offenses was not shown to be a primary activity of the group. The court noted that a remand would not violate double jeopardy and that the requirement of Cal. Penal Code § 186.32 to provide information to the police did not violate constitutional provisions regarding due process, self-incrimination, free speech and association, privacy, delegation of authority, or cruel and unusual punishment.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment with respect to the order to register, affirmed in all other respects, and remanded to the juvenile court for a new disposition hearing.
