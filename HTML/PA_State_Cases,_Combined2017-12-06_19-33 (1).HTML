PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Commonwealth sought review of a decision of the Court of Common Pleas, Erie County, Criminal Division (Pennsylvania), which granted a new trial to defendant on grounds that his prosecution under 18 Pa.C.S. § 6111(g)(4) of the Pennsylvania Uniform Firearms Act of 1995, 18 Pa.C.S. § 6101 et seq., regarding the sale or transfer of firearms violated defendant's due process rights as well as the separation of powers doctrine.


OVERVIEW: Defendant sought to purchase a handgun. The state form only required identifying information under 18 Pa.C.S. § 6111(b)(1), but the federal form asked questions regarding such things as prior convictions. Defendant improperly answered "no" to questions as to prior felony convictions and prior commitments to a mental institution; defendant had four prior convictions and had been involuntarily committed on four occasions. The court reversed and remanded. Even though defendant gave false answers as to matters not specifically listed in § 6111(b), his prosecution did not violate due process because the statute was not vague as applied to defendant's particular conduct as 18 Pa.C.S. § 6105 prohibited certain persons from possessing a firearm, including those with prior felony convictions and those who had been involuntarily committed. Because § 6111(g)(4) was broadly worded, no evidence existed that it was to be read solely in conjunction with § 6111(b)(1); § 6111(g)(4) referred to the entire Firearms Act. Any materially false statement in connection with the purchase of the firearm, including on the federal form, would subject an individual to prosecution by the Commonwealth.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's order granting a new trial and reinstated the jury verdict. The court remanded the action for sentencing.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A jury convicted defendant of three counts of robbery, two counts of aggravated assault, and one count of possessing an instrument of crime. The Court of Common Pleas of Lehigh County, Criminal Division (Pennsylvania), sentenced defendant to life in prison, as authorized by 42 Pa.C.S. § 9714(a)(2). Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that a jury, rather than the judge, should have determined whether to sentence defendant to life imprisonment without the possibility of parole, pursuant to 42 Pa.C.S. § 9714. The superior court concluded that because the protections of the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments of the United States Constitution did not extend to the fact of prior convictions and because it was solely the existence of two prior convictions that caused defendant to be eligible to possibly be sentenced within a range of increased penalties, the trial court properly imposed the judgment of sentence. Because the trial court had the statutory discretion, but was not mandated to impose a life sentence, the trial court did not err in its imposition of sentence. The superior court further determined that the trial court did not violate defendant's constitutional rights by considering facts that were not determined by the jury.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed.
