DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED IN PART, REVERSED IN PART, REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: All of the parties appealed from the order of the Superior Court of Maricopa County (Arizona), which found that portions of the Cockfighting Statutes, Ariz. Rev. Stat. §§ 13-2910.03 and 13-2910.04, were unconstitutional and enjoined their enforcement in plaintiffs' action seeking a declaratory judgment and a preliminary injunction.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs brought suit against defendant State of Arizona seeking a declaratory judgment that the Cockfighting Statutes, Ariz. Rev. Stat. §§ 13-2910.03 and 13-2910.04, were unconstitutional and a preliminary injunction enjoining their enforcement. The Citizens Against Cockfighting were not allowed to intervene because the trial court found that their interests were represented by the defendant. The trial court found that portions of the statutes were unconstitutional and enjoined their enforcement. All of the parties appealed. Judgment was reversed as to that part of the trial court's order that enjoined the enforcement of Ariz. Rev. Stat. §§ 13-2910.03(A)(1) and 13-2910.04, because the statutes were neither vague nor overbroad and they did not violate Ariz. Const. art. XXII, § 1; therefore the trial court abused its discretion in declaring them unconstitutional. In all other respects the judgment was affirmed.


OUTCOME: That part of the trial court's order that enjoined the enforcement of portions of the Cockfighting statutes was reversed because the statutes were neither vague nor overbroad and they did not violate the Arizona Constitution; therefore the trial court abused its discretion in declaring them unconstitutional. In all other respects the judgment was affirmed.

Ladewig v. Ariz. Dep't of Revenue


TX 1997-000075


ARIZONA TAX COURT


204 Ariz. 352; 63 P.3d 1089; 2003 Ariz. Tax LEXIS 1; 392 Ariz. Adv. Rep. 48



January 21, 2003, Decided  
February 3, 2003, Filed



DISPOSITION: Costs and attorney's fees awarded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff taxpayer filed an administrative refund claim in a class action suit against defendant Arizona Department of Revenue, claiming that its denial of analogous deductions for dividends received from corporations not doing more than half their business in Arizona was unconstitutional as a violation of the Commerce Clause.


OVERVIEW: The court had to determine the reasonable amount of attorneys' fees and costs to be awarded to class counsel. The common fund doctrine was appropriate for application in the case. The abandonment of the lodestar methodology was inappropriate. The case was quite simple and there were never any genuine issues of material fact in dispute. The parties were not required to engage in any significant discovery. There was no competent evidence that the investment of time by either firm as a result of their undertakings in this case, cost either of the firms other significant business opportunities. Since the case did not involve typical class action type of injury, and there was not a class of individuals who were ready, willing and able to negotiate a contract for representation at the beginning, the market approach in awarding attorneys' fees had little validity when placed in the context of reality. The court would apply an average hourly rate for counsel's efforts at $ 250 per hour and multiply it by the lodestar factor, resulting in an hourly fee of $ 1,500 per hour. Attorneys' fees were to be awarded in an amount of $ 15 million.


OUTCOME: The court awarded class counsel their costs and reasonable attorneys' fees in the amount of $ 16,061,815.
