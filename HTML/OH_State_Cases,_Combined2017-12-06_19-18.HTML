State v. Linville

COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, FOURTH APPELLATE DISTRICT, ROSS COUNTY


2009-Ohio-313; 2009 Ohio App. LEXIS 265



January 20, 2009, File-stamped


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Discretionary appeal allowed by State v. Linville, 121 Ohio St. 3d 1499, 2009 Ohio 2511, 907 N.E.2d 324, 2009 Ohio LEXIS 1494 (2009)
Reversed by, Remanded by, in part In re Sexual Offender Reclassification Cases, 2010 Ohio 3753, 2010 Ohio LEXIS 1931 (Ohio, Aug. 17, 2010)


DISPOSITION:  [**1] JUDGMENT AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner inmate sought judicial review in the Ross County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) of his reclassification as a Tier III sex offender under R.C. Chapter 2950, as amended by Am. Sub. S.B. 10, Gen. Assem. (Ohio 2007) (S.B. 10). The trial court denied his request for appointed counsel and overruled his petition. He appealed.


OVERVIEW: The inmate said retroactively applying S.B. 10 to him was an ex post facto law. The appellate court held S.B. 10's amendments did not require a departure from holdings that R.C. Chapter 2950 was civil, so they were not ex post facto laws, nor was their retroactive application unconstitutional, under Ohio Const. art. II, § 28, because the legislature intended to apply them retroactively, but they were remedial. The inmate was not entitled to appointed counsel because (1) S.B. 10 was civil, and (2) he had no substantial liberty interest in his prior classification. His reclassification did not offend separation of powers because (1) such classification was a legislative function, (2) his classification was a collateral consequence of his criminal conduct, and (3) he had no reasonable expectation R.C. Chapter 2950 would not be amended, so S.B. 10 did not abrogate a final judicial determination, nor did it interfere with judicial power to sentence a sex offender, as it was not punitive. His reclassification was not a multiple punishment barred by double jeopardy. He had no standing to contest residency restrictions in R.C. 2950.034(A), nor was the issue ripe, due to his incarceration.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, the Director of the Ohio Department of Commerce, sought review of a judgment from the Franklin County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio), which granted summary judgment in favor of appellee, a decedent's estate executor, in the executor's class action, alleging that the Director's retention of interest earned on unclaimed funds pursuant to R.C. § 169.08(D) violated the Takings Clause. The executor cross-appealed.


OVERVIEW: When the executor's mother died, she left unclaimed funds. The executor made a claim and he was paid the principal amounts, interest until it was statutorily deemed not recoverable under § 169.08(D), less an administrative fee. He filed the class action based on Ohio's Unclaimed Funds Act, codified at R.C. § 169.01 et seq., and sought a declaration that the Director's retention of interest earned on unclaimed funds pursuant to R.C. § 169.08(D) violated the Takings Clause of the U.S. and Ohio Constitutions. The trial court certified the class and determined the membership. The trial court held that the retention of interest under § 169.08(D) was unconstitutional, it granted the executor a permanent injunction, and it stayed the decision pending appeal. On appeal, the court looked to other states' unclaimed property statutes and found that there was nothing unconstitutional by the Director's retention of interest in the UFA. Although there was no presumption of abandonment in the UFA and title admittedly remained with the owner, custody passed to the State. Further, as the property lapse was triggered by the owner's inaction rather than overt state action, no taking occurred.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment of the trial court and remanded the matter for further proceedings.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In an original proceeding, relators citizens filed a complaint in mandamus against respondents county officials seeking (1) a declaratory judgment that Am. Sub. H.B. 509, Gen. Assem. (Ohio 2012) (H.B. 509), abolishing the current Sandusky County, Ohio, County Court, was unconstitutional and (2) to order respondents to hold an election of judges for the current county court.


OVERVIEW: H.B. 509 appointed two judges to a new "Sandusky County Municipal Court" until an election in November 2013, by amending R.C. 1901.01, 1901.02, and 1901.08. Amended R.C. 1901.01 and 1901.02, abolishing one court and creating a new court, was constitutional because the legislature had such power in Ohio Const. art. IV, § 1. The citizens were entitled to a writ of mandamus because (1) they were partly entitled to the relief sought by showing parts of H.B. 509 were unconstitutional, (2) the officials had a legal duty to have judges elected, and, (3) since the November 2012 election was less than two months away, there was no adequate legal remedy.


OUTCOME: The petition was granted.

The employer appealed.


OVERVIEW: In previous proceedings, portions of the subrogation statute, Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 4123.931, were found to be unconstitutional. The employer then argued that the prior subrogation statute was still effective. The former subrogation statute provided for subrogation to the employee's rights, not the administratrix's. Therefore, the trial court properly ordered the escrowed funds to be distributed to the administratrix.

State v. Grate


CASE NO. 2008-T-0058


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, ELEVENTH APPELLATE DISTRICT, TRUMBULL COUNTY


2009-Ohio-4452; 2009 Ohio App. LEXIS 3769



August 28, 2009, Decided

Discretionary appeal allowed by, Review pending at State v. Grate, 124 Ohio St. 3d 1415, 2009 Ohio 6816, 919 N.E.2d 214, 2009 Ohio LEXIS 3660 (2009)
Certification granted by, Review pending at State v. Grate, 124 Ohio St. 3d 1414, 2009 Ohio 6816, 919 N.E.2d 213, 2009 Ohio LEXIS 3703 (2009)

PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] 
Civil Appeal from the Trumbull County Court of Common Pleas, Case No. 2008 CV 195.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner sex offender was convicted of three counts of gross sexual imposition and classified as a sexually oriented offender. He was later notified that he had been reclassified as a Tier II Sex Offender under the Ohio Adam Walsh Act (Act), R.C. Chapter 2950. He filed a petition to contest the application of the Act to him. The Trumbull County Court of Common Pleas (Ohio) granted the State's motion for summary judgment. The offender appealed.


OVERVIEW: The sex offender argued that the trial court erred by finding R.C. Chapter 2950 constitutional because it violated the constitutional doctrine of separation of powers. The appellate court held that, because the offender's sentence had become final several years prior to the Adam Walsh Act, it was beyond the power of the legislature to vacate or modify it. Since the offender's classification as a sexually oriented offender with definite registration requirements constituted a final order of the lower court, he could not, under separation of powers and res judicata principles, be reclassified under the provisions of the amended Act with differing registration requirements. The reclassification, as a practical matter, nullified that part of the trial court's original judgment ordering him to register for a period of 10 years as a sexually oriented offender. There was no exception to the rule that final judgments could not be legislatively annulled in situations where the legislature had enacted new legislation. However, the legislation did not violate the ex post facto clause, or double jeopardy, because it was civil and nonpunitive.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court reclassifying the sex offender as Tier II Sex Offender was reversed. However, the offender was required to continue registering as a sexually oriented offender pursuant to the trial court's original judgment entry.

Spivey v. Keller


CASE NUMBER 6-04-09


COURT OF APPEALS OF OHIO, THIRD APPELLATE DISTRICT, HARDIN COUNTY


2004-Ohio-6667; 2004 Ohio App. LEXIS 6041



December 13, 2004, Judgment Entry


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Discretionary appeal allowed by Spivey v. Keller, 105 Ohio St. 3d 1543, 2005 Ohio 2188, 827 N.E.2d 326, 2005 Ohio LEXIS 989 (2005)
Affirmed without opinion by Spivey v. Keller, 107 Ohio St. 3d 100, 2005 Ohio 5973, 2005 Ohio LEXIS 2695 (2005)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] CHARACTER OF PROCEEDINGS: Civil Appeal from Common Pleas Court, Domestic Relations Division.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellee grandmother sued appellant father in the Hardin County Court of Common Pleas, Domestic Relations Division (Ohio), to obtain visitation with her grandchild under Ohio Rev. Code Ann. §§ 3109.11 and 3109.051. Appellant father said § 3109.051 was unconstitutional, and the Attorney General intervened to defend the statute's constitutionality. The trial court found the statute constitutional and ordered visitation, and the father appealed.


OVERVIEW: The father objected to being ordered to allow grandparent visitation. The appellate court held Ohio Rev. Code Ann. § 3109.051(D) required a trial judge considering such visitation to review 16 factors. This analysis satisfied dicta in Troxel v. Granville by giving "special weight" to a parent's decision to decline grandparent visitation rights. Given the range and specificity of the factors, the trial court could not only give the parental decision the requisite "special weight," but could also consider the child's best interest and balance that interest against the parent's choice, so Ohio Rev. Code Ann. §§ 3109.051 and 3109.11 were constitutional. The trial court weighed each factor and its impact on the father, grandmother, and child. The father's decision to limit visitation was given its required "special weight," but, despite that "special weight," it was found that it was in the child's best interest to grant the grandmother some visitation. This was not an abuse of discretion. The trial court found the father's concerns went to the nature, quality, and duration of visitation, rather than to its prohibition.


OUTCOME: The trial court's judgment was affirmed.
