Williams v. State, 669 N.E.2d 1372, 1996 Ind. LEXIS 124 (Ind., 1996)



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from the Lake Superior Court's (Indiana) denial of his petition for post-conviction relief challenging his conviction and death sentence for the murder of three people.


OVERVIEW: Defendant appealed from the denial of his petition for post-conviction relief challenging his conviction and death sentence for the murder of three people. Defendant claimed that his appellate counsel labored under a conflict of interest because he was appointed to represent both defendant, and a co-defendant, and did not focus on co-defendant's involvement in the crime. To prevail on the claim that his appellate counsel labored under a conflict of interest, defendant was required to demonstrate that his counsel had an actual conflict of interest that adversely affected counsel's performance. The mere possibility of a conflict was not sufficient to impugn a criminal conviction. Moreover, joint representation was not per se evidence of ineffective assistance. Rather, defendant was required to show that his counsel's representation was fully inadequate. Denial of defendant's petition for post-conviction relief affirmed. Because defendant did not demonstrate how his defense was adversely affected by his counsel's performance or by his counsel's dual representation, defendant's ineffective assistance of appellate counsel claim failed.


OUTCOME: Denial of petition for post-conviction relief affirmed. Because defendant did not demonstrate how his defense was adversely affected by his counsel's performance or by his counsel's dual representation, defendant's ineffective assistance of appellate counsel claim failed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant State appealed an order by the Pulaski Circuit Court (Indiana) granting summary judgment in favor of plaintiff superior court judge in action challenging the constitutionally of a law abolishing the superior court in which plaintiff was the sitting judge.


OVERVIEW: Indiana Legislature passed a statute purporting to abolish a state superior court in which plaintiff was the sitting judge. Plaintiff filed suit, challenging the constitutionally of the law as a violation of the separation of powers. On appeal, the court found the law unconstitutional, reasoning that any attempt by the legislature to abolish a superior court in the middle of a judge's term interfered with the judicial power of the courts and was therefore unconstitutional.
