Even for those who record an intimate act and intend for it to remain secret, a danger existed that the recording could find its way into the public sphere, haunting the child participant for the rest of his or her life. It was reasonable to conclude that persons 16 and 17 years old, although old enough to consent to sexual relations, might not fully appreciate that today's recording of a private, intimate moment might be the Internet's biggest hit next week. It was reasonable to conclude that criminalizing the making of recordings depicting persons under 18 years of age engaged in sexually explicit conduct furthered the goal of protecting those persons from the reputational harm that would occur if the recordings were distributed.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

Polikov v. Neth

SUPREME COURT OF NEBRASKA


270 Neb. 29; 699 N.W.2d 802; 2005 Neb. LEXIS 117



June 24, 2005, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from the District Court for Lancaster County: Bernard J. McGinn, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellees, a county safety program and a county attorney, argued that the Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 29-3601 through 29-3609 (2004) pretrial diversion scheme violated the constitutional principle of separation of powers by infringing upon a county attorney's prosecutorial discretion. The District Court for Lancaster County (Nebraska) permanently enjoined the enforcement of §§ 29-3601 through 29-3609. Appellant department of motor vehicles appealed.


OVERVIEW: Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 29-3601 through 29-3609 (2004) purported to authorize and regulate pretrial diversion programs. The supreme court concluded that the power to design a formal pretrial diversion program was a legislative function and that thus, §§ 29-3601 through 29-3609 did not violate the separation of powers clause. Sections 29-3601 through 29-3609 regulated the prosecutor's ability to design a formal diversion program as he or she saw fit. The county attorney argued that the power to design a formal pretrial diversion program was an executive function, and he relied on the power of prosecutorial discretion and that the charging function was also broad enough to allow him to formalize diversion practices. But, the supreme court concluded that formalizing the diversion process was something separate and apart from the mere exercise of the charging function and was better understood as a legislative function. Formal pretrial diversion did not represent a natural outgrowth of the charging function, but, rather, a substantial change in the way society responded to crime. Thus, the district court erred in holding that §§ 29-3601 through 29-3609 were unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was reversed.
