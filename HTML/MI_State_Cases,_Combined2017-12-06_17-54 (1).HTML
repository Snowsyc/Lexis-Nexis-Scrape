PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In class actions against defendants, Michigan Public Service Commission, Michigan Department of Treasury, and the State of Michigan, plaintiff trucking companies and intervenor trucking companies claimed that fees collected under Mich. Comp. Laws §§ 478.1, .2(1), and .2(2) were unconstitutional and preempted by federal law. After summary disposition was granted to defendants, plaintiffs and intervenors appealed.


OVERVIEW: The Michigan Motor Carrier Act, Mich. Comp. Laws § 475.1 et seq., required intrastate and interstate motor carriers to pay annual fees. Plaintiffs and intervenors claimed that the fees were preempted by 49 U.S.C.S. § 11501 and 49 U.S.C.S. § 11506, now 49 U.S.C.S. § 14504, and the § 610(h) of the Federal Aviation Administration Authorization Act of 1994 (FAAAA) and that the state laws violated Equal Protection and the Commerce Clause. The fee laws also did not violate the Commerce Clause, finding that there was no showing that the regulatory fee regulated interstate commerce or that the fee was excessive in relation to Michigan's substantial interest in regulating safety on its highways.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's summary disposition ruling in favor of defendants.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. Jurisdiction is not retained.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner taxpayer challenged a decision from the Michigan Tax Tribunal, which affirmed respondent township's calculation of the taxable value of the taxpayer's real property for the 2000 tax year.


OVERVIEW: At the time a residence was appraised in 1999, it was still under construction. Thereafter, the township assessed the property as a "complete new construction." The taxpayer then filed a petition challenging the valuation. After the tribunal determined that the township properly calculated the value of the residence, the taxpayer sought review. The court determined that the township was precluded from reappraising the entire property in 2000 without regard to the taxable value of the partially completed residence. Mich. Const. art. IX, § 3, as amended by Mich. Proposal A, limited increases on property taxes as long as it was owned by the same party. Therefore, the taxable value was the lesser of the property's taxable value in the immediately preceding year minus any losses, multiplied by the lesser of 1.05 or the inflation rate, plus all additions. There was no exception provided for new construction that was partially completed in one year and fully completed in the subsequent tax year. However, the court noted that the tribunal did not err in denying the taxpayer's request to supplement the record because the evidence was not submitted in a timely manner.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the tribunal's decision to affirm the township's valuation of the taxpayer's real property. However, the tribunal's decision to deny the taxpayer's request to supplement the record was affirmed. The case was remanded for further proceedings.

DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded for proceedings consistent with this opinion. We do not retain jurisdiction.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff personal representative sued a hospital for medical malpractice and wrongful death. The hospital settled the case for $ 460,000; $ 220,000 was allocated to the wrongful death and $ 240,000 for decedent's pain and suffering. The personal representative then sued defendant nursing home for the same wrongful death in the Branch Circuit Court (Michigan). The jury awarded $ 300,000 in total wrongful death damages. The nursing home appealed.


OVERVIEW: The nursing home maintained that the judgment should have been reduced to reflect the personal representative's earlier settlement with the hospital. The appellate court found that the hospital and the nursing home were jointly and severally liable for decedent's wrongful death and that the common-law rule of setoff was applicable. Thus, the appellate court concluded that the $ 300,000 verdict would be reduced by the $ 220,000 settlement payment made by the hospital to the personal representative with regard to wrongful death. The hospital and nursing home through successive negligent acts, produced a single, indivisible injury, i.e., the death of the decedent and thus were jointly and severally liable for wrongful death. The appellate court found that comprehensive tort reform legislation was silent on the issue of common-law setoff. Therefore, the appellate court determined that the legislature intended that the principle of one recovery and the common-law rule of setoff, in the context of joint and several liability cases, would remain intact after the amendment of Mich. Comp. Laws § 600.2925d. Pursuant to the setoff, the appellate court reduced the judgment to $ 80,000.


OUTCOME: The appellate court reversed and reduced the amount of the judgment to $ 80,000 and the amount of prejudgment interest on the entire amount accordingly.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Livingston Circuit Court, Michigan, entered judgment declaring appellant property owner's zoning referendum petition invalid and enjoined the requisite certification of the petition in favor of appellees, township and landowners. The owner appealed.


OVERVIEW: The owner argued that the township board did not comply with the Township Rural Zoning Act (TZRA), Mich. Comp. The owner could not effectively contest the action of the township by a referendum election. A variance was distinct from an ordinance or an amendment to an ordinance as contemplated by the TRZA. The simple act of publishing a notice of adoption of a consent judgment failed to transform the judgment into a zoning ordinance, as contemplated by the TRZA.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.
