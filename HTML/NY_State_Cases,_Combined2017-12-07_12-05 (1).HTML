People v. Brodeur


2009KN015198


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, KINGS COUNTY


40 Misc. 3d 1070; 969 N.Y.S.2d 774; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3095; 2013 NY Slip Op 23246



July 18, 2013, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Reversed by, Dismissed by, in part People v. Brodeur (Christopher), 2017 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 400 (N.Y. App. Term, Jan. 24, 2017)


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Aggravated Harassment -- Aggravated Harassment in Second Degree -- True Threat

1. Defendant's verbal threats to kill the complainant, who had recently evicted defendant from a shared loft space by unilaterally changing the locks, did not constitute the requisite "true threat" to support a finding of guilt of attempted aggravated harassment in the second degree (Penal Law §§ 110.00, 240.30 [1] [a]). Defendant's statements would not have been taken by a reasonable person in complainant's position to constitute a true threat, and were not taken by the complainant to constitute anything more than the hyperbole and exaggeration which the complainant well knew at the time the words were spoken were defendant's hallmark and stock in trade. A reasonable person in the complainant's position should have realized that defendant would become incensed upon being removed from the loft space, and should have expected a harsh response from anyone who had been locked out, let alone from defendant whom the complainant knew to be harsh in his treatment of even his best friends. The complainant also knew defendant's propensity for exaggeration of every perceived wrong in aid of making whatever point he desired at the moment. Furthermore, email exchanges showed that the complainant did not deem his life to be in danger. With regard to the complainant's prompt reporting of defendant's threats to the police, it was apparent that both endeavored to use the criminal laws to obtain whatever advantage they could against each other.

Crimes -- Aggravated Harassment -- Aggravated Harassment in Second Degree -- True Threat

2. Defendant was guilty of attempted aggravated harassment in the second degree (Penal Law §§ 110.00, 240.30 [1] [a]) for placing a "wanted" poster on the complainant's front door that contained the complainant's name and a caricature, stated in large print that the complainant was a violent drug dealer and child molester, and importuned observers to call 911 to procure the complainant's arrest. The poster amounted to a "true threat," as required to constitute a crime under Penal Law § 240.30 (1), in that a reasonable person in the complainant's position, seeing the poster, would have deemed the poster to constitute a real threat that he or she might be arrested at any moment, and perhaps suffer physical harm if the police considered him or her to be a criminal. The complainant was alarmed, afraid and annoyed upon seeing the poster on his door, which was defendant's purpose and intention. The act of placing the poster on the complainant's door, and not merely the contents of the poster, constituted the crime, and was an intolerable invasion of the complainant's zone of privacy, as well as a true threat.

Crimes -- Stalking -- Stalking in Fourth Degree -- Course of Conduct

3. Defendant, who verbally threatened to kill the complainant and placed a "wanted" poster on the complainant's front door, after the complainant had evicted defendant from a shared loft space by unilaterally changing the locks, was not guilty of stalking in the fourth degree. Pursuant to Penal Law § 120.45 (1), stalking in the fourth degree requires a "course of conduct ... likely to cause reasonable fear of material harm to the physical health, safety or property" of a specific person. Based on the context in which they were made, defendant's statements, other than the poster, would not have caused a reasonable person in the complainant's position to fear such harm. As the poster constituted only a single act, a course of conduct could not be proved.

Crimes -- Harassment -- Harassment in Second Degree -- Legitimate Purpose for Conduct

4. Defendant, who verbally threatened to kill the complainant and placed a "wanted" poster on the complainant's front door, was not guilty of harassment in the second degree under Penal Law § 240.26 (3), which requires proof that the defendant "engages in a course of conduct or repeatedly commits acts which alarm or seriously annoy [another] person and which serve no legitimate purpose." While defendant's conduct "seriously annoyed," if not alarmed, the complainant, the conduct had a legitimate purpose--to seek restoration to what he believed to be his proper role in a performing arts project centered around a shared loft space from which he was ejected by the complainant's unilateral changing of the locks. Moreover, unlike subdivisions (1) and (2) of Penal Law § 240.26, which require only a single act, subdivision (3) requires a course of conduct or repeated acts. One who is evicted by self-help may not be found guilty under Penal Law § 240.26 (3) by demanding reinstatement, even if that demand is repeated, and even if the evictor is alarmed or seriously annoyed by that demand.

Concerned Home Care Providers, Inc. v. New York State Dept. of Health


15593/2013


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, SUFFOLK COUNTY


41 Misc. 3d 278; 969 N.Y.S.2d 743; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2934; 2013 NY Slip Op 23236

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Summary judgment denied by, Summary judgment granted by, Complaint dismissed at, Motion denied by Concerned Home Care Providers, Inc. v New York State Dept. of Health, 2014 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3455 (N.Y. Sup. Ct., July 29, 2014)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-As the matter was not brought to enjoin the action of any public officer, board, or municipal corporation, but, rather, the regulations implemented by the Department of Health, a transfer of venue under CPLR 6311 was inapplicable; [2]- As both Executive Order 38 itself and the Department of Health regulations in 22 NYCRR 1002, regulating financial assistance for health-related activities, were in accordance with the specific delegation of authority in Public Health Law §§ 201, 206 and the legislative policy in N-PCL § 508 concerning the expenditure of funds by not-for-profit corporations, the fact that the governor chose an executive order to pursue that policy did not violate the doctrine of separation of powers.


OUTCOME: Motions denied.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Trial -- Place of Trial -- Action against State Agencies and Officials -- Discretionary Change of Venue 

1. Defendants, the Governor and the Department of Health (DOH), were denied a change of venue from Suffolk to Albany County, in an action for declaratory and injunctive relief challenging certain DOH regulations and the executive order that called for them, because defendants identified no nonparty witnesses who would have been affected. A change of venue under CPLR 510 (3) is only warranted where it would promote the convenience of material witnesses and the ends of justice, and the change must be supported with detailed relevant information establishing that the convenience of the nonparty witnesses would be enhanced. Although CPLR 6311 would lay venue in an application to restrain a state officer in the judicial department where the officer is located, the matter was not brought to enjoin the action of any public officer or board but, rather, regulations implemented by DOH.

Administrative Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Separation of Powers 

2. Regulations limiting the amount of state funds used by health care providers for administrative expenses and executive compensation (10 NYCRR part 1002), which were called for [***2] by defendant Governor and enacted by defendant Department of Health (DOH), were promulgated pursuant to DOH's specific statutory authority and were well within legislatively mandated policy, and therefore did not violate the separation of powers. DOH has the authority to regulate financial assistance granted by the State for health-related activities (Public Health Law § 201 [1] [o], [p]) and, among other things, to enter into and negotiate the terms of contracts and subcontracts for health care services (Public Health Law § 206 [3], [6]). Also, the regulations were consistent with the policy, stated elsewhere by the legislature, that not-for-profit entities such as plaintiff were to use their funds for the programs they run (N-PCL 508). In addition, the legislature had not previously attempted and failed to enact similar legislation, and the rules went into effect following a notice of proposed rulemaking, a comment period, and numerous changes in response.

Injunctions -- Preliminary Injunction -- Where Available 

3. In an action challenging the constitutionality of regulations limiting the amount of state funds used by health care providers for administrative expenses and executive compensation (10 NYCRR part 1002), which were called for by defendant Governor and enacted [***3] by defendant Department of Health, a preliminary injunction prohibiting the continued implementation of the regulations was unwarranted. To obtain the extraordinary relief of a preliminary injunction, the moving party must demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, a likelihood of success on the merits, that the movant will suffer irreparable injury absent the relief, and that a balance of the equities favors the moving party. Here, plaintiff failed to show a likelihood of success, as the regulations were the product of a constitutional exercise of power, its claims of harm were speculative, and the legislative goal of requiring health care provid ers to use state funds for the programs they were designed to run outweighed the harm alleged by plaintiff.

Empire Gen Holdings, Inc. v. Governor of the State of New York


4450-12


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, ALBANY COUNTY


40 Misc. 3d 984; 967 N.Y.S.2d 919; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2683; 2013 NY Slip Op 23213



June 25, 2013, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-Taxpayers' challenge to the constitutionality of the redevelopment tax credit under Tax Law §§ 33 and 34 did not survive challenge by a dismissal motion, as their tangible property tax credit was deferred prior to its vesting pursuant to Tax Law § 21(a)(3), such that the credit was nothing more than an expectancy interest which was insufficient to support a takings clause violation; [2]-The taxpayers failed to state a substantive due process/anti-retroactivity challenge because they lacked a vested property right when the tax credit deferral provisions were enacted; [3]-The taxpayers had no contract entitling them to the full tax credit, such that they did not have a violation of the Contracts Clause; [4]-There was no viable equal protection claim because the challenge was not based on similarly-situated groups and the rational basis of the legislation was not negated.


OUTCOME: Motion granted; complaint dismissed.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Taxation -- Exemptions -- Redevelopment Tax Credit -- Takings Clause Challenge to Delay of Redevelopment Tax Credit 

1. Plaintiffs failed to state valid Takings Clause challenges to the tax credit deferral provisions in Tax Law §§ 33 and 34, which prohibited plaintiffs from claiming in tax year 2010 the entire $86,951,916 redevelopment tax credit to which they were entitled in connection with their construction of a natural gas fired electric generating plant placed into service in September 2010. The tax credit deferral provisions reduced plaintiffs' 2010 tax credit to $1,663,633 and deferred plaintiffs' receipt of the balance of the full redevelopment tax credit to future years. Tax Law § 21 (a) (3) established plaintiffs' credit and provides, in pertinent part, that it is "allowed for the taxable year in which such qualified tangible property is placed in service on a qualified site with respect to which a certificate of completion has been issued to the taxpayer." As all of Tax Law § 21 (a) (3)'s elements were satisfied in September 2010, it was not until then that plaintiffs' right to their full redevelopment tax credit vested. That credit, however, was deferred in August 2010 when the tax credit deferral provisions became effective. Because plaintiffs' [***2] credit was deferred before it vested, it was nothing more than an expectancy interest, and was an insufficient basis upon which to find a Takings Clause violation.

Taxation -- Exemptions -- Redevelopment Tax Credit -- Due Process Clause Challenge to Delay of Redevelopment Tax Credit

2. Plaintiffs failed to state valid substantive Due Process Clause challenges to the tax credit deferral provisions in Tax Law §§ 33 and 34, which prohibited plaintiffs from claiming in tax year 2010 the entire $86,951,916 redevelopment tax credit to which they were entitled in connection with their construction of a natural gas fired electric generating plant placed into service in September 2010. The tax credit deferral provisions reduced plaintiffs' 2010 tax credit to $1,663,633 and deferred plaintiffs' receipt of the balance of the full redevelopment tax credit to future years. Tax Law § 21 (a) (3) established plaintiffs' credit and provides, in pertinent part, that it is "allowed for the taxable year in which such qualified tangible property is placed in service on a qualified site with respect to which a certificate of completion has been issued to the taxpayer." As all of Tax Law § 21 (a) (3)'s elements were satisfied in September 2010, it was not until then that plaintiffs' right to [***3] their full redevelopment tax credit vested. That credit, however, was deferred in August 2010 when the tax credit deferral provisions became effective. Because plaintiffs' credit was deferred before it vested, plaintiffs failed to state a cognizable vested property interest entitled to due process protection against retroactive taxation.

Taxation -- Exemptions -- Redevelopment Tax Credit -- Contracts Clause Challenge to Delay of Redevelopment Tax Credit 

3. Plaintiffs failed to state valid Contracts Clause challenges to the tax credit deferral provisions in Tax Law §§ 33 and 34, which prohibited plaintiffs from claiming in tax year 2010 the entire $86,951,916 redevelopment tax credit to which they were entitled in connection with their construction of a natural gas fired electric generating plant placed into service in September 2010. The tax credit deferral provisions reduced plaintiffs' 2010 tax credit to $1,663,633 and deferred plaintiffs' receipt of the balance of the full redevelopment tax credit to future years. As plaintiffs had no contract entitling them to the full redevelopment tax credit, they had no Contracts Clause cause of action based on its deferral. Plaintiffs' action in entering into a brownfield site cleanup agreement [***4] covering the land where the plant was constructed, remediating the contaminated land and constructing the plant did not clearly indicate the legislature's intention to bind the State to paying the full redevelopment tax credit without deferral. Nor do the Brownfield Cleanup Program and its related tax credits include such a clear indication. Moreover, the New York Constitution provides that tax exemptions are freely repealable.

Taxation -- Exemptions -- Redevelopment Tax Credit -- Equal Protection Clause Challenge to Delay of Redevelopment Tax Credit 

4. Plaintiffs failed to state valid Equal Protection Clause challenges to the tax credit deferral provisions in Tax Law §§ 33 and 34, which prohibited plaintiffs from claiming in tax year 2010 the entire $86,951,916 redevelopment tax credit to which they were entitled in connection with their construction of a natural gas fired electric generating plant placed into service in September 2010. The tax credit deferral provisions reduced plaintiffs' 2010 tax credit to $1,663,633 and deferred plaintiffs' receipt of the balance of the full redevelopment tax credit to future years. Plaintiffs' claim that the tax credit deferral provisions' application to brownfield redevelopment investors [***5] but not to film production credit (Tax Law § 24) investors violated equal protection principles was not valid. The brownfield redevelopment investors and film production credit investors were not similarly-situated groups. Moreover, the tax credit deferral provisions did not apply solely to brownfield redevelopment investors, but rather applied to more than 30 separate tax credits. In addition, such distinction could have been rationally based upon differences between the structure of each credit, the commercial desirability of each credit or the fiscal impact on the state of each credit.

Mongielo v. Cuomo

SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, ALBANY COUNTY


40 Misc. 3d 362; 968 N.Y.S.2d 828; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2158; 2013 NY Slip Op 23169

CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs sued defendants, the New York Governor, Senate, and Assembly, alleging that the New York Secure Ammunition and Firearms Enforcement Act of 2013, Penal Law art. 265 and 400, and other state gun laws violated U.S. Const. amend. II. Defendants moved to dismiss for failure to state a claim. The court found the motion well taken because plaintiffs did not challenge any specific provisions of these laws and, based on precedent from New York and federal courts, they could not demonstrate that the laws were unconstitutional in all respects and under all applications.


OUTCOME: The complaint was dismissed.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law -- Right to Bear Arms -- Challenge to New York's Entire Body of Law Governing Firearms and Ammunition

Plaintiffs' complaint seeking, among other things, a declaration that New York's entire body of law governing the sale, possession and use of firearms and ammunition is null and void as repugnant to the Second Amendment of the United States Constitution was dismissed for failure to state a cause of action. Plaintiff's complaint was directed generally at the State's authority to regulate the sale, possession and use of firearms and ammunition. It did not challenge any particular provision of the relevant law, which includes the New York Secure Ammunition and Firearms Enforcement Act of 2013 and Penal Law articles 265 and 400, or otherwise raise an as-applied challenge. Duly enacted statutes enjoy a presumption of constitutionality and a party who asserts that a statute is facially unconstitutional must demonstrate beyond a reasonable doubt that the statute suffers from wholesale constitutional impairment. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that prohibitions such as those on the possession of firearms by felons or the mentally ill, those forbidding the carrying of firearms in sensitive places like schools and government buildings, and those imposing conditions and qualifications on the commercial sale of arms are presumptively lawful regulatory measures. As such, plaintiffs could not demonstrate that the laws they challenged were unconstitutional in all respects and under all applications.

Bransten v. State of New York

SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY

OVERVIEW: HOLDING: [1]-Accepting as true the allegations of the complaint filed by plaintiffs, current and retired members of the state judiciary, the decision of defendant New York State to reduce its contribution to the justices' health insurance benefits constituted an unconstitutional intrusion into the judiciary's compensation, which was guarded by the Compensation Clause of the New York Constitution.


OUTCOME: The State's motion to dismiss was denied, except that the John and Mary Doe plaintiffs were dismissed from the action without prejudice.


HEADNOTES

Judges -- Judicial Salaries -- Reduction in Percentage of State's Contribution Toward Health Insurance Premiums

1. In a declaratory judgment action challenging the decision by defendant State to reduce defendant's contribution to justices and judges' health insurance benefits, plaintiffs sufficiently stated a claim that Civil Service Law § 167 (8) violates the New York State Constitution's Compensation Clause (art VI, § 25) as applied to plaintiffs. After the Commission on Judicial Compensation, charged with examining the adequacy of judicial salaries and benefits, issued its report recommending judicial pay increases in three phases, Civil Service Law § 167 (8) was amended to allow the president of the Civil Service Commission to extend the terms of an agreement between the legislature and certain public sector unions reducing the percentage of defendant's contribution toward employees' health insurance premiums to unrepresented state employees and retirees, including plaintiffs. Although the amendment does not single out judges, the Compensation Clause singly protects judges from overly broad laws that have the direct effect of diminishing their compensation. The amendment has a unique impact upon the judiciary by virtue of the fact that it diminishes the compensation the judiciary is guaranteed to receive. Contributions for health insurance benefits which are deducted from a judge's paycheck are directly related to the amount of salary paid to a judge. Defendant's withdrawal of its contributions which comprise compensation, which is what section 167 (8) as applied to judges accomplishes, stands upon different footing than a nondiscriminatory, generally applied tax imposed against the compensation of all citizens by the government in its status as a sovereign.

Judges -- Judicial Salaries -- Reduction in Percentage of State's Contribution Toward Health Insurance Premiums

2. In a declaratory judgment action challenging the decision by defendant State to reduce defendant's contribution to justices and judges' health insurance benefits, defendant was not entitled to dismissal of plaintiffs' complaint as to the retired plaintiff justices. Plaintiffs, current and retired Supreme Court justices and associations representing Supreme Court justices, sufficiently stated a claim that Civil Service Law § 167 (8) violates the New York State Constitution's Compensation Clause (art VI, § 25) as applied to plaintiffs. The Compensation Clause expressly protects the compensation of retired justices by providing that the compensation of "a retired judge or justice shall be established by law and shall not be diminished during the term of office for which he or she was elected or appointed." Defendant's explanation that the terms "retired judge" and "during the term of office" are not incongruous because "during the term of office" renders the no-diminution guarantee applicable to those judges who have obtained a two-year appointment upon certification pursuant to Judiciary Law § 115 (1) and (2) was raised for the first time in its reply papers and was not considered.

Parties -- Unknown Parties

3. In a declaratory judgment action challenging the decision by defendant State to reduce defendant's contribution to justices and judges' health insur ance benefits, plaintiffs "John and Mary Does 1-2,000, current and retired judges and justices of the Unified Court System of the State of New York," were dismissed from the action without prejudice. There was no basis to permit the caption to remain in its state without a showing of a substantial privacy right or class certification status.

McDonald v. New York City Campaign Fin. Bd.


100038/2013


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


40 Misc. 3d 826; 965 N.Y.S.2d 811; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 1929; 2013 NY Slip Op 23153



May 1, 2013, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed in part and modified in part by McDonald v. New York City Campaign Fin. Bd., 117 AD3d 540, 985 NYS2d 557, 2014 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 3530 (N.Y. App. Div. 1st Dep't, May 15, 2014)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs sued defendants, a city and its campaign finance board, seeking to enjoin it from enforcing an ordinance extending the voluntary contribution limits and restrictions applicable to candidates receiving public matching funds to "non-participating" candidates who were ineligible for such funds. Defendants moved to dismiss for failure to state a claim. The court upheld the ordinance, finding it was neither preempted by Article 14 of New York's Election Law nor inconsistent with it, because Article 14 did not address locally funded publicly financed elections for wholly local offices.


OUTCOME: The suit was dismissed.


HEADNOTES

Municipal Corporations -- Home Rule Powers -- Public Campaign Financing -- Contribution and Other Limits 

1. Amendments to the New York City Campaign Finance Act extending the contribution limits and other restrictions of defendant City's public campaign financing system even to "non-participating" candidates (Administrative Code of City of NY § 3-719 [2] [b]), ineligible to receive matching public funds, were permissible exercises of local legislative authority. Every local government has power to adopt local laws not inconsistent with the State Constitution or any general law relating to its property, affairs or government and, specifically, the mode of selection and removal of its officers and employees (NY Const, art IX, § 2 [c] [i], [ii] [1]; Municipal Home Rule Law § 10 [1] [i], [ii] [a] [1]). Municipal home rule powers are liberally construed (NY Const, art IX, § 3 [c]; Municipal Home Rule Law § 51) and local laws will be upheld unless they have no reasonable basis. Here, the City Council had found the legislation necessary to reduce undue influence and increase public confidence and participation in local elections. Defendant City could address those goals under its power to protect the welfare and well-being of its citizens as well.

Local Laws -- Preemption by State -- State and Local Election Laws 

2. Amendments to the New York City Campaign Finance Act (CFA) extending the contribution limits and other restrictions of defendant City's public campaign financing system even to "non-participating" candidates (Administrative Code of City of NY § 3-719 [2] [b]), ineligible to receive matching public funds, were not preempted by the Election Law or inconsistent with state law. Preemption can be inferred where the state legislature has set forth a comprehensive and detailed regulatory scheme, but local governments have great latitude in addressing local issues, even when the state has already legislated in those areas. Here, although the CFA imposed greater restrictions on candidates for certain local offices than the Election Law, the restrictions had to be viewed as part of defendant City's public financing system as a whole, and the Election Law is silent on the issue of publically financed elections. The mere fact that the local law dealt with matters already touched upon by state law did not render it invalid. Moreover, the CFA amendments were aimed at promoting ethics in local government and reducing the influence of money on city elections and were complementary to state law.

Moreover, the ordinance did not infringe on a parent's right to substantive due process since the exceptions contained in the ordinance offered parents significant flexibility and autonomy in supervising their children.

Marone v. Nassau County


003630-2012


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NASSAU COUNTY


39 Misc. 3d 1034; 967 N.Y.S.2d 583; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 1492; 2013 NY Slip Op 23123



March 24, 2013, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Petitioners, inmates and civil liberties organization, moved for an order of mandamus pursuant to CPLR art. 78 and a county charter directing respondent county executive to appoint members to the county correctional center board of visitors. The court found, inter alia, that the inmates had "public interest" standing to petition for a CPLR 7803(1) writ of mandamus. The county charter clearly directed the county executive to fill the seven-member board in a manner consistent with the county charter. Consequently, the county executive would be ordered to do so.


OUTCOME: Motion granted.


HEADNOTES

Parties -- Standing -- Challenge to Governmental Action -- Injury-in-Fact 

1. Petitioner prisoners, who each alleged that they received improper medical care at the Nassau County Correctional Center and continued to have untreated or poorly treated medical conditions, had standing to challenge respondent County Executive's failure to appoint a seven-member Board of Visitors with a wide range of powers to oversee the prison as required by Nassau County Charter § 2004. To establish standing to challenge a governmental action, a petitioner must show an injury-in-fact that falls within the zone of interests or concerns sought to be promoted or protected by the statutory provision under which the agency has acted. Here, the Charter provision was enacted in an attempt to address systematic failures at the jail with regard to neglecting human rights and dignity that had resulted in preventable deaths, a number of lawsuits and United States Department of Justice intervention. Thus, petitioners' alleged injuries fell within the zone of interests sought to be protected.

Parties -- Standing -- Challenge to Governmental Action -- Public Interest

2. Petitioner prisoners had standing under the doctrine of public interest to challenge respondent County Executive's failure to appoint a seven-member Board of Visitors with a wide range of powers to oversee the Nassau County Correctional Center as required by Nassau County Charter § 2004. In matters of great public interest, a citizen may maintain a mandamus proceeding to compel a public officer to do his or her duty. Every citizen has the right to compel the performance by public officers of the duty imposed upon them of executing laws of the state which are enacted for the benefit of the community. The Charter provision satisfied this standard inasmuch as it was enacted in an attempt to address systematic failures at the jail with regard to neglecting human rights and dignity that had resulted in preventable deaths, a number of lawsuits and United States Department of Justice intervention.

Proceeding against Body or Officer -- Mandamus -- When Remedy Appropriate

3. Petitioner prisoners were entitled to a writ of mandamus compelling respondent County Executive to appoint a seven-member Board of Visitors with a wide range of powers to oversee the Nassau County Correctional Center as required by Nassau County Charter § 2004. The remedy of mandamus is available to compel a governmental officer to perform a ministerial duty, but does not lie to compel an act which involves an exercise of judgment or discretion. Mandamus will lie to compel acts that public officials are duty bound to perform regardless of how they may exercise their discretion in doing so. Here, the language of the Charter provision stated that "[t]here shall be within the Division of Corrections a Nassau County Correctional Center Board of Visitors. It shall consist of seven members, including a chairperson, each of whom shall be appointed by the County Executive subject to confirmation by the County Legislature." While mandamus would be inappropriate to compel respondent to appoint specific members to the Board, the language clearly directs the County Executive to fill the seven-member Board of Visitors in a manner consistent with the Charter.

Proceeding against Body or Officer -- Mandamus -- When Remedy Appropriate -- Academic and Moot Questions

4. A proceeding seeking mandamus to compel respondent County Executive to appoint a seven-member Board of Visitors with a wide range of powers to oversee the Nassau County Correctional Center as required by Nassau County Charter § 2004 was not rendered moot by respondent's submission of four Board member nominee names to the County Legislature for approval. While General Construction Law § 41 allows a four-member quorum of a seven-member body to exercise full power of the body, the Charter provision clearly directs the County Executive to appoint a seven-member Board. Had the drafters intended to allow the County Executive to establish a four-member Board that could vote in the absence of three of the members of a seven-member Board, they could have done so. In any event, the issue would only be moot if respondent had submitted the requisite number of names to the legislature and they were approved.

Atlas Henrietta, LLC v Town of Henrietta Zoning Bd. of Appeals


12/9441


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, MONROE COUNTY


46 Misc. 3d 325; 995 N.Y.S.2d 659; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 6666; 2013 NY Slip Op 23473



February 19, 2013


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Reported at Atlas Henrietta, LLC v Town of Henrietta Zoning Bd. of Appeals, 2014 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4366 (N.Y. Sup. Ct., Feb. 19, 2014)
Subsequent appeal at, Decision reached on appeal by Matter of Atlas Henrietta, LLC v Town of Henrietta Zoning Bd. of Appeals, 120 AD3d 1606, 992 NYS2d 667, 2014 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 6481 (N.Y. App. Div. 4th Dep't, Sept. 26, 2014)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-A town zoning board of appeals properly upheld three notices of violation issued against property owners (POs) pursuant to Henrietta, N.Y., Zoning Code § 295-2(B) because they had rented their properties to unrelated individuals and the properties did not qualify as legal prior existing uses; [2]-The POs' claim under CPLR 3001 that their properties were prior legal existing uses was procedurally defective because their challenge to the constitutionality of the former definition of "family" was time-barred under CPLR 213; [3]-The new definition of "family" in the zoning code amendment was not unconstitutional because it was a valid exercise of the municipality's authority.


OUTCOME: POs' petition and complaint denied.


HEADNOTES

Municipal Corporations--Zoning--Authority of Zoning Board of Appeals to Review Constitutionality of Town Zoning Code's Prior Definition of "Family"

1. Upon petitioners/plaintiffs' challenge to a determination of respondent/defendant town zoning board of appeals upholding notices of violation issued against them for renting numerous houses in a residential district to groups of unrelated college students allegedly failing to satisfy the amended definition of "family" set forth in the town zoning code, respondent/defendant's failure to address the constitutionality of the zoning code's pre-amendment definition of "family," which allegedly applied to petitioners/plaintiffs' rentals, and thereby find that petitioners/plaintiffs' rentals constituted prior legal existing uses, was neither arbitrary and capricious nor affected by an error of law. Given that the powers conferred on a zoning board of appeals by statute do not include the ability to review the validity of legislative enactments such as zoning codes and that the constitutionality of the pre-amendment definition of "family" was never judicially determined, respondent/defendant was required to apply the presumption of validity to that definition, notwithstanding the opinion of the Town Attorney that the pre-amendment definition was unconstitutional.

Municipal Corporations--Zoning--Notice of Violation--Probable Cause Not Required

2. A determination of respondent/defendant town zoning board of appeals, which upheld notices of violation (NOVs) issued against petitioners/plaintiffs for renting numerous houses in a residential district to groups of unrelated college students allegedly failing to satisfy the amended definition of "family" set forth in the town zoning code, was not set aside as an error of law or as arbitrary and capricious on the ground that the NOVs lacked probable cause. The NOVs were unambiguous in their wording that any legal action, civil or criminal, would not be commenced until after 30 days in order to allow time for petitioners/plaintiffs to respond. Although a criminal proceeding is commenced only upon the filing of an accusatory instrument (see CPL 1.20 [16], [17]), the NOVs were not one of the permitted forms of an accusatory instrument (see CPL 1.20 [1]), nor were they appearance tickets commencing a criminal proceeding as they did not demand petitioners/plaintiffs' presence in a local criminal court (see CPL 1.20 [26]).

Limitation of Actions--When Cause of Action Accrues--Claim for Judgment Declaring Town Zoning Code's Prior Definition of "Family" Unconstitutional

3. Upon a determination of respondent/defendant town zoning board of appeals upholding notices of violation (NOVs) issued against petitioners/plaintiffs for renting numerous houses in a residential district to groups of unrelated college students allegedly failing to satisfy the amended definition of "family" set forth in the town zoning code, petitioners/plaintiffs' claim for a judgment declaring that the pre-amendment definition of "family," which allegedly applied to their rentals, was unconstitutional and thus that their rentals constituted prior legal existing uses was time-barred. Petitioners/plaintiffs had standing to contest the zoning code's definition of "family" before it was amended, even without having yet been issued an NOV, because their properties were subject to the zoning code's rental restriction and they would have suffered financial injury if it was enforced against them. Moreover, given that a declaratory judgment claim challenging a legislative act, such as a zoning ordinance, accrues when the law was passed, petitioners/plaintiffs here did not receive the benefit of a running accrual up until the amendment of the zoning code's definition of "family." Even affording petitioners/plaintiffs the latest possible date as the accrual date when they were first subject to the zoning code based upon their ownership and rental of the properties, the statute of limitations expired before their commencement of the current action.

Municipal Corporations--Zoning--Town Zoning Code's Definition of "Family" Not Exclusionary Zoning

4. Petitioners/plaintiffs, who were issued notices of violation for renting numerous houses in a residential district to groups of unrelated college students allegedly failing to satisfy the amended definition of "family"--four or more persons living together as a traditional family or the functional equivalent thereof--set forth in the town zoning code, did not overcome the presumption of constitutionality afforded to the definition and meet their heavy burden of proof to invalidate it as exclusionary zoning, and the definition was thus sustained as a valid exercise of the municipality's authority. Although the power to zone is broad, a municipality may not legitimately exercise its zoning power to effectuate discrimination. Therefore, to be valid, a zoning ordinance must have been enacted in furtherance of a legitimate governmental purpose and there must be a reasonable relation between the end sought to be achieved and the means used to achieve it. Here, the zoning code's definition of "family"--which did not exclude all students, or even all nontraditional family units, but only those groups of four or more unrelated persons who are not the functional equivalent of a traditional family--was not exclusionary zoning, especially given that its presumption--that four or more unrelated persons do not constitute the functional equivalent of a traditional family--was rebuttable. Rather, the rental prohibition was enacted to ensure the quiet family nature of the town's residential sections--a legitimate governmental function--while still permitting reasonable rental situations and accommodating alternative familial living arrangements.

People v. Pelegrin


2012BX018174


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, BRONX COUNTY


39 Misc. 3d 788; 959 N.Y.S.2d 401; 2013 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 474; 2013 NY Slip Op 23035



January 14, 2013, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Defendant's motion to dismiss his operating a motor vehicle under the influence of alcohol or drugs and other charges under CPL 170.30(1)(f) was denied. Defendant's equal protection rights under U.S. Const. amend. V and XIV and N.Y. Const. art. I, § 11 were not violated based on the police department's failure to offer him a physical coordination test because he could not speak English. The language classification was rationally related to the legitimate state interest in avoiding inaccuracies, avoiding confusion, and complicating the investigatory process.


OUTCOME: Motion to dismiss denied.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Pretrial Motions -- Dismissal on Constitutional Grounds

1. In a prosecution for driving while intoxicated, defendant's motion to dismiss on equal protection and due process grounds was properly brought under CPL 170.30 (1) (f), given that alleged constitutional violations of this sort are not otherwise cognizable under the Criminal Procedure Law.

Motor Vehicles -- Physical Coordination Tests -- Failure to Offer Test to Non-English Speaking Defendant -- Rational Basis 

2. On a motion to dismiss driving while intoxicated charges on the ground that they were based on violations of defendant's equal protection rights, the police officer's decision not to offer defendant a physical coordination test due to a "language barrier" was subject only to rational basis review. When discrimination based on a suspect classification is established, by showing either that a law or policy is facially discriminatory or that it is intentionally applied in a discriminatory manner, strict scrutiny applies. Although it is often inextricably intertwined with ethnicity, classifications based on language alone are not suspect. Thus, for the policy of denying the test to non-English speakers to receive heightened scrutiny, defendant had to prove, with statistics or other evidence, that it had a disparate impact on a suspect class and that the police intended to discriminate against the class. Defendant offered no competent evidence concerning the number of Hispanics and/or non-English speakers who were denied the test on the basis of language compared with non-Hispanics and/or English speakers, Hispanic or otherwise. Even if he had shown a disparate impact, his conclusory assertion that police had mistreated him "because he looked and sounded Hispanic" was unsupported by evidence of intentional discrimination.

Motor Vehicles -- Physical Coordination Tests -- Failure to Offer Test to Non-English Speaking Defendant -- Rational Basis 

3. Defendant's equal protection rights were not violated when, due to a "language barrier," police did not offer him a physical coordination test before testing his breath and charging him with driving while intoxicated because the policy of withholding the test from non-English speaking suspects who will not understand it was reasonably related to legitimate state interests. State action will be upheld unless the disparate treatment is so unrelated to any legitimate purpose that it is irrational. Here, the policy was rationally related to state interests in avoiding inaccuracies, confusion, or other complications in the investigatory process. Likewise, a claim, as a class of one, that he had been intentionally treated differently from others similarly situated would have failed as well, as the difference in treatment requires only a rational basis.

Motor Vehicles -- Physical Coordination Tests -- Failure to Offer Test to Non-English Speaking Defendant -- Procedural Due Process 

4. Defendant failed to establish that a protected liberty or property interest was at stake when, after pulling him over on suspicion of driving while intoxicated, police would not offer him a physical coordination test before testing his breath and charging him. The basic inquiry in procedural due process cases is whether governmental action has deprived an individual of a liberty or property interest within the meaning of the Due Process Clause. Regarding his proffered interest in obtaining potentially exculpatory evidence, while the People must provide access to certain evidence in their control, law enforcement has no affirmative obligation to gather on-the-scene evidence concerning potential defenses. Also, defendant failed to show that driving while intoxicated suspects have an absolute right to a physical coordination test or that the test is a non-discretionary benefit conferred on suspects by the police department.

Motor Vehicles -- Physical Coordination Tests -- Failure to Provide Interpreter

5. Defendant, a non-English speaker who had been charged with driving while intoxicated, was not entitled to have a translator present while being administered pre-arrest sobriety tests. A well-established precept of due process is that non-English speaking criminal defendants are entitled to an interpreter so that they can meaningfully participate in the trial and assist in their own defense. However, the right to an interpreter is only available once adversary judicial proceedings have been initiated.

Motor Vehicles -- Physical Coordination Tests -- Failure to Offer Test to Non-English Speaking Defendant -- Substantive Due Process -- Fundamental Rights 

6. No fundamental right of defendant's had been implicated by a police officer's decision not to offer him a physical coordination test before testing his breath and charging him with driving while intoxicated, such that the officer's conduct would warrant strict scrutiny. In addition to disparate treatment on the basis of suspect classifications, strict scrutiny also applies if a fundamental right is at issue. However, defendant's purported rights to the coordination test or to have a translator present to administer it were not deeply rooted in the nation's history and tradition for purposes of substantive due process analysis.

Damages are incidental if a monetary award is an automatic consequence of the equitable relief granted or, put differently, if a grant of the relief that is the primary aim of the proceeding makes it a statutory duty of the respondent to pay the petitioner the sum sought. Here, upon the regulation's annulment, petitioners sought to recover the losses they incurred by reason of their compliance [***2] with the regulation. 2. Having prevailed in a CPLR article 78 proceeding to annul a regulation of respondent New York City Taxi &Limousine Commission prohibiting the owners of taxicabs who lease the cabs to drivers from collecting sales tax in addition to the maximum permitted lease rates, petitioner taxicab owners were not permitted to sever and convert their claim for non-incidental monetary relief to a plenary action as respondents were immune from liability for damages arising out of the regulation's enactment, which involved the exercise of administrative judgment and expertise and the evaluation of policy. Constitutional Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Prohibition against Feeding Wild White-Tailed Deer Unconstitutionally Vague on its Face

1. 6 NYCRR 189.3 (b), which provides that "[n]o person shall feed wild white-tailed deer," is unconstitutionally vague on its face as it does not adequately define the offense with sufficient clarity so that a person of ordinary intelligence would understand the type of conduct which is prohibited. "Feed" or "feeding" is defined as the act of using, placing, giving, exposing, depositing, distributing or scattering any "material" that attracts wild white-tailed deer to feed on (6 NYCRR 189.2 [f]), but 6 NYCRR part 189 fails to define "material." Thus, the regulation, as written, allows law enforcement agents to pursue some cases and not others, depending on how they arbitrarily define "material" and/or prohibited conduct. The failure to provide even minimal guidelines to law enforcement, thereby allowing for unpredictable and inconsistent enforcement and prosecution, violates the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause.

Constitutional Law -- Validity of Regulation -- Prohibition against Feeding Wild White-Tailed Deer Unconstitutionally Vague as Applied

2. The act of feeding or attracting white-tailed deer can be an act of expressive conduct of conservation prohibited by the content-neutral regulation in question. Content-neutral regulations of time, place, and manner of expression are enforceable if they are narrowly tailored to serve a significant government interest. It leaves no alternative for the lawful feeding of other animals that inhabit the woods, fields and backyards, as any material that incidentally attracts deer places someone in violation. 1. The information charging defendant with disorderly conduct in violation of Penal Law §240.20 (5) following his arrest at a protest, based on allegations that he was among a group of people on a sidewalk one afternoon, "jumping up and down" and "thereby causing people to walk around" them and into the street, was facially insufficient. 2. Buffalo United Charter School v. New York State Pub. Empl. Relations Bd.


I2011-1248


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, ERIE COUNTY


37 Misc. 3d 294; 943 N.Y.S.2d 868; 2012 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 1897; 2012 NY Slip Op 22113



April 24, 2012, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Reserved by Buffalo United Charter Sch. v New York State Pub. Empl. Relations Bd., Council of Sch. Supervisors & Adm'rs, Local 1, AFSA, 107 AD3d 1437, 965 NYS2d 905, 2013 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 4143 (N.Y. App. Div. 4th Dep't, June 7, 2013)


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Petitioners, charter schools and manager, filed a CPLR art. 78 petition to review a decision by respondent Public Employment Relations Board (PERB) that determined certain assistant principals were neither managerial nor confidential. The court found, inter alia, that the Legislature preempted the conflicting application of the joint public-private employment doctrine with respect to charter schools. Therefore, PERB had jurisdiction over the matter. However, PERB erred in concluding that the assistant principals at issue were not "managerial" or "confidential" under Education Law § 2854(3)(a).


OUTCOME: Petition denied, except to extent that decision regarding assistant principals annulled, and administrative law judge's decision reinstated.


HEADNOTES

Civil Service -- Public Employment Relations Board -- Jurisdiction -- Applicability of Joint Public-Private Employment Doctrine to Charter Schools

1. Respondent Public Employment Relations Board's (PERB) joint public-private employment doctrine, which provides that the Taylor Law (Civil Service Law § 200 et seq.) applies only to employment that is unequivocally public and prohibits PERB from exercising jurisdiction in cases in which an employment relationship involves joint public and private employers, is inconsistent with the Charter Schools Act (Education Law art 56) and thus did not apply to proceedings involving petitioner charter schools which had contracted with a private corporation for operation of the schools and employment of the schools' staff. While the Charter Schools Act is silent regarding the joint public-private employment doctrine, the language in Education Law § 2854 (1) (a), stating that "to the extent that any provision of [article 56] is inconsistent with any other state or local law, rule or regulation, the provisions of [article 56] shall govern and be controlling," expresses a legislative intent to preempt the conflicting application of the joint public-private employment doctrine with respect to charter schools. Moreover, application of the joint public-private employment doctrine here would essentially abrogate provisions that apply to charter schools generally, without any exemption for charter schools associated with private entities, particularly Education Law § 2854 (3) (c-2), which requires charter schools to follow principles of "employer neutrality" as guaranteed by recourse to Civil Service Law § 209-a regarding "improper employment practices," and section 2855 (1) (d), which provides for termination of a charter based on a charter school's intentional employee rights violations under the Taylor Law.

Labor Unions -- National Labor Relations Board -- Jurisdiction -- Charter Schools

2. Respondent Public Employment Relations Board's (PERB) determination that petitioner charter schools, which had contracted with a private corporation for operation of the schools and employment of the schools' staff, were political subdivisions of the state under 29 USC § 152 (2) and were thus exempt from the jurisdiction of the National Labor Relations Board was not arbitrary and capricious. An employer is considered a political subdivision of the state if it is administered by individuals who are responsible to public officials or to the general electorate. Here, notwithstanding that the schools' governing bodies were not appointed or subject to removal by public officials, PERB analyzed the Charter Schools Act and the legislative intent in making its determination. Under the circumstances, including that there is no settled federal law on the issue, the court could not substitute its own interpretation.

Civil Service -- Public Employees' Fair Employment Act -- Charter Schools -- Designation of Managerial and Confidential Employees

3. Respondent Public Employment Relations Board erred in determining that petitioner charter school, which had contracted with a private corporation for operation of the school and employment of the school's staff, could not designate all assistant principals as "managerial" or "confidential" employees under Civil Service Law § 201 (7) (a). A plain reading of the Charter Schools Act stating that "an employee of a charter school shall be deemed a public employee" (Education Law § 2854 [3] [a]) is that the Legislature intended to have the Taylor Law applicable to charter schools. The Legislature did not expressly employ any language precluding the designation of "managerial" or "confidential" employees and there was no legislative intent to treat charter schools and traditional public schools differently in that regard. Moreover, nothing in the Charter Schools Act required petitioner to designate a chief executive officer before employees can be designated "managerial" or "confidential." The private corporation had employed an individual to serve in the functional capacity of a chief executive officer and a formal designation by the school board of trustees was immaterial. In addition, the record substantially demonstrated that the assistant principals were functioning in a "confidential" capacity.

Constitutional Law -- Contract Clause -- Impairment of Contract

4. A determination by respondent Public Employment Relations Board declaring that, for purposes of the Taylor Law (Civil Service Law § 200 et seq.), petitioner charter schools, which had contracted with a private corporation for operation of the schools and employment of the schools' staff, were the sole employers of their employees, did not unconstitutionally impair the corporation's obligations under the management agreements with the schools. The determination did not address questions outside what is permitted under the Taylor Law, and therefore did not declare that the schools are employers for all purposes or disengage the corporation from the substantial management role it plays pursuant to the agreements. The Charter Schools Act (Education Law art 56) expressly allows the type of contractual arrangements at issue here, and there is no prohibition against delegation of the schools' operational and management functions.

SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, KINGS COUNTY


36 Misc. 3d 191; 946 N.Y.S.2d 417; 2012 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 1763; 2012 NY Slip Op 22104



April 16, 2012, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Defendant filed a motion to preclude his retrial for second-degree murder and first- and second-degree manslaughter on ground of double jeopardy. The court found, inter alia, that the evidence at defendant's first trial was legally sufficient to support a charge of second-degree manslaughter under Penal Law § 125.15(1). However, because the indictment did not contain such a charge as a lesser-included offense of a dismissed second-degree murder charge, double jeopardy precluded a retrial on the manslaughter charge.


OUTCOME: Motion granted to extent that indictment dismissed.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Double Jeopardy -- Retrial -- Dicta

1. A prior court decision vacating defendant's second-degree murder conviction based solely on a finding that defendant had received ineffective assistance of counsel did not provide a basis to bar defendant's retrial based on the legal sufficiency of the evidence, despite comments made by the court in the prior decision concerning the lack of evidence to show that defendant had intentionally shot anyone. Whatever comments were made in the prior decision concerning the legal sufficiency of the evidence were dicta given that the issue of legal sufficiency of the evidence to sustain the murder charge was not before the court on defendant's motion to vacate the judgment.

Crimes -- Vacatur of Judgment of Conviction -- Review of Sufficiency of Evidence by Trial Court before Retrial

2. In a prosecution in which the defendant was successful on his CPL article 440 motion in vacating his murder conviction on the ground of ineffective assistance of counsel and the People chose not to take an appeal from that decision, there was no sound policy reason why the trial court, in deciding defendant's motion to bar retrial on double jeopardy grounds, should not undertake the same review of the legal sufficiency of the evidence at the first trial that an appellate court would have undertaken on defendant's direct appeal if the murder conviction had been upheld on defendant's motion. Generally, where a new trial is ordered for reasons other than insufficiency of the evidence, the trial court's denial of a trial order of dismissal reviewing the legal sufficiency of the evidence is deemed to be an adequate protection to prevent an unconstitutional retrial. Here, however, information was brought to the attention of the trial court following the denial of defendant's motion for a trial order of dismissal that the defendant, in fact, did not kill the person he was seen chasing in a surveillance video, and that the victim was a bystander to the chase. Defendant, despite whatever remedy might exist on appeal should there be a conviction after a second trial, had never been afforded a proper review by the trial court of the legal sufficiency of the evidence at the first trial, since defendant's motion to vacate the judgment was grounded on ineffective assistance of counsel. Such a review would furnish the protection against defendant being unconstitutionally tried for a second time if the evidence at the first trial was legally insufficient.

Crimes -- Vacatur of Judgment of Conviction -- Judicial Economy

3. In a prosecution in which defendant's motion to vacate a judgment convicting him of second-degree murder and criminal possession of a weapon was granted as to the murder charge on the ground of ineffective assistance of counsel, considerations of judicial economy as well as law and equity favored the trial court's review of the legal sufficiency of the evidence presented at defendant's first trial upon defendant's motion to bar retrial on double jeopardy grounds. Should the court find the evidence insufficient and dismiss the murder charge, the People would have the right to appeal that decision in which case the sufficiency of the trial evidence concerning the murder charge could be reviewed concurrently with defendant's appeal from that same trial which resulted in the weapons conviction. Because defendant's murder conviction was vacated on grounds of ineffective assistance of counsel, the court's decision not to vacate that conviction on other grounds, such as insufficiency of evidence or prosecutorial misconduct, did not adversely affect the defendant. Therefore, issues concerning the sufficiency of the evidence or prosecutorial misconduct regarding the dismissed murder conviction would not be reviewable by the Appellate Division on either the defendant's direct appeal from the judgment convicting him of the weapons charge or on his potential appeal from the denial of his motion to vacate the judgment.

Crimes -- Murder -- Transferred Intent

4. In the prosecution of defendant on second-degree murder and related charges, the jury's finding of defendant's intent to kill the person he had been chasing could not be "transferred" to provide legally sufficient evidence of defendant's intent to kill the person actually killed where the jury's finding of an intent to kill was based on the argument that because defendant killed the person being chased, defendant's chase demonstrated a homicidal intent towards that person. It was determined after trial that the victim was a bystander to the chase and not the person being chased. As defendant did not kill the person being chased, the jury's finding of an intent to kill that person was undermined and could not serve as a basis to conclude that defendant had a homicidal intent. Moreover, there was no evidence that defendant actually attempted to shoot the person he was chasing, even though a surveillance video showed that defendant had an opportunity to do so, particularly when the person fell to the pavement.

Crimes -- Murder -- Discharge of Gun -- Sufficiency of Evidence

5. In the prosecution of defendant, who was seen on a surveillance video chasing someone other than the victim, a bystander to the chase, the evidence was legally insufficient to show that defendant had either an intent to kill or cause serious physical injury to anyone, which are elements necessary to support charges of second degree murder or the lesser included offense of first degree manslaughter. The video did not depict the shooting itself, and the trial evidence did not establish whether the single, fatal shot was fired before or after the chase. Moreover, the evidence at trial did not establish where, at the time the gun was discharged, the defendant, the person being chased and the actual victim were situated in relation to each other, or to the scene depicted in the video, or even what they were doing at the time. Without knowing where the person being chased was in relation to defendant at the time of the shooting, it was complete speculation to assert that defendant intended to shoot that person or anyone else when the gun discharged and struck the actual victim. There was no evidence other than defendant's own statement to show what defendant was doing when the gun discharged, and that statement asserted that the gun accidentally discharged when someone in a crowd grabbed defendant's arm.

Crimes -- Manslaughter -- Reckless Behavior

6. In the prosecution of defendant, who was seen on a surveillance video chasing someone other than the victim, a bystander to the chase, the evidence was legally sufficient to support a conviction for manslaughter in the second degree (Penal Law § 125.15 [1]). Defendant's own statement claimed that he brought what he believed to be an unloaded gun to the scene merely to scare people, as evidenced by his chase, and that he acted in response to a volatile street-gang territorial dispute over his presence at the scene earlier that evening. Furthermore, the jury would have had a right to reject the self-serving portion of defendant's statement claiming that he did not believe the gun was loaded but that, nevertheless, he carried it into the teeth of a potential gang confrontation. Accordingly, knowingly bringing a loaded gun into such a situation and then chasing someone with it, where there were numerous other people present, may qualify as reckless behavior under Penal Law § 15.05 (3), and when such behavior results in the shooting death of another, a charge of second-degree manslaughter would be established.

Crimes -- Lesser Included Offense -- Retrial of Homicide Charge

7. In the prosecution of defendant arising from the shooting death of the victim, because the trial evidence was legally insufficient to establish that the defendant intentionally shot anyone, double jeopardy principles barred the People from re-trying defendant on charges of second degree murder or the lesser included offense of first-degree manslaughter. Accordingly, because the murder charge was the only remaining charge in the indictment, the indictment was dismissed. However, the trial evidence was legally sufficient to establish the charge of manslaughter in the second degree, also a lesser included offense of second-degree murder on which the jury was charged but on which it did not reach a verdict, because, as instructed, that charge was not considered once the jury had decided to convict defendant of murder. Consequently, even though the evidence was found to have been legally insufficient to sustain the murder conviction, double jeopardy principles did not bar a retrial on a lesser included offense which was charged to the jury but on which no verdict was returned. Because the indictment did not contain a charge of second-degree manslaughter as a lesser included offense of the now dismissed charge of murder in the second degree, the defendant could not now be tried on a charge of second-degree manslaughter. Therefore, the People were granted leave to re-present appropriate charges to another grand jury.

CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Defendant's motion to dismiss was denied as Penal Law § 120.05(12) was constitutional under the Equal Protection Clause, U.S. amend. V and XIV. Since age was not a suspect classification, it was not entitled to the strict scrutiny standard of review, but was subject to a rational basis standard of review. In enacting Penal Law § 120.05(12), the New York legislature had concerns about protecting the elderly who were particularly susceptible to crimes of violence as a result of their age, which was a legitimate state interest. Section 120.05(12) effectuated that interest.


OUTCOME: Motion denied.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Assault -- Constitutionality of Penal Law § 120.05 (12) -- Equal Protection

1. Penal Law § 120.05 (12), which provides that a person is guilty of assault in the second degree when, among other things, he or she causes physical injury to a victim who is 65 years of age or older and he or she is more than 10 years younger than the victim, does not violate the Equal Protection Clause. Since age is not a suspect classification, the age provision is subject only to the rational basis standard of review, which tests whether the Legislature was protecting a legitimate state interest in enacting the statute. Here, the statute was enacted to protect senior citizens, who are generally more vulnerable to injury and less able to protect themselves than younger persons, and the statute protects that legitimate interest.

Crimes -- Assault -- Constitutionality of Penal Law § 120.05 (12) -- Facial Challenge for Overbreadth 

2. Penal Law §120.05 (12), which provides that a person is guilty of assault in the second degree when, among other things, he or she causes physical injury to a victim who is 65 years of age or older and he or she is more than 10 years younger than the victim, is not facially unconstitutional as overbroad. A party mounting a facial constitutional challenge bears the substantial burden of demonstrating that in any degree and in every conceivable application, the law suffers wholesale constitutional impairment. Here, the statute is clear in its application and can be applied constitutionally. Simply because Penal Law § 120.05 (12) is a strict liability statute with respect to the age elements does not render the statute overbroad, as a variety of strict liability statutes in New York impose criminal liability regardless of knowledge of the victim's age.

Crimes -- Assault -- Constitutionality of Penal Law § 120.05 (12) -- Statute Not Arbitrary and Capricious

3. Penal Law § 120.05 (12), which provides that a person is guilty of assault in the second degree when, among other things, he or she causes physical injury to a victim who is 65 years of age or older and he or she is more than 10 years younger than the victim, is not arbitrary and capricious in deeming individuals age 65 or older to be in need of enhanced protection under the Penal Law. In order for a law to be arbitrary and capricious, it must be founded on prejudice or preference rather than on reason or fact and lack a rational basis. Here, the Legislature's goal was to protect individuals age 65 and older, who may suffer deteriorating physical capabilities, from assault by younger, more physically able persons, and its determination that such persons need additional protection is reasonable. Nor is the statute arbitrary on the ground that it does not apply to offenders once they reach age 65, as the purpose is to protect the elderly from younger, more physically capable individuals.

Crimes -- Assault -- Constitutionality of Penal Law § 120.05 (12) -- Vagueness 

4. Penal Law § 120.05 (12), which provides that "[a] person is guilty of assault in the second degree when ... [w]ith intent to cause physical injury to a person who is sixty-five years of age or older, he or she causes such injury to such person, and the actor is more than ten years younger than such person," is not unconstitutionally vague. A statute is vague if it is not sufficiently precise and clear as to its coverage. Here, the legislative history establishes that liability does not require proof that a defendant knew or had reason to know of the victim's age, and the Legislature made clear that proof of such knowledge is not required.

Crimes -- Assault -- Constitutionality of Penal Law § 120.05 (12) as Applied

5. Penal Law § 120.05 (12), which provides that a person is guilty of assault in the second degree when, among other things, he or she causes physical injury to a victim who is 65 years of age or older and he or she is more than 10 years younger than the victim, was not unconstitutional as applied to defendant, despite his contention that he neither targeted the elderly nor was motivated by the victim's age. The statute addresses "predatory attacks" on senior citizens, but its application is not limited to such attacks. Here, at the time of the assault, defendant was 47 years old and the victim was 68 years old, both satisfying the statute's age requirements.

DISTRICT COURT OF NEW YORK, SECOND DISTRICT, NASSAU COUNTY

CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff insurer, alleging it was subrogated to the rights of its insured, moved for a default judgment against defendant motorist due to his failure to file an answer. Although the insurer filed the action over two years after the motorist's accident with the insured, its papers were silent as to whether its insured filed her own lawsuit against motorist. Therefore, the viability of its claim to recoup first party benefits was not clear under Insurance Law § 5104(b), and it was thus not entitled to a default judgment as to that claim.


OUTCOME: The court denied plaintiff's motion, without prejudice to renewal.


HEADNOTES

Courts -- Jurisdiction -- Monetary Limit of District Court -- Separate Causes of Action Joined in Single Complaint 

1. District Court had jurisdiction over plaintiff's action even though it was comprised of two causes of action which, in the aggregate, sought damages of $21,000. Monetary claims brought in District Court are ordinarily limited to cases where the amount sought to be recovered does not exceed $15,000 (UDCA 202). However, unlike County Court's jurisdictional limit, which applies to the entire complaint rather than each cause of action, where several causes of action are asserted in the same complaint in District Court and each of them would be within the jurisdiction of the court if sued upon separately, the court has jurisdiction of the action (UDCA 211).

Courts -- Jurisdiction -- Monetary Limit of District Court -- Distinct Causes of Action Arising from Single Transaction or Occurrence 

2. District Court had jurisdiction over plaintiff insurer's action, as subrogee, seeking damages of $21,000 in the aggregate, as the complaint properly asserted two causes of action, each of which was within the court's $15,000 jurisdictional limit (UDCA 211), even though both causes of action involved only one wrong by defendant--the negligent operation of a motor vehicle. Although a lower court's jurisdictional limits cannot be circumvented by the simple expedient of splitting a claim into separately stated "causes of action," distinct causes of action may arise from a single transaction or occurrence. Where, as here, a single wrongful act caused injury to both the person and property of another, distinct causes of action arose. While both claims were asserted by plaintiff subrogee under the provisions of the same insurance policy and based on the same occurrence, the first cause of action, to recoup basic first-party no-fault benefits, involved "economic losses" (e.g., medical expenses), whereas the second cause of action, for supplementary uninsured motorist benefits, involved payments for the insured's pain and suffering.

Insurance -- No-Fault Automobile Insurance -- Action by or on Behalf of Covered Person against Noncovered Person -- Plaintiff Insurer Must Allege Whether Insured Commenced Separate Lawsuit 

3. In an action by plaintiff insurer to recoup basic no-fault benefits paid to its insured from defendant, a noncovered person under no-fault who was in a motor vehicle accident with plaintiff's insured, plaintiff was not entitled to a default judgment because, although plaintiff provided proof of the default, plaintiff's papers were silent as to whether its insured commenced her own lawsuit against defendant and, thus, failed to allege sufficient facts establishing a legally viable cause of action. While the complaint and affidavits included a sufficient allegation that defendant was not a "covered person," equally essential is an allegation that plaintiff's insured failed to commence a lawsuit of her own seeking damages for personal injuries (see Insurance Law § 5104 [b]). If such action had been brought, the insurer's remedies would be limited to lien rights against any recovery by the insured. On the other hand, if the insured had commenced no action within two years after accrual of a claim, then, and only then, would the insurer have a cause of action for the amount of first-party benefits paid to its insured. (Id.)

Insurance -- Automobile Insurance -- Uninsured Motorist Endorsement -- Subrogation Claim against Uninsured Motorist for Noneconomic Damages 

4. In an action by plaintiff insurer, pursuant to Insurance Law § 3420 (f) (2), to recoup supplementary uninsured motorist benefits paid to its insured in settlement of her claim for pain and suffering, plaintiff was not entitled to a default judgment against defendant uninsured motorist because, although plaintiff provided proof of the default, plaintiff neither properly pleaded nor proved by party affidavit that its insured suffered a "serious injury" under the No-Fault Law (Insurance Law § 5102 [d]). An insurer suing a noncovered person upon a subrogated claim for supplementary uninsured motorist benefits should be required to plead and prove the insured's serious injury in order to obtain a judgment against the uninsured motorist. An insured could not obtain payment of uninsured motorist benefits for pain and suffering without proof of serious injury and, to the extent an insurer acquires subrogation rights by paying supplementary uninsured motorist benefits pursuant Insurance Law § 3420 (f), such payments are properly made only in cases involving serious injuries. Here, the complaint included no factual allegations that satisfied the definition of "serious injury," and the medical records submitted did not suffice under the circumstances, since no effort was made to show how they demonstrated that plaintiff's insured had sustained a serious injury.

CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: The trial court dismissed an information charging defendant with an Administrative Code of the City of NY § 20-453 violation for facial insufficiency since the condoms wrapped in political messages sold fell within the written matter exception. The New York City Department of Consumer Affairs had construed the written matter exception to apply to items bearing political messages falling within the same category as the condoms. A reasonable consumer of these condoms was unlikely to purchase them for use during sexual intercourse; the item would likely be purchased due to the political message.


OUTCOME: Motion was granted, in part, and denied, in part.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Constitutional Law - Freedom of Speech - Sale of Condoms Bearing Political Messages on Their Packaging without Vendor's License 

An information charging defendant with selling condoms bearing political messages on their packaging without a vendor's license in violation of Administrative Code of the City of New York § 20-453 was facially insufficient since the condoms fell within the written matter exception contained in the statute, which specifically exempts vendors of newspapers, periodicals, pamphlets "or other similar written matter" from the attendant licensing requirements. The construction of the written matter exception by the Department of Consumer Affairs, the agency in charge of administering the ordinance, as applicable to "items bearing political messages" such as t-shirts, buttons and flags was entitled to be upheld as it was not irrational or unreasonable, and the condoms were not so dissimilar as to fall outside the same category of items bearing political messages. Additionally, the reasonable consumer of the condoms was unlikely to purchase them for their usual purpose, as a barrier device during sexual intercourse, but rather because of the political message contained on the wrapper. Moreover, the applicability of the exception does not rest on the item's similarity to the items specifically referenced in the statute. To further the legislature's intent of promoting free speech and freedom of press and preventing the statute from unconstitutionally restricting expression protected by the First Amendment, the term "other similar written matter" means similar insofar as it is a vehicle for speech and expression akin to a newspaper, periodical, book or pamphlet. Since the political message on the condom packaging was inextricably intertwined, defendant was engaged in a form of protected expression, despite that the condoms themselves had some other purpose or intrinsic value.

Further, it was not established that American citizens overwhelmingly chose air guns, as they have chosen handguns, for the core lawful purpose of self-defense. The air pistol ban is rationally related to legitimate state interests and thus passes the rational basis test.

People v Walters


45772


CITY COURT OF NEW YORK, WATERTOWN


30 Misc. 3d 737; 913 N.Y.S.2d 893; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 5891; 2010 NY Slip Op 20491



December 8, 2010, Decided 
December 8, 2010, Entered


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant pled guilty to driving while intoxicated pursuant to Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1192(3). The matter was before the court for sentencing under Leandra's Law. L 2009, ch. 496.


OVERVIEW: The court noted that the costs of interlock devises was indeterminate. The Division of Criminal Justice Services (DCJS) approved seven qualified manufacturers and published of list of each provider's prices. The listed prices constituted the maximum fees permitted by 9 NYCRR 358.5(c)(3), but those costs were not final, as § 358.5 permitted DCJS unilateral discretion to raise the rates. The court concluded that the legislature's failure statutorily to fix the ignition interlock costs presented a significant due process problem, because potential offenders and the general public could not determine the punishment for violating the law, given that the ultimate costs were subject to administrative fiat. Because defendant was not afforded notice of the interlock fine amounts, the rule of lenity forbid the court from ordering him to finance interlock installation and maintenance. The court further concluded that the State's failure to provide adequate legal guidance to judges making indigency determinations threatened defendant's right to equal protection of the law, as similarly situated defendants statewide had no assurance their financial circumstances would receive similar treatment.


OUTCOME: Defendant was to pay a $ 500 fine and a $ 395 surcharge-victim fee. His driver's license was revoked for six months. He was given a conditional discharge to successfully complete a Drinking Driver Program authorized by New York State within one year. For a six month period, he was prohibited from operating a vehicle without an ignition interlock. He had 10 business days to have an ignition interlock installed in any auto he chose to drive.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law -- Validity of Statute -- Leandra's Law -- Cost of Installation and Maintenance of Ignition Interlock Devices -- Due Process 

1. For purposes of sentencing under Leandra's Law (L 2009, ch 496), defendant could not be compelled to pay for the installation and maintenance of any ignition interlock device that he was required to have installed on any of his vehicles because the State has failed to provide adequate notice of the installation and maintenance costs to satisfy due process (see US Const Amend XIV; NY Const, art I, § 6), given that the costs are statutorily classified as criminal fines (Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1198 [5] [a]). Since interlock costs are not statutorily fixed but subject to a regulatory scheme which permits the Division of Criminal Justice Services (DCJS) unilateral discretion to raise rates at any time upon a request by a qualified manufacturer for a rate adjustment (9 NYCRR 358.5 [b] [2]), potential offenders and the general public cannot determine the punishment for violating the law. This ambiguity required invocation of the rule of lenity, a statutory construction doctrine rooted in due process that protects defendants against ambiguities informing the scope of and punishments required by criminal statutes and requires that defendants receive the lesser of the sanctions constructively afforded by the statute. Further, DCJS's failure to submit a final, determinate price list for interlock services for publication by the Secretary of State, in violation of the requirement under the New York Constitution that newly-enacted rules be filed with the Secretary of State (see NY Const, art IV, § 8) compounds the notice problem. That the State has designed the system such that no final cost can be ascertained does not suspend its obligation to publish a statement of those costs, given that it elected to classify them as criminal fines.

Constitutional Law -- Validity of Statute -- Leandra's Law -- Indigency Determinations -- Equal Protection 

2. The State's failure to provide adequate legal guidance to judges making indigency determinations under Leandra's Law (L 2009, ch 496), which requires that all first-time, misdemeanor driving while intoxicated offenders install ignition interlock devices in every automobile they own or operate for at least six months, and, barring indigency, that offenders pay for installation and maintenance of the interlock devices, violates equal protection because it may lead to arbitrary enforcement (US Const Amend XIV; NY Const, art I, § 11). Under rational basis scrutiny, since Leandra's Law provides every court with essentially standardless discretion in determining ability to pay, similarly situated defendants statewide have no assurance that their financial circumstances will receive similar treatment. The lack of guidance could be used to pressure courts to help maintain the indigency-waiver system the Division of Criminal Justice Services has devised by suggesting that issues of systemic integrity should inform indigency determinations. That is, judges might fear that granting too many indigency waivers could undermine the ignition interlock program by making it unprofitable for qualified manufacturers who have contracted with the State to provide and maintain interlock services. Accordingly, Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1198 (4) and 9 NYCRR 358.8 are unconstitutional to the extent that they require the court to make indigency determinations without a statutory metric for ascertaining indigency. Therefore, the court was barred from granting any indigency waivers.

Constitutional Law -- Validity of Statute -- Leandra's Law -- Requirement That Ignition Interlock Device be Installed in Every Automobile Defendant Owns is Overbroad 

3. The requirement under Leandra's Law (L 2009, ch 496) that a defendant convicted of driving while intoxicated install an ignition interlock device in every automobile he or she owns or operates (Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1193 [1] [b] [ii]) is unconstitutionally overbroad, under a rational basis equal protection analysis. Therefore, the installation requirement is limited to any car a defendant chooses to operate.

Rampolla v Banking Dept. of the State of New York


110107/10


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


31 Misc. 3d 161; 916 N.Y.S.2d 492; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 6366; 2010 NY Slip Op 20542



December 2, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed by Matter of Rampolla v. Banking Dept. of the State of New York, 93 AD3d 526, 940 NYS2d 257, 2012 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 2045 (N.Y. App. Div. 1st Dep't, Mar. 20, 2012)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner applicant sued respondents, the Banking Department of the State of New York and the Deputy Superintendent of Banks, seeking an order annulling their denial of his application for a mortgage loan originator (MLO) license and a writ of mandamus compelling them to review his application for an MLO license under the criteria set forth in Correction Law § 753(1) and (2). Respondents moved to dismiss the petition.


OVERVIEW: While working in the mortgage banking field, petitioner was convicted of making a false statement in a mortgage loan application. He later received a certificate of relief under Correction Law § 701 removing all legal bars to employment and licensure except those pertaining to firearms and eligibility for public office. He satisfied all of the educational requirements for an MLO license and passed the written test, but respondents denied his application for an MLO license under Banking Law § 599-e due to his prior conviction. The court held that applicants with an unpardoned felony conviction involving fraud, dishonesty, or a breach of trust, as of § 599-e's effective date, could not be licensed as MLOs. Even if § 753 applied, the Department had discretion under § 701(2)(d) to deny him a license based on his conviction. Respondents' five to six month delay in processing his application was not grounds for relief. As the ability of felons to obtain employment and licensing was not eradicated by § 599-e, there was no violation of the Contract Clause, U.S. Const. art. I, § 10. Having no property right in an MLO license, the applicant was not deprived of property without due process.


OUTCOME: The petition was denied and respondents' motion to dismiss the proceeding was granted.


HEADNOTES

Licenses -- Mortgage Loan Originator -- Prior Felony Conviction

1. Petitioner, who was convicted 17 years previously of the felony of making a false statement in a mortgage loan application but possessed a certificate of relief from disabilities, was not entitled to an order compelling the Banking Department to review his application for a mortgage loan originator license under the criteria set forth in Correction Law § 753. The Banking Department based its decision on recently enacted Banking Law article 12-E, specifically section 599-e thereof, which provided that no license shall issue to a person convicted of a felony involving fraud or dishonesty. As neither Correction Law § 753 nor certificates of relief were referenced in the new statute and absent any indication that the Legislature was unaware of section 753 and its prior integration with the Banking Law, an irrefutable inference must be drawn that what is omitted or not included was intended to be omitted or excluded. That both Correction Law § 701, providing for the issuance of certificates of relief from disabilities, and Banking Law § 599-e apply "[n]otwithstanding any other law" was immaterial, as the latter was enacted after the former, and thus, the Legislature could not have intended that the Correction Law apply notwithstanding any subsequently enacted legislation. Rather, the Legislature signaled its intentional omission from article 12-E of any requirement that an applicant's certificate of relief be considered.

Licenses -- Mortgage Loan Originator -- Delay in Processing Application

2. Petitioner, who was convicted 17 years previously of the felony of making a false statement in a mortgage loan application but possessed a certificate of relief from disabilities, did not establish that the delay by the Banking Department in processing his application for a license to be a mortgage loan originator constituted a basis for ordering the Department to review his application pursuant to the criteria set forth in Correction Law § 753. Petitioner's interest in being licensed as a mortgage loan originator did not rise to the level of a property right; rather, it was an interest contingent on the meeting of certain legal requirements. Moreover, the delay in reviewing petitioner's application, resulting in the application of stricter new rules concerning applicants with felony convictions, was no more than five to six months, and there was no evidence that respondents awaited the passage of the new rules in order to deprive petitioner of a license.

Licenses -- Mortgage Loan Originator -- Impairment of Contract

3. Petitioner, who was convicted 17 years previously of the felony of making a false statement in a mortgage loan application but had a certificate of relief from disabilities, did not establish that the Banking Department's denial of his application for a license to be a mortgage loan originator pursuant to stricter new rules codified in article 12-E of the Banking Law constituted an unconstitutional impairment of contracts. While the Banking Department relied on the conviction to deny petitioner the license, which was not a matter of contract, article 12-E did not preclude him from pursuing other employment and did not preclude all ex-offenders from becoming mortgage loan originators, just those with a specified criminal history directly related to the mortgage loan industry. Thus, as the ability of felons to obtain employment and licensing had not been completely eradicated by the recent enactments, there was no basis for finding any potential violations of the Contract Clause of the United States Constitution.

Licenses -- Mortgage Loan Originator -- Due Process

4. Petitioner, who was convicted 17 years previously of the felony of making a false statement in a mortgage loan application but had a certificate of relief from disabilities, was not deprived of due process by the Banking Department's refusal to issue him a license to be a mortgage loan originator pursuant to new, stricter rules codified in article 12-E of the Banking Law. The issuance of a certificate of relief never guaranteed success in obtaining a license. Consequently, absent automatic entitlement to be licensed as an mortgage loan originator, even upon application of Correction Law § 753 which sets forth factors to be considered concerning a previous conviction where a certificate of relief has been granted, petitioner had not established a legitimate claim of entitlement to the license, and thus had not shown that he had a property interest in being licensed.

Licenses -- Mortgage Loan Originator -- Damage to Reputation Following Denial of Application

5. In a proceeding arising out of the denial by respondents Banking Department and Deputy Superintendent of Banks of petitioner's application for a mortgage loan originator's license based on his prior felony conviction, petitioner failed to establish that his reputation had been damaged as a result of respondents' determination. Although respondents alerted petitioner's employer that he had been denied the license, there was no public dissemination of it, and as his employer was previously aware that petitioner possessed a certificate of relief from disabilities, it was already aware of his conviction. Also, information regarding petitioner's prior conviction was a matter of public record, which did not constitute dissemination of the underlying information.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, the property clerk of police department, filed a civil forfeiture action against defendant vehicle owner, seeking to forfeit his car as an instrumentality of a crime. Defendant moved to dismiss.


OVERVIEW: Police seized defendant's car after arresting and charging him with traffic violations and other crimes. Defendant argued that 1) under 38 RCNY § 12-36(a), plaintiff had to commence the action within 25 days after he filed his demand for the return of his car and provided the district attorney's release; and 2) under CPLR § 306-b, plaintiff was required to serve process no later than 15 days after the expiration of the 25-day statute of limitations, but did not do so. The court agreed. The 25-day limitations period set forth § 12-36(a) was a statute of limitations. As the limitations period was less than four months, under CPLR § 306-b, plaintiff was required to serve the process with 15 days after the last day of the 25 day limitations period. HEADNOTES

Forfeitures and Penalties -- Forfeiture of Proceeds or Instrumentality of Crime -- Civil Forfeiture Action

In a civil forfeiture action brought by plaintiff Property Clerk of the New York City Police Department pursuant to Administrative Code of City of NY § 14-140 based upon the seizure of defendant's vehicle as an instrumentality of a crime following defendant's arrest for various Vehicle and Traffic Law violations, plaintiff's service of process on defendant more than three months after defendant filed a demand for the return of his vehicle was untimely. Plaintiff was required, under 38 RCNY 12-36 (a), to commence the action within 25 days of receiving defendant's demand for the return of his vehicle and the District Attorney's release. The 25-day period set forth in the governing regulations for the Property Clerk to commence a forfeiture proceeding (see 38 RCNY 12-36 [a]) is a judicially inspired, albeit untraditional statute of limitations, and where, as here, the applicable statute of limitations is four months or less, CPLR 306-b requires that service of process be made not later than 15 days "after the date on which the applicable statute of limitations expires." As plaintiff failed to complete service of process no later than 15 days after the last day of the 25-day limitations period it otherwise had to commence the forfeiture action, dismissal of the action was warranted.

State of New York v Nelson


30044/2010


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


30 Misc. 3d 715; 911 N.Y.S.2d 594; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 5546; 2010 NY Slip Op 20459



November 16, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed by Matter of State of New York v Nelson, 89 AD3d 441, 932 NYS2d 42, 2011 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 7632 (N.Y. App. Div. 1st Dep't, Nov. 3, 2011)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent, a prisoner who was designated as a sex-offender, was the subject of a petition for sex offender civil management pursuant to Sex Offender Management and Treatment Act (SOMTA), Mental Hygiene Law, art. 10, ch. 7, (2007). He moved to dismiss the petition on the basis that certain provisions of the statute as applied to him were violative of the United States and New York State constitutions.


OVERVIEW: The prisoner was convicted of kidnapping, promoting prostitution and bail jumping. He received concurrent indeterminate sentences of 9-18 years incarceration on the kidnapping charge, 4-8 years on the promoting prostitution charge and 3-6 years on the bail jumping charge. According to the State, he restrained the victim, repeatedly raped her, forced her to engage in prostitution, beat her and forced her to ingest narcotics. The prisoner also had prior convictions for unlawful imprisonment, attempted assault and forcing a person to engage in prostitution. The court found that there was probable cause to believe the prisoner was a detained sex offender who suffered from a mental abnormality. The court held it was clear that the Legislature intended the challenged provision under Mental Hygiene Law § 10.03 to be civil rather than punitive. The court determined that four of the Mendoza-Martinez factors weighed towards finding that the statute was not punitive and three towards the opposite conclusion. A consideration of the factors as a whole, however, supported the view that the retroactive sexually motivated felony designation was not punitive. There was no due process violation.


OUTCOME: The motion to dismiss was denied.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law -- Ex Post Facto Law -- Sex Offender Civil Management -- Retroactive Designation of Nonsexual Criminal Offense as Sexually Motivated 

1. Respondent was not entitled to the dismissal of a petition seeking sex offender civil management on the basis that the designation of a nonsexual offense committed prior to the effective date of the Sex Offender Management and Treatment Act (SOMTA) (Mental Hygiene Law art 10) as a sexually motivated felony imposes retroactive punishment in violation of the Ex Post Facto Clause (US Const, art I, § 9 [3]). The retroactive designation of previous criminal conduct as sexually motivated was intended to define which nonsexual felony offenders could be subject to SOMTA, not to impose a criminal sanction. Moreover, the Legislature denominated a SOMTA jury's determination of whether an offender's previous conviction was sexually motivated as part of the civil management process, and the classification is contained in a civil statute, the Mental Hygiene Law, as part of a civil proceeding with a potential consequence of civil management. Furthermore, the retroactive sexually motivated felony classification serves a purpose other than punishment--determining an offender's civil management eligibility--and it is not constitutionally excessive in relation to such purpose as it is a rational method to capture offenders who have committed sexually motivated crimes and may suffer from a mental abnormality. Rather, the retroactive sexually motivated felony designation provides for the treatment and management of sexual offenders suffering from a mental abnormality for the purpose of protecting public safety.

Crimes -- Sex Offenders -- Civil Commitment or Supervision -- Applicability of Statute of Limitations to Retroactive Designation of Prior Felony as Sexually Motivated 

2. In a proceeding seeking sex offender civil management based on a sexual motivation determination for a designated felony committed prior to the effective date of the Sex Offender Management and Treatment Act (SOMTA) (Mental Hygiene Law art 10), the failure of SOMTA to impose a five-year limitations period in connection with the determination that an offender's previous felony conviction was "sexually motivated" does not violate due process and equal protection. While there is a risk of an erroneous deprivation of a person's liberty interest inherent in the absence of a statute of limitations, there is no procedural due process violation. The procedures of SOMTA which would be used to subject respondent to civil management based upon his 1992 felonies militate against such risk. The burden of proving sexual motivation is on the State and a sexual motivation finding is usually made with the benefit of extensive information about an offender's behaviors and motivations over the course of his or her lifetime. Moreover, the ultimate deprivation of liberty under the statute can only occur upon a finding that respondent currently suffers from a mental abnormality, and while the retrospective designation of a prior felony as sexually motivated is a necessary prerequisite to such a finding, it cannot alone establish it. Nor is there an equal protection violation. SOMTA is narrowly tailored to meet a compelling state objective--the protection of the public from dangerous sex offenders--because, without the challenged provision, offenders like respondent would not be covered by SOMTA at all. Furthermore, a statute of limitations is not required for a retroactive sexual motivation designation because the determination has no effect on a respondent's criminal conviction, but is only a screening device to determining civil management eligibility. Further, the requirement that an offender be a detained sex offender under SOMTA imposes an effective temporal limitation in a wide range of cases on the ability of the State to allege that a prior designated felony was sexually motivated.

OVERVIEW: The police observed two groups of young people facing each other near a school. The police affiant stated that two groups were "threatening" each other. The affidavit did not recount the words that the officer deemed "threatening." Ten individuals comprised one group and 20 comprised the other. Respondent was seen in the group of 20 individuals. HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Riot -- Violent and Tumultuous Conduct 

1. In a juvenile delinquency proceeding brought against respondent, who was alleged to have been part of one of two groups of young people "threatening" each other, the count in the petition charging respondent with riot in the second degree (Penal Law § 240.05) based on allegations that some of the 20 members of respondent's group were "reaching for their waistbands," that some members possessed a golf club, a broomstick and a belt, and that one member threw a glass bottle when the police attempted to disperse the groups was facially insufficient. An individual violates Penal Law § 240.05 when he or she, simultaneously with four or more other persons, engages in tumultuous and violent conduct and thereby intentionally or recklessly causes or creates a grave risk of causing public alarm. Here, the petition failed to state any act of tumultuous and violent conduct engaged in by respondent. Nor did it allege facts specific to respondent from which it might be inferred that respondent shared a community of purpose with others engaged in violent and tumultuous conduct, which went beyond his mere presence at the scene.

Crimes -- Unlawful Assembly -- Intent 

2. In a juvenile delinquency proceeding brought against respondent, who was alleged to have been part of one of two groups of young people "threatening" each other, the count in the petition charging respondent with unlawful assembly (Penal Law § 240.10) based on allegations that some of the 20 members of respondent's group were "reaching for their waistbands," that some members possessed a golf club, a broomstick and a belt, and that one member threw a glass bottle when the police attempted to disperse the groups was facially insufficient. An individual violates Penal Law § 240.10 when he or she assembles with four or more other persons for the purpose of engaging or preparing to engage with them in tumultuous and violent conduct likely to cause public alarm, or when, being present at an assembly which either has or develops such purpose, he or she remains there with the intent to advance that purpose. Here, the petition did not allege conduct, words or acts specific to respondent which supported an inference that respondent's purpose in being present with the group was to engage in imminent violent and tumultuous conduct. The only allegation made that was specific to respondent was that he was observed to be part of the group.

Crimes -- Violation of Municipal Code -- Aggressive Solicitation -- Constitutionality of Prohibition against Aggressive Solicitation
