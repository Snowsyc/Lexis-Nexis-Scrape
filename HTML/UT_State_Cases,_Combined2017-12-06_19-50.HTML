OUTCOME: The court affirmed the grant of summary judgment as to the medical center, but reversed the grant of summary judgment as to the foster parents. The matter was remanded for further proceedings.

DISPOSITION: Trial court ruling reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: As a result of being stopped at a vehicle checkpoint, defendant was charged with various drug possession crimes, a seatbelt violation, driving under the influence of drugs or alcohol, and speeding. The Sixth District Court, Richfield (Utah), denied defendant's motion to suppress then accepted his conditional guilty plea on all counts. Defendant appealed the denial of his motion to suppress.


OVERVIEW: While stopped at a vehicle checkpoint, another driver complained about defendant's reckless driving. Defendant was driving with only a vest n, in March with the windows down. Defendant refused to allow a search of the car and he subsequently failed field sobriety tests. His passenger then admitted to drugs in the car. On appeal the court held that the motion to suppress should have been granted because the application for the vehicle checkpoint was too broad to be valid under Utah Code Ann. § 77-23-104 (1999). The checkpoint at issue was purported in the application to be a drivers license checkpoint. However, the checkpoint plan included several purposes, including an undefined cursory review for violations regarding exterior lights and other safety devices. The court held that the discretion allowed to officers by such a description was inherently unreasonable under Utah. Const. art. I, § 14, because there was also no guidance to assure that such checks were consistent across all vehicles. Moreover, pretextual checkpoint stops were impermissible under the same constitutional provision.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's ruling denying defendant's motion to suppress.
