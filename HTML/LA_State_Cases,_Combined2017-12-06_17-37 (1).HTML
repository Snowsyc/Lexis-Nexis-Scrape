DISPOSITION: REVERSED AND REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The State charged defendant with soliciting an undercover officer to engage in "unnatural oral copulation for compensation." The Twenty-Fourth Judicial District Court, Parish of Jefferson, Louisiana, held that the United States Supreme Court in Lawrence v. Texas, rendered Louisiana's "crime against nature" statute, La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:89(A)(2) unconstitutional, and granted defendant's motion to quash. The State appealed.


OVERVIEW: La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:89(A)(1) prohibited crimes against nature, same being the unnatural carnal copulation by a human being with another of the same sex or opposite sex or with an animal. La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:89(A)(2) provided that the solicitation by a human being of another with the intent to engage in any unnatural carnal copulation "for compensation" was a crime against nature. In Lawrence, the United States Supreme Court had recognized a due process right of liberty of consenting adults to engage in private sexual relations without intervention of the government. However, the court held the trial court's reliance on the latter decision, and its conclusion that the statute was enforced in a discriminatory manner, was misplaced. The United States Supreme Court noted that Lawrence did not involve public conduct or prostitution, and thus, left unaffected any offenses charging such behavior. Thus, the United States Supreme Court's decision did not impact Louisiana convictions charged under La. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 14:89(A)(2), involving solicitation to engage in unnatural carnal copulation "for compensation," or cases interpreting that section of the statute.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's granting of defendant's motion to quash and remanded the matter to the trial court for further proceedings.
