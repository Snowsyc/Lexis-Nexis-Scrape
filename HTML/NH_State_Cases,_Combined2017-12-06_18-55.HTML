February 17, 2005, Submitted  
April 22, 2005, Opinion Issued


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Released for Publication May 24, 2005.


PRIOR HISTORY: Grafton.



DISPOSITION: Vacated and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant, who was convicted of burglary and aggravated felonious sexual assault, appealed an order of the Superior Court (New Hampshire) which denied his motion to modify his sentence. Defendant was serving two consecutive sentences of seven and one-half to fifteen years.


OVERVIEW: In imposing defendant's sentence, the trial court required defendant to attend and complete a particular treatment program. However, the treatment program was terminated while defendant was imprisoned. Thereafter, defendant attended a program which replaced the treatment program. The court noted that the new program was an educational program and not a treatment program. Therefore, the trial court declined to suspend his sentence. The appellate court held defendant preserved the issue for appeal. The trial court's order did not violate defendant's due process rights under either the New Hampshire Constitution or the Federal Constitution. The trial court could not impose impossible conditions upon defendant's motion for suspension of his sentence. The appellate court noted that its holding did not mean that defendant was entitled to a suspension of his sentence, however. It meant only that it was an error for the trial court to rely upon his failure to complete the treatment program, or an equivalent program, as a basis for its denial.


OUTCOME: The order was vacated and the matter was remanded.

Taylor v. Town of Plaistow


No. 2004-337


SUPREME COURT OF NEW HAMPSHIRE


152 N.H. 142; 872 A.2d 769; 2005 N.H. LEXIS 57


February 9, 2005, Argued  
April 22, 2005, Opinion Issued


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Released for Publication May 24, 2005.


PRIOR HISTORY: Rockingham.
Town of Plastow Bd. of Selectmen v. Town of Plastow Zoning Bd. of Adjustment, 146 N.H. 263, 769 A.2d 397, 2001 N.H. LEXIS 67 (2001)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant vehicle dealership sought review of a decision of the Superior Court (Rockingham, New Hampshire) which upheld the validity of a zoning ordinance enacted by appellee town.


OVERVIEW: The town adopted an amendment to Plaistow, N.H., Zoning Ordinance § 300:14(b) that required a minimum of 1,000 feet between vehicular dealerships in a Commercial I district. The dealership filed an application for a variance. After the matter eventually went to the appellate court, the variance was granted. The dealership subsequent sought a declaration that § 300:14(b) was unconstitutional and requested damages for the time during which the dealership owned the property but could not use it for vehicular sales. The trial court found that the town primarily focused on aesthetics, safety and planning concerns when drafting and enacting the 1,000-foot buffer between vehicular dealerships. The appellate court held those were legitimate reasons for which a town could exercise its zoning power. The ordinance was rationally related to legitimate town goals and, therefore, comported with the due process requirements of the New Hampshire Constitution. The classification created by the ordinance bore a fair and substantial relationship to the town's goals of preserving aesthetics and ensuring traffic safety. Thus, there was no equal protection violation.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed.

Baines v. N.H. Senate President


No. 2004-707


SUPREME COURT OF NEW HAMPSHIRE


152 N.H. 124; 876 A.2d 768; 2005 N.H. LEXIS 54


February 16, 2005, Argued  
April 20, 2005, Opinion Issued


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Released for Publication April 20, 2005.
Rehearing denied by Baines v. NH Senate President, 2005 N.H. LEXIS 118 (N.H., June 13, 2005)


PRIOR HISTORY: Merrimack.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners, cities and mayors, filed suit against respondents, the New Hampshire Senate President and the House Speaker, to have 2004 N.H. Laws ch. 200 declared unconstitutional on grounds that, inter alia, it was a "money bill" that should have originated in the New Hampshire House of Representatives pursuant to the Origination Clause, N.H. Const. pt. II, art. 18. The New Hampshire superior court denied the petition; petitioners sought review.


OVERVIEW: Petitioners argued that, as well as violating N.H. Const. pt. II, art. 18 by originating in the New Hampshire Senate, 2004 N.H. Laws ch. 200 (the statute) was materially different from the bill that the legislature had passed and thus violated N.H. Const. pt. II, art. 2, 20, 37 and 44 and N.H. Rev. Stat. Ann. §§ 14:8 and 20:2-a. The high court disagreed with both contentions. It initially held that whether the statute violated §§ 14:8 and 20:2-a was a nonjusticiable political question, but whether it violated the mandatory provisions of N.H. Const. pt. II, art. 2, 20, 37, and 44 was justiciable. As the statute raised the education property tax rate, it was a "money bill." However, since House members made up a majority of the conference committee, which added the "money bill" elements to the statute, and the House was the first body to approve the "money bill" portion, N.H. Const. pt. II, art. 18 was not violated. The legislative journals were inconclusive and insufficient to defeat the presumption that a change the conference committee made to the bill's wording was in accord with the legislature's intent. The other change cited by petitioners was merely technical, not material.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

State v. MacMillan


No. 2004-402


SUPREME COURT OF NEW HAMPSHIRE


152 N.H. 67; 872 A.2d 1031; 2005 N.H. LEXIS 45


January 19, 2005, Argued  
April 1, 2005, Opinion Issued


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Released for Publication May 24, 2005.
Rehearing denied by State v. MacMillan, 2005 N.H. LEXIS 91 (N.H., May 10, 2005)


PRIOR HISTORY: Rockingham.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was indicted on one count of certain prohibited uses of computer services. The State filed a motion in limine asking the Rockingham Superior Court (New Hampshire) to admit a detective's testimony concerning his Internet conversation with defendant. The trial court denied the motion. The State appealed.


OVERVIEW: The detective posed as a 14-year-old girl in an Internet chat room, and defendant, believing that he was dealing with a teenaged girl, solicited a sex act for money. Earlier, the trial court held that the detective's interception was illegal under N.H. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 570-A:1 (2001), and the State did not appeal. In its own motion, the State argued that exclusion of the detective's testimony about the communication was not required by N.H. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 570-A:6 (2001) since the detective's knowledge of the communication was obtained through his own observations, not through the illegal interception. The appellate court agreed. Under N.H. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 570-A:2, I(c), the detective would not have been disclosing information "obtained through the interception" because his knowledge about the contents of the conversation came from his personal observations as a participant. Assuming the detective relied on his own memory of the conversation, his testimony would be the same regardless of whether the interception occurred and was thus admissible. Since the testimony was admissible, the procedural requirements of N.H. Rev. Stat. Ann. § 570-A:9 did not apply.


OUTCOME: The order was reversed, and the case was remanded.
