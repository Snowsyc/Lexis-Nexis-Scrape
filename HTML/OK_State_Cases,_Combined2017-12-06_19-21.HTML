PRIOR HISTORY: APPEAL FROM THE DISTRICT COURT OF OKLAHOMA COUNTY, OKLAHOMA. HONORABLE NOMA GURICH, JUDGE.



DISPOSITION: District court declaratory judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellee restaurant and appellee beverage association sought a declaratory judgment, challenging the constitutionality of Okla. Stat. art. 37, § 598 (Supp. 1997), which attempted to grant authority to the appellant Oklahoma Alcoholic Beverage Laws Enforcement Commission to regulate 3.2 (low-point) beer. The District Court of Oklahoma County, Oklahoma, granted the judgment, and it found the statute was unconstitutional. The commission appealed.


OVERVIEW: The restaurant held a license to sell alcoholic beverages for consumption on its premises. The commission permitted the restaurant to establish a bar area to serve people over the age of 21. An audit of the restaurant showed that the sale of food did not exceed the income derived from the sale of alcoholic beverages and 3.2 beer. As a result, the commission informed the restaurant that a sign would be posted at the door saying "21 only." The restaurant argued that the commission lacked the power to do what it planned to do, and the restaurant risked losing its family restaurant status. The commission argued that the controlling statute, Okla. Stat. tit. 37, § 598 (Supp. 1997), was not unconstitutional, as it simply attempted to regulate who would enter the premises. The appellate court determined that the statute was unconstitutional, as it included low-point beer in the definition of alcoholic beverages.


OUTCOME: The appellate court affirmed the trial court's judgment.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant inmate sued defendant attorney in the District Court of LeFlore County (Oklahoma) for legal malpractice in his defense against first degree murder charges. The inmate was convicted and sentenced to life without the possibility of parole. The trial court granted the attorney's motion to dismiss the negligence suit on the ground that the inmate's petition failed to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. The inmate appealed.

OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, a veterinarian, sued defendants, a television station and a reporter for the station, in the District Court of Creek County, Oklahoma, for defamation and false light invasion of privacy. The district court denied the station's motion for a directed verdict and awarded judgment for compensatory and punitive damages based on the jury's verdict, together with prejudgment interest and costs. PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, retired police officers, filed an application seeking credit toward their pensions pursuant to Okla. Stat. tit. 72, § 67.13a (Supp. 1997). OVERVIEW: The officers had all retired from the military. They filed an application with the board to receive credit toward their pensions as war veterans. Pursuant to Okla. Stat. tit. 23, § 6 (1991), they were entitled to prejudgment interest from the dates of their retirements.


OUTCOME: The district court correctly held that the officers were entitled to military service credit toward their retirement benefits, but it erred in denying their request for interest. Mattingly v. Court on the Judiciary, Trial Div.


No. CJAD-2000-1


COURT ON THE JUDICIARY OF OKLAHOMA, APPELLATE DIVISION


2000 OK JUD 1; 8 P.3d 943; 2000 Okla. JUD LEXIS 3



July 5, 2000, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] ORIGINAL ACTION FOR WRIT OF PROHIBITION.



DISPOSITION: ORIGINAL JURISDICTION PREVIOUSLY ASSUMED, WRIT OF PROHIBITION GRANTED, PETITIONER'S PETITION DISMISSED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner judge filed an application for writ of prohibition, and for an order staying proceedings in the Court on the Judiciary, Trial Division which alleged misconduct that was not serious enough to require removal or compulsory retirement.


OVERVIEW: The Court on the Judiciary, Trial Division erred when it denied petitioner judge's motions challenging its jurisdiction. Okla. Const. art. 7-A §§ 1, 4 required judicial complaints to be brought for no other purpose than that of removing or retiring the judge who was the subject of the proceeding. The Council on Judicial Complaints and the Court on the Judiciary lacked jurisdiction to discipline judges whose claimed misconduct would not lead to removal from office. The complaint lodged against petitioner by an unsuccessful litigant in a divorce case was likely based, not on dissatisfaction with the alleged rule violation, but on the results of the decision. In the petitioner's 11 year career as an associate district judge he was seldom reversed on appeal. Petitioner's violations were not continued willful failure to comply with rules and directives. The court held the proceeding against petitioner did not rise to the level of misconduct envisioned by the framers of Okla. Const. art. 7-A § 1 et seq. to invoke the jurisdiction of the Court on the Judiciary.


OUTCOME: Writ of prohibition granted. The Council on Judicial Complaints and the Court on the Judiciary lacked jurisdiction to consider allegations of misconduct by petitioner judge that, even if true, would not call for the judge's removal from office or compulsory retirement. Discipline for minor judicial misconduct was within the exclusive jurisdiction of the supreme court.
