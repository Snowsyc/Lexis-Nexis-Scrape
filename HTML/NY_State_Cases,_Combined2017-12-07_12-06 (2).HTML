People v. Jakubowitz


IND. NO. 3867/99


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, BRONX COUNTY


184 Misc. 2d 559; 710 N.Y.S.2d 844; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 215



May 24, 2000, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] As Corrected July 24, 2000.



DISPOSITION: Defendant's motion for a Sandoval hearing is granted. The motion is otherwise denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed motions to dismiss defendant's indictments for practicing law without being admitted and as an attorney who was disbarred or suspended, criminal possession of forged instruments and stolen property, and grand larceny in violation of N.Y. Jud. Law §§ 478 and 486 and N.Y. Penal Law § 165.60(2) based on insufficiency of evidence, the rule of lenity, duplicity, and lack of jurisdiction or venue.


OVERVIEW: Defendant lawyer was suspended from the practice of law. Defendant met a client, alleged that he was an attorney, and undertook to prepare and file a mechanic's lien for the client. Defendant signed the client's name to documents without the client's knowledge or authorization. A grand jury indicted defendant for practicing law without being admitted and as a suspended attorney, criminal possession of forged instruments and stolen property, and grand larceny. Defendant filed motions to dismiss based on insufficient evidence, the rule of lenity, duplicity, and lack of jurisdiction or venue. The court denied defendant's motions in part and held that plaintiff state presented legally sufficient evidence to support the charges and that the grand jury was correctly charged. N.Y. Jud. Law § 478 was not unconstitutionally vague because it clearly prohibited a non-licensed person from practicing law. The preparation and filing of legal documents and appearing to be an attorney were prohibited. Jurisdiction and venue were proper. The court dismissed the practicing law charges for duplicity and held that a person was able to hold himself out as an attorney without ever practicing law.


OUTCOME: The court granted and denied defendant's motions to dismiss criminal charges in part and held that the charges were supported by sufficient evidence, that a grand jury was properly charged, that a statute prohibiting suspended attorneys from engaging in the practice of law was not unconstitutional, that jurisdiction and venue were proper, but that the practicing law charges were duplicitous.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law - Validity of Statute - Judiciary Law § 478 

1. Judiciary Law § 478, which is not a model of clarity but which clearly prohibits a nonlicensed individual from practicing law by appearing as an attorney in a court of record in New York, rendering legal services, or holding himself or herself out as being entitled to practice law as aforesaid or in any other manner, is not unconstitutionally vague. The prohibited practice of law includes the rendering of legal advice and preparation of legal papers in New York even if performed out of court and with respect to foreign law. While there are grey areas in the law which might render the prosecution of a defendant who inadvertently overstepped such bounds inappropriate on constitutional grounds, the conduct defendant is accused of, preparation in New York of legal documents for filing out of State and holding himself out as an attorney, has been prohibited since at least 1957.

Crimes - Unauthorized Practice of Law - Duplicitous Counts in Indictment

2. In a prosecution for, inter alia, unauthorized practice of law, the first two counts of the indictment, both of which charge defendant with violating Judiciary Law § 478 within a certain time period by holding himself out as an attorney and by engaging in the practice of law in the Bronx without being duly admitted to practice in New York State, are duplicitous in that both counts charge more than one crime. While holding oneself out as an attorney and practicing law both violate Judiciary Law § 478 when performed by one not licensed to practice, one can hold oneself out as an attorney without ever actually practicing law. Accordingly, they are separate crimes which must be set forth in separate counts of an indictment.

Crimes - Unauthorized Practice of Law - Venue

3. Bronx County is the proper venue for a criminal prosecution for second degree criminal possession of a forged instrument, third degree grand larceny, and third degree criminal possession of stolen property where it is alleged that defendant came to the complainant's apartment in the Bronx; misrepresented that he was authorized to practice law for the purpose of obtaining a $ 4,000 retainer, $ 1,000 of which was then paid; and thereafter signed the complainant's name to a mechanic's lien and related documents and filed them in New Jersey without showing them to the complainant and without his authority, even though there was no evidence that the documents were issued or forged or possessed by the defendant in the Bronx. The original misrepresentation, which evidences the defendant's intent to defraud or deceive, took place in the Bronx, and the forgery then helped perpetuate this misrepresentation.

People v. Knowles


INDICTMENT NO. 99-1138


COUNTY COURT OF NEW YORK, RENSSELAER COUNTY


184 Misc. 2d 474; 709 N.Y.S.2d 916; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 166



May 15, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Defendant's motion to dismiss based upon the unconsitutionality of the statute as applied is denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant moved to dismiss the indictment against him under N.Y. Agric. & Mkts. Law § 353-a, which related to aggravated cruelty to animals, arguing that statute was unconstitutional as vague and overbroad, violating due process provisions of U.S. Const. amend. XIV and the New York State Constitution.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was seen to kick a young dog down the walkway, then pick it up and throw it up against a brick wall. The court denied defendant's motion to dismiss his indictment under N.Y. Agric. & Mkts. Law § 353-a because the statute defining the aggravated cruelty to animals offense was vague and overbroad, violating due process provisions of U.S. Const. amend. XIV and the state constitution. The Constitution did not require impossible standards. It was enough that language conveyed sufficiently definite warnings to proscribe conduct when measured by common understanding and practices. Courts were allowed to apply a reasonable construction to carry out legislative intent and to sustain the validity of a statute. Whether or not a statute is unconstitutionally vague as applied, the actual conduct of the person attacking the statute must be considered, and not with respect to hypothetical situations at the periphery of the statute's scope or with respect to the conduct of other parties who might not be forewarned by the broad language. A person of ordinary intelligence would realize defendant's alleged conduct was precisely the sort of activity intended to be covered by the statute.


OUTCOME: Motion to dismiss was denied, because the language used in the statute to define "aggravated cruelty" conveyed sufficiently definite warnings to proscribe conduct when measured by common understanding and practices, and when specifically applied to defendant's alleged conduct, which was to kick a dog and throw it against a wall.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law - Validity of Statute - Agriculture and Markets Law § 353-a 

1. Agriculture and Markets Law § 353-a, which makes it a felony to intentionally kill or intentionally cause serious physical injury to a companion animal with aggravated cruelty and no justifiable purpose, is not unconstitutionally vague or overbroad. The term "aggravated cruelty" is defined in the statute as conduct intended to cause extreme physical pain, or conduct done or carried out in an especially depraved or sadistic manner. Although the phrases "extreme physical pain" and "especially depraved or sadistic manner" are not defined within the statute, the failure to define every word in a criminal statute does not make the statute unconstitutionally vague. The phrase "extreme physical pain" uses ordinary terms to express ideas that find adequate interpretation in everyday usage and understanding, and it is not so vague that persons of common intelligence must necessarily guess at its meaning and differ as to its application. Similarly, a person of ordinary intelligence, when considering the phrase "especially depraved or sadistic manner" as a whole, would understand what conduct is prohibited by the statute. "Depraved" and "sadistic" have a common meaning in everyday usage, and although describing these words with the intensive "especially" may be superfluous, when considered together the combined words convey the clear intent to punish only the most serious and egregious conduct.

Constitutional Law - Validity of Statute - Agriculture and Markets Law § 353-a 

2. Agriculture and Markets Law § 353-a, which makes it a felony to intentionally kill or intentionally cause serious physical injury to a companion animal with aggravated cruelty and no justifiable purpose, is not unconstitutionally vague as applied to defendant, who is accused of kicking an eight-month-old, 28-pound dog down a walkway and then picking it up and throwing it against a brick wall. A person of ordinary intelligence would realize that defendant's actions are precisely the sort of actions intended to be covered by the statute.

People v. Target Adver. Inc.


Docket No. 1999SN068431, Docket No. 1999SN096338, Docket No. 2000SN004891


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


184 Misc. 2d 903; 708 N.Y.S.2d 597; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 168



May 15, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed a motion to dismiss three summonses for violations of New York City, N.Y., R. tit. 34, § 4-12(j)(1), arguing that the traffic rule barring a type of vehicular advertising violated U.S. Const. amend. I as an impermissible restriction on commercial speech.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was issued three summonses for violations of New York City, N.Y., R. tit. 34, § 4-12(j)(1), which barred the operation of vehicles solely for the purpose of displaying commercial advertising. Defendant moved to dismiss the summonses, arguing the rule violated U.S. Const. amend. I as an impermissible restriction on commercial speech. The court held commercial speech was afforded less constitutional protection than noncommercial speech. The court held the rule at issue satisfied the four-part Central Hudson test for commercial speech regulation. Plaintiff asserted a substantial governmental interest, as the purpose of the rule was to promote traffic safety and alleviate traffic congestion. The court held the rule at issue advanced plaintiff's substantial interest, as there was a direct correlation between the vehicles removed from the streets and a decrease in traffic. The court held the rule was not more extensive than necessary, because exceptions to the rule allowed businesses that wished to advertise to have a means to accomplish their result, such as advertising on buses and taxis.


OUTCOME: The court denied defendant's motion to dismiss the summonses, because the rule banning the use of vehicles for advertising did not violate the First Amendment, as the rule was not unnecessarily broad, and was meant to help control traffic congestion.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law - Validity of Regulation - Prohibition of Vehicles Used Solely for Commercial Advertising

1. The failure of the Board of Aldermen of the City of New York in enacting the predecessor to RCNY 4-12 (j) (1), which prohibits the operation on City streets of vehicles used solely for the purpose of commercial advertising, to include legislative findings that identify the substantial interest sought to be advanced by the restriction on commercial advertising, does not render the rule unconstitutional. A governmental body is not required to make any specific type of legislative findings or even to specify its purpose contemporaneously with the enactment of legislation that regulates commercial speech.

Constitutional Law - Validity of Regulation - Prohibition of Vehicles Used Solely for Commercial Advertising

2. RCNY 4-12 (j) (1), which prohibits the operation on City streets of vehicles used solely for the purpose of commercial advertising, does not violate the First Amendment of the United States Constitution as an impermissible restriction on commercial speech. In reviewing a regulation that impinges on commercial speech, a court must determine whether the speech concerns lawful activity and is not misleading, whether the asserted government interest is substantial, whether the regulation directly advances the governmental interest asserted, and whether it is not more extensive than is necessary to serve that interest. Although RCNY 4-12 (j) (1) does not purport to ban only advertising that concerns unlawful activity or is misleading, the government's interest in the regulation of traffic is substantial, the rule advances the government's interest in controlling traffic in that the ban on advertising-only vehicles lessens the amount of potential traffic on City streets, and the rule is narrowly and appropriately tailored. Businesses that wish their advertisements to be seen on City streets but are prohibited from operating vehicles solely for that purpose have a means available to accomplish that result through the purchase of advertisements on buses, taxis or sanitation trucks, as well as the purchase of stationary billboard space.

People v. Webb


Docket Nos. 99C009516 & 99N109498


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


184 Misc. 2d 508; 709 N.Y.S.2d 369; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 185



May 8, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Defendant's motion seeking discovery in docket number 99N109498 is granted to the extent of the information provided in the People's response and voluntary disclosure form. Defendant's motion for a Huntley/Dunaway hearing in docket number 99N109498 is granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was charged with violating New York City, N.Y., Rules of the City of New York, tit. 21, § 1050.4(c) by selling farecards to other users and taking money from others and then using farecard to allow those persons to enter subway. Defendant moved for discovery and Huntley/Dunaway hearing.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was seen receiving money from individuals in exchange for which he swiped a farecard through the turnstile in order to allow them to enter the subway. On another occasion, defendant was seen selling a farecard. The violations were combined for disposition. New York City, N.Y., Rules of the City of New York, tit. 21, § 1050.4(c) contained a generalized prohibition that did not distinguish between conduct calculated to cause harm and conduct that was essentially innocent, thereby failing to give adequate notice of forbidden conduct. Therefore, the charge that defendant "allowed" persons who had given him money to enter the subway by swiping a farecard was dismissed, because it relied on the unconstitutionally vague portion of the statute. The charge that defendant sold a farecard did not need to be dismissed, because the portion of the statute which prohibited sales was not vague.


OUTCOME: Defendant's motions for discovery and Huntley/Dunaway hearing were granted as to charge of selling farecard. The information charging defendant with allowing persons who had given him money to enter subway by swiping farecard was dismissed because underlying portion of statute was void for vagueness.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law - Validity of Regulation - Vagueness - Unauthorized Sale of Transit Authority Fare Media

1. That portion of 21 NYCRR 1050.4 (c) which prohibits the unauthorized sale of fare media (i.e., the various instruments issued by or on behalf of the New York City Transit Authority to use for the payment of fare, including tokens, passes, farecards, transfers, tickets and vouchers) provides people of ordinary intelligence with adequate notice of the forbidden conduct, and offers objective standards to law enforcement personnel, and therefore is not unconstitutionally vague. Accordingly, an accusatory instrument which alleges that defendant sold a MetroCard need not be dismissed. Only "authorized agents" of the Authority may accept or solicit compensation for the use of time-based MetroCards (i.e., cards permitting an unlimited number of uses in a certain time period); there is no similar restriction for value-based cards. Although defendant suggests that section 1050.4 (c)'s prohibition against the sale of MetroCards is applicable to time-based cards only, and that, therefore, the violation information is facially insufficient because it does not state which type of MetroCard was sold by defendant, section 1050.4 (c) makes unlawful the unauthorized sale of all fare media. Defendant has failed to provide any support for the proposition that when the Authority adopted its tariff, it intended to amend section 1050.4 (c) by permitting the sale of value-based cards. Therefore, there is no need to state which type of MetroCard is being sold. The accusatory instrument is facially sufficient and shall not be dismissed.

Constitutional Law - Validity of Regulation - Vagueness - Unauthorized Sale of Transit Authority Fare Media

2. That portion of 21 NYCRR 1050.4 (c) which forbids "authoriz[ing] access to or use of" the subway is unconstitutionally vague, since this generalized prohibition does not distinguish between conduct calculated to cause harm and conduct that is essentially innocent. Accordingly, it fails to give adequate notice of what conduct is forbidden. In addition, this portion of the rule affords law enforcement personnel total discretion in determining whom they will arrest. The violation information does not allege that defendant sold a MetroCard, but that he "allow[ed]" certain persons who had given him money to enter the subway station by swiping a MetroCard. Because the accusatory instrument relies upon the "authoriz[ing] access" language--the portion of section 1050.4 (c) which is unconstitutionally vague--it is dismissed.

People v. Santiago


Indict. # 210/99; Index # 3903/99


COUNTY COURT OF NEW YORK, MONROE COUNTY


185 Misc. 2d 138; 712 N.Y.S.2d 244; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 276



May 5, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Motion of the "intervenors" granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Intervenors, television station and others, filed an order to show cause in the action of plaintiff, state, brought against defendant, criminal, requesting permission to: intervene, televise the trial and any post-conviction proceedings, apply former N.Y. Jud. Law §218 and administrative rule guidelines, declare N.Y. Civ. Rights §52 unconstitutional, and take still photographs/images.


OVERVIEW: Intervenors, television station and others, filed a show cause order in the action of plaintiff, state, against defendant, criminal, seeking to: intervene, televise the trial and any post-conviction proceedings, apply former N.Y. Jud. Law §218 and other administrative rules, declare N.Y. Civ. Rights §52 unconstitutional, and take still photographs/images. The court considered two issues: (1) whether or not § 52 violated either the federal or state constitution and, (2) if it did, whether allowing the press to broadcast the trial violated the defendant's right to a fair trial. The court found that it had jurisdiction to hear the application, granted intervention and found independent and compelling authority in N.Y. Const. art. 1, § 8. It concluded that § 52 was unconstitutional and that the constitutionality presumption had been overcome beyond a reasonable doubt. Since § 52 was invalid, the court allowed television cameras and still photos in the court, but imposed standards and safeguards, under § 218 and other administrative rules, for the audio-visual coverage designed to protect the rights of all parties to a fair trial.


OUTCOME: Intervention allowed, state civil rights law was unconstitutional, and court allowed television cameras and still photographs in the courtroom, but imposed standards and safeguards to protect the rights of all parties to a fair trial.


HEADNOTES

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings - Authority of County Court to Determine Application for Permission to Televise and Photograph Trial

1. County Court has the authority to hear the application by the proposed intervenors, members of the press, for permission to televise and photograph defendant's trial and any postconviction proceedings. Barred by Civil Rights Law § 52 from entering the public courtroom with their video and audio equipment, the broadcast media are most directly affected by the statute and, thus, are in the best position to argue its unconstitutionality. Important interests are best championed by those most directly affected by their impairment, and the fact that there may be other forums in which similar relief may be obtained does not diminish County Court's authority to do likewise.

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings - Constitutionality of Ban against Coverage - Applicability of First Amendment

2. In determining an application by the proposed intervenors, members of the press, for permission to televise and photograph defendant's trial and any postconviction proceedings, County Court need not determine whether the ban against audio-visual coverage in the courtroom of judicial proceedings contained in Civil Rights Law § 52 violates the First Amendment to the United States Constitution, as independent and controlling authority may be found in article I, § 8 of the New York Constitution. 

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings - Constitutionality of Ban against Coverage - Applicability of New York Constitution

3. New York's constitutional free press provision (NY Const, art I, § 8), with roots deeper and older than those of the Federal Constitution's First Amendment, forms an appropriately independent State constitutional basis for evaluating Civil Rights Law § 52, which bans audio-visual coverage in the courtroom of judicial proceedings. Textually different from the First Amendment, article I, § 8 of the New York Constitution begins by affirmatively stating New Yorkers' rights to freedom of speech and of the press, followed by a prohibition that no legislative enactment may be passed abridging them. There is no such affirmative statement in the First Amendment.

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings - Ban against Coverage is Unconstitutional under New York Constitution

4. Civil Rights Law § 52, which bans audio-visual coverage in the courtroom of judicial proceedings, violates the New York Constitution. Article I, § 8 of the New York Constitution affirmatively recites every citizen's rights to freedom of speech and of the press, and prohibits the passage of any law which would "restrain or abridge the liberty of speech or of the press." Civil Rights Law § 52 restrains and abridges the liberty of the press by controlling how television and radio journalists may report the trial; it diminishes and reduces the scope of their coverage. Moreover, the statute's own legislative history supports the conclusion that Civil Rights Law § 52 is unconstitutional.

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings - Applicability of Ban against Coverage to Still Photography

5. The ban against audio-visual coverage in the courtroom of judicial proceedings contained in Civil Rights Law § 52 does not apply to the taking of still photographs and images. Accordingly, in a proceeding to determine an application by the proposed intervenors, members of the press, for permission to televise and photograph defendant's trial and any postconviction proceedings, the application by one of the proposed intervenors to have a photographer take still photographs during the trial is granted.

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings - Fair Trial Considerations

6. In a proceeding to determine an application for permission to televise and photograph defendant's trial and any postconviction proceedings, the proposed intervenors, members of the press whose application was granted, must comply with County Court's guidelines for audio-visual coverage, which are tailored to the specifics of defendant's case but are otherwise identical to Rules of the Chief Administrator of the Courts (22 NYCRR) part 131. By declaring Civil Rights Law § 52, which bans audio-visual coverage in the courtroom of judicial proceedings, unconstitutional, County Court must institute standards and safeguards to be utilized for the audio-visual coverage of the case designed to protect the rights of all parties to a fair trial. The guidelines for audio-visual coverage contained in Rules of the Chief Administrator of the Courts (22 NYCRR) part 131 well served fair-trial interests during the 10-year experiment with cameras in the courtroom.

Adler v. Jackson


INDEX NO.; 22618/99


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, QUEENS COUNTY


185 Misc. 2d 45; 712 N.Y.S.2d 240; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 279



April 10, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Petitioner's request for relief in the nature of mandamus annulling the DMV's denial of a renewal license and directing the issuance of such a license, and for an order awarding attorneys fees and costs is denied, and the petition is dismissed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner filed a petition for a writ of mandamus asking the court to compel respondent Commissioner of Motor Vehicles to issue a renewal driver's license to petitioner after respondent failed to renew petitioner's license due to petitioner's failure to provide respondent with petitioner's social security number.


OVERVIEW: The court denied petitioner's request for a writ of mandamus. Petitioner filed an action asking the court to compel respondent Commissioner of Motor Vehicles to issue a renewal driver's license to petitioner. Respondent denied the renewal because petitioner refused to disclose his social security number. The court held that mandamus would only be granted where petitioner had a clear legal right to relief. In this case, the court held that respondent did not have the discretion to renew a driver's license without a social security number. Furthermore, the court held that petitioner's disclosure of his social security number was not a violation of the Privacy Act of 1974, 5 U.S.C.S. § 552A. Therefore, the court denied petitioner's application for a writ of mandamus.


OUTCOME: The court denied petitioner's application for a writ of mandamus asking the court to compel respondent Commissioner of Motor Vehicles to issue a renewal driver's license to petitioner after respondent failed to renew petitioner's driver's license. The court held that petitioner did not show a clear legal right to the remedy sought.


HEADNOTES

Proceeding against Body or Officer - Mandamus - Issuance of Driver's License Absent Applicant's Social Security Number

1. Mandamus does not lie to compel the Department of Motor Vehicles (DMV) to issue a driver's license to an applicant who refuses, on privacy grounds, to supply his Social Security number and who alleges that he is a noncommercial driver and is not liable for child support. Petitioner has not established a clear right to relief and is explicitly asking that the Commissioner of DMV perform a discretionary act that is not premised on any specific authority. Vehicle and Traffic Law § 502 was amended to require all applicants for driver's licenses and renewal licenses to include their Social Security numbers on their application, and the Legislature did not include any exceptions. The Commissioner, therefore, is mandated to enforce the statutory provisions as written and cannot be compelled to make any exceptions. The fact that petitioner is a noncommercial driver and has no children and no child or marital support obligations is irrelevant. To the extent that petitioner may seek to challenge the constitutionality of section 502, a CPLR article 78 proceeding is not the proper method of testing the general constitutionality of a statute. The Commissioner's refusal to process petitioner's license renewal application, based upon petitioner's refusal to comply with the provisions of section 502, does not constitute a denial of due process.

Motor Vehicles - Driver's License - Refusal of Applicant to Provide Social Security Number - Federal Privacy Claim

2. An applicant for a driver's license who refused on privacy grounds to provide his Social Security number, and was therefore denied a license renewal, has no claim under the Federal Privacy Act of 1974 (see generally, 5 USC § 552a). Although the protection afforded by the Act is broad, its civil remedy provision is far less expansive and does not apply against State agencies or State or local officials. Federal law provides that a State may, in the administration of any driver's license law, utilize Social Security numbers for identification purposes and may require disclosure as a condition of obtaining a driver's license. The personal identifying information that is regulated by Federal law is a thing in interstate commerce; the sale or release of that information in interstate commerce is therefore the proper subject of congressional regulation; and Congress in enacting the Driver's Privacy Protection Act, which regulates the disclosure of personal information contained in the records of State motor vehicle departments, did not run afoul of the principles of Federalism contained in the Constitution. Petitioner's concern regarding the sharing of identifying information, including Social Security numbers, as permitted under the Driver's Privacy Protection Act by the Department of Motor Vehicles, does not establish any right to a renewal license, in the absence of furnishing his Social Security number, as required by Vehicle and Traffic Law § 502.

Motor Vehicles - Driver's License - Refusal of Applicant to Provide Social Security Number - Right to Interstate Travel

3. The denial of a driver's license to an applicant who refused on privacy grounds to provide his Social Security number, and who alleged that he is a noncommercial driver who cannot be liable for child support, did not deprive the applicant of his right to interstate travel. The applicant does not have a fundamental right to drive a motor vehicle, and the Department of Motor Vehicles did not unconstitutionally impede his right to interstate travel by denying him a driver's license.

Coleman v. O'Shea


INDEX NO.: 30380-1997


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NASSAU COUNTY


184 Misc. 2d 238; 707 N.Y.S.2d 308; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 123



March 27, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Motion granted allowing News 12 Long Island's audio/video coverage beginning with opening statements and following the established restriction on coverage.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Intervenor news station applied for an order seeking permission to conduct televised coverage of a civil trial.


OVERVIEW: Intervenor news station applied for an order seeking permission to conduct gavel to gavel audio/visual coverage of a civil trial. The court found that it had to determine whether N.Y. Civ. Rights Law § 52, which prohibited televising proceedings in which the testimony of witnesses by subpoena could be taken, required them to deny the application. The court found that N.Y. Civ. Rights Law § 52, which made no provision for consent of those who testified by compulsion, was unconstitutional as being arbitrary and capricious by denying free speech in violation of the U.S. Const. amend. I and the N.Y. Constitution, art. I, § 8. The court additionally determined that N.Y. Civ. Rights Law § 52 had no application to the civil claim at issue, because the mere possibility of compulsory appearance was insufficient to withhold the broadcast.


OUTCOME: Motion granted; the statute which prohibited televising proceedings in which the testimony of witnesses by subpoena could be taken, was unconstitutional as being arbitrary and capricious by denying free speech.


HEADNOTES

Courts - Audio-Visual Coverage of Judicial Proceedings

 Civil Rights Law § 52, which prohibits the televising of judicial proceedings, is unconstitutional. The ban prohibits televising proceedings in which witnesses may be compelled to testify, but no provision is made for consent of those who testify by compulsion. On that basis alone, section 52 is unconstitutional as being arbitrary and capricious by denying constitutional free speech protections. The initial adoption of section 52 was unconstitutional. Further, the failure to include safeguards to ameliorate the effect of denying coverage to a segment of the press in the face of consent, by those required to participate in the proceeding, violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Additionally, section 52 has no application in the instant civil claim because the mere possibility of compulsory appearance is insufficient to withhold the broadcast. A determination must be made whether the issuance of a judicial subpoena will be withdrawn by its applicant-intervenor or struck by the court. The word "may," when addressing compulsory process, is itself sufficiently indefinite as to be arbitrary and capricious. The public need for the telecast of the proceedings is clear: as many as 1.3 million people may be directly or indirectly affected by the outcome of this trial, and confining the courtroom to its seating capacity will not foster this need.

In re Certificate of Relief from Civil Disabilities ex rel. C.P. Ward, Inc.


20-171


COUNTY COURT OF NEW YORK, MONROE COUNTY


184 Misc. 2d 57; 707 N.Y.S.2d 605; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 99



March 15, 2000, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant corporation sought a certificate of relief from civil disabilities under the New York State Corrections Law after its conviction in federal court for violating the Sherman Antitrust Act.


OVERVIEW: Defendant corporation pleaded guilty to a violation of the Sherman Antitrust Act wherein it admitted corporate responsibility for conduct of its agents in rigging of various bids for utility construction with another corporation, and was sentenced accordingly. Defendant used certain explosives and magazines to mine sand and gravel and for excavation and construction projects. These explosives were an integral part of defendant's business. The Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms had taken the position that the felony conviction prohibited possession by defendant of such explosives and detonating devices. Defendant sought a certificate of relief from civil disabilities under the New York State Correction Law rather than federal law because the provisions in the federal law were not available to them. The court dismissed defendant's application because it found that it did not have the authority to the grant the application under N.Y. Correct. Law § 702 despite defendant's equal protection challenges.


OUTCOME: Application for a certificate of relief from civil disabilities denied because, despite defendant's equal protection arguments, the court did not have the authority to grant the application under the New York State Correction Law.


HEADNOTES

Prisons and Prisoners - Certificate of Relief from Disabilities - Availability to Defendant Convicted in Federal Court

County Court does not have the authority to issue a certificate of relief from civil disabilities pursuant to Correction Law § 702 (1) to a corporate defendant convicted in Federal court and prohibited from using the explosives that are an integral part of its business because of the conviction. The plain language of the statute, which states that "[a]ny court of this state may, in its discretion, issue a certificate of relief from disabilities to an eligible offender for a conviction that occurred in such court, if the court ... imposed a revocable sentence," limits County Court's ability to provide such relief. The statute does not deny equal protection of the law to a citizen whose conviction is obtained in Federal court, because the remedy can be obtained through an application to the Board of Parole for "any eligible offender who resides within this state and whose judgment of conviction was rendered by a court in any other jurisdiction" (Correction Law § 703 [1] [b]). Moreover, State courts should not be required to accept applications for certificates for relief in connection with Federal convictions and act upon them without the type of information that would be available to Federal authorities just because Federal certificates of relief are unavailable due to a Federal regulation which expressly excludes funding and appropriations for the processing of such certificates. A challenge to the authority which promulgated the regulations prohibiting the funding of such activity would be the more appropriate remedy.

State v. Monoco Oil Co.


Index No. 99/10268


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, MONROE COUNTY


185 Misc. 2d 742; 713 N.Y.S.2d 440; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 357



February 25, 2000, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff State and plaintiff New York State Department of Environmental Conservation sought preliminary injunction in public nuisance suit against defendants to restrain defendants from accepting, receiving, or storing asphalt emulsion or other asphalt product or derivative at its plant.


OVERVIEW: The matter was before the court on an order to show cause brought by plaintiff State of New York and plaintiff New York State Department of Environmental Conservation. The State alleged a public nuisance and sought a preliminary injunction enjoining the defendants from accepting, receiving, or storing any additional asphalt, asphalt emulsion, or other asphalt product or derivative. The issue presented was whether the nuisance could be reliably and effectively abated by the defendants through the installation of equipment which they sought to employ or whether the grant of the preliminary injunction was the only effective remedy. Municipal Corporations - Zoning - Operation of Liquid Asphalt Storage Facility

1. In a public nuisance action involving a liquid asphalt storage facility based, inter alia, upon defendants' alleged violation of intervenor Town's commercial zoning ordinance, the mere conversion of the subject facility from a fuel oil to an asphalt storage facility is not violative of that portion of the zoning ordinance that allows a premises to be used for "[r]eceiving, storing, repackaging and selling at wholesale completely manufactured, assembled, processed and finished products." The additional step of heating the asphalt material at the facility does not alter the wholesale nature of the facility's operation. The asphalt comes to the plant in heated rail cars or tankers and is transferred to storage tanks where it continues to be heated. It is then transferred to the tanker truck for consumption in a heated state. Under these circumstances, the heating of the asphalt is not processing, but merely a necessary condition for handling of the material. Moreover, the parties have stipulated that the fuel oil was also subject to a heating process.

Constitutional Law - Validity of Statute - Town Odor Ordinance

2. In a public nuisance action involving a liquid asphalt storage facility based, inter alia, upon defendants' alleged violation of intervenor Town's commercial zoning ordinance, that portion of the ordinance which allows a premises to be used only for the storage of products that are not "unreasonably odorous ... and the handling of which will not disseminate noise, smoke vapor or any substance detrimental to the health, well-being and general safety of the community," is not unconstitutionally vague. Although there is no objective standard by which it can be determined whether something is "unreasonably odorous," when linked with the language in the ordinance prohibiting the emission of substances that are detrimental to the health, well-being and general safety of the community, the Town's ordinance is sufficiently "informative on its face" to be constitutional under the void-for-vagueness doctrine.

Nuisance - Public Nuisance - Emission of Noxious Odors - Consideration of Potential Economic Impacts of Preliminary Injunction

3. In a public nuisance action involving a liquid asphalt storage facility in which the State of New York and the intervenor Town seek a preliminary injunction enjoining defendants from accepting, receiving or storing any additional asphalt, asphalt emulsion or other asphalt product or derivative at the plant, and compelling defendants to lawfully remove any such material presently at the plant within 30 days, plaintiffs have sufficiently demonstrated that the emission of fumes from the facility has created a public nuisance and are entitled to a preliminary injunction. The conduct of defendants in allowing the continued emission of noxious odors and fumes from the facility has caused deleterious health effects and discomfort to a considerable number of persons in the vicinity of the facility, and has caused the public irreparable harm. Furthermore, in fashioning a remedy in a public nuisance action, the court need not address the economic impacts of the preliminary injunction. In the context of a public nuisance lawsuit, the rule in New York is that the nuisance will be enjoined despite a marked disparity between the effect of the injunction and the effect of the nuisance. In this case, defendants have failed to demonstrate that their proposed remedial measures will effectively rectify the problem of noxious odors and fumes.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant State moved for summary judgment alleging that claimant employees' recovery of overtime under Fair Labor Standards Act, 29 U.S.C.S. § 201 et seq., was precluded for claims accruing prior to limitation period of Court of Claims Act, and that claimants could not claim liquidated damages or attorneys' fees.


OVERVIEW: Claimant employees sought to recover overtime compensation under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), 29 U.S.C.S. § 201 et seq., plus liquidated damages and attorney fees, and defendant state alleged that the Court of Claims Act (Act) precluded claims arising more than 180 days prior to filing the claims. The court held that, although the Act waived defendant's sovereign immunity to suit under FLSA, defendant was entitled to condition its waiver upon compliance with the Act's time limitation. The FLSA did not override defendant's immunity and, pursuant to defendant's conditional waiver, the court lacked jurisdiction to consider claims accruing prior to the limitation period. Also, because defendant's waiver placed it in the position of a private employer, liquidated damages under FLSA were available to claimants, and the Act's prohibition on attorneys' fees awards did not apply because the prohibition was not a limitation of the immunity waived by defendant.


OUTCOME: Summary judgment was granted in part because defendant State's waiver of sovereign immunity was properly limited to timely claims and thus precluded claimant employees' overtime claims accruing prior to the limitation period, but denied in part because neither liquidated damages nor attorneys' fees were proscribed by the waiver.


HEADNOTES

State - Claim against State - Fair Labor Standards Act - Applicability of Court of Claims Act Six-Month Statute of Limitations

1. A claim against the State seeking unpaid overtime compensation, liquidated damages and attorneys' fees pursuant to the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 USC § 201 [FLSA]) is subject to the six-month Statute of Limitations contained in Court of Claims Act § 10 (4). The State's waiver of its sovereign immunity, which extends to claims for wages under the FLSA, is conditioned upon compliance "with the limitations of this article" (Court of Claims Act § 8). Thus, the State's waiver of immunity is expressly conditioned upon meeting the jurisdictional limitations contained in Court of Claims Act article II, including the limitations in section 10 as to timely service and filing of a claim.

State - Claim against State - Fair Labor Standards Act - Availability of Liquidated Damages

2. In a claim brought by State employees seeking to recover unpaid overtime compensation pursuant to the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 USC § 201 [FLSA]), claimants' request for liquidated damages is proper and will not be dismissed. Liquidated damages under the FLSA may be recovered against a private employer. They constitute compensation, not a penalty or punitive damages, and except as otherwise limited by its express terms, Court of Claims Act § 8 subjects the State to liability in the same manner as a private person or corporation.

State - Claim against State - Fair Labor Standards Act - Availability of Attorneys' Fees

3. In a claim brought by State employees seeking to recover unpaid overtime compensation pursuant to the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 USC § 201 [FLSA]), claimants' request for attorneys' fees is proper and will not be dismissed. The State, having waived its sovereign immunity with regard to FLSA actions except as otherwise conditioned in Court of Claims Act article II, is subject to the provisions of the FLSA authorizing the recovery of counsel fees. The proscription against the award of attorneys' fees contained in Court of Claims Act § 27 is inapplicable, because section 27 is contained in article III, not article II of the Court of Claims Act.

HEADNOTES

Crimes - Sentence - Constitutionality of Death Penalty Sentencing Provision Requiring Timely Service of Notice of Intent to Offer Psychiatric Evidence at Penalty Phase

1. CPL 400.27 (13) (b), which provides that when either party intends to offer psychiatric evidence at the sentencing phase of a capital case the party must, within a reasonable time prior to trial, serve upon the other a written notice of intent to present psychiatric evidence, is not unconstitutional. A statute is presumed valid, and invalidity must be demonstrated beyond a reasonable doubt. Here, the defense offers no precedent or argument that meets the heavy burden required. There is no real showing that the provision drives a wedge between an attorney and client. 2. In a capital murder case, defense counsel's request to defer service of a notice of intent to offer psychiatric evidence at the penalty phase of the trial, until completion of the guilt-innocence phase, is denied. Under CPL 400.27 (13) (b), a party "must" serve the notice within a "reasonable time prior to trial." The language of the statute is mandatory. Requiring the notice, and the statement specifying the witnesses and the nature and type of psychiatric evidence sought to be introduced, does not implicate defendant's right to remain silent or his right to counsel. The notice requirements allow the People to prepare their case in a timely manner (a fair and orderly way) and to anticipate and plan for possible psychiatric mitigation issues, while eliminating the need to assemble and prepare potential assets and resources for mitigation issues that will not be raised. The notice also furthers the court's ability to comply with CPL 400.27 (1), which requires the court to promptly conduct the sentencing proceeding upon conviction.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff race track sued defendant state for declaratory relief that N.Y. Rac. Pari-Mut. Wag. & Breed. Law §318 (4) was unconstitutional, and moved for summary judgment.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff operated a racetrack and featured off-track pari-mutuel betting. Defendant State amended N.Y. Rac. Pari-Mut. Wag. & Breed. Law §318 (4) to allow it to tax earnings of plaintiff on its out-of-state simulcast transmissions to Connecticut, and plaintiff had paid about $ 2,500,000 in taxes under protest. Plaintiff argued the tax was unconstitutional because: (1) it discriminated against interstate commerce, (2) was barred by federal preemption, (3) violated the equal protection clauses of the federal and state constitutions and (4) improperly attempted to tax events outside New York State. The court granted relief on the first ground. A valid state tax affecting interstate commerce must: (1) have a sufficient nexus to activity being taxed in state, (2) be fairly apportioned, (3) not discriminate against interstate commerce; and (4) be fairly related to the benefits provided. The tax imposed was not compensatory, but directly burdened interstate commerce and was declared unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: Relief was granted because the tax was discriminatory and not compensatory and violates the commerce clause of the federal constitution. It was declared unconstitutional and defendant state was enjoined from further collections thereunder.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law - Validity of Statute - Tax Imposed on Harness Racing Fees

1. Racing, Pari-Mutuel Wagering and Breeding Law § 318 (4), which imposes a tax on harness racing fees received by plaintiff Yonkers Racing Corporation for simulcasting its races to Connecticut off-track betting operators, is not unconstitutional as an improper tax of events outside New York State, since the tax applies to an event originating in New York, not something beyond the State's borders.

Constitutional Law - Equal Protection of Laws - Tax Imposed on Harness Racing Fees

2. Racing, Pari-Mutuel Wagering and Breeding Law § 318 (4), which imposes a tax on harness racing fees received by plaintiff Yonkers Racing Corporation for simulcasting its races to Connecticut off-track betting operators, does not violate the Equal Protection Clause, notwithstanding that it applies only to plaintiff.

Statutes - Federal Preemption - Tax Imposed on Harness Racing Fees

3. Racing, Pari-Mutuel Wagering and Breeding Law § 318 (4), which imposes a tax on harness racing fees received by plaintiff Yonkers Racing Corporation for simulcasting its races to Connecticut off-track betting operators, is not preempted by the Interstate Horseracing Act of 1978 (15 USC § 3001 et seq.), which was enacted to ensure that the States cooperate with one another in the acceptance of legal interstate wagers on horse races. There is no preemption, as Congress has not fully legislated on the subject, nor is there any conflict between the tax imposed and the policy set forth in the Federal statute. Moreover, the contract here is with an agency of the State of Connecticut, and there is no proof of any violation or undue burden with regard to the Interstate Horseracing Act.

Constitutional Law - Commerce Clause - Tax Imposed on Harness Racing Fees

4. Racing, Pari-Mutuel Wagering and Breeding Law § 318 (4), which imposes a tax on harness racing fees received by plaintiff Yonkers Racing Corporation for simulcasting its races to Connecticut off-track betting operators, is an unconstitutional burden on interstate commerce. To be valid, a State tax affecting interstate commerce must have a sufficient nexus to the activity being taxed in the State, be fairly apportioned, not discriminate against interstate commerce, and be fairly related to the benefits provided. A facially discriminatory tax may still survive Commerce Clause scrutiny if it is a truly compensating tax designed simply to make interstate commerce bear a burden already borne by intrastate commerce. To sustain a discriminatory tax, the State must identify the intrastate burden it is attempting to compensate for, show that the tax on interstate commerce is roughly approximate to the tax on intrastate commerce, and show that the events taxed are substantially equivalent. The State has failed to demonstrate any roughly equivalent tax on intrastate simulcasting of horse races to in-State off-track betting locations. Therefore, the tax is discriminatory, and not compensatory, and violates the Commerce Clause.

People v. Rogers


Docket # 25014


CITY COURT OF NEW YORK, WATERTOWN


183 Misc. 2d 538; 703 N.Y.S.2d 891; 2000 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 26



January 12, 2000, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [***1] Defendant's motion to dismiss granted.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed a motion to dismiss the State's claim that he violated the N.Y. Agric. & Mkts. Law §353 when he docked his puppy's tail himself using a rubber band to accomplish the result.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued N.Y. Agric. & Mkts. Law §353 was unconstitutionally vague because a person of common intelligence could not ascertain what conduct was proscribed by the statute. Defendant stated he thought it was all right to dock his puppy's tail himself. Unfortunately, the puppy had to be euthanized due to the condition of its tail. Defendant's motion to dismiss the State's lawsuit was granted. The court held docking a dog's tail did not require professional supervision, and appellant was only required to avoid "unjustifiable physical pain" and "suffering," pursuant to N.Y. Agric. & Mkts. Law §350(2), when engaging in such conduct. The statutory words "unjustifiably" and "unjustifiable" as applied to the conduct of defendant were in violation of the Due Process Clause due to vagueness because a reasonable person in defendant's circumstances would not have notice his conduct placed him at risk of violating the law.


OUTCOME: Defendant's motion to dismiss was granted; docking a dog's tail without the assistance of a veterinarian was not proscribed by statute. Applicable statute was constitutionally vague as applied to defendant and the act of docking a dog's tail was not conduct calculated to cause harm but, rather, essentially innocent conduct.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law - Validity of Statute - Vagueness of Agriculture and Markets Law §§ 353 and 350 (2) 

 Agriculture and Markets Law § 353, which makes it a misdemeanor to unjustifiably torture, injure, maim or kill any animal, and section 350 (2), which defines torture or cruelty as every act, omission or neglect whereby unjustifiable physical pain, suffering or death is caused or permitted, are unconstitutionally vague as applied to defendant who used a rubber band to dock his puppy's tail, which became infected and resulted in euthanization of the puppy. Vagueness challenges to statutes which do not involve First Amendment freedoms are examined in light of the facts of the case. The statute must provide sufficient notice to a person of ordinary intelligence of what conduct is prohibited. The Agriculture and Markets Law permits justifiable maiming or mutilation such as clipping a dog's ears. Ear clipping and tail docking are common practices, and docking a tail is not implicitly proscribed by statute. However, the statute prescribes that the ear clipping procedure be done by a veterinarian, but no procedure is prescribed for tail docking. Thus, an ordinary person of common intelligence is only required to avoid "unjustifiable physical pain" and "suffering" (Agriculture and Markets Law § 350 [2]) when engaging in such conduct. Moreover, the prefix modifier "un" before the term "justified" offers no guidance because the term "justified" is undefined. This generalized law fails to distinguish between conduct calculated to cause harm and conduct that is essentially innocent, thereby failing to give adequate notice of what conduct is prohibited.
