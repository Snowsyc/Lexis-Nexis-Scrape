PRIOR HISTORY:   [**1] Certiorari to the Colorado Court of Appeals. Court of Appeals No. 97CA1996.



DISPOSITION: JUDGMENT AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners sued respondent, alleging that a sanitation permit fee imposed on their townhouse development project was an taking in violation of U.S. Const. amend. V. The District Court of Summit County (Colorado) denied the claim, and the Court of Appeals affirmed. Petitioners sought review.


OVERVIEW: Petitioners, developers of a townhouse project, sued respondent sanitation district, contending that respondent's "plant investment fee" (PIF) assessment for their project was an unconstitutional taking. The trial court held that the constitutional takings analysis was inapplicable, and that even if it were, respondent satisfied the test because the PIF was roughly proportional to the impact of the project on the district facilities. The court of appeals affirmed, and the supreme court granted certiorari review. The supreme court also affirmed, holding that the PIF was a valid, legislatively established fee that was reasonably related to respondent district's interest in expanding its infrastructure to account for new development, and that respondent's specific PIF assessment on petitioners' project was fairly calculated and rationally based. As such, the PIF did not fall into the narrow category of charges that were subject to a Nollan/Dolan takings analysis.


OUTCOME: Judgments of the court of appeals and trial court affirmed. The plant investment fee was not imposed adjudicatively in the Nollan/Dolan sense, and was not an assessment unique to petitioners. The fee was neither a land use regulation nor an exaction of property as a condition of development. Consequently, it was not subject to a constitutional takings analysis.

DISPOSITION: REVERSED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Case challenging creation of lab school at juvenile corrections facility was granted certiorari to state supreme court, after appellate court reversed administrative law judge's decision upholding reorganization agreement and related teacher transfers, to address whether transfers violated existing law.


OVERVIEW: A reorganization leading to creation of a lab school at a juvenile facility included transferring facility teachers to other positions in petitioner Department of Human Services (DHS) or to positions exempt from the state personnel system. Respondent teachers sued petitioner agencies, contending the transfers and authorizing agreement violated the Civil Service Amendment to the state constitution and state statutes. Although an administrative law judge upheld the agreement and the transfers, the court of appeals reversed. The court granted certiorari to address whether the transfers violated existing law and whether DHS had statutory authority to execute the contract with the college to run the school. It reversed, holding the agreement and exemption of lab school positions from the state personnel system were constitutional, because the instructors were college employees exempt from the personnel system. It concluded respondent teachers were not improperly transferred, as they retained full pay, status, and tenure.


OUTCOME: Court reversed, holding agreement and exemption of lab school positions from state personnel system was constitutional because new instructors were effectively employees of state college and thus exempt from state personnel system. Complainant teachers were not improperly transferred within civil service system, as they retained full pay, status, and tenure.
