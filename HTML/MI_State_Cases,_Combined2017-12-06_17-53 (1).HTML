CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, pumping company, filed a complaint against defendant, township, alleging exclusionary zoning of land application sites, exclusionary zoning of septage storage tanks, and violations of due process and equal protection. Both parties filed motions for summary disposition. The Leelanau Circuit Court, Michigan, granted summary disposition in favor of the township and the company appealed.


OVERVIEW: Part of the company's business involved applying septage to land application sites in the township. The township enacted a zoning ordinance regulating land application of septage in the township. Also, the four bases offered by the township supporting the ordinance were valid and justifiable.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff, city, initiated an action for declaratory relief, seeking a declaration that MCL 247.183, as amended by 2005 PA 103, was unconstitutional. The Ingham Circuit Court, Michigan, granted summary disposition in favor of defendants, company and State of Michigan, and denied the cross-motion for summary disposition filed by the city. The city appealed.


OVERVIEW: The company sought and received approval from the Michigan Public Service Commission authorization to replace an existing liquid-petroleum pipeline with a new larger diameter pipeline. 2005 PA 103 amended MCL 247.183 to remove the consent requirement. The city argued that MCL 247.183, as amended by 2005 PA 103, purported to permit the company to construct the pipeline using the city's streets without obtaining the city's consent required by Const. 1963, art VII, § 29. The appellate court ruled that MCL 247.183, as amended by 2005 PA 103, was not unconstitutional. MCL 247.183(2) merely limited a local governments authority to grant or withhold consent to the use of a narrow class of public property by a specific type of utility, and it was a proper exercise of the Michigan Legislature's authority to limit the manner and circumstances under which a city could grant or withhold consent under Const. 1963, art VII, § 29. Under its plain language, MCL 247.183(2) permitted a qualified utility to act without consent of any kind from a local government.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent child challenged a decision from the Calhoun Probate Court (Michigan), which granted summary disposition in favor of petitioner widow in a dispute over dower.


OVERVIEW: When her husband died, the widow sought to take her dower interest under MCL 70.2202(2)(c) because the will left her nothing. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Pursuant to the Michigan Customer Choice and Electricity Reliability Act, Mich. Comp. Laws § 460.10 et seq., appellee Michigan Public Service Commission (PSC) adopted administrative rules governing service quality and reliability standards for electric distribution systems, and authorizing automatic penalties for the failure to meet those standards. Appellant utilities and utility associations filed suits challenging the rules.


OVERVIEW: Appellants argued that PSC lacked the statutory authority to impose automatic penalties for a utility's failure to meet performance standards. The court disagreed. Mich. Comp. Laws § 460.10p(5) required the PSC to adopt performance standards, and § 460.10p(8) authorized it to levy penalties on a utility that failed to meet those standards. As the imposition of automatic penalties was not left to the PSC's unfettered discretion, its enactment of the rules providing for penalties was not an unconstitutional assumption of legislative power. The PSC's interpretation and application of § 460.10p(8) was reasonable, and was thus entitled to deference.


OUTCOME: The PSC's orders were affirmed.
