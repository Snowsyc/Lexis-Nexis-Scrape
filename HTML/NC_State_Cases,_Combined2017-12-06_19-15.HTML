CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs appealed the orders of the Guilford County Superior Court (North Carolina), granting defendants' motions to dismiss plaintiffs' claims for violations of state constitutional rights and wrongful death.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs, decedent's estate and wife, filed a federal complaint against defendants, state highway patrol and state officials, alleging violation of decedent's federal constitutional rights and related state claims. Pendergraph v. N.C. Dep't of Revenue


09 CVS 19607


NORTH CAROLINA SUPERIOR COURT, WAKE COUNTY


2011 NCBC 8; 2011 NCBC LEXIS 3



March 8, 2011, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Subsequent appeal at Pendergraph v. N.C. Dep't of Revenue, 2012 N.C. App. LEXIS 272 (N.C. Ct. App., Feb. 21, 2012)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, two classes of members of state retirement systems, filed an action challenging the constitutionality of N.C. Gen. Stat. § 105-134.6(b)(6), which subjected their retirement benefits to state income tax. Defendants, state agencies and officers, filed a N.C. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss the class action for failure to state a claim upon which relief could be granted and a petition for judicial review.


OVERVIEW: Defendants argued that plaintiffs' complaint should be dismissed because plaintiffs were not vested in either a local governmental employees' retirement system or a teachers' and state employees' retirement system when the General Assembly enacted § 105-134.6(b)(6). Plaintiffs argued that a contractual relationship was formed between themselves and the State when they began their employment. Thus, the State's post-hiring attempt to change the taxability terms of their retirement benefits was an unconstitutional impairment of contract and deprivation of property without due process. Upon review, the court followed established precedent and concluded that the contract between plaintiffs and the State did not form until they became vested in their retirement benefits. Thus, they did not have a contractual right to rely on the terms of their retirement plans prior to their vesting dates. Plaintiffs failed to state a claim for violation of the Contract Clause of the U.S. Constitution. As there was no contractual obligation, they failed to state claims for due process violations under the federal and state constitutions.


OUTCOME: The court granted defendants' motion to dismiss the class action complaint and petition for judicial review. The court dismissed the action.

Sunbelt Rentals, Inc. v. Head & Engquist Equip., L.L.C.


00-CVS-10358


NORTH CAROLINA SUPERIOR COURT, MECKLENBURG COUNTY


2003 NCBC 6; 2003 NCBC LEXIS 9



July 31, 2003, Decided

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff local corporation commenced an action against defendants, a foreign corporation and its employee, for violations of North Carolina's Unfair and Deceptive Trade Practices Act (UDTPA) and the Trade Secrets Protection Act (TSPA). The court found that defendants had violated both; thus, the local corporation filed a motion for attorneys' fees pursuant to N.C. Gen. Stat. §§ 75-16.1 and 66-154.

First Union Corp. v. Suntrust Banks, Inc.


No. 01-CVS-10075


NORTH CAROLINA SUPERIOR COURT, MECKLENBURG COUNTY


2001 NCBC 7; 2001 NCBC LEXIS 4



June 26, 2001, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Injunction denied by First Union Corp. v. Suntrust Banks, Inc., 2001 NCBC LEXIS 6 (2001)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff banks filed a motion to dismiss the counterclaim of defendant bank that the use of the non-termination and option provisions in the merger between plaintiffs violated O.C.G.A. 13-8-2, the Constitution of the State of Georgia, and Georgia common law.


OVERVIEW: Defendant's counterclaim against plaintiffs alleged that the non-termination and option provisions of the merger agreement between plaintiffs constituted a breach on the part of one bank's board of directors of the fiduciary duty owed its shareholders and that the other bank aided and abetted the breaches of fiduciary duty and that the provisions restrained trade. Plaintiffs sought to dismiss the counterclaim. The court held that North Carolina law applied to the merger because North Carolina had a significant relationship because plaintiffs were headquartered in North Carolina. Additionally, any injury defendant suffered as a shareholder of one bank was by virtue of the fiduciary agreement, which was governed by North Carolina law. Under the applicable North Carolina Unfair and Deceptive Trade Practices Act, defendant did not have a cause of action because N.C.G.S. ch. 75 did not apply to the sale of securities. Therefore, defendant could not challenge the merger on antitrust grounds under North Carolina law.


OUTCOME: The court granted plaintiffs' motion to dismiss.
