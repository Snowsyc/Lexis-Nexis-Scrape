CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, the Secretary of the California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation (CDCR), appealed from orders of the San Diego County Superior Court, California, enjoining CDCR from enforcing the residency restriction of "Jessica's Law," codified in Pen. Code, § 3003.5, subd. (b), on the ground that the blanket restriction was unconstitutional as a parole condition as applied to registered sex offenders on parole in San Diego County.


OVERVIEW: The court found the blanket residency restriction, as applied in San Diego County, excessive and unduly broad in relation to its purpose - namely, to establish predator free zones around schools and parks where children gather. The statute limited the housing choices of all sex offenders identically, without regard to the type of victim or the risk of reoffending. The blanket restriction effectively barred sex offender parolees from living in about 97 percent of the existing multifamily rental property that otherwise would be available to them, including in affordable housing located in downtown San Diego where the record showed they were more apt to receive other needed and vital services. The blanket restriction exceeded the scope of its stated objective - the protection of children - because as applied it eliminated nearly all existing affordable housing in the county for sex offender parolees, in essence banishing them from living within most if not all of the county, and because it treated all parolees the same regardless of whether the parolee's crime involved the victimization of children or adults (and thus the need for the residency restriction in the first place).


OUTCOME: The orders were affirmed.

Superior Court of Contra Costa County, No. J0801378, Lois Haight, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant juvenile, a serial sex offender, appealed an order of the Contra Costa County Superior Court, California, committing him to the California Division of Juvenile Facilities (DJF) after several failed alternative dispositions. At the time he was adjudicated a ward of the court, the juvenile was not eligible for a DJF commitment, and he was placed there under an amendment to Welf. & Inst. Code, § 731, enacted after his adjudication.


OVERVIEW: The court held that the amendment to Welf. & Inst. Code, § 731, was intended to be retroactive. The juvenile's criminal behavior took place before the enactment of the amendment, and those statutory changes were being applied to alter the legal consequences of his violation of Pen. Code, § 288, subd. (a) by making him eligible for a DJF commitment. The court concluded that application of the amendment to § 731 to permit commitment of wards like the juvenile in the DJF was not an ex post facto violation because there was nothing so punitive in the statute's purpose or effect in the juvenile's case that would warrant the court's disregard of the legislature's express intent in enacting it. The amendment did not affix culpability or seek retribution for criminal conduct. The juvenile had suffered a loss of liberty and the stigma of adjudication long before his commitment to DJF, and his confinement was not prolonged by § 731.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the dispositional order.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant probationer sought review of a judgment from the Superior Court of San Diego County (California), which revoked his probation for attempted possession of a controlled substance for sale.


OVERVIEW: The probationer was ordered to complete a drug court treatment program as a condition of probation. After he absconded from the program, the drug court summarily revoked his probation and issued a bench warrant for his arrest. Thereafter, the drug court held a hearing and revoked probation. At a subsequent evidentiary hearing, the probationer testified in mitigation. The court held that the use of an unsworn warrant to arrest the probationer pursuant to Pen. Any inadequacy of notice at the first hearing was not prejudicial because the probationer knew the nature of the alleged violation; moreover, the second hearing was adequate.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The federal Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals requested an answer to the following question: "Whether, under California law, the default rule of contract interpretation is (a) that the law in effect at the time of a plea agreement binds the parties, or (b) that the terms of a plea agreement may be affected by changes in law."


OVERVIEW: The court slightly rephrased the question as: "Under California law of contract interpretation as applicable to the interpretation of plea agreements, does the law in effect at the time of a plea agreement bind the parties or can the terms of a plea agreement be affected by changes in the law?" The court observed that the general rule in California was that a plea agreement was deemed to incorporate and contemplate not only the existing law but the reserve power of the State to amend the law or enact additional laws for the public good and in pursuance of public policy. The court found that it followed, also as a general rule, that requiring the parties' compliance with changes in the law made retroactive to them did not violate the terms of the plea agreement, nor did the failure of a plea agreement to reference the possibility the law might change translate into an implied promise the defendant would be unaffected by a change in the statutory consequences attending his or her conviction. To that extent, then, the terms of the plea agreement could be affected by changes in the law.


OUTCOME: The court accepted the request and provided a response to the question.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant union sought review of orders from the Superior Court of Los Angeles County (California), which, in a dispute concerning health benefits for county employees who were union members, denied mandate relief against respondent county agencies as to claims of failure to bargain in good faith and management interference.


OVERVIEW: During negotiations with the union, the county adhered to a firm policy enforcing an ordinance, Los Angeles County, Cal., Ord. No. 5.27.020(K). (L), which provided that only unrepresented employees could participate in two health benefit programs. A supervisor circulated a letter suggesting that employees could save their benefits by means of decertification. County management instructed him to refrain from any further decertification efforts. When negotiations concluded without agreement on the health benefits issue, the county unilaterally denied participation in the two plans to union members. The court held that the county's exclusion of union members from health benefit plans that were available to unrepresented county employees violated Cal. Gov't Code § 3504.5(c), which prohibited local authorities from providing different health benefits to unionized employees than to non-unionized employees unless the union agreed. Although enacted after the negotiations took place, § 3504.5(c) was retroactive to a date prior to the county's denial of participation to union members. The court concluded that the supervisor's letter did not significantly influence the negotiations.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the denial of mandate relief as to the claim of failure to bargain in good faith and remanded with instructions to grant relief and direct the reinstatement of the union members' benefits. The court affirmed the denial of mandate relief as to the claim of management interference.

PROCEEDING to review a decision of the Workers' Compensation Appeals Board, WCAB Case No. LAO 671427 (ADJ 1898212), WCAB Case No. LAO 688269 (ADJ 957820).



DISPOSITION: Annulled.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner hotel petitioned for review of a decision of the California Workers' Compensation Appeals Board, which found that respondent employee was entitled to vocational rehabilitation benefits pursuant to Lab. Code, former § 139.5, which was repealed effective January 1, 2009.


OVERVIEW: The court concluded that because the Board's decision was not a final determination of the employee's right to vocational rehabilitation benefits and because former § 139.5 had been repealed, the employee was not entitled to such benefits. There was no indication in any legislation passed in the same session in which former § 139.5 was repealed that the legislature intended to save rehabilitation rights or remedies from and after January 1, 2009, or indefinitely. There was no newly enacted statute, nor was the effective sunset date extended before January 1, 2009, or thereafter. The legislature, in effect, preserved or saved vocational rehabilitation claims for nearly five years, but did not save nonfinal rehabilitation rights as of or past January 1, 2009. Although the legislature provided for the possibility of a later statute that would delete or extend the January 1, 2009 date, no such statute was ever enacted. The legislature repealed not only former § 139.5, but also other statutes governing rehabilitation benefits and services, as well as the regulations to implement those benefits and services. Thus, there was no residual application of any repealed statute.


OUTCOME: The Board's decision was annulled.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, farm workers and their union, sought review of a judgment from the Superior Court of Los Angeles County (California), which dismissed their complaint for declaratory and injunctive relief against respondents, the State of California and two state agencies, alleging that a heat illness prevention regulation was inadequate.

CASE SUMMARY:

OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that the trial court's imposition of a $ 20 security fee upon him pursuant to Pen. Code, § 1465.8, violated ex post facto laws. Section 1465.8 did not become effective until two months after defendant's robbery was committed. The court held that the court security fee was valid. The fee could be imposed upon a defendant who committed his crime before the effective date of the statute without offending ex post facto principles. The legislature imposed the fee for a nonpunitive purpose, and the fee did not enhance defendant's punishment for conduct previously committed. Although the robbery offense was committed before the law was enacted, the proceedings for which the fee was collected took place almost exclusively after the effective date of the statute. Thus, the change in the law to collect a $ 20 fee for court security did not carry with it a propensity for unfairness disfavoring retroactive application. COURT OF APPEAL OF CALIFORNIA, SECOND APPELLATE DISTRICT, DIVISION TWO


165 Cal. App. 4th 109; 80 Cal. Rptr. 3d 326; 2008 Cal. App. LEXIS 1145; 66 U.C.C. Rep. Serv. 2d (Callaghan) 206



June 26, 2008, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Superior Court of Los Angeles County, No. BC368041, Ann I. Jones, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, an assignee of a lottery prize winner, challenged a judgment of the Superior Court of Los Angeles County, California, dismissing its action against respondent California State Lottery Commission. The assignee had sought declaratory relief, contending that the Commission was required by law to recognize and accommodate an assignment of the last three years of the prize winner's lottery winnings.


OVERVIEW: The prize winner had entered into a written agreement whereby she sold and assigned her right, title, and interest in the final three jackpot prize payments. The court held that the application of California law governing the interpretation of conflicting statutes compelled the conclusion that the provisions of the California State Lottery Act, Gov. Code, § 8880 et seq., prevailed over the conflicting provisions of Cal. U. Com. Code, § 9406, subd. (f). The legislature did not express a specific intent to override the Lottery Act. The California Uniform Commercial Code could and should be harmonized with Gov. Code, § 8880.325. Section 8880.325, subd. (b), did not permit the assignment at issue. The assignee's position that the assignment was an involuntary assignment, made "in the context" of a marital dispute, was belied by its own allegations and judicially noticed facts regarding another state's judgment. Regardless of whether § 8880.325, subd. (h)'s specific ban on assignment of the last three years of prize payments applied, the assignee had failed to raise any specific applicable exception to § 8880.325's general prohibition on assignment of lottery prizes.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment.

Petitioners sought declaration that statutory revisions to Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §6079.1(a) and §6086.65(a) violated separation of powers provision of Cal. Const. art. III, § 3.


OVERVIEW: Petitioners, previously appointed judges of the State Bar Court, contended the amendments to Cal. OUTCOME: Petition denied. OVERVIEW: Civ. Code, § 2527, required processors to obtain and transmit drug processing cost reports to its clients. In affirming the judgment for the processors, the court held that § 2527 and related enforcement provisions violated Cal. Const. art. I, § 2. The statute was properly classified as true compelled speech, and the fact that the speech concerned statistical information did not make it less entitled to First Amendment scrutiny. Civ. Proc., § 425.16, the anti-strategic lawsuit against public participation (anti-SLAPP) statute. The trial court had no choice but to grant an anti-SLAPP motion and to award fees to the processors.


OUTCOME: The judgment and order were affirmed.
