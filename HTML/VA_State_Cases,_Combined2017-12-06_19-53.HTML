Smith v. Commonwealth

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
FROM THE CIRCUIT COURT OF THE CITY OF RICHMOND. Clarence N. Jenkins, Jr., Judge.
Smith v. Commonwealth, 2012 Va. LEXIS 223 (Va., Dec. 12, 2012)



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Pursuant to a plea agreement, a sex offender had pled guilty to carnal knowledge of a minor. He later sued the Commonwealth, contending that the legislature's reclassification of this offense as "sexually violent offense" violated his contractual and constitutional rights. The Circuit Court of the City of Richmond, Virginia, granted summary judgment to the Commonwealth. The offender appealed.


OVERVIEW: When the offender was convicted, carnal knowledge of a minor was classified as a non-violent sex offense. After an amendment to Va. Code Ann § 9.1-902, his conviction was retroactively reclassified as a "sexually violent offense," and he became subject to more stringent registration requirements. He argued that his plea agreement was a contract that incorporated the sex offender registration laws in existence at the time of the agreement, and that the Commonwealth violated his rights under Va. Const. art. I, § 11 and Va. Code Ann. § 1-239 by amending the registration laws and retroactively enforcing them against him. The high court disagreed. The reclassification of the crime was an exercise of the state's regulatory police power and not a breach of contract. As he had no vested contractual rights with respect to the registration requirements in effect when he entered into his plea agreement, the subsequent reclassification of his conviction was not an unconstitutional taking under art. I, § 11. Nor was it a violation of procedural due process, as convicted sex offenders had no fundamental right to rely on the civil legislative scheme in existence at the time of pleading guilty.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant juvenile pleaded guilty to felony vandalism. His plea agreement expressly waived his statutory right of appeal to the circuit court. The juvenile filed an appeal, which the Circuit Court of Chesterfield County (Virginia). Juvenile appealed.


OVERVIEW: Juvenile argued, inter alia, he had a statutory right under Va. Code Ann. § 16.1-296(A) to an appeal from the juvenile court to the circuit court and as a matter of law, this right could not be waived. The court of appeals disagreed. It would be anomalous that the most venerable constitutional rights could be implicitly waived but this particular statutory right could not be expressly waived. Most courts were persuaded that because other important constitutional rights of the defendant could be waived by plea agreement, the right to appeal, which was not even guaranteed by the Constitution, but by statute, should also be subject to waiver. The circuit court found juvenile knowingly and intelligently waived his right of appeal under § 16.1-296(A) and properly dismissed the juvenile's appeal. The juvenile did not claim the prosecutor, the district court, or anyone else coerced him into making the agreement. Nor did he contend the agreement was in any way unconscionable. Instead, the juvenile merely asserted the circuit court should have declared the agreement unenforceable as a matter of law.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.
