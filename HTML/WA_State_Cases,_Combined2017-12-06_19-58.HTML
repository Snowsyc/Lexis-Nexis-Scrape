CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a judgment of the Spokane Superior Court (Washington) that convicted him of two counts of first-degree rape and one count of second-degree assault with sexual motivation, arguing that the trial court erred in admitting evidence of his prior rape conviction and that the assault should have merged into the rape since it was an element of the rape and had no independent purpose.


OVERVIEW: Approximately 14 months after being released from prison after being convicted of rape, defendant raped two women within days of each other, strangling each one in the commission of the sexual assault. PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from King County Superior Court. Docket No: 08-2-23083-0. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 04/24/2009. Judge signing: Honorable Michael J Fox.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant city approved a temporary use agreement (TUA) that permitted defendant church to use its property to host a homeless encampment. Plaintiff citizens group filed a complaint, challenging the TUA. The group asserted claims alleging due process violations, nuisance, and violations of 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983. The King County Superior Court, Washington, granted the city's motion for summary judgment. The group appealed.


OVERVIEW: The church was located in a single-family residential zone. The city code did not permit a temporary encampment in a single family residential area. The TUA permitted the homeless encampment to be operated on the church's property for a period of up to 93 days. The court concluded that the city's approval of the TUA was a land use decision within the meaning of the Washington Land Use Petition Act (LUPA), Wash. Rev. The group's failure to challenge the city's decision in a timely LUPA petition barred its due process claims, including its claims for damages under 42 U.S.C.S. § 1983, because those claims were simply challenges to the approval of the TUA.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: On remand, the Lewis County Superior Court (Washington) granted the State's request to impanel a jury to consider the exceptional sentencing factor under Wash. Rev. Code § 9.94A.535(2)(c), rejected defendant's arguments, stayed the sentencing, and certified an order to the court to consider whether the court had jurisdiction to impanel a jury under Wash. Rev. Code § 9.94A.537(2). Defendant sought discretionary review, which was granted.


OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that the trial court erred when it granted the State's motion to impanel a jury. The court agreed that defendant was not entitled to resentencing within the standard range because Wash. Rev. Code § 9.94A.535(2)(c) authorized the sentencing court to consider the free crimes factor without a jury finding. The court remanded for resentencing with instructions. OVERVIEW: Prior to his demise, the decedent was admitted to the nursing care facility. 155 Wn. App. 691; 229 P.3d 943; 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 869


December 3, 2009, Oral Argument 
April 27, 2010, Filed

As amended by order of the Court of Appeals August 5, 2010.


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: On reconsideration by, Amended by West v. Stahley, 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 1678 (Wash. Ct. App., Apr. 27, 2010)
Review denied by State ex rel. W. v. Stahley, 170 Wn.2d 1022, 245 P.3d 772, 2011 Wash. LEXIS 20 (Wash., Jan. 5, 2011)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Thurston Superior Court. Docket No: 07-2-02101-6. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 05/19/2008. Judge signing: Honorable H Christopher Wickham.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, a citizen and an individual, sued defendants, a city, a city council, a property owner, and a company, challenging an engineering permit issued to the owner. Under Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 12(b)(6), the Thurston Superior Court (Washington) dismissed the petition under Land Use Petition Act (LUPA), Wash. Rev. Code ch. 36.70C. The citizen appealed. The owner and company sought fees on appeal under Wash. R. App. P. 18.9.


OVERVIEW: The citizen argued that the trial court erred in dismissing his petition. The court affirmed. For purposes of Wash. Rev. Code § 36.70C.060, regardless of whether a petitioner was the owner of the property or an aggrieved person, LUPA required the petitioner to exhaust administrative remedies. Exhausting administrative remedies was always a condition precedent to challenging a land use decision that was subject to review under LUPA. The citizen had actual notice of the city's decision to grant the engineering permit, but the citizen did not file an administrative appeal until more than 14 days later. Thus, for purposes of Olympia, Wash., Municipal Code § 18.75.020, the citizen failed to exhaust his administrative remedies. Equitable tolling would not cure the citizen's failure. Like the 21-day statute of limitation under Wash. Rev. Code § 36.70C.040, exhausting administrative remedies was a fundamental tenant under LUPA, and the failure to do either was an absolute bar to bringing a LUPA petition to superior court. For purposes of Wash. R. App. P. 18.9, the citizen's appeal was frivolous and the company and owner were entitled to fees and costs.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed. The owner and company were entitled to reasonable fees and costs in defending this frivolous lawsuit.

OVERVIEW: Defendant raised various issues on appeal, but the court affirmed. PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Pierce County Superior Court. Docket No: 06-2-09885-0. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: March 2, 2007. Judge signing: Honorable Stephanie A Arend, Craig C. Stewart.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant Washington Department of Labor and Industries sought review of a judgment of the Pierce County Superior Court (Washington), which found that the Department could not seek reimbursement from a portion of respondent employee's third party recovery compensating him for his pain and suffering pursuant to Wash. Rev. Code. § 51.24.030. The employee asked the court to uphold an award of attorney fees and to award attorney fees on appeal.


OVERVIEW: The Department argued that the statutory reimbursement use of the term recovery included all damages except loss of consortium and that it was entitled to seek reimbursement for the pain and suffering portion of the employee's damages. On appeal, the court held that because the Department did not compensate the employee for his pain and suffering, it could not be reimbursed from that portion of the employee's award. The pain and suffering portion of the employee's third party damages was not a recovery as it was defined under Wash. Rev. Code § 51.24.030(5). The court further noted that the legislative history did not provide evidence that the legislature intended to allow the Department to access the pain and suffering portion of a third party recovery to reimburse it for money it paid to compensate an injured worker's other losses. Finally, Wash. Rev. Code § 51.24.030 did not give the employee adequate notice that third party settlement funds earmarked as compensation for their personal pain and suffering were subject to distribution under Wash. Rev. Code § 51.24.060 to reimburse the Department for payments it made to compensate the employee for other losses.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the lower court's judgment and awarded the employee attorney fees on appeal.

OVERVIEW: Defendant argued that Washington's sentencing statute, Wash. Rev. Code § 9.94A.589, violated his right to equal protection under the U.S. and Washington Constitutions. Moreover, the sentencing court had objective information about defendant's crimes to support the sentence it imposed.


OUTCOME: The judgment and sentences were affirmed.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Clallam Superior Court. Docket No: 05-2-01226-1. Date filed: November 6, 2006. Judge signing: Honorable George Lamont Wood, William Knebes.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff prison inmate sued defendants, the Washington Department of Corrections (DOC) and several DOC employees, alleging that defendants violated his First Amendment rights and his substantive due process rights, and retaliated against him for exercising his First Amendment rights. Pursuant to Wash. Super. Ct. Civ. R. 12(b)(6), the Clallam County Superior Court, Washington, dismissed the inmate's suit. The inmate appealed.


OVERVIEW: The DOC infracted the inmate for referring to a correctional center's superintendent as "anti-male--a lesbian" in a letter to the DOC Secretary. It based the infraction on Washington's criminal libel statute, Wash. Rev. Code § 9.58.010. The court concluded that Washington's criminal libel statutory scheme, Wash. Rev. Code §§ 9.58.010 and 9.58.020, was facially unconstitutional for overbreadth and vagueness, and that the DOC based the infraction on an unconstitutional statute. The scheme did not meet constitutional standards under Garrison v. Louisiana because it permitted prosecution of persons for making false statements without actual malice and or making true statements without good motive or intent. The inmate established a cognizable retaliation claim. The inmate alleged that the DOC retaliated against him because it did not issue the infraction until nearly three months after he attempted to send the letter to the DOC Secretary, around the time he was actively pursuing litigation against prison officials.


OUTCOME: The judgment of dismissal was reversed, the inmate's infraction was vacated, and the case was remanded for further proceedings at which the inmate would be allowed to raise his claims for damages against the DOC for violating his First Amendment rights, violating substantive due process, and retaliating against him.

CASE SUMMARY:

The Thurston Superior Court (Washington) concluded that the State was solely responsible for the care and treatment of long-term public mental health patients under the Involuntary Treatment Act, Wash. Rev. Code ch. 71.05. The parties cross-appealed.


OVERVIEW: The State contracted with the county for the use of inpatient psychiatric facilities. The issues presented on appeal, among others, were whether the: (1) Department of Social and Health Services was financially responsible for the care of long-term patients; (2) legislative amendments applied to the grant of injunctive relief; (3) Department could withhold liquidated damages; (4) Department illegally required the county to use Medicaid funds. The appellate court granted the State's motion to strike in part and denied sanctions against the county.
