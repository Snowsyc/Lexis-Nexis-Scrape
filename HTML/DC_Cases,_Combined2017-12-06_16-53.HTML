CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Following a bench trial, defendant was found guilty of a single count of criminal contempt for failure to comply with a court order to pay child support, in violation of D.C. Code § 46-225.02 (2001). The Superior Court of the District of Columbia sentenced defendant to 180 days in jail and ordered defendant to pay $ 50 into the Victims of Crime Compensation Fund. Defendant appealed.


OVERVIEW: Defendant contended that the presumption of willfulness created by D.C. Code § 46-225.02 violated the Due Process Clause of the Fifth Amendment to the Constitution of the United States. Defendant also maintained that there was insufficient evidence beyond a reasonable doubt to find defendant guilty of criminal contempt. The court of appeals held that D.C. Code § 46-225.02 does not unconstitutionally shift the burden of proving willfulness from the government to the defendant, nor did the trial court shift the burden in this case. The appellate court further held that under § 46-225.02, defendant bore the burden of production, or the presentation of evidence showing an inability to pay, but that, as usual, the government bore the burden of persuasion, that is, the burden of proving willfulness as an element of criminal contempt. The appellate court held that the government sustained its burden of proving willfulness under § 46-225.02 and that the evidence was sufficient to permit the trial court to find beyond a doubt that defendant voluntarily impaired defendant's ability to comply with the court order to pay child support and thus was guilty of contempt under § 46-225.02.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the trial court was affirmed.
