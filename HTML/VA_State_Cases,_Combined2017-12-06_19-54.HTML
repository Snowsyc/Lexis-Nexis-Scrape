53 Va. Cir. 128; 2000 Va. Cir. LEXIS 171



June 23, 2000, Entered



DISPOSITION:  [**1] Roses petition denied and case remanded to Juvenile and Domestic Relations Court for any further proceedings.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant foster parents sought visitation with the child, asserting that their former role as the child's foster parents established them as persons with a legitimate interest in custody under Va. Code Ann. § 20-124.2, after an order awarded full custody of the child to his biological father.


OVERVIEW: Appellant foster parents who kept minor child while his birth father was in prison moved for visitation with the child, arguing they were persons with a legitimate interest in the custody of the child under Va. Code Ann. § 20-124.2. Concerning custody determinations, Va. Code § 20-124.2(B) stated that the court had to give primary consideration to the best interests of the child. The court had to give due regard to the primacy of the parent-child relationship, but upon a showing by clear and convincing evidence that the best interest of the child would have been served thereby, could have awarded custody or visitation to any other person with a legitimate interest. The court noted the statute had to be construed to require that a court had to find actual harm to the child's health or welfare without such visitation to be constitutional under U.S. Const. amend. XIV. The foster parents could not have met the actual harm standard.


OUTCOME: Appellants' petition was denied and case remanded to juvenile and domestic relations court for further proceedings because the child's health and welfare would not have suffered actual harm from a denial of visitation with his foster parents, assuming they were persons with a legitimate interest in the child's welfare.

Patrick v. McHale


CH96-940


CIRCUIT COURT OF CHESTERFIELD COUNTY, VIRGINIA


54 Va. Cir. 67; 2000 Va. Cir. LEXIS 366



June 7, 2000, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In response to plaintiff's application for rezoning of a property from agricultural to residential, defendant, the Chesterfield County Board of Supervisors, voted to deny the owner's request. The owner filed a motion for declaratory judgment.


OVERVIEW: The owner had presented evidence that his proposed rezoning to a residential area was a reasonable use of the land, because property surrounding the owner's parcel was zoned similarly. The board presented evidence that the owner's property bordered two large parcels that were zoned agricultural. The circuit court held that since the underlying zoning and the new zoning were both appropriate for the lot in question a classic case of a fairly debatable issue was presented. Thus, the board's decision to deny the owner's rezoning application was fairly debatable. The owner claimed that the board required a cash proffer or a dedication of public facilities from him as a condition precedent to the board's approval of the new zoning. The circuit court found that the board did not require a cash proffer or a dedication where fifty percent of all zoning applications were approved without the maximum proffer and that any and all proffers from zoning applicants were made voluntarily. The circuit court further held that the board's action did not constitute a taking where the owner could build a residence by obtaining a variance.


OUTCOME: The owner's claims were overruled.

Industrial Dev. Auth. v. Mohler


Case No. CL99-8127


CIRCUIT COURT OF ALBEMARLE COUNTY, VIRGINIA


51 Va. Cir. 449; 2000 Va. Cir. LEXIS 68



April 3, 2000, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff agency moved for judgment seeking a final order validating certain conduit revenue bonds and certain proceedings relating to their authorization and issuance. Defendant taxpayers challenged constitutionality of issuance of these bonds as violating the Establishment of Religion Clauses in the U.S. Const. amend. I and Va. Const. art. I, § 16.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff Industrial Development Authority sought an order validating conduit revenue bonds and connected proceedings, for the benefit of an accredited, non-profit, non-denominational Christian day school, to finance projects authorized by the Industrial Development and Revenue Bond Act, Va. Code Ann. § 15.2-4900 et seq. Defendant taxpayers challenged its constitutionality under the Establishment of Religion Clauses in the state and federal constitutions. The court found denial of the bonds based on the school's alleged pervasively religious viewpoint would infringe its free speech rights. There was no Establishment Clause violation. The evidence did not prove the school was "pervasively sectarian," and the proposed financing was assumed to relate to buildings and facilities within a properly delimited project. Lacking a compelling government interest, it would be impermissible viewpoint discrimination to deny validation under a broad neutral government program in which other church-affiliated institutions had taken part, solely on the basis of religious viewpoint.


OUTCOME: Motion for judgment was granted and issuance of conduit bonds and related proceedings were validated pursuant to statute. There was no state or federal constitutional violation, as the institution to receive funding did not have a primary purpose of religious training, was not pervasively sectarian, and it was not substantially subsumed by a religious mission.

Fears v. Virginia State Bar


Case No. LE-1283-3


CIRCUIT COURT OF THE CITY OF RICHMOND, VIRGINIA


51 Va. Cir. 367; 2000 Va. Cir. LEXIS 50



March 1, 2000, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Complainants filed declaratory judgment action against defendants Commonwealth and state bar association, and sought summary judgment declaring Va. Code Ann. §§ 6.1-2.19 - 6.1-2.29, the Consumer Real Estate Settlement Protection Act or CRESPA, unconstitutional for allowing non-lawyers to practice real estate law.


OVERVIEW: Complainant attorney, who had a real estate practice, filed a declaratory judgment action against defendant state bar association and Commonwealth, challenging the practice of allowing non-lawyers to handle real estate closings and paperwork. After a hearing, the court permitted intervention as party plaintiff by a real estate attorneys league. Complainants sought a judgment declaring Va. Code Ann. §§ 6.1-2.19 - 6.1-2.29, the Consumer Real Estate Settlement Protection Act (CRESPA), unconstitutional. They argued CRESPA violated the Special Legislation clause of the Virginia Constitution, due process and equal protection rights, and the state separation of powers doctrine, and cited the Unauthorized Practice of Law Opinion No. 183. The court found for defendants, holding the Unauthorized Practice of Law Advisory Opinion No. 183 approved by the state Supreme Court exempted CRESPA licensed persons from such charges.


OUTCOME: Judgment entered for the Commonwealth, despite finding challenged legislation authorized non-lawyers to handle some real estate law, as those persons were shielded by judicial exemption. Special Legislation clause was not implicated. Complainant's status as real estate attorney did not implicate constitutional protection of relevant fundamental right nor equal protection.

Virginia Mut. Ins. Co. v. Burgess


Case No. CL99-591


CIRCUIT COURT OF THE CITY OF ROANOKE, VIRGINIA


51 Va. Cir. 269; 2000 Va. Cir. LEXIS 30



January 26, 2000, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff insurance company filed a motion requesting a trial, arguing Va. Code Ann. § 38.2-2231 (1994) was unconstitutional in that it deprived it of the right to file lawsuits in the Commonwealth of Virginia, its constitutional right to trial by jury, and its right to appellate review.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiff insurance carrier was subrogated to its insured's property damage claim and sued defendant insurance carrier to recover the amount of its payment to its insured and to recover its insured's deductible. Defendant argued plaintiff did not have a right to sue for damages because Va. Code Ann. § 38.2-2231 (1994) mandated arbitration of disputed liability and damage claims between insurers. Plaintiff argued Va. Code Ann. § 38.2-2231 (1994) was unconstitutional as it deprived it of the right to file lawsuits in the courts of the Commonwealth of Virginia, it denied it its constitutional right to trial by jury, and denied it its right to appellate review. The court held Va. Const., art. I, § 11 provided a guarantee of a trial by jury only to those who had a right to a jury trial at common law and that corporations had such rights at common law. The court granted plaintiff's request holding Va. Code Ann. § 38.2-2231 (1994) was contrary to those preserved rights and was therefore unconstitutional.


OUTCOME: Trial granted, because at common law corporations had the right to sue and be sued and as that right was afforded in the Virginia Constitution, the statute at issue was contrary to those preserved rights and was therefore unconstitutional, void, and of no force or effect.
