CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: After the death of a child's father, the commissioner granted joint custody and primary placement to the child's stepmother under Del. Code Ann. tit. 13, § 733 (2009). CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A mother, a guardian, and the guardian's former husband filed consolidated petitions in regard to visitation as to the mother's child. The court had granted guardianship of the child to the guardian based on the mother's consent and the father's default.


OVERVIEW: According to an evaluating doctor, the child was not as invested in her relationship with the guardian's former husband and would rather spend time with her mother. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants, a husband and wife, sought review under Del. Code Ann. tit. 13, § 905 of a decision of appellee, the Department of Services for Children, Youth and Their Families, which denied their adoption application. They moved for reargument after the family court found that the law required the case to be decided on the record the Department made in denying their application. OVERVIEW: Appellants argued that pursuant to the Administrative Procedures Act, Del. OUTCOME: The family court denied appellants' motion for reargument and denied the Department's motion.

OVERVIEW: Rehabilitation, particularly as to youthful offenders, was a compelling State interest and it included the ability to expunge records. He had successfully completed a sex related rehabilitation program, and had been a model probationer. His guidance counselor testified he was a model student with no involvement in any controversies in the community, particularly any sexually questionable controversies. He was found to no longer be a risk to society. The State failed to demonstrate a material objection.


OUTCOME: The petition for expungement was granted. The juvenile's name was to be immediately removed from the Delaware Sex Offender Registry, and all obligations he previously had under the registration and notification requirements of the Delaware Sex Offender Registry and Notification statutes were to cease.

State v. Spady


Case No.: 0711031808, Cr.A. Nos.: K08-02-2437, K08-02-2438, K08-02-2439, K08-02-2440, K08-02-2441, K08-02-2442


COURT OF COMMON PLEAS OF DELAWARE, KENT


2008 Del. C.P. LEXIS 22



November 7, 2008, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was charged with six counts of inhumane handling, care and treatment of dogs in violation of Del. Code Ann. tit. 7, §§ 1702 and 1704, all misdemeanors. She moved to dismiss the charges on the grounds that the court of common pleas did not have jurisdiction to hear the charges against her because they did not constitute crimes. Additionally, she contended that the statutes were constitutionally void for vagueness.


OVERVIEW: Defendant, the owner/operator of a kennel, was charged with violating Del. Code Ann. tit. 7, §§ 1702(e), 1704(a)(3), (e)(3), (e)(5), and (e)(4). Pursuant to Del. Code Ann. tit. 7, § 1714, the penalty to be imposed for a violation of each of those statutes was a fine. The court noted that, pursuant to Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, § 233, should a fine be imposed for conduct prohibited by statute, the conduct was a criminal offense, unless otherwise specified by the General Assembly. Since the charges against defendant would result in a fine if she was convicted, her conduct was considered a criminal offense. The provisions under which she was charged were silent as to the type of offense the prohibited conduct constituted. Therefore, pursuant to Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, § 233(c), those offenses had to be considered unclassified misdemeanors. The court had original jurisdiction to hear defendant's case under Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, § 2701(b). Each of the statutes under which she was charged were clear on their face. It was not necessary for people of common intelligence to guess at their meaning and to differ as to their application. Thus, they were not constitutionally void for vagueness.


OUTCOME: Defendant's motion to dismiss was denied.

Defendant filed a motion to suppress the field test and blood tests on the basis her initial detention was not supported by reasonable, articulable suspicion and her arrest was not supported by probable cause in violation of her U.S. Constitutional Fourth Amendment rights.


OVERVIEW: Defendant moved to suppress the forced extraction of her blood at the police barracks on the basis that taking her blood in an unsanitary environment violated her rights against reasonable search and seizure under the provision of Del. State v. Gentry

June 8, 2007, Decided


NOTICE:

THIS OPINION HAS NOT BEEN RELEASED FOR PUBLICATION. UNTIL RELEASED, IT IS SUBJECT TO REVISION OR WITHDRAWAL.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In two separate cases, defendants made motions to dismiss charges of lewdness under Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, § 1341. Defendants specifically claimed that the statute was surplusage under existing Delaware law when compared with the indecent exposure statute, Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, §§ 764, 765, and that the lewdness statute was unconstitutionally vague and overbroad.


OVERVIEW: First defendant allegedly committed a lewd act by rubbing his crotch and licking his lips while looking at the alleged victim. Second defendant allegedly committed a lewd act by exposing his genitals to, and beginning to masturbate in the presence of, another alleged victim. Defendants argued that lewdness, which was not statutorily defined, had to be defined under common law, which required exposure of the naked body or genitals as an element, thus rendering Del. Code Ann. tit. 11, § 1341 surplusage based on the codification of indecent exposure. Based on the legislative history, the court determined that the terms used in the lewdness statute did not necessarily include an exposure of the genital organs and, thus, the statute could not be invalidated as surplusage. While defendants claimed that § 1341 was unconstitutionally vague as it would prohibit activities constituting an exercise of protected expressive or associational rights, the court held that the statute's terms narrowed criminal punishment to those acts that a person knew were likely to be observed by others and knew were likely to cause affront or alarm in the observer. Thus, the motions to dismiss were denied.


OUTCOME: The court denied both defendants' motions to dismiss.

Demby v. Wilmington Hous. Auth.

COURT OF COMMON PLEAS OF DELAWARE, NEW CASTLE


2003 Del. C.P. LEXIS 33



October 2, 2003, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Appellant's Motion for Judgment for Relief under Court of Common Pleas Civil Rule 60(b) GRANTED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent, a housing authority, filed an eviction action against petitioner, a tenant. A magistrate entered a judgment by admission against the tenant pursuant to Del. J.P. Ct. Civ. R. 54(g). The tenant filed a motion for relief from judgment under Del. C.P. Ct. Civ. R. 60(b).


OVERVIEW: The tenant argued that the judgment by admission was defective because the tenant did not acknowledge the judgment in writing. The court held that a party who appeared and entered on the record a valid judgment by admission pursuant to Del. J.P. Ct. Civ. R. 54(g) had to do so in writing and fully explain the parameters of the judgment by admission to make a knowing, voluntary waiver of the party's trial and appeal rights. Further, to consent in writing or orally, a party defendant had to understand and acknowledge the full parameters of the judgment the defendant was consenting to. The court found that the use of the disjunctive "and/or" in Rule 54(a) created both ambiguity and confusion. The court found that the tenant did not make such an acknowledgment in writing, and therefore the judgment by admission was void. The court further held that the tenant did not consent to eviction or possession by the housing authority by paying the judgment entered by the magistrate because the tenant was not advised that the housing authority was going to be granted possession of the leased premises.


OUTCOME: The tenant's motion for relief was granted.

COURT OF COMMON PLEAS OF DELAWARE, NEW CASTLE


2002 Del. C.P. LEXIS 69



December 9, 2002, Decided



DISPOSITION:  [*1] Defendant's Motion to Suppress for lack of probable cause to arrest defendant for driving under influence of alcohol heard, and denied.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A defendant was charged with driving under the influence of alcohol under Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4177(a), leaving an injury accident scene under Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4202(a), removing a vehicle from an accident scene under Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4206, and failing to report an accident involving alcohol under Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4203(a)(3). The defendant's motion to suppress was denied.


OVERVIEW: Testimony did not establish a required element of Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4177 (a)(5), showing that the defendant's alcohol concentration was the result of an amount of alcohol present in, or consumed by, the person when that person was driving. At no time during the State's case did any witness give testimony that the defendant had been drinking at, or prior to, the time of the accident. The State had the burden of producing such evidence. The defendant was not guilty, under Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4177(a) or Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4203(a)(3), of driving under alcohol's influence or failing to report an accident involving alcohol. As to the additional charges of violating of Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4202(a) and Del. Code Ann. tit. 21, § 4206, the state had proven the elements of the offenses, and the defendant was guilty of leaving an injury accident and taking his car from the scene.


OUTCOME: The defendant was found not guilty of driving under alcohol's influence or failing to report an accident involving alcohol. He was found guilty of leaving an injury accident and taking his car from the scene.
