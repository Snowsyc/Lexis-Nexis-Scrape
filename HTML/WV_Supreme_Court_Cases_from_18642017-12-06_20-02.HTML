December 11, 2008, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from the Circuit Court of Kanawha County. The Honorable Paul Zakaib, Jr., Judge. Civil Action No. 05-C-1910.



DISPOSITION: REVERSED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In appellee citizens' declaratory judgment action for an order directing the state legislature to pass legislation enabling an election on a proposed reform of a county's government, appellants, the state house of delegates' speaker and the state senate president, challenged the determination of the Circuit Court of Kanawha County (West Virginia) that the legislature had a duty under W. Va. Const. art. IX, § 13 to pass enabling legislation.


OVERVIEW: The question presented was what requirements, if any, were imposed on the Legislature by W. Va. Const. art. 9, § 13, to act on the petition. The court held that art. 9, § 13 did not require the Legislature to enact legislation enabling the reformation upon receipt of a reformation petition. Receipt of a county commission's request to act on a petition signed by ten percent of that county's voters to reform county government simply triggered the legislative process. The Legislature retained its discretion to approve or reject a bill authorizing a county-wide election on the requested reformation. The Legislature was not compelled to approve legislation authorizing a county-wide election on the reformation proposed in the petition if the Legislature concluded that the proposed reformation would violate the constitution. If a petition for reformation of county government was presented to the Legislature pursuant to art. 9, § 13 and the legislative process did not result in the enactment of enabling legislation prior to the end of the legislative session, then, in order for a subsequent Legislature, during its two year term, to address the issue, a new petition had to be submitted.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the determination.

OVERVIEW: The administratrix claimed that the doctrine of respondeat superior or vicarious liability should have been considered. The court disagreed. W. Va. SUPREME COURT OF APPEALS OF WEST VIRGINIA


208 W. Va. 448; 541 S.E.2d 326; 2000 W. Va. LEXIS 101


April 18, 2000, Submitted  
July 13, 2000, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [**1] Justice Maynard filed a Concurring Opinion December 5, 2000.


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Workers' Compensation Appeal Board. Appeal No. 49310. Claim No. 93-29111. Appeal No. 49309. Claim No. 92-68781.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants employees challenged the judgment of the Workers' Compensation Appeal Board (West Virginia) denying them workers' compensation benefits for occupational pneumoconiosis.


OVERVIEW: Appellants sought workers' compensation benefits for occupational pneumoconiosis. The Workers' Compensation Appeal Board denied their claims because their ventilatory function testing showed that they had no pulmonary functional impairment. Appellants challenged the denial of their claims, contending that W. Va. Code St. R. tit. 85, § 1-20.8.5(b) did not require the exclusive use of the Kory predicated normal values in determining whether a claimant suffered from pulmonary functional impairment. On appeal, the court affirmed. The court interpreted the rule as requiring the exclusive use of the Kory predicted normal values in interpreting the results of ventilatory function tests in occupational pneumoconiosis claims. The court held that the obvious clerical error in the numbering of the rule did not change their interpretation given the legislature's intent. The board's decision was entitled to deference because their longstanding policy in requiring the use of the Kory predicted values was consistent with the rule's plain meaning.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed because the court interpreted W. Va. Code St. R. tit. 85, § 1-20.8.5(b) as requiring the exclusive use of the Kory predicated normal values in determining whether a workers' compensation claimant suffered from pulmonary functional impairment.

DISPOSITION: REVERSED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant physician challenged the judgment of the Kanawha County Circuit Court (West Virginia) upholding appellee West Virginia Board of Medicine's decision to deny appellant's application to renew his medical license based on child support arrearages.


OVERVIEW: Appellant's medical license was revoked after he failed to answer questions regarding child support obligations in his renewal application. At the time, appellant's failure to pay child support for three children had resulted in substantial child support arrearages. The trial court affirmed the decision. On appeal the court reversed, holding that appellee did not have the authority, acting on its own without any involvement of a circuit court, to take action relative to a license renewal application pursuant to W. Va. Code § 48A-5A-5(c). Because the trial court based its ruling on its assertion that appellee's decision to deny appellant's application was mandated by § 48A-5A-5(c), the court reversed its decision because no circuit court had determined that appellant's child support payments were in arrearages.


OUTCOME: Judgment reversed because appellee had no authority to, on its own, deny appellant's application to renew his medical license based upon child support arrearages where such determination had not been previously made by a circuit court.

DISPOSITION: REVERSED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant city challenged the judgment of the Mercer County Circuit Court (West Virginia) entered in favor of appellee former employee in his action to recover unused sick leave pay that accrued during his employment with appellant, brought under the West Virginia Wage Payment and Collection Act, W. Va. Code § 21-5-1 et seq.


OVERVIEW: When appellee resigned from being a police officer, he requested from appellant payment for all unused sick leave that he had accrued. When appellant refused, appellee filed suit under the West Virginia Wage Payment and Collection Act, W. Va. Code § 21-5-1 et seq. The trial court found in favor of appellee and denied appellant's post-trial motions. On appeal, the court reversed. The court held that the Act applied to government employers as well as nongovernment employers, because to construe the Act otherwise would lead to an absurd or unconstitutional result. However, the court held that the trial court erred in denying appellant's motion for judgment as a matter of law because the uncontroverted evidence showed that the terms of appellee's employment with appellant did not obligate appellant to pay him unused sick leave upon separation from employment because appellant had an unwritten policy of never paying unused sick leave to separated police officers.


OUTCOME: Judgment reversed because appellant had an unwritten policy of never paying unused sick leave to separated police officers, and therefore appellant did not have to pay appellee unused sick leave under the West Virginia Wage Payment and Collection Act.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs, student and his parents, appealed the judgment of the Circuit Court of Webster County (West Virginia), which upheld the decision of defendant board of education expelling plaintiff student for one year for possession of a firearm on school property in violation of the Productive and Safe Schools Act, W. Va. Code § 18A-5-1a (1996).


OVERVIEW: Defendant board of education expelled plaintiff student for one year for possession of a firearm on school property in violation of the Productive and Safe Schools Act (Act), W. Va. Code § 18A-5-1a (1996). The trial court upheld defendant board's decision. Plaintiffs, student and his parents, appealed. The court held that plaintiff student had several opportunities for exoneration. The principal could have found at the informal hearing that plaintiff student's situation was not a violation of the Act. However, the principal, defendant board, and the superintendent -- the fact finders under the Act -- all determined that plaintiff student's actions in having the gun tucked into his pants on school property constituted a violation of the Act. Consequently, the judgment was affirmed.


OUTCOME: The judgment upholding plaintiff student's expulsion was affirmed. Although plaintiff student had several opportunities for exoneration, the principal, defendant board of education, and the superintendent -- the fact finders under the Productive and Safe Schools Act (Act) -- all determined that plaintiff student's actions in having the gun tucked into his pants on school property violated the Act.

DISPOSITION: REVERSED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant challenged the grant of summary judgment to appellees by the Circuit Court of Webster County (West Virginia), in appellees' class action suit challenging the validity of a fire service fee used to support appellant's volunteer fire department.


OVERVIEW: Appellees, a group of town residents, filed a class action suit against appellant township, challenging appellant's fire service fee, used to support the local volunteer fire department. The trial court found that the appellant was applying its fee in an unconstitutional fashion by using the fee to support a volunteer fire department that also protected non-residents. The court reversed, noting that although the relevant ordinance only allowed the fire fee money to be spent in town, the fire department also received money from other sources. The court held that as long as appellant continued to use any funds generated by the fire service fee solely for fire protection efforts, and provided that the amounts contributed by appellant to the fire department continued to reasonably reflect the true cost of providing fire protection to the citizens, the fire protection fee was valid.


OUTCOME: Trial court reversed. Because the fire department received money from sources other than the town's residents, it could continue to provide fire protection outside the town. Amounts contributed by appellant had only to reasonably reflect the true cost of providing fire protection to the citizens.

Circuit Court of Hancock County (West Virginia) certified two questions to the court.


OVERVIEW: Decedent, an employee of defendant city, was working at the city garage when a municipal garbage truck positioned on a hydraulic lift or hoist fell on him, causing his death. Because decedent had no dependents, the workers' compensation benefits available to his estate were limited to $ 5000 in funeral expenses pursuant to W. Va. Code § 23-4-4(a) (1995). OUTCOME: Answering trial court's rephrased certified question in the affirmative, court held that the Governmental Tort Claims And Insurance Reform Act unambiguously provided that defendant city was immune from liability for decedent's death because plaintiff executrix's wrongful death action resulted from a claim covered by workers' compensation law in the form of reasonable funeral expenses.

Va. Code § 20-4-7(5) (1994).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of possession of a controlled substance with intent to deliver. On appeal, defendant contended the circuit court erred in denying his motion to suppress evidence of marijuana discovered by conservation officers in their search of defendant's car. OUTCOME: The court reversed the order denying defendant's motion to suppress evidence and remanded the case because the evidence was clearly insufficient to establish reasonable suspicion to stop defendant's vehicle.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner inmate filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus alleging that during her trial on the charge of first degree murder, the Circuit Court of Harrison County (West Virginia) failed to advise her of her right to testify, and that jury instructions given by the circuit court on the use of a deadly weapon deprived her of a fair trial.


OVERVIEW: Petitioner was convicted for the first degree murder of her husband. Her direct appeal, state petition for post-conviction writ of habeas corpus, and federal petition for habeas corpus relief were all refused. Petitioner then filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus in state court, alleging a failure to advise her of her right to testify and improper jury instructions. The court denied the writ. OUTCOME: Writ was denied. Petitioner failed to demonstrate that the failure to provide Neuman instruction resulted in a constitutional violation. The jury was properly instructed regarding the burden of proof and the defense of insanity.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Relators petitioned for a writ of mandamus to compel respondents supreme court justice and Secretary of State of West Virginia, to have respondent judge declared ineligible as a candidate for election due to his status as an incumbent.


OVERVIEW: Respondent incumbent judge was appointed and was currently filling the remainder of an unexpired term, which ran until December 31, 2004. On January 29, 2000, he filed a certificate of candidacy for election to a separate 12-year term on the Supreme Court of Appeals of West Virginia. When he refused to withdraw his candidacy, relator pro se attorney petitioned the same Supreme Court of Appeals for a writ of mandamus, seeking to have respondent declared ineligible. OUTCOME: Writ of mandamus was granted because the limitations or regulations on political activities, which included the restriction affecting eligibility for election to the state supreme court, arose from the State's compelling and permissible interest in its judiciary.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Juvenile defendant sought to prohibit the Circuit Court of Preston County (West Virginia), in its juvenile court capacity, from enforcing its order disqualifying defendant's court-appointed counsel from further representation due to apparent impropriety arising from her having formerly represented another juvenile who was expected to be a state's witness.


OVERVIEW: Juvenile defendant sought to prohibit enforcement of an order disqualifying defendant's court-appointed counsel. Defendant's counsel had formerly represented another juvenile in the same matter, and he was expected to appear as a state's witness against defendant. Defendant contended that the parties had waived any potential conflict of interest. However, the waiver did not deprive the trial court of its discretion, particularly when a juvenile attempted to waive his rights. OUTCOME: Writ of prohibition from enforcement of order disqualifying counsel was denied. The decision whether to disqualify counsel, even when conflict had been waived, was within the trial court's discretion. No appropriate grounds for issuing a writ of prohibition had been presented.
