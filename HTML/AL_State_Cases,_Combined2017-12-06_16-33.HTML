CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: HOLDINGS: [1]-A statute of limitations did not apply to a landowner's declaratory judgment claims contesting a city's permitting ordinances because a current and ongoing infringement of property rights was alleged, and it was not the ordinances' enactment or an instance of their enforcement that was the claims' gravamen; [2]-A two-year limitations period applied to his damages claim based on the city's past specific acts allegedly interfering with his property use because he made no meaningful argument supported by authority that the statute did not apply; [3]-That statute of limitations did not bar the claim because it ran from a 2011 stop-work order, instead of a 2008 stop-work order, since each time the city enforced its ordinances to stop the landowner's work, it committed a new act on which a new claim was based, and the claim was filed within the statutory period.

Const. amend. 342, by creating an absolute debt at once, payable over the course of many years out of future collections of existing tax revenues otherwise destined for the county's general fund. No new tax or revenue source was created to help fund the retirement of the bond indebtedness; rather, the components of the county tax revenues were all established and existing revenue sources for the county's general fund. There was no yearly recurring "quid pro quo."


OUTCOME: The decision of the trial court was reversed. The case was remanded for further proceedings.
