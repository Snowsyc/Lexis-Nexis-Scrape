Souter v. County of Warren

2011 Va. App. LEXIS 32



February 1, 2011, Decided


NOTICE: PURSUANT TO THE APPLICABLE VIRGINIA CODE SECTION THIS OPINION IS NOT DESIGNATED FOR PUBLICATION.


PRIOR HISTORY:  [*1] 
FROM THE CIRCUIT COURT OF WARREN COUNTY. Dennis L. Hupp, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant was convicted by the Circuit Court of Warren County (Virginia) of violating former Warren County, Va., Code § 123-2(H). She appealed, challenging the constitutionality of the noise ordinance, former Warren County, Va., Code §§ 123-1 and 123-2.


OVERVIEW: The appellate court held that former § 123-1 prohibited any unreasonably loud or disturbing sound of such character, intensity, volume, carrying power or duration as to disturb the comfort, repose, health or safety of any individual. When used in this context, such adjectives were inherently vague because they required persons of average intelligence to guess at the meaning of the words. Noise that one person might consider loud and disturbing might not disturb the sensibilities of another listener. Thus, these phrases did not provide a degree of definiteness sufficient to save the ordinance from the vagueness challenge. The per se violations were defined as constituting, "unreasonably loud or disturbing sound" and could not be evaluated separately from the vague terms. Because §§ 123-1 and 123-2 were not in conflict, they had to be read together. The unconstitutional language could not be severed to give the remaining per se provisions a definite and permissible construction because the vague language affected the content of the entire ordinance. The entire ordinance was unconstitutionally vague.


OUTCOME: The conviction was reversed.

PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed from a judgment of the Circuit Court of the City of Lynchburg (Virginia) imposing a five-year sentence for possession of a firearm by a convicted felon under Va. Defendant argued that the mandatory sentencing provision of § 18.2-308.2 violated Va. Const. art. III, § 1.


OVERVIEW: Historically, the power of punishment was vested in the legislative, not in the judicial department. The development of mandatory minimum sentences reflected the traditional legislative role in creating statewide uniformity for all sentences for the specified crimes. Code Ann. § 18.2-308.2 and instead sentence him under Va. Code Ann. § 18.2-10(f).

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: On remand from the Supreme Court of Virginia, the court sought to resolve declaratory judgment actions filed by plaintiffs, a hierarchical church and its diocese, against defendants, seven dissident local churches, the local churches' amended counterclaims, and a question described regarding one local church's endowment fund. McEachin v. Bolling


Case No.: CLII-5456


CIRCUIT COURT OF THE CITY OF RICHMOND, VIRGINIA


84 Va. Cir. 76; 2011 Va. Cir. LEXIS 189



December 16, 2011, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff State Senator filed an action seeking a declaration that defendant Lieutenant Governor was not entitled to vote to break a tie on certain matters during the upcoming session of the General Assembly. He filed a motion for a temporary injunction pursuant to Va. Code Ann. § 8.01-620 et seq. to enjoin the Lieutenant Governor from casting any tie-breaking vote.


OVERVIEW: The circuit court held that the Senator was unlikely to be unsuccessful on the merits because of ripeness and separation of powers under Va. Const. art. III, § 1. No action had been taken by the Lieutenant Governor or the General Assembly. For the circuit court to intervene before the legislative process had even begun would be tantamount to issuing an impermissible advisory opinion. It was speculative that an evenly divided Senate would vote along party lines. The circuit court could not intervene in the normal operating procedures of the Senate and enjoin one of the highest officials of the Commonwealth from performing his constitutional duties. The power of an injunction or a declaratory judgment action could not be used to force parties in disagreement to negotiate a compromise. There was no showing of irreparable harm because the Senator could not show he would actually suffer any harm at all. The Senator was unable to show that a balance of the equities weighs in his favor. Both sides could potentially be injured. The public interest would not be served by having a deadlocked Senate that was unable to agree on organization.


OUTCOME: The circuit court denied the Senator's motion for temporary injunction.
