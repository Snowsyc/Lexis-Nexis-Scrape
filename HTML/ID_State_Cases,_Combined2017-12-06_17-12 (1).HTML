PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the District Court of the Seventh Judicial District of the State of Idaho, Bonneville County. Honorable Ted V. Wood, District Judge.



DISPOSITION: The decision of the district court granting summary judgment in favor of the Idaho State Tax Commission is affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs appealed a decision of the District Court of the Seventh Judicial District, Bonneville County (Idaho), granting summary judgment in favor defendant government in an action alleging that the Petroleum Clean Water Trust Fund Act, Idaho Code §§ 41-4908 - 4909 (1990), was an unconstitutional and improper exercise of the legislature's authority.


OVERVIEW: The legislature passed the Petroleum Clean Water Trust Fund Act (the Act), Idaho Code §§ 41-4908 - 4909 (1990), to insure owners and operators of petroleum storage tanks against possible releases of petroleum from leaky storage tanks. The funds for the trust came from the imposition of a one cent per gallon "transfer fee" on all petroleum products delivered or stored in Idaho, imposed upon the first licensed distributor who received the product within the state. Plaintiff oil companies filed suit against defendant government, alleging that the Act was an unconstitutional and improper exercise of the legislature's authority. Plaintiffs appealed a trial court decision granting summary judgment in favor defendant. The appeals court affirmed because the legislation served the legitimate purpose of protecting the environment and was rationally related to the goal of minimizing the products' potential harmful effects. The court also held that the Act's classification scheme, distinguishing between off-road and on-road gas, was proper since it was rationally based on findings that 20 percent of all fuel was used for off-road purposes, while 80 percent was used on the highway.


OUTCOME: The appeals court affirmed the decision upholding the validity of the statute because the legislation served the legitimate purpose of protecting the environment, was rationally related to the government's goal of minimizing the potential harmful effects of petroleum products, and was not enacted in an irrational or arbitrary way.
