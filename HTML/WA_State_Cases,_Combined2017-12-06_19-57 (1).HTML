State v. Thompson, 2012 Wash. App. LEXIS 1428 (Wash. Ct. App., June 18, 2012)



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of orders from the King County Superior Court (Washington), which, in three separate trials, convicted him of assault in the first degree with sexual motivation, attempted indecent liberties, three counts of unlawful imprisonment with sexual motivation, rape, and murder in the first degree.


OVERVIEW: In three separate trials, defendant was convicted of numerous charges stemming from a series of violent crimes. On appeal, the court found that the trial court did not violate his Wash. Const. art. I, § 22 right to conflict-free counsel when it refused to appoint an attorney to replace his counsel. Communication between defendant and counsel collapsed early in their relationship. The trial court did not violate defendant's right to due process by ordering that he be held in restraints during all three trials. He had engaged in numerous episodes of assaultive or threatening behavior in the jail. He also repeatedly threatened to kill his attorney and the prosecutor.


OUTCOME: The judgments were affirmed.

PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from King County Superior Court. Docket No: 09-2-33205-3. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 11/18/2010. Judge signing: Honorable James D Cayce.



DISPOSITION: Reversed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant county court clerk sought review of a judgment from the King County Superior Court (Washington), which granted respondent tenants' motion to redact the record of an unlawful detainer action following a settlement of that action.


OVERVIEW: The tenants sought an order requiring the substitution of their initials for their full names in the court's records, alleging that they had been denied rental housing based upon the record of the unlawful detainer action. The court found the redaction order improper because it conflicted with both the constitutional presumption of open courts in Wash. Const. art. I, § 10 and the legislative policy determination in Wash. Rev. Code § 19.182.040(1)(b) allowing consumer reporting agencies to report lawsuits within seven years. Automatic limitations could not be placed on the openness of court proceedings; rather, a case-specific inquiry was required. Absent constitutional, statutory, or court rule protection for unlawful detainer defendants, such extraordinary relief as the tenants sought was inappropriate where that relief would thereafter be warranted for all similarly-situated litigants. The trial court overvalued the tenants' asserted interest when it found under Wash. Gen. R. 15(c)(2) that their interest outweighed the public's constitutional right of open access to court records. The settlement of the unlawful detainer action did not establish that it had been wrongfully filed.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the judgment.

Reversed by State v. Hinton, 2014 Wash. LEXIS 159 (Wash., Feb. 27, 2014)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Cowlitz Superior Court. Docket No: 09-1-01154-8. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 07/21/2010. Judge signing: Honorable James J Stonier.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of an order from the Cowlitz County Superior Court (Washington), which denied his motion to suppress and convicted him of attempted possession of heroin.


OVERVIEW: A police detective acquired the smartphone of a suspected drug dealer. While the phone was in the detective's possession, a text message from defendant appeared on its screen, asking the dealer to call him. Posing as the dealer, the detective replied to defendant's text message. The two men proceeded to exchange several text messages, eventually arranging a drug transaction, which led to defendant's conviction for attempted possession of heroin. On appeal, the court held that the detective did not violate Wash. Const. art. I, § 7 or the Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution, U.S. Const. amend. IV, when he used the dealer's phone to read and reply to the text messages defendant sent the dealer. Defendant's text messages to the dealer's phone were not private affairs. Defendant transmitted messages to a device over which he had no control. By doing so, defendant voluntarily ran the risk that his messages, once delivered, would be received by whomever possessed the phone, and he had no control over what that person might do with that message. The Fourth Amendment did not protect defendant's misplaced trust that the message actually would reach the intended recipient.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the judgment of the trial court.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant sought review of an order from the Cowlitz County Superior Court (Washington), which denied his motion to suppress and convicted him of attempted possession of heroin.


OVERVIEW: A police detective acquired the smartphone of a suspected drug dealer. The detective looked through the phone's contents and replied to a text message from defendant. Through a series of text messages from the dealer's phone, the detective and defendant agreed to meet for a drug transaction, which led to defendant's conviction for attempted possession of heroin. On appeal, defendant claimed that the trial court erred in denying his motion to suppress because the detective violated Washington's Privacy Act, Wash. Rev. Knudsen v. Wash. State Exec. Ethics Bd.


No. 26680-0-III


COURT OF APPEALS OF WASHINGTON, DIVISION THREE


156 Wn. App. 852; 235 P.3d 835; 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 1470



July 13, 2010, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Spokane Superior Court. Docket No: 07-2-01612-5. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 11/19/2007. Judge signing: Honorable Maryann C Moreno.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent Washington State Executive Ethics Board found that appellant college teacher violated Wash. Rev. Code §§ 42.52.160(1), 42.17.190 and Wash. Admin. Code § 292-110-010 by her use of state resources, her computer, for lobbying. The Spokane Superior Court (Washington) affirmed the Board's decision. The teacher appealed.


OVERVIEW: The teacher sent an email from a college computer that encouraged recipients to urge state legislators to approve bills that would provide tenure-like protections for certain teachers. The Board found that the teacher violated statutes and an administrative rule by using her state resources for lobbying. The trial court affirmed the Board's order and the court affirmed as well. Although the teacher argued that her email did not result in private benefit or gain for herself or others, Wash. Rev. Code § 42.52.160(1) did not require such, but only required that the email be sent for that purpose. The Board did not err in finding that the teacher violated the statute. The teacher's conduct constituted lobbying, for purposes of Wash. Rev. Code § 42.17.190 and former Wash. Rev. Code § 42.17.020(27), as she admitted she was trying to influence the passage of two bills by encouraging others to contact their legislators. The court agreed that it could not consider the constitutionality of the enforcement of the employer's policy because the Board's consideration was limited, but the court also determined the argument was unpersuasive on the merits.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed.

State v. Cross

COURT OF APPEALS OF WASHINGTON, DIVISION TWO


156 Wn. App. 568; 234 P.3d 288; 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 1385


February 23, 2010, Oral Argument 
June 29, 2010, Filed

As amended by order of the Court of Appeals July 1, 2010.


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Amended by State v. Cross, 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 1401 (Wash. Ct. App., June 29, 2010)
Review pending at State v. Cross, 2010 Wash. LEXIS 881 (Wash., Oct. 6, 2010)
Review granted by, in part, Remanded by State v. Cross, 172 Wn.2d 1009, 260 P.3d 208, 2011 Wash. LEXIS 726 (Wash., Sept. 7, 2011)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Pierce County Superior Court. Docket No: 08-1-01990-1. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 10/24/2008. Judge signing: Honorable Bryan E Chushcoff, Susan Serko, Lisa Worswick.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed a judgment of the Pierce County Superior Court (Washington) that convcited him of unlawful possession of a firearm, gross misdemeanor harassment, resisting arrest, and obstructing a law enforcement officer, arguing that the evidence was insufficient to support his gross misdemeanor harassment conviction.


OVERVIEW: A police officer effected a traffic stop and subsequently placed the driver under arrest. Defendant, a passenger in the vehicle, attempted to flee the scene. He was apprehended and, after a struggle with the police officer, was placed in handcuffs and seated in the patrol car. While in the back seat of the patrol car, defendant threatened to "kick [the officer's] ass." His threats earned him a charge of gross misdemeanor harassment in addition to the offenses for which he was arrested. On review, the court held that the evidence was sufficient to support defendant's harassment conviction under Wash. Rev. Code § 9A.46.020(1) because the officer took defendant's threats seriously in light of the fact that he had previously been assaulted by handcuffed individuals. The officer testified that a person in handcuffs could assault an officer without using his hands; such a person could, for example, kick or bite or headbutt the arresting officer. Defendant's threat, as well as his belligerent and assaultive conduct before and during his arrest, allowed the jury to find beyond a reasonable doubt that the officer's concern for his safety was reasonable.


OUTCOME: The judgment of conviction was affirmed.

Erdman v. Chapel Hill Presbyterian Church


No. 40247-5-II


COURT OF APPEALS OF WASHINGTON, DIVISION TWO


156 Wn. App. 827; 234 P.3d 299; 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 1379; 109 Fair Empl. Prac. Cas. (BNA) 1419


May 13, 2010, Oral Argument 
June 29, 2010, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Reconsideration denied by, Amended by Erdman v. Chapel Hill Presbyterian Church, 2010 Wash. App. LEXIS 1637 (Wash. Ct. App., June 29, 2010)
Review granted by Erdman v. Chapel Hill Presbyterian Church, 170 Wn.2d 1010, 245 P.3d 772, 2010 Wash. LEXIS 1041 (2010)
Reversed by, Remanded by Erdman v. Chapel Hill Presbyterian Church, 2012 Wash. LEXIS 672 (Wash., Oct. 4, 2012)


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] 
Appeal from Pierce County Superior Court. Docket No: 08-2-09228-9. Judgment or order under review. Date filed: 03/27/2009. Judge signing: Honorable R Worswick Lisa.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant employee challenged an order from the Pierce County Superior Court (Washington), which dismissed her claims against respondents, a church and its pastor, for negligent infliction of emotional distress, wrongful discharge, retaliation, harassment, negligent supervision and retention, violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 U.S.C.S. § 2000e-2(a)(1), and violation of the Washington Law Against Discrimination (WLAD).


OVERVIEW: The employee believed that tours of religious and historical sites led by the pastor possibly jeopardized the church's tax exempt status. After she was terminated, she brought an action against respondents. The trial court dismissed the claims. On appeal, the court found that because inquiries into "the mind of the church" created an unconstitutional government entanglement with the church, the trial court did not abuse its discretion in foreclosing inquiry into the church investigative committee's thought processes during discovery. Further, an employment decision by the church session committee recommending the employee's termination based on an evaluation of her actions did not give rise to a negligent infliction of emotional distress claim against the church. However, the trial court erred in dismissing the employee's claims for the pastor's negligent infliction of emotional distress based on his abusive behavior toward her. The ecclesiastical abstention doctrine did not bar such a claim against him. Finally, the plain language in WLAD's religious employer exemption, Wash. Rev. Code § 49.60.040(11), barred the employee's harassment and wrongful discharge claims.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the trial court's discovery rulings and the dismissal of the claims against the church for negligent infliction of emotional distress, wrongful discharge, retaliation, and violation of WLAD. The court reversed the dismissal of the negligent infliction of emotional distress claim against the pastor. The court reversed the dismissal of the Title VII claims against the church. The court remanded the case for further proceedings.
