CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The Maryland State Ethics Commission determined that appellant lobbyist knowingly and willingly violated Md. Code Ann., State Gov't § 15-713(1) (2004), by being engaged for lobbying purposes for compensation that was contingent upon executive or legislative action, and, pursuant to Md. Code Ann., State Gov't § 15-405(e), sanctioned the lobbyist. The Circuit Court for Howard County (Maryland) affirmed. The lobbyist appealed.


OVERVIEW: The lobbyist was engaged by a businessman to lobby for a business entity. The lobbyist registered as a lobbyist for the business entity and billed for legislative expenses under a contract which included a fee agreement, the language of which provided for compensation of one percent of all first year receivables for each facility, location, or contract the lobbyist was involved in securing for the business entity with any government entity, unit, or agency in the State of Maryland. The lobbyist insisted that the language regarding compensation was added to the fee agreement at the businessman's request, but the lobbyist did not call the businessman to testify. On appeal, the court found that (1) the Commission's findings were supported by substantial evidence; (2) the lobbyist's registration was required under Md. Code Ann., State Gov't § 15-701; and (3) the Commission did not improperly apply Md. Code Ann., State Gov't § 15-405(e) retroactively to sanction the lobbyist. Additionally, the Commission did not err by drawing an adverse inference under the missing man rule from the businessman's failure to testify.


OUTCOME: The judgment of the circuit court was affirmed.

COSTS TO BE PAID FIFTY PER-CENT BY APPELLANTS AND FIFTY PERCENT BY THE INDIVIDUAL APPELLEES.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant property owners requested three variances from a county board of appeals to construct a home on their property. Appellees, the county and the neighbors, objected to the variances. The board denied the variances. The owners timely appealed the board's decision to the Circuit Court for Anne Arundel County (Maryland). The circuit court affirmed the board's decision, denying the requested variances. The owners appealed.


OVERVIEW: The owners requested the variances to permit them to build a home on one of their two property lots. Two of the owners' requests were for variances from the Maryland critical area program. The third request was for a variance from a setback requirement under general zoning requirements. On appeal, the court found that the board applied the correct law in interpreting Anne Arundel County, Md., Code art. 3, § 2-107(b) (1985) (current version at Anne Arundel County, Md., Code art. 3, § 1-207) as requiring compliance with all of the criteria under Md. Therefore, on remand, the board was to provide an explanation in determining whether the owners' proposal met the variance criteria and, specifically, whether they would be denied reasonable and significant use of the entire lot. The board was also to consider one owners' likely disability, if any, and make appropriate findings.


OUTCOME: The circuit court's judgment was reversed, and the matter was remanded to the circuit court with instructions to vacate the decision of the board and to remand the matter to the board for further proceedings.

Comptroller of the Treasury v. Clise Coal, Inc.

DISPOSITION:  [***1] JUDGMENT OF THE CIRCUIT COURT FOR ALLEGANY COUNTY REVERSED TO THE EXTENT THAT IT REVERSED THE TAX COURT; OTHERWISE AFFIRMED. COSTS TO BE PAID BY APPELLEE/CROSS-APPELLANT.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, the Comptroller of the Treasury, challenged the order of the Circuit Court for Allegany County (Maryland), which reduced a motor fuel tax assessment against appellee, a coal company, from $ 9,036.28 to $ 5,491.90. On cross-appeal, appellee challenged the methodology used by appellant and asserted that the Maryland Tax Court abused its discretion by denying its request for a jury trial.


OVERVIEW: Appellee held a special fuel user license that allowed it to purchase fuel in bulk without having to pay motor fuel tax to its seller. Each month, appellee was required to file a return in appellant's office setting forth the amount of taxable fuel used and pay the tax on that fuel. In making the reduction, the trial court reasoned that a portion of the assessment, affirmed by the Tax Court, was not supported by substantial evidence. The court held that there was substantial evidence in the record to support appellant's assessment based on the testimony of appellant's inspector, who testified that two of appellee's drivers were found using untaxed dyed fuel on the highway during a random inspection. The only evidence appellee introduced to demonstrate that appellant's assessment was incorrect was the testimony of its two principals, which the Tax Court properly found insufficient. The court also upheld appellant's determination that appellee's records were inadequate and that the methodology used in calculating the assessment was correct. Appellee failed to show any abuse of discretion on the part of the Tax Court in denying its request for a jury trial.


OUTCOME: The court reversed, in part, the order of the trial court reducing the motor fuel tax assessment and affirmed, in part, the order denying appellee's request for a jury trial. The court's decision affirmed the Tax Court's decision in its entirety.

Dove v. Childs


No. 233, September Term, 2006


COURT OF SPECIAL APPEALS OF MARYLAND


173 Md. App. 602; 920 A.2d 511; 2007 Md. App. LEXIS 51



April 4, 2007, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Circuit Court for Howard County. Diane O. Leasure, JUDGE.



DISPOSITION:  [***1] JUDGMENT AFFIRMED. COSTS TO BE PAID BY APPELLANTS.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: After giving notice of their intention to close a mobile home park, appellee park owners filed holdover proceedings against appellant tenants. The Circuit Court for Howard County (Maryland) granted the owners summary judgment, holding that the rental agreements had terminated, the tenants had received proper notice, and the owners' action did not constitute a retaliatory eviction. The tenants appealed.


OVERVIEW: The tenants' claims were not moot even though the park had closed and the restitution of possession judgment had been enforced as the tenants had pending claims against the owners in another case. Moreover, the issues were important to the public and were likely to recur in the future with respect to other parks. The decision to close the park did not amount to a change in use under Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. 8A-202(c)(3) as the clear intent in enacting that provision was to provide park residents protection only as long as the property was used as a mobile home park. Thus, Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 8A-1201 did not apply. The owners had satisfied all of the elements of Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 8A-1702(b)(2) where they gave the tenants a one-year notice of the park closure and the rental agreements had terminated no later than the date the park had closed. The retaliatory eviction provisions in Md. Code Ann., Real Prop. § 8A-1301 and Howard County, Ind. Code § 16.516 did not apply as the owners had a right to cease operating as a mobile home park upon giving the requisite notice. At the end of the notice term, they had the right to initiate tenant holdover proceedings.


OUTCOME: The judgment was affirmed. Costs were to be paid by the tenants.

PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Circuit Court of Washington County. John H. McDowell, JUDGE.



DISPOSITION:  [***1] JUDGMENT AFFIRMED. COSTS TO BE PAID BY APPELLANT.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: The circuit court (Maryland) granted defendant city's motion for summary judgment on appellant adult business's claim that the city's adult business ordinance violated the adult business's free speech rights under U.S. Const. amend. I and Md. Const. Decl. Rights art. 40. The adult business appealed.


OVERVIEW: The ordinance prohibited adult businesses from completely closing any area used for viewing motion pictures and set forth requirements for denying an adult business license. The adult business, which was granted a license pursuant to the ordinance, did not dispute the circuit court's ruling that the ordinance was a content-neutral time, place, and manner restriction that left open ample alternative channels for communication. The adult business argued that the ordinance was not narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest because the ordinance's legislative record contained no preenactment evidence demonstrating that the ordinance was intended to address specific negative secondary effects. The court held that the ordinance was constitutional because (1) the city relied on reports from citizens regarding illegal drug activity and sexual activity at the adult business and on the observations of city officials concerning dangerous conditions on the premises and (2) the evidence the city relied upon was reasonably believed to be relevant to the ordinance's purposes of promoting safe and sanitary conditions and combating the spread of sexually transmitted disease.


OUTCOME: The court affirmed the circuit court's opinion.
