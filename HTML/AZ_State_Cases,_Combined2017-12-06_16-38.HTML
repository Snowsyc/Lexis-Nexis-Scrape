State v. Wolter


1 CA-CR 98-0971


COURT OF APPEALS OF ARIZONA, DIVISION ONE, DEPARTMENT A


197 Ariz. 190; 3 P.3d 1110; 2000 Ariz. App. LEXIS 7; 313 Ariz. Adv. Rep. 10



January 20, 2000, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Petition for Review DENIED on September 26, 2000 by Arizona Supreme Court CR-00-0134-PR.


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Superior Court of Maricopa County. Cause No. CR 98-06895. The Honorable Thomas W. O'Toole, Judge.



DISPOSITION: AFFIRMED AS MODIFIED; REMANDED FOR RESENTENCING.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant appealed his Superior Court of Maricopa County (Arizona) conviction of one count of controlling property of another, knowing or having reason to know that the property was stolen under Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 13-1802(A)(5), a class 4 felony pursuant to Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 13-1802(D).


OVERVIEW: Defendant was convicted of class 4 felony theft of property he knew or had reason to know was stolen, after purchasing a motorcycle at a swap meet. The amount defendant paid for the motorcycle was undisputed, but the jury was instructed to value the property, pursuant to Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 13-1801, on the date it was originally stolen approximately a year earlier. The court upheld the conviction, holding that the trial court's instruction regarding valuation was not an unconstitutional comment on the evidence, but that it was an incorrect statement of the law that required remanding for resentencing as a class 6 felony. The trial court incorrectly interpreted the date for determining value, which properly was the date that defendant committed the theft. This interpretation harmonized applicable statutes, was fairer to defendant, made the punishment fit the crime, and comported with the rule of lenity.


OUTCOME: Conviction was affirmed, but remanded for resentencing; jury instruction that property should be valued on date of original theft, not date defendant obtained the property, was not an improper comment on the evidence, but was an incorrect statutory interpretation. Valuing the property on date defendant committed the theft was fairer.

In re Roy L.


1 CA-JV 99-0097


COURT OF APPEALS OF ARIZONA, DIVISION ONE, DEPARTMENT D


197 Ariz. 441; 4 P.3d 984; 2000 Ariz. App. LEXIS 4; 312 Ariz. Adv. Rep. 19



January 13, 2000, Filed


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY:  [***1] Amended By Order Filed February 3, 2000.


PRIOR HISTORY: Appeal from the Superior Court of Maricopa County. Cause No. JV 139074. The Honorable Eileen Willett, Commissioner.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant, a juvenile offender, appealed from orders entered by the Superior Court of Maricopa County (Arizona), which denied his motions to suppress and for judgment of acquittal, and from his adjudication as a delinquent.


OVERVIEW: Appellant, a juvenile, appealed from the denials of his motions to suppress and for judgment of acquittal and from his adjudication as a delinquent. Appellant argued that the investigatory stop was an unreasonable seizure under U.S. Const. amend. IV because the officer lacked reasonable suspicion of any criminal activity. When the officer drove to the market, the juvenile seemed to be trying to avoid him, which coupled with the unidentified student's statement to the security guard, gave the officer reasonable suspicion to stop and question the juvenile. Appellant also claimed that his motion for acquittal should have been granted because the State failed to prove beyond a reasonable doubt all the elements of Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 13-3111(A), possession of a firearm by a minor. The court held that the State was not required to prove operability of the firearm as an element of the offense.


OUTCOME: The adjudication of delinquency and disposition were affirmed. The trial court did not err in denying the juvenile's motions to suppress and for judgment of acquittal. The police officer had reasonable suspicion to conduct a Terry stop.
