Matter of Keith B. (Stephanie W.)


[NO NUMBER IN ORIGINAL]


FAMILY COURT OF NEW YORK, CLINTON COUNTY


29 Misc. 3d 969; 908 N.Y.S.2d 559; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4690; 2010 NY Slip Op 20401



September 30, 2010, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Neglect action against respondent mother by petitioner County Department of Social Services; the issue was raised as to whether placement should be with the father or a different dispositional alternative.


OVERVIEW: Based upon the mother's voluntary admissions, the court found that the child was in fact a neglected child as that term was defined by Family Ct Act § 1012 and that the mother was the person responsible for the neglect. Subsequently, a dispositional hearing was held and the issue was raised regarding placing the child with his father, who had not been named as a respondent. The court found, inter alia, that the father had had extremely limited (if any) contact with the child. Because the child had never resided in the father's home, an order of disposition placing the child with someone other than the father would not be an order removing the child from his father's home. In addition, there were no limitations in Family Ct Act §§ 1027(b)(i), 1052, 1055 that prohibited placement of the child with someone other than the father. Accordingly, the court concluded that it had options under Family Ct Act § 1035(d)(ii) to determine the child's placement. Although the cases were distinguished, the court respectfully suggested that the limitations imposed by Telsa Z., be reconsidered.


OUTCOME: The court concluded that it could issue an order of disposition that served the best interest of the child.


HEADNOTES

Parent and Child -- Abused or Neglected Child -- Disposition on Adjudication -- Family Court Not Obligated to Place Neglected Child with Nonrespondent Parent

Where respondent mother had been found to have neglected her son in a child protective proceeding without any similar finding of neglect having been made against the biological father, who did not reside with respondent and their child and had only extremely limited contact with the child, Family Court was not obligated to place the child with the nonrespondent father if it determined that it was in the best interest of the child to select a different dispositional alternative pursuant to Family Court Act § 1052. Since the child never resided with the nonrespondent father, an order of disposition placing the child with the Commissioner of the local Department of Social Services or someone other than the father would not be an order removing the child from his father's home and thus would not violate the holding in Matter of Telsa Z. (Rickey Z.--Denise Z.) (71 AD3d 1246, 897 NYS2d 281). The limitations on placement imposed by Matter of Telsa Z. where the neglected child resides in the home of a nonrespondent parent should be reconsidered to allow the Family Court, after making a finding that a child has been neglected or abused upon a full fact-finding hearing, to issue any statutorily authorized dispositional order that serves the best interest of the child.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondents, city human resources administration (HRA) and Office of Temporary Disability and Assistance (OTDA), filed motions to dismiss petitioner mother's CPLR art. 78 petition--regarding their decisions as to her eligibility to receive child care services--for lack of ripeness and failure to exhaust her administrative remedies.


OVERVIEW: After HRA denied the mother's application for benefits, OTDA found that she was eligible for benefits. The gravamen of the mother's petition was that 18 NYCRR 385.4(a)(1)(ii) conflicted with Social Services Law §§ 330 and 332-a. The court found, inter alia, that the purported conflict between the regulation and the statutes did not necessarily lead to the conclusion that they were inconsistent or that the mother would be entitled to child care benefits. The ODTA was entitled to an opportunity to interpret the regulation and determine if it could be reconciled with the statutes. Accordingly, because the mother failed to exhaust her administrative remedies, she was not entitled to art. 78 relief.


OUTCOME: The motions were granted, the petition was denied, and the proceeding was dismissed without prejudice.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Administrative Law - Failure to Exhaust Administrative Remedies - Termination of Public Assistance Recipient's Child Care Benefits 

A CPLR article 78 proceeding commenced by petitioner, a public assistance recipient with three minor children and a health-related disability, challenging the determination of respondent New York City Human Resources Administration (HRA) to terminate her child care benefits while she was enrolled as a student in a licensed practical nursing program because she was not engaged in a work-related activity was dismissed due to petitioner's failure to exhaust available administrative remedies before the New York State Office of Temporary Disability and Assistance (OTDA). HRA's determination followed OTDA's fair hearing decision in petitioner's favor which ruled that HRA's initial determination to terminate petitioner's child care benefits was "not correct," and directed HRA to provide petitioner with those benefits "if so eligible." Under these circumstances, although petitioner was not required to seek another fair hearing to review HRA's determination, petitioner should have filed a complaint with the Commissioner of OTDA pursuant to 18 NYCRR 358-6.4 (c) challenging the adequacy of HRA's compliance with the fair hearing decision before seeking judicial intervention. OTDA's expertise was required to determine whether the regulation relied upon by HRA in terminating petitioner's child care benefits (see 18 NYCRR 385.4 [a] [1] [ii]) conflicted with Social Services Law §332-a. Furthermore, there was no evidence that filing a complaint with the Commissioner of OTDA would be futile.

Matter of DiPietro v New York State Bd. of Elections


2010-9997


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, ERIE COUNTY


30 Misc. 3d 449; 911 N.Y.S.2d 762; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 5254; 2010 NY Slip Op 20435



September 27, 2010, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner candidate challenged a determination by defendant state board of elections that voided his nominating petitions pursuant to Election Law § 16-102 for insufficient signatures.


OVERVIEW: The candidate submitted a petition for the independent nomination for the Tea Party for the office of the New York State Senate. The court found, inter alia, that the effect of removing the phrase "who also is qualified to sign the petition" from Election Law § 6-140(1)(b) was to remove the only basis for the requirement that a witness not have signed an earlier, valid petition. The only requirement remaining for a witness to a nominating petition was to be a duly qualified voter of the state. Therefore, after restoring the erroneously voided signatures, the nominating petition contained signatures in excess of the number required by § 16-102. Consequently, the candidate's name was to be restored to the ballot for the office he sought.


OUTCOME: The determination was reversed, and the candidate's name was restored to the ballot.


HEADNOTES

Elections -- Nominating Petitions -- Qualification of Witness

1. The Board of Elections improperly invalidated signatures on a nominating petition on the ground that the witnesses had previously signed a designating petition for the same office. The effect of the Legislature removing the phrase "who also is qualified to sign the petition" from Election Law §6-140 (1) (b) was to remove the only basis for the requirement that a witness not have signed an earlier, valid petition. It is clear that the only requirement now for a witness to a nominating petition is to be a duly qualified voter of the state. To require more is to attempt to regraft requirements from a phrase that is no longer part of the applicable statute. There are anomalies in the statute, but to continue a restriction without a basis would do violence to the plain meaning of the statute.

Elections -- Nominating Petitions -- Residence of Witness

2. Signatures on a nominating petition witnessed by two people were improperly voided by the Board of Elections on the ground that the address given on their sheets was different from the address in the voting records. They lived in the same house, but the county had changed the address to facilitate 911 responses, which resulted in their residence having two addresses. They still received mail from the county Board of Elections at their new address. This was not a case where a witness had moved. Therefore, they had provided the correct address of their residence on the nominating petition and the signatures they witnessed should not have been stricken.

Elections -- Nominating Petitions -- Residence of Witness

3. Signatures on a nominating petition witnessed by a person who had married and moved into his new address in late November of 2009, but witnessed the signatures at issue in mid-August 2010 and used his new address, which was different from his old address, were properly stricken. The witness was not in the process of moving from one address to another during the signature-gathering period, and the concerns of rapid and efficient verification of signatures within the restrictive time periods imposed by the Election Law and facilitating the discovery of fraud were raised by the discrepancy in his addresses.

Matter of Kaminski G.


D-16836/10


FAMILY COURT OF NEW YORK, QUEENS COUNTY


29 Misc. 3d 805; 908 N.Y.S.2d 328; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4336; 2010 NY Slip Op 20366



September 2, 2010, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Respondent juvenile filed a Family Ct Act § 315.1(1)(b) motion to dismiss a petition charging him with acts that, were he an adult, would constitute the crimes of, inter alia, attempted first-degree robbery.


OVERVIEW: The petition alleged that the juvenile possessed, used, or threatened the immediate use of a dangerous instrument, a baseball bat, in order to effectuate the forcible taking of the victim's property. The family court found, inter alia, that while the petition as a whole contained non-hearsay factual allegations that, if true, established the commission of the crime of first-degree robbery, a juvenile offense, the presentment agency only charged the juvenile with an attempt to commit that crime, which was not a juvenile offense. Accordingly, pursuant to § 315.1(1)(b), dismissal of the petition was required for lack of jurisdiction.


OUTCOME: The petition was dismissed.


HEADNOTES

Courts -- Family Court -- Lack of Original Jurisdiction Over Acts for Which Juvenile Subject to Criminal Prosecution

Family Court lacked original subject matter jurisdiction over a juvenile delinquency petition charging the 14-year-old respondent with committing acts constituting the crimes of attempted robbery in the first degree and other completed offenses since the nonhearsay factual allegations, if true, established that respondent committed acts constituting the completed crime of robbery in the first degree. Family Court has exclusive jurisdiction over a juvenile delinquent, who, by definition, is a person over 7 and less than 16 years of age and who, having committed acts that would constitute a crime if committed by an adult, is not criminally responsible for such conduct by reason of infancy or is the defendant in an action ordered removed from a criminal court pursuant to CPL article 725 (Family Ct Act § 301.2 [1]). Juveniles between the ages of 13 and 15 who are charged with certain enumerated, serious crimes of violence are "juvenile offenders." Juvenile offenders are excluded from the definition of juvenile delinquents and are deprived of the defense of infancy and subject to criminal prosecution for the commission of an enumerated crime. A 14 year old who is criminally responsible for acts constituting first-degree robbery is a juvenile offender. (See Penal Law § 10.00 [18]; § 30.00 [2]; CPL 1.20 [42].) To allow the presentment agency here to draft the accusatory instrument in order to evade limitations placed upon Family Court's jurisdiction would impermissibly permit a litigant to confer subject matter jurisdiction upon a court which is not vested with original jurisdiction over the proceeding. Accordingly, the juvenile delinquency petition was dismissed as jurisdictionally defective (see Family Ct Act § 315.1).

People v Laureano


2010CN002635


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


29 Misc. 3d 664; 906 N.Y.S.2d 753; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4073; 2010 NY Slip Op 20347



August 23, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: As Corrected September 8, 2010


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed a motion for an order dismissing the charge of criminal trespass that was filed against him as facially insufficient pursuant to CPL 170.30 (1) (a), 100.15 (3), and 100.40 (1) (a), and for other relief; defendant claimed that the count failed to make out lack of permission or authority to enter or remain, an element of all trespass offenses.


OVERVIEW: A police officer observed defendant enter a subway station through the turnstiles and gates by using a student MetroCard that was issued for the exclusive use of another person. Defendant told the officer that he had borrowed his sister's card to take his girl to school. Defendant claimed that the subway area beyond the turnstiles was a public place because the transit authority was a public entity and therefore he had a right to be there unless he lawfully and personally had been ordered to leave. The court disagreed. While the subway system was publicly owned, offered as a public service, and was created to perform a governmental function, the transit authority required that a fare to use the subway system be paid at a turnstile. Consequently, the area beyond the turnstiles was not open to members of the public--unless they swiped a valid fare card to pass inside. Therefore, entry into the subway system beyond the turnstiles without paying the fare constituted third-degree criminal trespass under Penal Law § 140.10. Consequently, he was not entitled to relief §§ 170.30 (1) (a), 100.15 (3), and 100.40 (1) (a). However, he was entitled to Mapp/Dunaway and Huntley hearings.


OUTCOME: The motion to dismiss the trespass count was denied; Mapp/Dunaway and Huntley hearings were to be conducted, the motion to reserve the right to make further motions was denied, and the People's motion to compel limited reciprocal discovery was granted.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Criminal Trespass -- Entering Subway System without Paying Required Fare

Defendant could lawfully be prosecuted for criminal trespass in the third degree (Penal Law § 140.10) based upon an accusatory instrument alleging that a police officer observed him enter a subway station beyond the turnstiles using a nontransferable student MetroCard belonging to another person. The statute proscribes knowingly entering or remaining unlawfully (see Penal Law § 140.00 [5]) in a building or upon real property "which is fenced or otherwise enclosed in a manner designed to exclude intruders." Although the subway system is publicly owned, the New York City Transit Authority is empowered to require that a fare be paid to use those facilities (see Public Authorities Law § 1204 [5-a]). The presence of a turnstile inside a subway station makes the real property within "fenced or otherwise enclosed in a manner designed to exclude" persons who have not paid a fare. The area beyond the turnstiles is not open to members of the public unless they swipe a valid fare card to pass inside. Consequently, entry into the subway system beyond the turnstiles without paying the required fare constitutes criminal trespass in the third degree, and the accusatory instrument was thus facially sufficient for pleading purposes.

People v Larsen


2010CN002426


CRIMINAL COURT OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


29 Misc. 3d 423; 906 N.Y.S.2d 709; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3563; 2010 NY Slip Op 20300



July 30, 2010, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants filed motions to dismiss the complaints against them for facial insufficiency pursuant to CPL 170.30; defendants claimed that their activity fell within the First Amendment exceptions in Administrative Code of the City of NY § 20-453.


OVERVIEW: Defendants were each charged with violating § 20-453, for displaying and offering for sale condoms on the street without a general vendor license. The condoms contained written messages that tied safe sex with safe political practices. The court found, inter alia, that although the condoms communicated messages arguably designed to stimulate political awareness and conversation, the political references were used as a marketing device to boost sales. Accordingly, the condoms did not fall within the First Amendment exceptions to § 20-453.


OUTCOME: The motions to dismiss were denied in the entirety.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law -- Freedom of Speech -- Street Sale of Message-Bearing Novelty Condoms without Vendor's License

Topical novelty condoms sold in message-bearing wrappers with the names and images of well-known political figures were not expressive items entitled to First Amendment protection so as to exempt street vendors of those items from New York City's general vendor licensing statute (Administrative Code of City of NY § 20-453). The products at issue do not fall within the statute's First Amendment exemptions from the licensing requirement for vendors of written materials such as newspapers and books and "other similar written matter," as well as vendors of various nonwritten objects such as paintings, photographs, prints and sculptures. Defendants' merchandise had a dominant nonexpressive purpose, with political references being used merely as a marketing device to boost sales rather than to promote any ideas or viewpoints. As such, the items were "commercial goods" that fell outside the scope of the First Amendment. Furthermore, the licensing statute, as applied, constituted a valid "time, place or manner" restriction on expression that was narrowly tailored to serve the significant governmental interest in keeping the public streets free of congestion and maintaining the City's tax base and economic viability. The statute provided defendants with ample alternative channels of communication aside from unlicensed street sales.

DISTRICT COURT OF NEW YORK, FIRST DISTRICT, NASSAU COUNTY


29 Misc. 3d 474; 907 N.Y.S.2d 563; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3567; 2010 NY Slip Op 20305



July 12, 2010, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Related proceeding at Levine v Traffic & Parking Violation Agency for Nassau County, 29 Misc. 3d 1205A, 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4740 (N.Y. Sup. Ct., Sept. 27, 2010)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Movant owner filed a CPL 170.40 application for an order dismissing two notices of liability in the interests of justice.


OVERVIEW: The owner received two notices that he was liable to pay fines of $ 50 after the driver of his vehicle did not stop at red lights. The court found, inter alia, that it lacked jurisdiction to issue a declaration regarding the legality of the current system for appointing judicial hearing officers to the Traffic and Parking Violations Agency. The court was aware of no legal requirement, and the owner cited none, that motorists be warned that a particular intersection was watched by a "red light camera" or that the owner be supplied with a sworn or affirmed certificate by a technician to serve as prima facie evidence of the facts contained therein. the owner's claims, regarding the time the yellow light lasted or that the light otherwise malfunctioned, were defenses more properly raised at the hearing on the merits. Inasmuch as the owner did not face a criminal conviction or conviction under the Vehicle and Traffic Law, his due process violation claim lacked merit. Since a notice of liability was not an accusatory instrument, the ultimate relief the owner sought was not available under § 170.40. the owner's remaining contentions were considered and found to be without merit.


OUTCOME: The application was denied.


HEADNOTES

Motor Vehicles -- Traffic Infractions -- Red Light Camera Program - Dismissal of Notices of Liability in Interests of Justice Not Available

Defendant was not entitled to dismissal, in the interests of justice on a motion made pursuant to CPL 170.40 to the president of the Board of the Judges of the District Court of Nassau County (see UDCA 2406), of two notices of liability sent by mail by the "Red Light Camera Division" of the Nassau County Parking and Traffic Violations Agency advising him that his vehicle was recorded passing a red light by an authorized red light camera (see Vehicle and Traffic Law § 1111-b; Local Law No. 12-2009 of County of Nassau). The extraordinary relief of dismissal in the interests of justice is available only in criminal cases commenced by the filing of an accusatory instrument (see CPL 170.30 [1] [g]). Since a notice of liability is not an accusatory instrument, the ultimate relief sought by defendant was not available. Furthermore, the court lacked jurisdiction to issue a declaratory ruling on defendant's claims regarding the validity of the red light camera program and the alleged improper appointment of judicial hearing officers to conduct hearings under the program. Defendant's remaining constitutional claims regarding the program lacked merit.

New York State Assn. of Enrolled Agents, Inc. v New York State Dept. of Taxation & Fin.

SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


29 Misc. 3d 332; 905 N.Y.S.2d 856; 2010 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3295; 2010 NY Slip Op 20293

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff association sought a judgment declaring that Tax Law § 32 was unconstitutional and that defendant department was not allowed to not require enrolled agents or their employees to register with the department or to pay any fees pursuant to § 32. The department moved to dismiss the complaint for failure to state a cause of action and sought a judgment declaring that § 32 was constitutional.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff owner brought a hybrid CPLR art. 78 proceeding against defendants city and New York City Department of Buildings (New York) (DOB), and others which, inter alia, challenged a declaration of immediate emergency relating to the owner's building and building violations and sought injunctive relief restraining further demolition of the building and enforcement of DOB actions. The city and the DOB moved to dismiss.


OVERVIEW: During the DOB's inspection of the building, it relied on the expertise of the Emergency Response Team and its weekend-duty engineer, who recommended that an immediate emergency be issued and that the building be demolished to a safe level. The court found that this was proper. The record reflected that the DOB followed necessary notice procedures for emergency demolition. The owner presented only evidence in the form of testimony by its engineer, whose statements that the building was structurally sound were based only on inspection after the top two floors had been demolished. A discrepancy in the professional opinions of the engineers alone did not invalidate the DOB's administrative determination. Absent a clear showing by the owner that the determination of the DOB was arbitrary and capricious or irrational, such determination should not have been disturbed. The evidence provided a rational basis for the DOB's determination that the building was in immediate danger of collapse. Given the exigent circumstances presented by the building's condition, the owner was not deprived of due process merely because it was not afforded any pre-deprivation notice or hearing.


OUTCOME: The motion to dismiss the petition/complaint for failure to state a claim and for lack of ripeness and jurisdiction was granted in its entirety.


HEADNOTES

Municipal Corporations -- Unsafe Buildings -- Emergency Demolition

1. The determination of the New York City Department of Buildings (DOB) declaring plaintiffs' five-story building to be in imminent danger of collapse and authorizing the immediate demolition of the top two floors of the building in order to reduce the building to a safe level was reasonable, rational and in compliance with law. DOB was entitled to rely on the expertise and experience of the emergency response team that inspected the building and its weekend-duty engineer, who recommended that an immediate emergency be issued. The fact plaintiffs' engineer gave a contrary professional opinion as to the condition of the building by itself was insufficient to invalidate DOB's administrative determination in the absence of a clear showing by plaintiffs that DOB's determination was arbitrary and capricious or in any way irrational. Moreover, the record demonstrated that DOB followed necessary notice procedures for emergency demolition (see NY City Charter § 643; Administrative Code of City of NY former §§ 26-235, 26-243, 26-127). Under these circumstances, the affidavit of plaintiffs' engineer was insufficient to raise a triable issue of fact requiring a trial pursuant to CPLR 7804 (h). Issues such as whether the building was actually in imminent danger of collapse and whether demolition was the only appropriate remedy were discretionary determinations of DOB entitled to deference.

Municipal Corporations -- Unsafe Buildings -- Emergency Demolition -- Due Process

2. Plaintiffs failed to state any due process claims regarding the constitutional sufficiency of the notice used to apprise them of the rationally-based determination of the New York City Department of Buildings (DOB) declaring their five-story building to be in imminent danger of collapse and authorizing the immediate demolition of the top two floors of the building in order to reduce the building to a safe level. The demolition of a building for the purpose of dealing with an emergency without a prior hearing does not give rise to a due process claim as long as there is a postdeprivation remedy. Here, given the exigent circumstances presented by the building's imminent danger of collapse, which necessitated prompt action by DOB, plaintiffs were not deprived of due process merely because they were not afforded any predeprivation notice or hearing. Rather, plaintiffs' opportunity to contest the propriety of the agency determination and seek money damages in a state court action provided them adequate postdeprivation remedies.

Constitutional Law -- Taking of Property -- Emergency Demolition of Unsafe Building

3. Plaintiffs failed to state a claim to recover damages for an unconstitutional "taking" with respect to the rationally-based determination of the New York City Department of Buildings (DOB) declaring their five-story building to be in imminent danger of collapse and authorizing the immediate demolition of the top two floors of the building in order to reduce the building to a safe level. The municipal demolition of an imminently dangerous structure in order to protect the public is an exercise of the police power and does not constitute a "taking." Furthermore, the court was required to defer to DOB's discretion in determining that the building was in imminent danger of collapse and required emergency demolition, as supported by the record.

Courts -- Ripeness Doctrine -- Claims Arising from Emergency Demolition of Unsafe Building -- Failure to Exhaust Administrative Remedies

4. Plaintiffs' claims against the City of New York and various municipal agencies based upon their potential liability arising from the partial demolition of their five-story building pursuant to the determination of the City's Department of Buildings (DOB) that the building was in imminent danger of collapse and required emergency demolition were not ripe for judicial review and were dismissed as premature. Despite their claims for monetary damages, plaintiffs were in fact seeking judicial review of administrative actions without first availing themselves of all administrative remedies. With regard to liens placed against the property by the Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) based upon the partial demolition of the building, plaintiffs failed to submit the required notice (see Administrative Code of City of NY § 27-2129), and thus could not contest the HPD's emergency repair charges in a judicial or administrative proceeding. Similarly, plaintiffs failed to file any administrative appeals challenging the propriety of DOB's issuance of notices of violation regarding the building. Furthermore, plaintiffs' unspecified claims against the New York City Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) likewise failed because LPC had yet to pursue any claims against plaintiffs based on the building and its demolition. Under these circumstances, it could not be said that any of the involved agencies rendered a final determination from which plaintiffs could appeal in a court of law.

Municipal Corporations -- Tort Liability -- Immunity for Discretionary Acts -- Claims Arising from Emergency Demolition of Unsafe Building

5. Plaintiffs could not recover money damages from the City of New York based upon the partial demolition of their five-story building pursuant to the determination of the City's Department of Buildings (DOB) that the building was in imminent danger of collapse and required emergency demolition. DOB's determination that an immediate emergency existed, warranting demolition of the building, was a discretionary act entitling the city defendants to full immunity from liability. DOB's determination could not be deemed to be ministerial merely because DOB was required to follow a prescribed set of procedures before authorizing an emergency demolition (see Administrative Code of City of NY former § 26-236). The determination of an emergency itself relied on the judgment of professionals, and was not improper merely because others would have reached a different conclusion as to the structural soundness of the building.

Torts -- Trespass -- Municipality's Emergency Demolition of Unsafe Building

6. Plaintiffs failed to state a trespass claim against the City of New York based upon the partial demolition of their five-story building pursuant to the determination of the City's Department of Buildings that the building was in imminent danger of collapse and required emergency demolition where it had been determined that the demolition of the building was not arbitrary and capricious, and therefore not unlawful.

Indemnity -- When Claim for Indemnification Available -- Municipality's Emergency Demolition of Unsafe Building

7. Plaintiffs failed to state a common-law claim for indemnification against the City of New York for claims raised by tenants in their five-story building that was partially demolished pursuant to the determination of the City's Department of Buildings that the building was in imminent danger of collapse and required emergency demolition. In order to state a claim for common-law indemnification, plaintiffs were required to allege that their injury was due solely to the negligence or wrongful acts of the city defendants, and plaintiffs failed to make any such allegations or even allege that the city defendants had any duty toward them.

Damages -- Punitive Damages -- Municipality's Emergency Demolition of Unsafe Building

8. Plaintiffs failed to state a claim for punitive damages against the City of New York and various city agencies and public officials based upon the partial demolition of their five-story building pursuant to the determination of the City's Department of Buildings that the building was in imminent danger of collapse and required emergency demolition. Even if plaintiffs' claims for declaratory and injunctive relief had survived dismissal, punitive damages are not recoverable against municipalities or public officials acting within the scope of their governmental duties.

OVERVIEW: While on parole for first- and second-degree robbery, defendant pleaded guilty to first-degree robbery. On the date of sentence, an assistant attorney general stated that no predicate statement was filed because of the plea. Thereafter, the Department of Correctional Services realized that defendant was a second violent felony offender and computed his time to run consecutively to the time he still owed on his original conviction. After his release from prison, defendant sued the State for compensation for wrongful confinement for the time period between September, 2005 and March, 2007. The court found, inter alia, that because defendant should have been aware that he should have been adjudicated a second violent felony offender under CPL 400.15, he could not have a legitimate expectation of the finality of an illegal sentence. The stated excuse of the assistant district attorney for not filing a predicate statement did not relieve the court of its obligation to resentence defendant in accordance with Penal Law § 70.45. Therefore, he could be resentenced without running afoul of the double jeopardy and due process clauses.


OUTCOME: The motion was denied, and the People were directed to file the appropriate second violent felony offender statement so that defendant could be arraigned on it, be adjudicated a second violent felony offender, and then be resentenced accordingly.


HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Sentence -- Second Violent Felony Offender -- Mandatory Resentencing to Correct Sentencing Error 

Defendant, who was not initially adjudicated as a second violent felony offender upon his 1992 robbery conviction as mandated by CPL 400.15, could lawfully be resentenced following his release from prison in 2007 in order to correct the sentencing error. Defendant had been released after the Department of Correctional Services recalculated his jail time to reflect that the 1992 sentence should run concurrently with his 1986 violent felony convictions, rather than consecutively, because he had not been adjudicated as a second violent felony offender. The sentencing court's inherent authority to correct an illegal sentence is not limited by CPL 440.40 (1), which affords the People one year to file a motion to set aside a sentence. Where the law mandates that a defendant be adjudicated a predicate felony offender but the sentencing court fails to do so, the error must be remedied by resentencing, regardless of the passage of time. Furthermore, resentencing defendant would not violate his constitutional right against double jeopardy or his right to due process. Since defendant was aware of the illegal sentence, he could not be deemed to have had a legitimate expectation of the finality of his sentence, nor could his resentencing be deemed a vindictive or retaliatory prosecution in response to his civil suit seeking compensation for his alleged wrongful confinement in the absence of any proof of an improper motive.

Crimes -- Predatory Sexual Assault against Child -- Rule of Lenity 

1. Defendant, who was charged with two counts of predatory sexual assault against a child for committing the crime of rape in the first degree against a female victim less than 13 years old when he was more than 18 years old (Penal Law § 130.96), was not entitled under the rule of lenity to reduction of those class A-II felony counts to the lesser class B felony of rape in the first degree (Penal Law § 130.35 [4]) because the two crimes have identical elements, but different permissible sentences and different plea bargaining restrictions (see Penal Law § 70.45 [2-a] [f]; § 70.80 [4] [a] [i]) and the statute provides no guidance as to when the lesser or greater crime should be charged other than as the result of a subjective assessment of the relative "heinous quality" of the particular defendant's acts. Crimes -- Disclosure -- School Records of Sexual Assault Victim 

3. Moreover, the records were not in the possession of the prosecution and thus were not discoverable.

4. Defendant, who was charged with two counts of predatory sexual assault against a child (Penal Law § 130.96), was entitled to in camera inspection via judicial subpoena duces tecum of the victim's mental health records. There were allegations that the victim suffered from bipolar disease and had a habit of lying on a regular basis. Since the victim's testimony would have direct bearing on defendant's guilt or innocence, information relevant to her credibility constitutes exculpatory material helpful to the defense that must be disclosed.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff state senators filed a motion for an order preliminarily enjoining defendants, governor, appointee, and secretary of state, from, inter alia, presiding over the senate; the defendants filed cross-motions for an order dismissing the complaint in its entirety and for an order changing the place of trial on the ground of improper venue.


OVERVIEW: After the 2009 election, a majority of the senators elected a temporary president and majority leader. Thereafter, two Democratic members joined 30 Republican senators in an effort to remove him as the temporary president. The governor appointed the appointee to break the resulting procedural deadlock. The court found, inter alia, that the senators properly characterized their action as one for a declaratory judgment. Since the governor was not authorized by N.Y. Const. art. IV, § 6 to appoint a lieutenant-governor, a fortiori he was not under a statutory duty to make the appointment. The defendants failed to make any showing to support their motion to change venue. Since resolution of the governor's power to make the appointment was not a task assigned to another branch of government, the controversy was not a political question. The senators alleged a usurpation of senate power that gives rise to a sufficient injury in fact falling within their zone of interest, which gave them standing. OUTCOME: The senator's motion was granted, the defendants' motions were denied, and the appointee was preliminarily enjoined from exercising any of the powers of the office of lieutenant-governor.



HEADNOTES

Trial -- Place of Trial -- Venue of Declaratory Judgment Action Challenging Authority of Governor to Appoint Lieutenant Governor to Fill Vacancy 

1. Nassau County was a proper venue under CPLR 509 for a declaratory judgment action challenging the authority of defendant Governor under NY Constitution, article IV, § 6 to appoint a Lieutenant Governor to fill a vacancy in that office. CPLR 506 (b), the special venue provision for a CPLR article 78 proceeding which requires that the proceeding be brought in any county within the judicial district "where the respondent made the determination complained of . . . or where the material events otherwise took place, or where the principal office of the respondent is located," was inapplicable, since the action could not properly be characterized an article 78 proceeding. A declaratory judgment action, as opposed to a more limited article 78 proceeding, is the appropriate vehicle to test the constitutionality of executive action. The separation of powers doctrine militates against the type of summary relief available in an article 78 proceeding where, as here, it is claimed that legislative or executive action is unconstitutional on its face. In addition, since plaintiffs' request for injunctive relief was incidental to their request for a declaratory judgment, the venue provision of CPLR 6311 (1) was also inapplicable to require that the place of trial be transferred to Albany County. Furthermore, plaintiffs failed to demonstrate that the inconvenience of unnamed material witnesses warranted a change of venue.

Courts -- Justiciable Questions -- Constitutional Challenge to Governor's Authority to Fill Vacancy in Office of Lieutenant Governor 

2. A declaratory judgment action challenging the authority of defendant Governor under NY Constitution, article IV, § 6 to appoint a Lieutenant Governor to fill a vacancy in that office presented a justiciable controversy. Although the dispute concerning the Governor's power to appoint a Lieutenant Governor arose from a "political context," it did not present a nonjusticiable political question that would violate the separation of powers doctrine. Furthermore, the fact that the Lieutenant Governor had already taken the oath of office did not render the action moot. A determination that the Governor was without power to appoint a Lieutenant Governor would have the immediate, practical consequence of preventing the Lieutenant Governor from continuing to perform the duties of that office or standing first in the governorship's line of succession.

Declaratory Judgments -- When Remedy Appropriate -- Constitutional Challenge to Governor's Authority to Fill Vacancy in Office of Lieutenant Governor -- Quo Warranto Proceeding Not Exclusive Remedy Available

3. A declaratory judgment action was the appropriate vehicle to challenge the authority of defendant Governor under NY Constitution, article IV, § 6 to appoint a Lieutenant Governor to fill a vacancy in that office. A quo warranto proceeding by the Attorney General (Executive Law § 63-b) was not the exclusive remedy available. Such a proceeding is intended to challenge the results of an election to avoid the risk of leaving a contested office vacant for a possibly protracted period. The action challenging the Governor's power to appoint a Lieutenant Governor does not involve a contested election.

Parties -- Standing -- Constitutional Challenge to Governor's Authority to Fill Vacancy in Office of Lieutenant Governor 

4. Plaintiffs, members of the New York State Senate, had standing to maintain a declaratory judgment action challenging the authority of defendant Governor under NY Constitution, article IV, § 6 to appoint a Lieutenant Governor to fill a vacancy in that office. Prior to the Governor's appointment, a temporary president elected by plaintiffs and the other members of the Senate would have presided over the Senate and also have stood next in the succession to the governorship (see NY Const, art III, § 9). Consequently, plaintiffs alleged a usurpation of Senate power that gives rise to a sufficient injury-in-fact falling within their zone of interest as to grant them standing to maintain the action.

Governor -- Powers of Governor -- Lack of Authority to Fill Vacancy in Office of Lieutenant Governor -- Preliminary Injunction 

5. The Governor lacked the authority pursuant to Public Officers Law § 43 to appoint a Lieutenant Governor to fill a vacancy in that office. NY Constitution, article IV, § 6, which requires that the Governor and Lieutenant Governor be elected at the same time, should also be read as requiring that the office of Lieutenant Governor remain vacant until such time as a Governor is elected. NY Constitution, article XIII, § 3, which authorizes the Legislature to determine how vacancies in "elective office" are filled but requires that the appointee run at the next election, cannot apply to the office of Lieutenant Governor. Consequently, Public Officers Law § 43, which authorizes the Governor to fill a vacancy in an "elective office" until the vacancy is filled by an election, cannot, consistent with NY Constitution, article IV, § 6, be interpreted to authorize the Governor to fill a vacancy in the office of Lieutenant Governor. Consequently, plaintiffs demonstrated their entitlement to a preliminary injunction preventing the Governor from filling a vacancy in the office of Lieutenant Governor.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed a motion to dismiss the indictment charging him with depraved indifference murder, pursuant to CPL 210.30, 210.20, 210.40, and on various other grounds. A federal court had previously vacated his conviction for depraved indifference murder after a Brady violation was established. Subsequently, the People sought to retry defendant on that count to which defendant has responded with his motion to dismiss.


OVERVIEW: Defendant's conviction resulted from the stabbing death of the victim in front of a night club. The Brady material involved a statement from another man that he had stabbed the victim two times before defendant allegedly stabbed the victim. The federal court had found that there was a reasonable probability that had such exculpatory information been disclosed to the defense, the case would have been different. The court denied defendant's motion to dismiss the indictment on an insufficiency ground and to reinspect the grand jury minutes as controlling case law provided no legal basis to grant the motion on those grounds. The court found that retrial was not barred on double jeopardy grounds since the conviction was vacated for the Brady violation, not on the ground that the evidence presented at trial was legally insufficient. The court found no merit to defendant's contentions that collateral estoppel or prosecutorial misconduct barred his retrial. The court further found that the interest of justice did not bar retrial since considering the factors set forth in CPL 210.40(1), defendant failed to make a prima facie showing of some compelling factor justifying such relief.

HEADNOTES

Crimes -- Murder -- Depraved Indifference Murder -- Retroactive Application of Judicial Decision 

1. Defendant, who faced retrial on a charge of depraved indifference murder after his conviction was vacated by a federal court on the ground of a Brady violation, was not entitled to dismissal of the indictment on the ground that the evidence adduced before the grand jury was legally insufficient to establish the offense of depraved indifference murder under current New York case law. Prior to the commencement of his trial in 2000, defendant had filed a motion to dismiss the indictment that was denied by Supreme Court on the ground that the evidence presented to the grand jury was legally sufficient. As a general rule, a court should not reconsider, disturb or overrule an order of another court of coordinate jurisdiction in the same action unless the defendant has made a showing that extraordinary circumstances exist which warrant a departure from the doctrine of the law of the case. At the time the determination was made with respect to defendant's prior motion to dismiss the indictment, People v Register (60 NY2d 270, 457 NE2d 704, 469 NYS2d 599 [1983]) was the controlling law in the State of New York, but the Court of Appeals subsequently overruled Register in People v Feingold (7 NY3d 288, 294, 852 NE2d 1163, 819 NYS2d 691 [2006]), holding that "depraved indifference to human life" is a culpable mental state. Feingold applied to all cases pending on direct appeal where the defendant had adequately challenged the sufficiency of the proof as to his or her depraved indifference murder conviction, but was not applicable to defendant, whose conviction became final on May 3, 2003.

Crimes- - Double Jeopardy -- Retrial on Depraved Indifference Murder Charge after Change in Law 

2. The retrial of defendant on a charge of depraved indifference murder after his conviction was vacated by a federal court on the ground of a Brady violation was not barred by the constitutional prohibition against double jeopardy. As a general rule, the double jeopardy doctrine does not preclude the retrial of a defendant who succeeds in having a conviction reversed on appeal, unless the conviction was reversed upon the ground that the evidence was legally insufficient, or the prosecutor engaged in prejudicial misconduct deliberately intended to provoke a mistrial. Here, defendant's conviction was vacated on the ground of a Brady violation, not on the ground that the evidence was legally insufficient, and no mistrial had been declared.

Crimes -- Collateral Estoppel -- Retrial on Depraved Indifference Murder Charge after Change in Law 

3. The retrial of defendant on a charge of depraved indifference murder after his conviction was vacated by a federal court on the ground of a Brady violation was not barred by the doctrine of collateral estoppel. Where, as here, a defendant has been acquitted by a jury of some of the charges in a multi-count indictment and the conviction has been reversed on appeal due to a trial error, the People are not foreclosed by collateral estoppel concerns from reprosecuting the defendant on the charge which resulted in conviction. At his first trial defendant was acquitted of the intentional murder charge, which indicated that the jury did not conclude that defendant intended to cause the victim's death. As a determination regarding the defendant's intent is not the sine qua non of a conviction of depraved indifference murder, the People were not foreclosed by collateral estoppel from retrying the depraved indifference murder charge.

Crimes -- Disclosure -- Failure to Disclose Exculpatory Material -- Order Barring Retrial Not Appropriate Remedy 

4. Defendant, whose depraved indifference murder conviction was vacated by a federal court, was not entitled to an order barring a retrial on the ground that, as the federal court had found, a Brady violation occurred during the first trial. An order barring the retrial in order to punish the People for the failure to disclose and to deter any future violations was not warranted, as the underlying principle of Brady v Maryland (373 US 83, 83 S. Ct. 1194, 10 L. Ed. 2d 215 [1963]) is not punishment of society for misdeeds of a prosecutor, but rather avoidance of an unfair trial to the accused.

Crimes -- Argument and Conduct of Counsel -- Order Barring Retrial Not Appropriate Remedy for Failure to Disclose Exculpatory Material 

5. Defendant, whose depraved indifference murder conviction was vacated by a federal court on the ground of a Brady violation, was not entitled to an order barring a retrial on the ground of prosecutorial misconduct. There was no reason to deviate from the federal court's determination that the appropriate remedial relief was a retrial.

Crimes -- Indictment -- Dismissal in Interest of Justice 

6. Defendant, who faced retrial on a charge of depraved indifference murder after his conviction was vacated by a federal court on the ground of a Brady violation, was not entitled to dismissal of the indictment in the interest of justice (CPL 210.20 [1] [i]; 210.40). Defendant failed to sustain his initial burden of making a prima facie showing of some compelling factor, consideration or circumstance which would render his conviction or prosecution on the underlying charges unjust. The Brady violation itself did not warrant a dismissal pursuant to CPL 210.40 (1) (e), and in view of the seriousness of the crime charged, the impact of the crime on the public interest weighed heavily against dismissal.

October 16, 2008, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner worker sought benefits from respondent insurer for a work site injury. The worker also settled a third-party action against respondent owner of the site where the injury occurred. The worker and the insurer sought a judicial determination of the amount of the lien to be enforced against the settlement under Workers' Compensation Law § 29(1), and of the insurer's obligation to make future payments to the worker.


OVERVIEW: The worker obtained a $ 600,000 settlement from the owner on the third party claim. The cost-of-litigation percentage was 21.56. The worker argued that the lien should have been limited because the $ 600,000 tort settlement represented only 24 percent of the counsel-estimated $ 2.5 million "full value" of the injured worker's case against the tortfeasor and the further fact that only 22 percent of the settlement proceeds included elements of damages for which the injured worker had been compensated by the insurer. The court disagreed. Pursuant to § 29(1), a compensation carrier was entitled to enforce its lien against the proceeds of any recovery from a third party, and the lien was enforceable against the entire amount of the recovery or settlement, albeit after subtraction of attorneys' fees and litigation costs. The funds to which the lien attached properly included sums for pain and suffering or other elements of damages not covered by workers' compensation. However, any portion of the settlement for loss of consortium was not subject to the lien, and the court found that it was appropriate to ascribe 10 percent of the settlement, $ 60,000, to that claim.


OUTCOME: The worker's petition was denied and the insurer's cross motion was granted. Among other things, the worker was ordered to pay the insurer $ 151,566 as its statutory net lien, with interest. In the case of weekly payments and qualifying medical reimbursements, the insurer was ordered to make payments at the rate of 21.56 percent of what such payments otherwise would have been, until its $ 259,100 credit was exhausted.


HEADNOTES

Workers' Compensation -- Carrier's Lien -- Reduction of Lien Based on Equitable Considerations 

Petitioner, a severely injured worker who received workers' compensation benefits from respondent and was awarded future benefits, was not entitled to a reduction in respondent's lien asserted against petitioner's third-party tort settlement based upon equitable considerations otherwise applicable to Medicaid liens under federal law. Under New York's workers' compensation scheme (Workers' Compensation Law § 29), the workers' compensation carrier is granted a lien against the full amount of any recovery or settlement in the third-party action, after subtraction of attorneys' fees and other litigation costs, regardless of whether the action may have been settled by the injured worker for less than its "full value" or whether the settlement included pain and suffering or other elements not covered by workers' compensation. The Legislature has deemed the lien provision to be an appropriate cost-containment measure in order to balance the goal of protecting injured workers against the necessity of protecting the legitimate business and economic interests of employers and their workers' compensation insurers. It would thus require legislative action to change the statutory scheme to allow for the reduction of the carrier's lien based on equitable considerations.

Hurrell-Harring v. State

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff indigents filed an action against defendant State, seeking a declaration that their constitutional rights were being violated due to systemic deficiencies in the manner of providing a defense to indigent individuals in five designated counties. They also sought injunctive relief requiring the State to provide a system of public defense in such counties. The State moved to dismiss the complaint.


OVERVIEW: The indigents had criminal charges pending against them in the counties. The State claimed that a declaratory judgment action could not be brought to challenge whether they were receiving effective assistance of counsel. The supreme court held that the indigents did not have an adequate remedy at law. They sought prospective relief based upon the likelihood that their constitutional rights would be violated. Their allegations illustrated their claims that the attorneys who represented indigent defendants in the counties were overworked and could not provide an appropriate defense. The indigents had standing to maintain the action. They were not seeking to assert the rights of third parties. Allegations of systemic deficiencies, which gave rise to a strong likelihood that the indigents' constitutional right to effective assistance of counsel would be violated, stated a justiciable cause of action. The counties were necessary parties. The complaint sought injunctive relief requiring criminal defense services to comply with constitutional and legal requirements, which, absent an amendment to County Law § 722, would require the counties to fund significant increases in attorneys fees.


OUTCOME: The supreme court conditionally denied the State's motion to dismiss. The indigents had to serve and file a second amended complaint adding the counties as parties defendant within 30 days. Unless the indigents complied with the condition, the State's motion to dismiss would be granted in its entirety. In that event, the State could submit a proposed judgment on notice to the indigents, supported by an affirmation of such non-compliance.

SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed in part and modified in part by Santora v. Silver, 61 AD3d 621, 878 NYS2d 33, 2009 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 3315 (N.Y. App. Div. 1st Dep't, Apr. 30, 2009)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendants, an assemblyman and former counsel, moved to dismiss plaintiff taxpayer's State Finance Law § 123-b action for, inter alia, lack of jurisdiction, failure to state a cause of action, and/or lack of standing.


OVERVIEW: The taxpayer sought money damages from the assemblyman and his former chief legal counsel for the sum paid by the state in settlement of a sexual misconduct/harassment action filed by a female employee of the Assembly who was allegedly raped and sexually harassed by former counsel. The action was settled with the state paying $ 500,000 and former counsel paying $ 7,500. The taxpayer claimed that the settlement should have come from the pockets of the assemblyman and former counsel, and not from the taxpayers. The court found, inter alia, that there was no allegation that the assemblyman, in the course of his duties as the Assembly Speaker, caused the wrongful expenditure, misappropriation, misapplication, or any other illegal or unconstitutional disbursement of state funds. It was the Attorney General, who was required to defend that assemblyman, who agreed to the settlement pursuant to Public Officers Law § 17(3)(b). The Attorney General's representation of the assemblyman did not entail the expenditure of public funds within the meaning of § 123-b. Finally, former counsel was not represented by the Attorney General and was personally responsible for part of the settlement.


OUTCOME: The motions to dismiss were granted, and the complaint was dismissed.


HEADNOTES

State -- Taxpayer Actions -- Wrongful Expenditure of State Funds

A citizen taxpayer action pursuant to State Finance Law § 123-b was dismissed pursuant to CPLR 3211 (a) (7) for failure to state a cause of action where it sought restitution to the State from the Speaker of the New York State Assembly and his former chief legal counsel for the sum paid by the State in settlement of a prior sexual misconduct/harassment action brought by a female employee of the Assembly who was raped by the chief legal counsel following a work-related function outside the office. The complaint failed to allege any illegal or wrongful expenditure of state funds on the Speaker's behalf, even if the payment of damages could have been avoided had the Speaker terminated counsel's state employment prior to the rape based on his history of sexual harassment of female employees. In any event, it was the determination of the Attorney General, made pursuant to Public Officers Law § 17 (3) (b), that the settlement of the action was in the best interests of the State, and plaintiff could not utilize a citizen taxpayer action to challenge the Attorney General's handling of civil litigation brought against the State and against a public officer in connection with his official duties. The Attorney General's representation of public officers does not entail the expenditure of public funds within the meaning of the State Finance Law nor did plaintiff allege any illegal or unconstitutional conduct in connection with the Attorney General's handling of the civil action. Furthermore, any judicial scrutiny of statutorily mandated nonfiscal activity of the Attorney General would be violative of the separation of powers doctrine.

CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant wife opposed plaintiff husband's motion for summary judgment in their divorce action on the ground that their separation agreement did not comport with the health care notice required by Domestic Relations Law § 177.


OVERVIEW: Approximately one year after the parties executed and recorded a separation agreement, the husband filed an action for divorce based upon conversion of the separation agreement. The wife claimed that the separation agreement was unenforceable because it was unconscionable and void because it was induced by misrepresentations. The court found that § 177 could not be literally applied without causing an absurd result. Accordingly, the court created a notice that contained the requisite information about the availability of health insurance required by § 177, and the parties executed it. As a result, the husband was entitled to summary judgment.


OUTCOME: The motion was granted; in light of delays caused by issues arising after submission of the motion, the court declined to grant any continuance to the wife.


HEADNOTES

Husband and Wife -- Divorce -- Awareness of Potential Loss of Health Insurance Coverage

In an action for divorce and ancillary relief based upon the parties' separation agreement, which agreement was executed before the effective date of the statutory requirement that parties to a divorce action be made aware of the potential loss of health insurance coverage (Domestic Relations Law § 177), plaintiff and defendant were required to execute a statement regarding their awareness of the potential loss of health insurance coverage as a prerequisite for being granted a conversion divorce. The statement, however, did not need to contain the exact language included in Domestic Relations Law § 177 (1), as a literal application of the statute would lead to an absurd result. Rather, after notice regarding the potential loss was provided by the court to the parties as required by Domestic Relations Law § 177 (2), it was sufficient to require them to recite slightly different language that satisfied the Legislature's intent.

Matter of Regenstreif v. Board of Assessors


1202/08


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NASSAU COUNTY


20 Misc. 3d 787; 859 N.Y.S.2d 857; 2008 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3379; 2008 NY Slip Op 28221



June 18, 2008, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioners limited liability company (LLC) and its member brought a CPLR art. 78 proceeding against respondents board and review commission, seeking an order vacating the decision of a hearing officer rendered at the conclusion of a small claims assessment review (SCAR) hearing pursuant to RPTL 730 which held that the SCAR provisions did not apply because the dwelling was owned by the LLC and thus was not "owner occupied."


OVERVIEW: The member was the sole organizer and member of the LLC, and there was no controversy that the property was the member's residence. The court found that the application for basic school tax assessment relief (STAR) benefits was denied without stating an explicit reason. The hearing officer's declination to consider the matter in a SCAR proceeding was without rational basis. RPTL 730 did not preclude consideration of homes whose title was held by an LLC. A CPLR art. 78 proceeding was an appropriate form for requesting relief. It would have been inappropriate for the court to determine the value of the subject premises on the limited record before it. The member and the LLC had the opportunity to seek review of the denial of their request for a basic school tax assessment relief exemption by the review commission, and therefore, failed to exhaust their administrative remedies. The member and the LLC failed to demonstrate that an exception to the exhaustion of administrative remedies doctrine was applicable.


OUTCOME: The petition was granted to the extent that the matter was remanded to the review commission with a directive that it schedule a SCAR hearing as soon as practicable, and that such hearing be conducted by a different hearing officer. The portion of the petition which sought to compel a basic STAR exemption was denied, without prejudice to renewal upon a final determination of the review commission.


HEADNOTES

Taxation -- Assessment -- Availability of Small Claims Assessment Review Where Limited Liability Company Holds Legal Title

1. The refusal of the Hearing Officer in a small claims assessment review (SCAR) proceeding to entertain petitioner's application for a basic school tax assessment relief exemption (see RPTL 425 [3]) for his one-family home on the ground that the dwelling was not "owner occupied" as required pursuant to RPTL 730 (1) (b) because the record owner of the property was petitioner's single-member limited liability company (LLC) lacked a rational basis. The statute was intended to provide an expedited method for the review of assessments on one-, two- and three-family "owner-occupied" homes used exclusively for residential purposes. The statute does not preclude review in a SCAR proceeding merely because an LLC owns the property. Here, it was undisputed that petitioner used the home as his residence, and he was thus eligible, as the beneficial owner, to utilize the SCAR procedure.

Taxation -- Assessment -- Availability of STAR Exemption Where Limited Liability Company Holds Legal Title

2. Petitioner was not disqualified from receiving a basic school tax assessment relief (STAR) exemption (see RPTL 425 [3]) for his one-family home used as his primary residence merely because the property was owned by his single-member limited liability company (LLC). The statute was designed to provide school tax relief to owners of one-, two- and three-family homes by providing an exemption for a designated portion of the total assessment of their property. The statute does not preclude a STAR exemption merely because legal title to the property is held by an LLC.

Administrative Law -- Failure to Exhaust Administrative Remedies -- Review of Denial of Application for STAR Exemption

3. In a CPLR article 78 proceeding challenging the denial of petitioner's application for a basic school tax assessment relief (STAR) exemption, the portion of the petition seeking to compel the respondents to grant a basic STAR exemption was dismissed due to petitioner's failure to exhaust available administrative remedies. Although the court held that the Hearing Officer improperly determined that the petitioner was disqualified from proceeding under the small claims assessment review provisions in seeking a STAR exemption, petitioner had the opportunity below to seek review of the denial of the exemption by the Assessment Review Commission, and could not demonstrate that further administrative proceedings would have been futile so as to avoid application of the exhaustion of administrative remedies doctrine.

Larabee v. Governor of State of N.Y.


112301/07


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK COUNTY


20 Misc. 3d 866; 860 N.Y.S.2d 886; 2008 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 3307; 2008 NY Slip Op 28217; 239 N.Y.L.J. 116



June 11, 2008, Decided


SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Affirmed by Larabee v. Governor of the N.Y., 880 NYS2d 256, 2009 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 4126 (N.Y. App. Div. 1st Dep't, June 2, 2009)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiff judges sued defendants governor, state senate, state assembly, and State, alleging that the judges had been improperly denied an increase in compensation in violation of the separation of powers doctrine. The judges moved for summary judgment.


OVERVIEW: Although the senate had passed a bill providing for a judicial pay raise, a companion bill was never passed by the assembly, apparently based on the governor's threat to veto it unless the legislature also adopted a campaign finance reform proposal. In a prior decision, the court found that the assembly refused to pass a bill for a judicial pay raise that did not also contain a legislative salary increase. No affidavit denying the existence of this linkage was filed, so the court found that the judges' claim with respect to the linkage was established, and that the agreed adjustment in judicial pay had not been enacted only because there was no agreement with respect to a legislative pay raise. There was no open policy issue to be resolved as all parties agreed that the judiciary was entitled to an adjustment and the amount thereof. In denying the entire judicial branch of government a pay raise for almost a decade, the political branches of the State government had used the judicial pay issue as a pawn in dealing with the unresolved political issue of legislative compensation, and this linkage was an unconstitutional interference on the independence of the judiciary.


OUTCOME: The judges' summary judgment motion was granted to the extent of declaring that the governor, the state senate, the state assembly, and the State, through the practice of linkage, had unconstitutionally abused their power by depriving the judiciary of any increase in compensation for almost a decade, and by directing those parties to remedy such abuse by proceeding in good faith to adjust the compensation of the judiciary.


HEADNOTES

Judges -- Disqualification -- Judicial Pay Increases -- Rule of Necessity

1. An action brought by four judges of the Unified Court System seeking a declaration that the legislature unconstitutionally deprived the judiciary of a salary adjustment since 1998 due to linkage of the judicial pay issue to measures dealing with legislative compensation and other unrelated substantive matters could appropriately be heard by a randomly assigned Justice of the Supreme Court pursuant to the rule of necessity, even though the Justice could be financially affected by the outcome of the case. Under the well-recognized rule of necessity, the personal interest of a judge in a particular case will not result in disqualification if the case cannot be heard otherwise. Here, since the state constitutional issues raised in the action under the separation of powers doctrine cannot be determined in any other forum than the State Supreme Court, the rule of necessity required that the assigned Justice hear and determine the issues presented.

Judges -- Judicial Salaries -- Constitutionality of Legislature's Failure to Increase Judicial Compensation under Separation of Powers Doctrine

2. The refusal of the State Legislature to provide the judiciary with a salary adjustment since 1998 due to linkage of the judicial pay issue to measures dealing with legislative compensation and other unrelated substantive matters constituted an abuse of power and unconstitutional interference with the independence of the judiciary under the separation of powers doctrine. The claim of linkage asserted by plaintiffs, four judges of the Unified Court System, was deemed to have been established for purposes of the motion for summary judgment due to the failure of any member of the Legislature or any executive official to submit an affidavit denying the existence of the linkage practice. It is the constitutional duty and the obligation of the Legislature, in order to insure the independence of the judicial branch of government, to provide compensation adequate in amount and commensurate with the duties and responsibilities of the judges involved. Although plaintiffs did not allege any specific interference with their independence in the performance of their judicial duties, they did demonstrate that the denial of a judicial pay increase for almost a decade due to the practice of linkage adversely affected the entire judiciary and was otherwise repugnant to the State's tripartite form of government. At this point, the proper remedy was limited to a declaration of unconstitutionality with the expectation that defendants would take appropriate remedial action.

20 Misc. 3d 235; 857 N.Y.S.2d 450; 2008 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 2406; 2008 NY Slip Op 28154

SUBSEQUENT HISTORY: Reversed by, Petition dismissed by, Judgment entered by Matter of New York Charter Schools Assn., Inc. v. DiNapoli, 60 AD3d 119, 871 NYS2d 497, 2009 N.Y. App. Div. LEXIS 154 (N.Y. App. Div. 3d Dep't, Jan. 15, 2009)


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: In a CPLR art. 78 proceeding/declaratory judgment action, respondents, the State, its comptroller, and the office of the state comptroller, made a motion for summary judgment dismissing the complaint by petitioners, charter schools and non-profit associations, seeking a declaration that the comptroller lacked authority to audit charter schools and a permanent injunction so that the comptroller could not conduct audits of charter schools.


OVERVIEW: Petitioners also sought a declaration that General Municipal Law § 33(2) and Education Law § 2854(1)(c) violated N.Y. Const. art. V, § 1. The court denied respondents' motion for summary judgment, finding the statutory provisions unconstitutional. While respondents argued that the charter schools lacked capacity to challenge the constitutionality of the statutes, the charter schools did not constitute political or governmental subdivisions of the State and, thus, were not subject to the same prohibition as municipalities. The comptroller could not be assigned administrative duties, such as the auditing of charter schools, as the charter schools were independent, not-for-profit education corporations. CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant moved pursuant to CPL 440.20 for an order setting aside his sentences with respect to his convictions for assault in the first degree under Penal Law § 120.10(1) and (3), criminal possession of a weapon in the second degree under Penal Law § 265.03(2), and criminal possession of a weapon in the third degree under Penal Law § 265.02(4) on the grounds that they were unauthorized, illegal, or invalid as a matter of law.


OVERVIEW: After counts alleging that defendant acted recklessly in aiming a loaded firearm toward a crowd of people were withdrawn, defendant pleaded guilty to the remaining counts in the indictment. The trial court noted, prior to accepting the guilty plea, that it could not promise what defendant's sentence would be. The People subsequently filed a violent predicate felony conviction statement which alleged that defendant was convicted of attempted assault upon a police officer. Based on that statement, to which defendant did not object, defendant was sentenced as a predicate violent felony offender. Defendant claimed that the predicate conviction was based on a jurisdictionally defective superior court information. Upon consideration, the court granted defendant's motion. With respect to the predicate conviction, after a felony complaint was filed and defendant executed a waiver of indictment, defendant pleaded guilty to two counts of attempted assault on a police officer under Penal Law §§ 110.00 and 120.08. However, the information improperly charged a nonexistent crime as one could not have a specific intent to cause an unintended crime and was, thus, defective under CPL 195.20.


OUTCOME: The court granted defendant's motion and ordered that defendant appear for the purposes of resentencing.


HEADNOTES

 [***1] Crimes -- Information -- Jurisdictionally Defective Information -- Failure to Charge Offense for Which Defendant Held for Action of Grand Jury 

Defendant was entitled to an order setting aside his sentence as a second violent felony offender, as his predicate violent felony convictions for two counts of attempted assault on a police officer were based on a jurisdictionally defective superior court information. In the prior prosecution defendant had been charged by felony complaint with attempted aggravated assault upon a police officer, but the superior court information to which he pleaded guilty contained two counts of attempted assault on a police officer. The offense he pleaded guilty to was not the same as, or a lesser included offense of, the offense for which he had been held for action of a grand jury (see CPL 195.20). The element of intent with respect to the crime of aggravated assault upon a police officer relates to the causing of physical injury, while the element of intent with respect to the crime of attempted assault of a police officer relates not to the causing of physical injury, but to the circumstances which make that result one for which defendant is strictly liable. As the lesser crime requires demonstration of an element not required by the greater, it is not a lesser included offense. Moreover, there is no express authorization for a superior court information to charge a nonexistent crime, and an attempt to commit the crime of assault on a police officer is a nonexistent crime, as one cannot have a specific intent to cause an unintended crime.

164th Bronx Parking, LLC v. City of New York


14232/2007


SUPREME COURT OF NEW YORK, BRONX COUNTY


20 Misc. 3d 796; 862 N.Y.S.2d 248; 2008 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 4079; 2008 NY Slip Op 28251; 239 N.Y.L.J. 19



January 17, 2008, Decided


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Petitioner applicant sought judicial review of the denial of his application, under Administrative Code of the City of NY § 20-321(b), by respondents Commissioner and Deputy General Counsel for the Department of Consumer Affairs of the City of New York, of a license to operate a parking garage.


OVERVIEW: After the applicant's first application was denied, the parties agreed it would submit a new one, which was also denied. The first denial said the applicant did not have a certificate of occupancy and questioned the accuracy of a residence address in the application. The certificate of occupancy was obtained and the address was explained, but respondents said the applicant was not of good character. The statute was unconstitutionally vague because it was unknown what conduct the applicant had to demonstrate to satisfy respondents it was "of good character." The words "good" and "character" were susceptible of multiple and varied meanings. A lack of interpretive statutory or regulatory provisions relegated enforcers and applicants to these two words. This was so imprecise that it was as if no standard of conduct was specified at all. The "good character" requirement did not distinguish between conduct that was permissible and impermissible in understandable terms with which an applicant could comply, so the denial was invalid. Neither the statute nor any regulation contained standards to guide the exercise of discretion.


OUTCOME: The denial of the applicant's application was annulled, and respondents were enjoined from denying the license to the applicant, until the statute was amended or respondents promulgated regulations remedying its vagueness. A determination of damages was left for a later stage of the proceeding.


HEADNOTES

Constitutional Law -- Validity of Statute -- Parking Garage Licensing Law Containing "Good Character" Condition Void for Vagueness

A municipal licensing statute authorizing the granting of licenses to operate a parking garage only to persons of "good character" (Administrative Code of City of NY § 20-321 [b]) is unconstitutionally vague on its face, as it lacks any ascertainable standards in the statute or the implementing regulations (6 RCNY 2-161) for evaluating a license applicant. The absence of objective standards as to what conduct an applicant must demonstrate to satisfy the "good character" condition creates the possibility of arbitrary and discriminatory denial of licenses by conferring unrestrained power on agency officials to consider conduct of poor character based merely on their own personal, subjective notions of "good character" unrelated to the applicant's fitness to operate a parking garage. The statute thus allows unfettered, arbitrary administrative decision making without a standard to enable judicial review.
