Jones v. State


S-16-0005

November 22, 2016, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [***1] Appeal from the District Court of Natrona County. The Honorable Daniel L. Forgey, Judge.


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: ISSUE: Whether cumulative punishment for attempted second-degree murder, under Wyo. Stat. Ann. §§ 6-2-104 and 6-1-301 (2015), and aggravated assault and battery, under Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 6-2-502(a)(ii) (2015), was barred by the Double Jeopardy Clauses of the Federal and Wyoming Constitutions as being multiple punishments for a single offense. HOLDINGS: [1]-Attempted second-degree murder and aggravated assault and battery each contained an element that was not contained in the other as attempted second degree murder required proof of malice, while aggravated assault and battery required use of a deadly weapon. Therefore the cumulative sentences which defendant received upon conviction for the separate offenses did not offend principles of double jeopardy; [2]-Defendant did not show that the statutes were unconstitutionally vague, either on their face or as applied to the facts of his case.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed.

No. 98-310


SUPREME COURT OF WYOMING


6 P.3d 1275; 2000 Wyo. LEXIS 147



June 23, 2000, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the District Court of Laramie County: The Honorable Nicholas G. Kalokathis, Judge.



DISPOSITION: Reversed and remanded.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellants sought relief from a decision of the District Court of Laramie County (Wyoming) denying appellants' claims for declaratory relief based on assertions that they were entitled to a rebate of excess recapture monies paid to the department of education pursuant to the recapture statute, Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 21-13-102.


OVERVIEW: Appellant school districts brought a declaratory judgment action claiming that they were entitled to a rebate of excess recapture monies paid to the department of education pursuant to the recapture statute, Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 21-13-102. The trial court denied appellants' claims and appellants sought review. The court reversed. The court concluded that the trial court erred in applying the rule of deference to the department's interpretation of § 21-13-102 because that provision was clear and unambiguous pursuant to its language. The court held that appellants were entitled to relief pursuant to § 21-13-102 because that provision required that if any school district rebated more revenue than the difference between actual revenue and 109 percent of foundation program costs based upon actual data, the department was to rebate the excess to that district.


OUTCOME: Trial court's decision was reversed and case was remanded. Court held that appellants were entitled to a rebate of excess recapture monies that they had paid to the department of education pursuant to the express terms of the recapture statute.

SUPREME COURT OF WYOMING


999 P.2d 649; 2000 Wyo. LEXIS 55



March 16, 2000, Decided


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] Appeal from the District Court of Natrona County. Honorable W. Thomas Sullins, Judge.



DISPOSITION: The order of judgment and sentence affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Appellant mother, convicted of felony child endangerment, appealed from a decision of the District Court of Natrona County (Wyoming), contending that constitutional and evidentiary errors required reversal of her conviction.


OVERVIEW: Appellant, convicted of felony child endangerment after delaying medical treatment for her severely burned daughter, appealed, contending constitutional and evidentiary errors required reversal. The court affirmed. Because appellant, free on bond, did not assert speedy trial rights until shortly before trial, prejudice due to pretrial anxiety generated by the lengthy delay was not sufficient to find constitutional rights violations. The child endangerment statute, Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 6-4-403, necessarily broad to protect children, was sufficiently definite to give notice concerning prohibited criminal conduct, thus, it was not unconstitutionally vague because it did not enumerate specific prohibited conduct. Although appellant claimed she was coerced to delay seeking medical treatment for her daughter, the record did not establish that she faced present, imminent or impending death or serious bodily harm, thus, the trial court did not err in refusing to give her defense instruction.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed. Appellant, free on bond, did not suffer significant prejudice when recording problems caused the lengthy trial delay. Although necessarily broad to protect children, the child endangerment statute was sufficiently definite concerning prohibited criminal conduct, therefore, it was not unconstitutionally vague by failing to enumerate specifically prohibited conduct.

OVERVIEW: Petitioner doctor challenged respondent Wyoming Board of Medicine's conditioning of petitioner's practice of medicine and requirement she pay certain costs of administrative proceedings. OUTCOME: The court reversed, finding the Wyoming Board of Medicine disciplined petitioner in violation of state statutes related to administrative due process and that the Wyoming statute allowing a lower standard for administratively disciplining the holder of a medical license versus other professions was unconstitutional under equal protection and due process.
