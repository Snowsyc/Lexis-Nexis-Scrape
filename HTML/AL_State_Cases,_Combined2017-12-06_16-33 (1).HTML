Appeal from Montgomery Circuit Court. (CC-10-1426). Tracy S. McCooey, Trial Judge.



DISPOSITION: REVERSED AND REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


OVERVIEW: Defendant's conviction for obstructing justice was reversed. It was error to deny defendant's suppression motion. The initial traffic stop was based on a mistake of law. Ala. Code § 32-5-215 did not prohibit driving with a cracked windshield. There were no specific ordinances related to the equipment on vehicles in the Montgomery Code of Ordinances. Neither the Alabama Code nor the Montgomery City Code had a general safety law. The officer had not been trained to make traffic stops based on cracked windshields. The traffic stop was improper under the objectively reasonable standard.


OUTCOME: Judgment reversed. Case remanded.

State v. Worley


CR-06-1879


COURT OF CRIMINAL APPEALS OF ALABAMA


102 So. 3d 435; 2011 Ala. Crim. App. LEXIS 37



May 27, 2011, Released


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] 
Appeal from Montgomery Circuit Court. (CC-07-475).
Ex parte Worley, 102 So. 3d 428, 2010 Ala. LEXIS 159 (Ala., Sept. 10, 2010)



DISPOSITION: REVERSED AND REMANDED.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: A trial court granted defendant's motion to dismiss felony charges under Ala. Code § 17-1-7(b). On appeal, the court reversed. The Alabama Supreme Court granted certiorari, reversed, and remanded to the court to evaluate the trial court's ruling in light of the evidence the State submitted in opposition to the motion to dismiss and to consider the questions of statutory interpretation that led the trial court to dismiss the felony charges.


OVERVIEW: Defendant was the former Secretary of State and had been charged with felony violations of abusing her office under § 17-1-7(b) as well as misdemeanor charges under § 17-1-7(c). The trial court dismissed the felony charges, claiming that the State was using the same evidence to charge her with both a misdemeanor and a felony. The court concluded that the State proffered evidence in support of the felony charges in additional to that it proffered for the misdemeanor charges, which the trial court failed to consider. The trial court erroneously viewed the felony statute as a broader, more general statute than the misdemeanor statute. The felony statute required that an official attempt to use her position to influence another's vote or political action while the misdemeanor statute was directed at the actions of an official or employee against a subordinate employee. They prohibited two different types of action and protected two different groups of people. The trial court's failure to consider all of the evidence proffered by the State, combined with its incorrect interpretation of the two statutes, led to the erroneous dismissal of the felony charges defendant.


OUTCOME: The court reversed the trial court's judgment and remanded for further proceedings.
