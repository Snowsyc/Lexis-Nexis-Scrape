N.B. v. Sybinski


No. 49A02-9904-CV-311


COURT OF APPEALS OF INDIANA, SECOND DISTRICT


724 N.E.2d 1103; 2000 Ind. App. LEXIS 255



February 28, 2000, Decided  
February 28, 2000, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] APPEAL FROM THE MARION SUPERIOR COURT. The Honorable John L. Price, Judge. Cause No. 49D11-9706-CP-926.



DISPOSITION: Affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Plaintiffs appealed from the decision of the Marion (Indiana) Superior Court, which granted summary judgment to defendants, ruling that the family cap policy of the state's assistance program was constitutional under the U.S. Const. amend. XIV and the Ind. Const. art. 1 § 12.


OVERVIEW: Plaintiffs, recipients of family assistance benefits who were no longer eligible for additional cash benefits upon the birth of an additional child, ("class") appealed the trial court's order in favor of defendant state, which held that the state assistance program's "family cap" policy was constitutional. The class asserted that the distinction between capped children living with their parents and those living with other caretakers lacked a rational basis and infringed on the fundamental right of family association. The court affirmed, holding that the rule did not require that capped children be removed from the home, but merely provided economic support for individuals who took on the responsibility of caring for someone else's child. As such, the cap was rationally related to its legitimate interest in altering the cycle of welfare dependency, and in finding appropriate placements for impoverished children. Nor did the cap infringe upon the right of family association.


OUTCOME: Summary judgment affirmed; the family cap policy was held to be rationally related to the state's legitimate interest in altering the cycle of welfare dependency, and in finding appropriate placements for impoverished children. The cap did not infringe upon the right of family association.

Griswold v. State


No. 32A01-9906-CR-219


COURT OF APPEALS OF INDIANA, FIRST DISTRICT


725 N.E.2d 416; 2000 Ind. App. LEXIS 175



February 18, 2000, Filed


PRIOR HISTORY:  [**1] APPEAL FROM THE HENDRICKS SUPERIOR COURT. The Honorable David H. Coleman, Judge. Cause No. 32D02-9906-CF-227.



DISPOSITION: Judgment affirmed.


CASE SUMMARY:


PROCEDURAL POSTURE: Defendant filed interlocutory appeal from judgment of Hendricks Superior Court (Indiana), which denied his second motion to suppress the results of a chemical test of his blood, which was administered after he was involved in an automobile accident that resulted in a fatality.


OVERVIEW: Defendant consented to a chemical blood test, which showed presence of marijuana and diazepam, after he struck and killed the driver of another vehicle. Defendant twice moved to suppress evidence of the chemical blood test. The motions to suppress were denied and defendant sought an interlocutory appeal, alleging that the chemical test was unconstitutional, under U.S. Const. amend. IV and Ind. Const. art. I, § 11, where the deputy did not have probable cause to believe defendant's driving was impaired. Judgment denying the motions to suppress was affirmed. The chemical blood test was not unconstitutional where Ind. Code § 9-30-7-3 did not require defendant to consent to the blood test, but merely provided that an officer could offer a chemical test to anyone believed to have been involved in a fatal accident. Defendant's consent to the chemical test removed the requirements for probable cause and a warrant.


OUTCOME: Judgment affirmed. Chemical blood test was not unconstitutional where it did not require defendant to consent to the blood test. Defendant's consent to the blood test after a fatal accident removed requirement for probable cause and warrant.
